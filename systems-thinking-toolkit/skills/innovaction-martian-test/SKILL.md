# InnovAction!™ — Difference-from-Existing Idea Generation (with Martian Test)

## R — Reading

> "Things that are new have at least one feature that is different."
> — Dennis Sherwood, Chapter 10

> "A complete description of chess, in the form of bullet points, at a
> sufficient level of detail to pass the Martian test, so that a Martian,
> when landing, could identify the game of chess and distinguish it from
> a game of football, a game of cards, or a board meeting, from your
> description alone."
> — Dennis Sherwood, Chapter 10

## I — Interpretation

Sherwood's branded method (InnovAction!™) rejects the blank-page myth of
creativity. Innovation does not happen in a greenfield — it happens on
ground you already know, by changing at least one feature. The method:
(1) compile a Martian-test-passing detailed bullet list of features of
the existing thing — context, product, industry, scenario; (2) pick ONE
feature; (3) ask "how could this be different?"; (4) elaborate
consequences. The Martian test is the operational completeness bar —
an outsider with zero context must be able to identify and distinguish
the thing from neighbors using your description alone. Without it the
feature list stays too thin to perturb productively, and the team ends
up generating trivial variants ("our product, but bigger"). With it,
each feature becomes a perturbation handle: regulatory regime, customer
demographics, tech stack, supply chain, capital cost, business-model
slice — each can be flipped to generate a non-trivial alternative.
"New must be different" is necessary; "different may be new" is enough
to start.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. Scenario-planning workshop producing trivial variants of today
   ("same world, GDP 5% higher").
2. Product team brainstorming a next-generation offering and stuck on
   blank-page incrementalism.
3. Business-model exploration where the team cannot articulate what
   would be *different* about a new offering.
4. Strategy off-site where the team needs 3-4 plausible 2030 worlds for
   stress-testing levers.
5. Innovation pipeline where ideas all cluster around the same dominant
   feature and need orthogonal perturbation.

## E — Execution

1. **Build the Martian-test-density feature list.** Enumerate the
   existing thing's features in bullet form at a level of detail
   sufficient for a context-free outsider to distinguish it from
   neighbors. Categories that work in business contexts: regulatory
   regime, customer demographics, technology stack, distribution
   channel, capital structure, supply chain, business-model slice
   (who pays whom for what), competitive landscape, switching cost,
   network density. Completion: 20-40 bullet features, each
   independently checkable, and the list passes a peer "could a
   Martian distinguish this from {neighbor thing}?" review.

2. **Select ONE feature per perturbation pass.** Perturbing multiple
   features simultaneously collapses the discipline (you're back to
   blank-page). One feature × one scenario. Completion: each
   scenario candidate has exactly one explicit perturbation locus.

3. **Ask "how could this feature be different?" — radically, not
   incrementally.** "Bigger market" is incremental; "customer is a
   regulator paying for prevention, not an end-user paying for cure"
   is radical. Aim for differences a Martian would also distinguish.
   Completion: each perturbation produces an output identifiable as
   substantively distinct from the source.

4. **Elaborate consequences.** Walk through which other features must
   then change, which lever settings the perturbed world requires.
   Combine with `strategy-lever-and-cascade` to feed the 3×N
   scenario-planning table. Completion: each perturbed feature paired
   with 3-5 downstream consequences and one or two implications for
   current lever settings.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use when:

- The team already uses TRIZ or morphological analysis as established
  practice — InnovAction!™ duplicates these methods with weaker
  empirical backing (see below). Stick to the existing practice.
- The decision needs a single recommended option, not a divergent
  ideation pass (e.g., investment commit, hire/no-hire). Use after
  divergence is complete, not as a substitute for convergence.
- Time horizon is short and execution-bound — InnovAction!™ produces
  worlds-and-options, not next-week tactical fixes.

### Author-warned failure modes

- **Skipping the Martian-test step.** Without the completeness bar,
  feature lists stay shallow and perturbations stay trivial. The
  test is the operational discipline that makes the method work.
- **Perturbing multiple features at once.** Collapses to blank-page
  brainstorming, which the method exists to reject.
- **Confusing "different" with "better."** The method generates
  *different* futures; whether they are *desirable* is a separate
  evaluation step (sk08 lever-reverse-engineering carries this load).

### Author's blind spots / period limitations — PROMINENT

These limitations are non-negotiable per user override and must be
visible to anyone activating this skill:

- **Strong overlap with TRIZ (Genrich Altshuller, 1946+).** TRIZ is a
  Soviet-origin systematic-innovation framework with 40 inventive
  principles, contradiction-matrix tooling, and decades of industrial
  application. InnovAction!™ is a less-developed cousin. If the
  team already uses TRIZ, this skill duplicates effort with weaker
  tooling.
- **Strong overlap with morphological analysis (Fritz Zwicky, ~1947).**
  Morphological analysis enumerates parameter dimensions and their
  possible values in a matrix, then explores combinations — structurally
  identical to "list features and perturb one at a time," with a
  longer empirical track record in aerospace, foresight, and policy
  analysis.
- **Sherwood-specific branding (™) — not externally validated against
  TRIZ in any controlled comparison.** The method appears in Sherwood's
  own "Smart Things to Know about Innovation and Creativity" and
  Chapter 10 of this book; no independent peer-reviewed comparison
  against TRIZ or morphological analysis exists in the verified-units
  evidence.
- **Risk: workshop output quality depends on facilitator skill, which
  Sherwood under-emphasizes.** Like all workshop methods, the quality
  of the resulting feature list and perturbations depends heavily on
  the facilitator's craft (which categories to enumerate, when to
  push for "more radical," when to stop). Sherwood presents the method
  as method-driven; in practice it is facilitator-driven.
- **Period: pre-Lean-Startup (2011), pre-Design-Thinking-mainstreaming
  (post-2010 IDEO/Stanford d.school wave), pre-Jobs-to-be-Done
  formalization (post-Christensen 2016).** Competing methods now have
  more evidence-based grounding: Lean Startup uses validated learning
  loops; Design Thinking uses user-observation discipline; JTBD
  anchors on causal customer-progress mechanics. InnovAction!™'s
  perturbation logic remains valid but the surrounding methodological
  landscape has moved forward.

### Author's broader blind spots (BOOK_OVERVIEW Section 3)

- **Consultant-rescue narrative arc.** Every Sherwood case ends in
  workshop lightbulb-moment. No documented failures, no engagement
  where InnovAction!™ produced trivial output the team rejected.
- **Manager-as-protagonist framing.** The method assumes a unitary
  decision-maker with intervention authority. Coordination failure,
  regulatory capture, principal-agent dynamics are outside frame.

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **TRIZ:** structurally similar perturbation logic but with empirically-
  validated inventive-principles catalog. Preferred when available.
- **Morphological analysis:** matrix-based parameter perturbation;
  longer track record.
- **SCAMPER:** simpler perturbation prompts (Substitute, Combine, Adapt,
  Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse); less rigorous than
  InnovAction!™ but quicker for short ideation passes.
- **Design Thinking / Lean Startup:** different methodological stance
  — start from user observation / hypothesis testing, not from
  feature perturbation. Complementary, not competing.
