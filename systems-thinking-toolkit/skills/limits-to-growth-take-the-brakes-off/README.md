# limits-to-growth-take-the-brakes-off

[English](README.md) | [日本語](README.ja.md) | [繁體中文](README.zh-TW.md)

Diagnose growth deceleration as an R-loop braked by a B-loop; relieve the constraint rather than push the engine.

## When to use

- A growth engine that used to compound is slowing despite more spend, more hires, or more campaigns each period.
- The default reflex in the room is "pedal harder" — double the budget, add headcount, run more experiments.
- You suspect a single binding constraint (capacity, talent, market segment, regulatory headroom) is absorbing every extra unit of input.

## How to invoke

```
/systems-thinking-toolkit:limits-to-growth
```

## What you get

- The central reinforcing loop drawn in ≤ 6 nodes.
- 3-7 candidate balancing loops, each named with its limiting resource.
- ONE named binding constraint, with the diagnostic test that confirmed it.
- A constraint-relief intervention (named, costed, timelined) with the explicit prediction that the R-loop will spin at approximately the pre-deceleration rate.
- The NEXT binding brake that will queue up after this one is relieved.

## Boundaries

- NOT for genuine exogenous shocks (demand crash, regulatory step, competitor entry) — those are different diagnoses.
- NOT for cases where the engine itself failed (pre-product-market-fit, quality regression, vicious-circle bust mode) — there is no R-loop to relieve.
- NOT for hard limits with no feasible relief — in that case, "take the brakes off" becomes "accept a smaller steady-state and stop burning cash."

## Source

Dennis Sherwood, *Seeing the Forest for the Trees* (2002), Chapter 8 (limits-to-growth + take-the-brakes-off) and Chapter 13 (car-dealership quantitative confirmation).
