# cld-overlay

Draw one Mermaid CLD per stakeholder perspective; overlay them on a shared canvas; find the policy that improves all CLDs (straddle finding). Stop forcing, start listening.

## When to use

- Two to four stakeholder groups are stuck in a recurring conflict, and the same fight keeps coming back every quarter or every retro despite repeated mediation.
- You need a wise policy that works across perspectives — a third option no individual group proposed because their own CLD did not contain it.
- The context is mediation, partnership repair, vendor-customer escalation, or M&A integration where each side's mental model is internally consistent within its own frame.

## How to invoke

`/systems-thinking-toolkit:overlay` or via the router `/systems-thinking-toolkit:stt`.

## What you get

- One CLD per stakeholder perspective, each drawn separately under `cld-craft` discipline (S/O signs in pen, fuzzy variables elevated, recognized as real by its drawing group).
- An overlay diagram (paper, whiteboard, or Mermaid `flowchart` with one `subgraph` per stakeholder) annotating the three signatures: shared nodes with conflicting S/O, one-side-only nodes, and synchronized R-loops.
- A named straddle policy traced through every stakeholder's CLD and validated as beneficial within each frame — plus an operational plan or hand-off to `team-mental-model`.

## Boundaries

- NOT for zero-sum disputes on a fixed resource (budget split, headcount allocation, fixed-pool bonus) — a straddle requires slack in a third dimension that does not exist there.
- NOT when key stakeholders are absent from the room (customers, regulators, unions, downstream users) — an internally elegant straddle drawn without them breaks on contact with the missing parties.
- NOT for single-decision-maker problems or power-asymmetric situations where one party can simply impose — surface the power dynamic first instead of sanitizing it as wise policy.
