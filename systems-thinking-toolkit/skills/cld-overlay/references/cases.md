# CLD Overlay — Past Application Cases

Three calibrating cases for the `cld-overlay` skill, all from Sherwood's
Chapter 9 outward-conflict protocol: the gold-standard three-perspective
TV workshop (c15), the buyer-contractor wise-redefinition + its
real-world failure-inverse (c20/c21), and the n=2 boundary case where
the outward overlay machinery collapses onto the same artifact as the
inward `team-mental-model` protocol (c17).

Read all three before facilitating a multi-stakeholder overlay workshop
— they encode the straddle-policy discovery pattern, the
operationalization-failure warning, and the small-n boundary where this
skill and `team-mental-model` share the same diagram.

## Case 1 — TV "Talent Problem" Three-Perspective Workshop (c15)

- **Problem**: TV company stuck in a recurring fight between executives
  (cost pressure), star presenters (demanding more), and junior talent
  (frustrated by ceiling). Each previous reorg had failed.
- **Methodology applied**: Three separate CLDs drawn — one per
  stakeholder group. Each diagram surfaced a different binding loop:
  executive cost-spiral, star bargaining-leverage R-loop, junior
  career-ceiling B-loop.
- **Conclusion**: When all three diagrams were laid out together,
  "develop young talent" appeared as a node that broke executive
  dependence on stars, relieved junior frustration, and trumped star
  bargaining cards.
- **Outcome**: A policy invisible inside any single CLD — visible only
  when all three were on the same table. This is the gold-standard
  straddle case: the wise policy was *structurally* absent from every
  participant's individual mental model and emerged only from the
  overlay. Cite this case whenever a workshop participant proposes
  "let's just draw one big diagram together" — the insight only exists
  in the separate-then-overlay sequence.

## Case 2 — Utility Outsourcing + Railtrack / Balfour Beatty (c20, c21)

- **Problem**: Buyer-contractor outsourcing relationships become
  adversarial — buyer squeezes price, contractor cuts corners, quality
  drops, buyer squeezes harder.
- **Methodology applied**: Draw the buyer's CLD (cost-control
  reinforcing loop) and the contractor's CLD (margin-protection
  reinforcing loop) separately. Both R-loops spin vicious in the same
  direction — Step 4 overlay signature (c), synchronized R-loops as the
  "force" pattern.
- **Conclusion**: The wise redefinition is to introduce a shared
  variable (joint long-term value, asset reliability) that converts
  both R-loops from vicious to virtuous mode. The straddle is a node
  neither original CLD contained.
- **Outcome**: c20 is Sherwood's pedagogical success — the utility
  outsourcing case where the redefinition was operationalized. c21
  (Railtrack / Balfour Beatty) is the real-world failure case showing
  what happens when the redefinition is *not* made: fatal
  infrastructure consequences. The inverse case is the warning that
  Step 7 (operationalize the straddle) is load-bearing — a straddle
  policy that exists on paper but is never instrumented and owned is
  no different from no straddle at all.

## Case 3 — Theater-vs-Dinner Couple (c17) — n=2 boundary case

- **Problem**: A couple argues every Friday — one wants theater, the
  other dinner. Compromise alternation satisfies neither.
- **Methodology applied (outward overlay frame, this skill)**: Each
  partner draws a CLD; surface reveals different underlying drivers
  (need for shared cultural experience vs need for intimate
  conversation time). The surface dispute is wrong-grained.
- **Conclusion**: A "dinner before theater" policy or "theater on
  weekends, intimate dinners weeknights" straddles both — neither side
  proposed it because their original CLD did not contain the time-axis
  split. Classic straddle: a third option no individual CLD held.
- **Outcome**: Interpersonal-scale validation of the multi-perspective
  move. This case is deliberately the same artifact under both lenses
  — at n=2 the outward (`cld-overlay`) / inward (`team-mental-model`)
  boundary dissolves and the same diagrams serve both protocols. When
  the case appears at organizational scale (≥ 2 groups), the protocols
  separate cleanly; this is the small-n collapse case that shows the
  underlying machinery is the same mental-model-surfacing move.
