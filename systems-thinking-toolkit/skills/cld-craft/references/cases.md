# CLD Craft — Past Application Cases

Ten calibrating cases for the merged `cld-craft` skill (v0.4 — absorbed
`loop-and-link-primitives` sk01+sk02):

- **Workshop discipline (sk03)**: UK back-office c01, TV "talent
  problem" c15, car-dealership c24 — boundary-via-dangles + Rule 4
  aggressive aggregation.
- **Fuzzy-variable elevation (sk04)**: generosity split-trick (Ch 4
  ce29), Skandia intellectual capital c26 — Rule 7 worked examples.
- **R-loop classification + spin trigger (sk01)**: Hatfield Rail c05,
  Ratner Jewelry c06 — vicious=virtuous structural-identity proof.
- **S/O link discipline (sk02)**: BIRTHS → POPULATION uniflow (Ch 4 /
  Ch 12), ce27 variance convention flip, Lake Chad c25 — Tier-1 → Tier-2
  Sterman escalation and convention audit.

Read all ten before running the full draw-classify-emit workflow.
Cases 1-5 calibrate Steps 1-10 (CLD drafting + fuzzy elevation); Cases
6-10 calibrate Step 11 (S/O signing + R/B classification + Mermaid
annotation per `loop-classification-protocol.md`).

## Case 1 — UK Investment Bank Back Office Workshop (c01) — BOTH layers

This case demonstrates both Sherwood's workshop discipline (sk03) AND
the R-loop classification primitives (sk01) on the same diagram. Read
twice: once for boundary discipline + fuzzy elevation, once for
vicious-spin trigger identification.

- **Problem**: Initial brainstorm produced ~30 candidate variables
  across IT, HR, ops, finance. Risk of cluttered-mesh failure (ce21).
  Trading volume rising, error rate rising, management exhausted.
  Headcount and IT spend already at ceiling; macho culture refused to
  name the binding constraint (ce05).
- **Methodology applied (drawing)**: Rule 1 declared boundary via
  dangles — VOLUME & VARIETY OF TRANSACTIONS as input dangle (external
  driver); IT SPEND as input dangle (executive decision). Rule 4
  suppressed all line-item costs into ABILITY TO COPE as a fuzzy
  aggregate. Rules 5/6 named all nodes as nouns. Rule 8 labeled every
  arrow with S/O in real time. Rule 12 threw out two earlier drafts.
  ABILITY TO COPE was elevated as the load-bearing fuzzy variable
  (Rule 7) — STRAIN ON MANAGEMENT followed.
- **Methodology applied (classification)**: The resulting loop was
  VOLUME & VARIETY OF TRANSACTIONS → WORKLOAD → ABILITY TO COPE (down)
  → INCIDENCE OF ERROR (up) → STRAIN ON MANAGEMENT → WORKLOAD. O-count
  was 0 (down-arrows are S-links between negatively-stated states, not
  O-links). Classified **reinforcing (R)**, currently spinning vicious.
- **Conclusion**: A five-node loop survived with two input dangles —
  compact enough to fit on one whiteboard; rich enough to explain the
  crisis. The vicious circle became visible only when fuzzy variables
  were on the page; the decision-relevant link (WORKLOAD → ABILITY TO
  COPE) was fuzzy on both ends and was the load-bearing connection.
  Same R-loop structure could spin virtuous if WORKLOAD were relieved
  (EFFECTIVE IT SYSTEMS dangle). The intervention point was the
  **trigger, not the loop**.
- **Outcome**: Lightbulb moment. Reframed the intervention from "more
  bodies / more IT" to "relieve workload by simplifying upstream
  transaction mix." Demonstrated the vicious=virtuous structural
  identity that calibrates every R-loop diagnosis hereafter.

## Case 2 — TV "Talent Problem" Three-Perspective Workshop (c15)

- **Problem**: Three stakeholder groups (executives, stars, juniors)
  each had a different account of why talent was leaking. Risk of
  one-perspective bias (Rule 10 failure).
- **Methodology applied**: Each group drew its own CLD independently.
  Rules 5/6 enforced noun-naming so the diagrams could be compared.
  Rule 10 surfaced disagreement: stars' diagram had a missing link
  executives had omitted. Iteration via Rule 11/12 produced a composite
  diagram both groups recognized as real.
- **Conclusion**: A wise policy emerged only when all three diagrams
  sat on the same table. A pre-workshop "objective" diagram by the
  executive sponsor would have failed Rule 10.
- **Outcome**: Composite CLD identified a leverage link the executives
  had not seen; the talent policy was rewritten around it.

## Case 3 — Car Dealership CLD → Plumbing Diagram (c24)

- **Problem**: Translation from a qualitative CLD to a quantitative
  ithink simulation. Risk of detail-explosion at the translation step
  (ce21).
- **Methodology applied**: Rule 4 ruthlessly suppressed; SATISFIED
  CUSTOMER BASE became a single stock rather than a customer-segment
  matrix. Dangle discipline: MARKET SIZE as input dangle (constraint);
  INVESTMENT RATIO as policy dangle (lever). Cloud symbols (g43)
  bounded the sources and sinks of customer flows.
- **Conclusion**: A simulation-ready diagram emerged with ~12 stocks
  and ~20 flows — small enough to run, rich enough to surface the
  saturation effect.
- **Outcome**: ithink simulation revealed that 50% investment ratio
  produces almost the same revenue as 100%, with double the retained
  earnings — the constraint-relief insight that connects to
  `limits-to-growth-take-the-brakes-off`.

## Case 4 — EFFECT OF GENEROSITY ON STAFF PRODUCTIVITY: Split Trick (Chapter 4, ce29)

- **Problem**: Is the generosity → productivity link S or O? Initial
  intuition says S (more pay → more effort). Sherwood's challenge: "if
  you are so generous as to be over the top, wouldn't the staff start
  taking advantage and slack off?" — pointing to an inverted-U.
- **Methodology applied**: Split into two parallel fuzzy intermediates
  per Rule 7: EFFECT OF GENEROSITY IN INCREASING PRODUCTIVITY (S,
  saturating curve flattens above moderate pay) and EFFECT OF
  GENEROSITY IN DEPLETING DRIVE (O, kicks in above threshold).
  Hand-drawn response curves for each.
- **Conclusion**: The single-label question was malformed. The split
  lets the CLD survive the non-monotonicity without lying — Rule 8
  stays honest because each replacement arrow now carries a clean
  single sign, which restores the even-O / odd-O arithmetic at Layer 2
  of the classification protocol.
- **Outcome**: Diagram correctly predicts inverted-U behavior.
  Decisions about generosity now reason about *which side of the
  threshold* the lever sits on, not just direction. This is the
  canonical worked example for the split-fuzzy-variable trick (Step 5)
  and the regime-dependence halt in `loop-classification-protocol.md`.

## Case 5 — Skandia Intellectual Capital Reporting (c26)

- **Problem**: Knowledge / intellectual capital is the canonical fuzzy
  stock. CFO orthodoxy says "not on the balance sheet → not real."
- **Methodology applied**: Skandia (1995) elevated intellectual capital
  to a formal reported stock, published *Visualizing Intellectual
  Capital* as a supplementary annual report. Edvinsson treated the
  fuzzy stock as measurable in principle even when no GAAP procedure
  existed.
- **Conclusion**: The "can't measure" objection is a cultural choice,
  not a methodological constraint. Fuzzy stocks can be operationalized
  when the organization commits.
- **Outcome**: First-mover precedent for intangibles reporting; cited
  by Sherwood as proof that the macho-back-office anti-pattern (ce05)
  is defeatable. This is the precedent to cite when stakeholders push
  back on Rule 7 elevation in Step 4b of the Execution flow.

## Case 6 — Hatfield Rail Crash & Railtrack Collapse (c05)

- **Problem**: Railtrack had been a darling of the privatized-rail era
  — share price up, contracts in, public trust climbing. One
  derailment at Hatfield (17 Oct 2000) preceded a collapse to
  bankruptcy within months.
- **Methodology applied**: Same reinforcing loop pattern as Case 1
  (reputation → contracts → revenue → maintenance investment → safety
  → reputation). O-count even — virtuous spin pre-Hatfield.
- **Conclusion**: No structural change occurred. A single external
  shock reversed the spin direction; the *same* R-loop drove the bust
  at the same pace it had driven the boom.
- **Outcome**: Bankruptcy within ~12 months. Predicted by the
  structure: an R-loop in vicious spin compounds at the same rate it
  compounded in virtuous spin. This case calibrates Step 11's R-loop
  flip-trigger naming protocol — a single external shock event is a
  legitimate trigger candidate.

## Case 7 — Ratner's Jewelry "We Sell Crap" (c06)

- **Problem**: UK jeweler with a successful teen-targeted reputation
  loop.
- **Methodology applied**: One R-loop on reputation; single utterance
  by Gerald Ratner at the 1991 Institute of Directors speech was the
  trigger ("we sell total crap").
- **Conclusion**: The reputation R-loop did not need to be rebuilt —
  it was already wired to spin either way. A single sentence flipped
  it.
- **Outcome**: Customer base evaporated within weeks. Business busted.
  Same R-loop, opposite spin, same compounding speed. This is the
  canonical proof that R-loop diagnosis must always be paired with
  *trigger* identification, not just structural classification.
  Calibrates the Layer-2 R-loop spin-direction protocol: a single
  utterance / signal is a legitimate flip-trigger candidate, not just
  shocks and saturation.

## Case 8 — BIRTHS → POPULATION Uniflow Sterman Fallback (Chapter 4 / Chapter 12)

- **Problem**: A modeler tries the reversibility test (Tier 1) on
  BIRTHS → POPULATION. Cause up (more births) → effect up (population
  rises). Cause down (fewer births) → effect... does not fall.
  Population doesn't drop when births drop; it merely stops growing.
- **Methodology applied**: The reversibility test produces an
  ambiguous answer. Fallback to Sterman (Tier 2): "When births are
  above what they would otherwise have been, is population above what
  it would otherwise have been?" Yes — unambiguously S.
- **Conclusion**: BIRTHS → POPULATION is an S-link; the reversibility
  test cannot prove it because births are a one-way (uniflow) inflow.
- **Outcome**: The CLD survives. Without the fallback, the modeler
  either mis-labels or omits the link. This is the canonical Tier-1 →
  Tier-2 escalation case for `loop-classification-protocol.md`.

## Case 9 — Variance Sign Convention Flip (Chapter 6, ce27)

- **Problem**: A team initially defines variance as BUDGET − ACTUAL
  (positive variance = over-budget = bad). Mid-project the controller
  redefines it as ACTUAL − BUDGET to align with finance convention.
  They forget to relabel the diagram.
- **Methodology applied**: Sherwood's convention audit: three of four
  S/O labels in the variance → action → actual → variance loop flip
  when the variance definition direction reverses. The diagram now
  claims an R-loop where reality has a B-loop.
- **Conclusion**: Words are load-bearing for diagram readability, not
  for system mechanics — but the labels *must* be re-audited when
  conventions change.
- **Outcome**: Caught in workshop; relabeled. Without the audit the
  team would have predicted runaway divergence where the system
  actually self-stabilizes. This is the canonical case for the
  variance convention audit step of `loop-classification-protocol.md`.

## Case 10 — Lake Chad Stock-and-Flow (c25, Chapter 12)

- **Problem**: Lake Chad shrank ~95% in 40 years. Modelers labeling
  INFLOW → LAKE VOLUME and OUTFLOW → LAKE VOLUME need to know which
  sign discipline applies.
- **Methodology applied**: Uniflow recognition. INFLOW is a one-way
  pipe (rainfall, river inflow); OUTFLOW is also one-way (evaporation,
  abstraction). Reversibility fails on both. Sterman test: when INFLOW
  is above-what-it-would-otherwise-have-been, LAKE VOLUME is above —
  S. When OUTFLOW is above, LAKE VOLUME is below — O.
- **Conclusion**: Stock-and-flow connections require Sterman discipline
  by default; reversibility is only for two-way logical links.
- **Outcome**: Diagram correctly distinguishes inflows (S to stock)
  from outflows (O to stock); arithmetic balance reveals the shrinkage
  as inevitable given the measured rates. Calibrates the "uniflow"
  audit flag in `loop-classification-protocol.md`.

## Case 11 — Plugin Self-Analysis (v0.8 modern agent-orchestration case)

- **Problem**: A solo developer (kouko) shipped 7 PRs of the same
  plugin (systems-thinking-toolkit v0.1.0 → v0.6.0) in a single day,
  with plugin mean evolving 105 → 109.67/A. Per-PR delta: +3.4 → +1.0
  → -0.5 → +0.5 → effectively monotone-decreasing. Multiple friction
  points encountered: parallel-subagent git index race; Sonnet 1M-context
  billing gate; Opus daily quota; audit subagent catching drift after
  each restructure with diminishing returns. The question: was
  continued rapid iteration paying off, or had it hit a wall?
- **Methodology applied**: cld-craft Step 1-11 on the developer's own
  prose summary of the 7-PR evolution.
  - Step 2 dangles: `([Developer Iteration Drive])` input, `([Anthropic
    Quota])` input, `{{Plugin Quality Target = 120}}` target, `((Plugin
    Mean Score))` output, `>Anthropic Rate-Limit Regime]` cloud.
  - Step 4 Rule 7 fuzzy elevation: `[Developer Cognitive Load]`
    (inverted-U / threshold response curve) + `[Plugin Internal
    Coherence]` (saturating curve).
  - Step 5 split-fuzzy-variable on `Drive → PR Throughput` (regime-
    dependent inverted-U): split into `Effect of Drive in Increasing
    Throughput` (S, saturating) + `Effect of Drive in Depleting
    Quality` (O, threshold-triggered).
  - Step 11 emission found one R-loop (virtuous spin, currently
    active) + two B-loops (Cognitive-Load brake + Limits-to-Growth
    brake on Internal Coherence). Behavioral signature line:
    `deceleration toward asymptote (per-cycle delta: +3.4 → +1.0 →
    -0.5 → +0.5 over 4 PRs)`.
- **Conclusion**: Classic limits-to-growth — the rapid-iteration engine
  (R-loop) is being braked by developer cognitive-load (binding B-loop)
  with audit-diminishing-returns queued as next brake. Per Step 11
  hand-off, the appropriate downstream is `cld-archetypes` Branch L,
  yielding "constraint-relief = cycle-boundary cognitive reset + scope
  quantization per PR" intervention. Case A4 in cld-archetypes/cases.md
  carries the archetype-level analysis.
- **Outcome**: First documented self-application of the plugin to its
  own development process. Demonstrates carry-1 cld-craft works on
  meta-context (LLM agent + human developer workflow), filling
  Sherwood's pre-platform-economy / pre-2010 agent-orchestration
  blind spot. Cross-ref: dogfood audit at
  `docs/superpowers/audits/2026-05-13-systems-thinking-toolkit-dogfood.md`
  Test A captures the full Mermaid block + dynamic predictions.
- **Calibration value**: this case is the canonical example of
  applying CLD discipline to **multi-agent / human-in-the-loop
  systems** — a domain category not in Sherwood 2002. The fuzzy
  variables (`Cognitive Load`, `Internal Coherence`) are
  agent-orchestration-specific and pair naturally with split-fuzzy-
  variable trick to handle the inverted-U response curve of
  iteration cadence on quality.

## Case 12 — Reductionism Failure (Sherwood Ch 1-3 pattern, v0.8 plugin contribution)

- **Problem**: A team confronts a recurring operational problem
  (declining customer satisfaction / engineering quality / sales
  productivity / team morale — the specific domain varies). The
  reductionist instinct: decompose the problem into independent
  parts, fix each part, expect aggregate improvement. The systems-
  thinking diagnosis: the decomposition itself destroys the
  connectedness that produced the emergent failure.
- **Methodology applied (NOT applied — this is the
  what-happens-without-CLDs case)**: the team attacks each part in
  isolation. Customer satisfaction drops → blame customer success
  team → add CS headcount. Engineering quality drops → blame
  juniors → add senior reviews. Sales productivity drops → blame
  pipeline → change CRM. Each fix locally rational; aggregate
  unchanged or worse.
- **Why the reductionist approach fails (Sherwood Ch 1 thesis
  operationalized)**:
  1. The named "parts" are NOT independent — they're nodes in a
     feedback loop. Fixing one part loads another (CS team grew →
     onboarding bandwidth dropped → new customer time-to-value rose
     → cohort retention dropped → CS load rose again).
  2. The emergent property (satisfaction / quality / productivity)
     belongs to the WHOLE system; it cannot be assigned to any
     single part. Decomposition silently denies the loop structure.
  3. The reductionist diagnostic — "which part is broken?" — yields
     false answers because every part is functioning to spec; the
     dysfunction is in the COUPLING between parts.
- **Conclusion**: This is the canonical "cost of NOT using a CLD"
  case. The intervention is NOT to fix harder; it's to STOP
  applying reductionist decomposition and run cld-craft. The team's
  first CLD invariably reveals a vicious R-loop (Case 1 back office
  pattern, Case 6 Hatfield trigger pattern) or a limits-to-growth
  with sliding brake (Case A1 industrial-revolution pattern in
  cld-archetypes/cases.md).
- **Outcome**: Calibrates the "when should I use cld-craft at all?"
  decision. Trigger phrases: "we keep fixing parts but the whole
  doesn't improve" / "every part is doing its job but the team is
  failing" / "we hired specialists and quality dropped" / "we fixed
  CS but retention didn't recover" / "each department is making its
  numbers but the company is sliding".
- **Sherwood provenance**: Ch 1-3 demonstrate this pattern via
  multiple unnamed-in-summary case studies; the plugin's BOOK_OVERVIEW
  (Stage 0) marks these as "demonstrate the cost of *not* using CLDs"
  (skeleton argument 1). This Case 12 is a META-CASE: it captures
  the reductionism-failure PATTERN rather than citing any specific
  Sherwood Ch 1-3 vignette verbatim. Treat it as a calibration
  exemplar for recognizing when a team needs cld-craft in the first
  place.
- **Anti-pattern to avoid (when applying this case)**: do not use
  Case 12 to dismiss every reductionist analysis as wrong. Many
  problems ARE locally reducible (a specific bug in one module;
  a specific underperforming sales rep; a specific equipment
  failure). Case 12 applies when the problem RECURS DESPITE
  successful local fixes — that recurrence is the loop signature.
  Sherwood's Rule 10 / stakeholder-recognition test is the
  pragmatic check: if stakeholders agree "we've been fixing parts
  but it keeps coming back", the case is in scope.
