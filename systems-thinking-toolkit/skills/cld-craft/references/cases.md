# CLD Craft — Past Application Cases

Five calibrating cases for the merged CLD-craft skill (`cld-craft`): three
from sk03 (workshop discipline) and two from sk04 (fuzzy-variable
elevation). Read all five before applying the skill — they encode the
boundary-discipline-via-dangles pattern, the split-fuzzy-variable trick
worked example, and the "fuzzy stock can be operationalized" precedent.

## Case 1 — UK Investment Bank Back Office Workshop (c01)

- **Problem**: Initial brainstorm produced ~30 candidate variables across
  IT, HR, ops, finance. Risk of cluttered-mesh failure (ce21).
- **Methodology applied**: Rule 1 declared boundary via dangles: VOLUME &
  VARIETY OF TRANSACTIONS as input dangle (external driver); IT SPEND as
  input dangle (executive decision). Rule 4 suppressed all line-item costs
  into ABILITY TO COPE as a fuzzy aggregate. Rules 5/6 named all nodes as
  nouns. Rule 8 labeled every arrow with S/O in real time. Rule 12 threw
  out two earlier drafts. ABILITY TO COPE was also elevated as the
  load-bearing fuzzy variable (Rule 7) — STRAIN ON MANAGEMENT followed.
- **Conclusion**: A five-node loop survived with two input dangles. Compact
  enough to fit on one whiteboard; rich enough to explain the crisis. The
  vicious circle became visible only when the fuzzy variables were on the
  page. The decision-relevant link (WORKLOAD → ABILITY TO COPE) was fuzzy
  on both ends and was the load-bearing connection.
- **Outcome**: Lightbulb moment for the team. Reframed the intervention
  from "more bodies / more IT" to "relieve workload by simplifying upstream
  transaction mix." The same diagram revealed both vicious and virtuous
  spin paths — would not have happened on the 30-node draft.

## Case 2 — TV "Talent Problem" Three-Perspective Workshop (c15)

- **Problem**: Three stakeholder groups (executives, stars, juniors) each
  had a different account of why talent was leaking. Risk of
  one-perspective bias (Rule 10 failure).
- **Methodology applied**: Each group drew its own CLD independently.
  Rules 5/6 enforced noun-naming so the diagrams could be compared. Rule
  10 surfaced disagreement: stars' diagram had a missing link executives
  had omitted. Iteration via Rule 11/12 produced a composite diagram both
  groups recognized as real.
- **Conclusion**: A wise policy emerged only when all three diagrams sat
  on the same table. A pre-workshop "objective" diagram by the executive
  sponsor would have failed Rule 10.
- **Outcome**: Composite CLD identified a leverage link the executives had
  not seen; the talent policy was rewritten around it.

## Case 3 — Car Dealership CLD → Plumbing Diagram (c24)

- **Problem**: Translation from a qualitative CLD to a quantitative ithink
  simulation. Risk of detail-explosion at the translation step (ce21).
- **Methodology applied**: Rule 4 ruthlessly suppressed; SATISFIED CUSTOMER
  BASE became a single stock rather than a customer-segment matrix. Dangle
  discipline: MARKET SIZE as input dangle (constraint); INVESTMENT RATIO
  as policy dangle (lever). Cloud symbols (g43) bounded the sources and
  sinks of customer flows.
- **Conclusion**: A simulation-ready diagram emerged with ~12 stocks and
  ~20 flows — small enough to run, rich enough to surface the saturation
  effect.
- **Outcome**: ithink simulation revealed that 50% investment ratio
  produces almost the same revenue as 100%, with double the retained
  earnings — the constraint-relief insight that connects to
  `limits-to-growth-take-the-brakes-off`.

## Case 4 — EFFECT OF GENEROSITY ON STAFF PRODUCTIVITY: Split Trick (Chapter 4, ce29)

- **Problem**: Is the generosity → productivity link S or O? Initial
  intuition says S (more pay → more effort). Sherwood's challenge: "if you
  are so generous as to be over the top, wouldn't the staff start taking
  advantage and slack off?" — pointing to an inverted-U.
- **Methodology applied**: Split into two parallel fuzzy intermediates:
  EFFECT OF GENEROSITY IN INCREASING PRODUCTIVITY (S, saturating curve
  flattens above moderate pay) and EFFECT OF GENEROSITY IN DEPLETING DRIVE
  (O, kicks in above threshold). Hand-drawn response curves for each.
- **Conclusion**: The single-label question was malformed. The split lets
  the CLD survive the non-monotonicity without lying — Rule 8 stays honest
  because each replacement arrow now carries a clean single sign.
- **Outcome**: Diagram correctly predicts inverted-U behavior. Decisions
  about generosity now reason about *which side of the threshold* the
  lever sits on, not just direction.

## Case 5 — Skandia Intellectual Capital Reporting (c26)

- **Problem**: Knowledge / intellectual capital is the canonical fuzzy
  stock. CFO orthodoxy says "not on the balance sheet → not real."
- **Methodology applied**: Skandia (1995) elevated intellectual capital to
  a formal reported stock, published *Visualizing Intellectual Capital* as
  a supplementary annual report. Edvinsson treated the fuzzy stock as
  measurable in principle even when no GAAP procedure existed.
- **Conclusion**: The "can't measure" objection is a cultural choice, not
  a methodological constraint. Fuzzy stocks can be operationalized when
  the organization commits.
- **Outcome**: First-mover precedent for intangibles reporting; cited by
  Sherwood as proof that the macho-back-office anti-pattern (ce05) is
  defeatable. This is the precedent to cite when stakeholders push back on
  Rule 7 elevation in step 4b of the Execution flow.
