# Loop and Link Primitives — Past Application Cases

Six calibrating cases for the merged primitives skill (`loop-and-link-primitives`):
three from sk01 (R/B loop classification — UK back-office c01, Hatfield Rail c05,
Ratner Jewelry c06) and three from sk02 (S/O link assignment — BIRTHS → POPULATION
uniflow, ce27 variance sign convention flip, Lake Chad c25). Read all six before
signing a link or classifying a loop — together they encode the
trigger-vs-structure distinction for R-loops, the uniflow Sterman discipline, and
the convention-flip audit pattern that catches a B-loop misread as R.

## Case 1 — UK Investment Bank Back Office "Carrying the Rock" (c01)

- **Problem**: Trading volume rising, error rate rising, management exhausted.
  Headcount and IT spend already at ceiling; macho culture refused to name the
  binding constraint.
- **Methodology applied**: Workshop drew the loop: VOLUME & VARIETY OF
  TRANSACTIONS → WORKLOAD → ABILITY TO COPE (down) → INCIDENCE OF ERROR (up) →
  STRAIN ON MANAGEMENT → WORKLOAD. O-count was 0 (the down-arrows are S-links
  between negatively-stated states, not O-links). Classified as reinforcing,
  currently spinning vicious.
- **Conclusion**: Same structure could spin virtuous if WORKLOAD were relieved
  (EFFECTIVE IT SYSTEMS dangle). The intervention point was the trigger, not the
  loop.
- **Outcome**: Reframed the problem from "we need more bodies" to "we need to
  flip the spin." Demonstrated the vicious=virtuous structural identity.

## Case 2 — Hatfield Rail Crash & Railtrack Collapse (c05)

- **Problem**: Railtrack had been a darling of the privatized-rail era — share
  price up, contracts in, public trust climbing. One derailment at Hatfield (17
  Oct 2000) preceded a collapse to bankruptcy within months.
- **Methodology applied**: Same reinforcing loop (reputation → contracts →
  revenue → maintenance investment → safety → reputation). O-count even —
  virtuous spin pre-Hatfield.
- **Conclusion**: No structural change occurred. A single external shock
  reversed the spin direction; the *same* R-loop drove the bust at the same pace
  it had driven the boom.
- **Outcome**: Bankruptcy within ~12 months. Predicted by the structure: an
  R-loop in vicious spin compounds at the same rate it compounded in virtuous
  spin.

## Case 3 — Ratner's Jewelry "We Sell Crap" (c06)

- **Problem**: UK jeweler with a successful teen-targeted reputation loop.
- **Methodology applied**: One R-loop on reputation; single utterance by Gerald
  Ratner at the 1991 Institute of Directors speech was the trigger.
- **Conclusion**: The reputation R-loop did not need to be rebuilt — it was
  already wired to spin either way. A single sentence flipped it.
- **Outcome**: Customer base evaporated within weeks. Business busted. Same
  R-loop, opposite spin, same compounding speed. This is the canonical proof that
  R-loop diagnosis must always be paired with *trigger* identification, not just
  structural classification.

## Case 4 — BIRTHS → POPULATION Uniflow Sterman Fallback (Chapter 4 / Chapter 12)

- **Problem**: A modeler tries the reversibility test on BIRTHS → POPULATION.
  Cause up (more births) → effect up (population rises). Cause down (fewer
  births) → effect... does not fall. Population doesn't drop when births drop;
  it merely stops growing.
- **Methodology applied**: The reversibility test produces an ambiguous answer.
  Fallback to Sterman: "When births are above what they would otherwise have
  been, is population above what it would otherwise have been?" Yes —
  unambiguously S.
- **Conclusion**: BIRTHS → POPULATION is an S-link; the reversibility test
  cannot prove it because births are a one-way (uniflow) inflow.
- **Outcome**: The CLD survives. Without the fallback, the modeler either
  mis-labels or omits the link. This is the canonical Tier-1 → Tier-2 escalation
  case.

## Case 5 — Variance Sign Convention Flip (Chapter 6, ce27)

- **Problem**: A team initially defines variance as BUDGET − ACTUAL (positive
  variance = over-budget = bad). Mid-project the controller redefines it as
  ACTUAL − BUDGET to align with finance convention. They forget to relabel.
- **Methodology applied**: Sherwood's convention audit: three of four S/O
  labels in the variance → action → actual → variance loop flip when the
  variance definition direction reverses. The diagram now claims an R-loop
  where reality has a B-loop.
- **Conclusion**: Words are load-bearing for diagram readability, not for
  system mechanics — but the labels *must* be re-audited when conventions
  change.
- **Outcome**: Caught in workshop; relabeled. Without the audit the team would
  have predicted runaway divergence where the system actually self-stabilizes.
  This is the canonical case for step 5 (convention audit) of the E flow.

## Case 6 — Lake Chad Stock-and-Flow (c25, Chapter 12)

- **Problem**: Lake Chad shrank ~95% in 40 years. Modelers labeling INFLOW →
  LAKE VOLUME and OUTFLOW → LAKE VOLUME need to know which sign discipline
  applies.
- **Methodology applied**: Uniflow recognition. INFLOW is a one-way pipe
  (rainfall, river inflow); OUTFLOW is also one-way (evaporation, abstraction).
  Reversibility fails on both. Sterman test: when INFLOW is
  above-what-it-would-otherwise-have-been, LAKE VOLUME is above — S. When
  OUTFLOW is above, LAKE VOLUME is below — O.
- **Conclusion**: Stock-and-flow connections require Sterman discipline by
  default; reversibility is only for two-way logical links.
- **Outcome**: Diagram correctly distinguishes inflows (S to stock) from
  outflows (O to stock); arithmetic balance reveals the shrinkage as inevitable
  given the measured rates.
