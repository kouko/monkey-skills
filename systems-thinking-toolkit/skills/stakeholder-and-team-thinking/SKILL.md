# Stakeholder & Team Thinking (Multi-Perspective Wise Policy + Mental-Model Harmony)

## R — Reading

> "A powerful way of identifying such policies and actions is by drawing causal loop diagrams from the perspectives of both parties and seeing if there are any policies that straddle both beneficially."
> — Dennis Sherwood, Chapter 9 (the outward / wise-policy claim)

> "A high-performing team is a group of people whose mental models are naturally in harmony, especially as regards fundamental values."
> — Dennis Sherwood, Chapter 9 (the inward / harmony claim)

> "In the context of teamwork, that is what leadership is all about: the active, continuous, pumping of energy into and through the team, especially when the going gets tough."
> — Dennis Sherwood, Chapter 9 (the sustaining-practice claim)

## I — Interpretation

Sherwood frames every collective — adversarial stakeholders or a high-functioning team — as a system whose behavior is an **emergent property of the underlying mental models** the participants hold about "how the world works." This single frame yields two paired but distinct protocols:

**The WHY (sk10's open-system framing).** A team is an **open thermodynamic system**: it sustains higher-than-random order only while energy keeps flowing through it from outside (leadership). The specific form of "order" that makes it high-performing is **mental-model harmony** — convergence on the underlying causal map, especially on fundamental values. Without continuous energy throughput, the team's order decays toward a random group of competent individuals who happen to share an office. The same frame, applied outward to groups in conflict, says: each side's mental model is internally consistent within its own frame; the surface dispute is rarely the actual disagreement.

**The HOW for outward conflict (sk09).** When two-to-four parties clash repeatedly, refuse the single-diagram trap. Each group draws its **own** CLD of the situation independently. Make the differences in causal beliefs visible on paper. Then search the *union* of diagrams for a policy or new variable that improves at least two of them beneficially — what Sherwood calls a **"straddle."** This is rarely the compromise midpoint; it is usually a third option neither side proposed because their original CLD did not contain it. The wisdom is not in adjudicating who is right. Both CLDs are right within their own frame; a higher-leverage intervention lives only in the intersection the multi-perspective view reveals.

**The HOW for inward sustainment (sk10).** When a team should share mental models but doesn't yet, the build process is the same surfacing-via-CLDs move, but the goal is **convergence**, not straddle-discovery. The maintain process is **continuous leadership energy** — active listening (Sherwood explicitly: passive listening fails), repeated value reinforcement, shared experience accumulation. The leader is **not a decision-maker on top of the system**; the leader is the energy pump that keeps the open system in its ordered state. Counter-intuitive prediction: a leader-less team is structurally a closed system and will decay regardless of individual talent. Individual excellence is necessary but not sufficient.

**The two protocols are a pair, not alternatives.** sk09 is *conflict resolution between groups whose mental models structurally differ* (find a straddle); sk10 is *team-building among people whose mental models can be harmonized* (negotiate convergence + sustain). Same machinery (mental-model surfacing via CLDs), opposite goal. At small scale (n=2 the theater-couple case, see `references/cases.md`) the boundary dissolves: the same CLD artifact serves both protocols. At organizational scale they are sequenced — sk09 surfaces *which* mental-model differences exist between groups; sk10 is the continuous-investment practice that brings them into harmony over time.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. **Outward**: Two departments (Engineering vs Product, Sales vs Operations, Risk vs Growth) have been fighting for months and the CEO's mediation keeps failing.
2. **Outward**: M&A integration where acquired company and acquiring company keep talking past each other on roadmap or culture.
3. **Outward**: Vendor-customer relationships sliding into adversarial mode (procurement squeeze, scope-creep).
4. **Outward**: Cross-functional initiative blocked because each function sees a different binding constraint.
5. **Outward**: Recurring family / partnership disputes where the same fight loops monthly.
6. **Inward**: Hired star engineers from FAANG; code reviews now contentious, decisions take 3x longer, morale dropping despite individual excellence rising.
7. **Inward**: A team that "used to be great" is degrading after the founding leader stepped back or left.
8. **Inward**: Rapid scaling (team doubled in 12 months) and the original cultural coherence is fraying.
9. **Inward**: Cross-functional task force assembled from competent individuals that consistently underperforms expectations.
10. **Hybrid**: Post-merger team integration — both protocols apply in sequence (sk09 first to surface between-group differences; sk10 second to sustain convergence among the integrated team).

### Language signals

- **Outward triggers**: "they keep arguing about X", "same fight every quarter / every retro", "we've tried compromise and nobody is happy", "stop forcing, start listening", "each side accuses the other of being short-sighted", "we need a wise policy".
- **Inward triggers**: "individually excellent but team is slow", "we built a band of brothers", "why are decisions taking forever?", "the team used to just *get* it", "we need more team-building", "code reviews / design reviews are contentious", "leadership vacuum".

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `cld-craft`: That skill teaches the craft of drawing one CLD well; this skill assumes that craft and adds the multi-CLD overlay move (outward) and the convergence-negotiation move (inward).
- vs. `loop-and-link-primitives`: Foundational ontology (R vs B classification + S/O signing). The team-as-open-system claim is grounded in the reinforcing-loop view of leadership-energy → harmony → performance → reputation → leadership-energy; recognising its collapse when energy stops pumping requires the R/B loop ontology.
- vs. `lever-vs-outcome-reframing` (sk07): sk07 is for a single decision-maker conflating KPIs with controls; this skill is for multiple decision-makers conflating their CLDs (outward) or a team whose members hold divergent causal beliefs (inward).
- **Internal distinction within this skill**: the outward and inward protocols share the mental-model-surfacing machinery but target different intervention points and have different success criteria. Outward succeeds when a straddle policy is found and validated against every CLD; inward succeeds when continuous-energy practices are installed and survive a quarter without leader-prompting. Do not mix the protocols in the same session — they require different facilitation stance (mediator vs energy-pump).

### Protocol O — Outward (multi-perspective wise policy)

1. **Identify the stakeholders** — list 2-4 groups in observable conflict. Halt if you cannot name a specific human group per CLD; "the market" is not a stakeholder.
2. **Convene each group separately** — never start with a joint session. Each group draws their own CLD of the situation, applying `cld-craft` (sk03+sk04 merged). Each diagram must satisfy Rule 8 sign-labeling and Rule 10 stakeholder-recognition discipline.
3. **Surface mental models, not positions** — facilitator asks "what causes what" questions, not "what do you want" questions. Each CLD should show causal beliefs the group treats as obvious.
4. **Overlay the diagrams** — lay all CLDs on one table or wall. Look for: (a) shared nodes labeled with conflicting S/O; (b) nodes appearing in one CLD but missing from others; (c) loops spinning the same direction (synchronized R-loops = "force" pattern).
5. **Search for the straddle** — identify a candidate policy, lever, or new variable that, if added, would improve at least two of the CLDs beneficially. Test it against each CLD in turn.
6. **Validate with all groups jointly** — only now bring the groups together to evaluate the candidate straddle against their own diagrams. Acceptance criterion: each group recognizes the policy as beneficial within their CLD (`cld-craft` Rule 10 — diagram must be recognized as real).
7. **Halt condition**: if no straddle exists across all CLDs, the dispute may be genuinely zero-sum — see Boundary. Do not manufacture a fake straddle.

### Protocol I — Inward (mental-model harmony + leadership energy)

1. **Diagnose: is the team an open or closed system?** — Is anyone investing continuous energy in surfacing mental models? "Continuous" means weekly+, sustained for quarters, not an annual offsite. If no, the team is functionally closed and decaying regardless of talent.
2. **Surface individual mental models** — each team member draws their own CLD of "what causes our team to succeed over the next 12 months." Use `cld-craft` craft. Halt if anyone refuses; mental-model surfacing requires voluntary participation.
3. **Compare CLDs side-by-side** — look for divergent S/O labels on shared nodes, missing nodes that one person treats as load-bearing, conflicting causal beliefs about fundamental values.
4. **Negotiate convergence on fundamental values** — focus on the value-level causality, not tactical disagreements. Sherwood is explicit: harmony "especially as regards fundamental values." Tactical divergence is healthy; value-causality divergence is the binding constraint.
5. **Install continuous energy practices** — schedule sustained shared-discussion time, not one-shot offsites. Active listening discipline (Sherwood p32 — passive listening fails). Repeated revisitation as new members join or context changes.
6. **Treat leadership as the energy pump** — leader's primary job is throughput, not decision-making. Halt condition: if leader role is unfilled or rotated quarterly, the open-system metaphor cannot operate; revert to other interventions.
7. **Re-surface mental models on team composition change** — every new joiner imports their prior team's mental model; re-run the convergence work, do not assume osmosis.

### When to run both (hybrid)

- Post-merger / post-acquisition team formation: run Protocol O first to surface between-group mental-model differences; run Protocol I second to sustain convergence among the integrated team.
- M&A success here looks like: Protocol O finds a straddle; Protocol I installs the energy practices that keep the straddle from being eroded by quarter-end pressure.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill when:

**Outward-protocol exclusions:**

- The dispute is **zero-sum on a fixed resource** (budget split, headcount allocation between rival teams with no slack). A straddle requires a third dimension that doesn't exist here. Sherwood's pedagogy quietly assumes there is always slack; in austerity, often there isn't.
- **Key stakeholders are absent from the room** — customers, regulators, unions, downstream users. Drawing CLDs only from internal perspectives produces an internally elegant straddle that breaks on contact with the missing parties.
- **Single decision-maker problem** — if one person holds authority and the others are advisors, this is not a multi-perspective problem; it is a `lever-vs-outcome-reframing` (sk07) problem.
- **Power asymmetry is the actual cause** — when one party can simply impose, the "wise policy" framing sanitizes a power play.

**Inward-protocol exclusions:**

- **Short-term task force or one-shot delivery team** — energy investment cost exceeds payback period. Use lighter coordination mechanisms.
- **Remote / distributed / asynchronous teams** — the book pre-dates 2020 hybrid work; the Nelson "shared dinners over years" pattern doesn't translate to async-first cultures. The energy-pumping metaphor assumes co-presence.
- **Psychological safety is the actual binding constraint** — if team members fear speaking up, surfacing mental models will produce performative agreement, not genuine surfacing. Fix safety first (see Edmondson's work) before applying this skill.
- **Cognitive-diversity-by-design teams** (red team, devil's advocate, audit) — harmony is *anti-goal* here; productive friction is the point.

### Author-warned failure modes

- **CEO-as-mediator anti-pattern (ce16, outward)**: a senior leader adjudicating right/wrong instead of surfacing mental models will reproduce the same fight. Sherwood: mediation by judgement fails; mediation by CLD facilitation succeeds.
- **Forcing the single consensus CLD (outward)**: a facilitator who insists on "one diagram" reverts the workshop to political negotiation and erases the mental-model gap that contains the actual insight.
- **Exhortation anti-pattern (c19 Coopers & Lybrand, inward)**: a leader who gives speeches about teamwork without investing continuous energy will produce a 100-individual-excellent / 0-team-coordination outcome. Speeches are not energy throughput.
- **Mistaking shared logo for shared mental models (inward)**: cultural artifacts (T-shirts, mission statements, all-hands) are decorative; without sustained CLD-level convergence work, mental models drift independently.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations

- **Power-and-politics sanitization** (BOOK_OVERVIEW Critique #2): Sherwood treats the buyer-contractor "wise redefinition" as if both leaders will act in long-term mutual interest. Real procurement runs on individual commissions, year-end bonuses, and career incentives — never drawn into the CLDs. If the actor's compensation is misaligned with the CLD's success metric, the wise policy is structurally undeliverable no matter how elegant the diagram.
- **Missing-stakeholders trap** (BOOK_OVERVIEW #5): Sherwood's TV-talent and theater-dinner cases include all relevant stakeholders by construction. In real corporate work, customers, regulators, unions, downstream teams, future-employees are typically absent — the CLD set is incomplete, and a "straddle" of the incomplete set can harm the absent parties.
- **"Mental-model harmony = best team" contradicts post-2010 research** (BOOK_OVERVIEW Unproven Assumption #4): Amy Edmondson's psychological-safety work and the cognitive-diversity literature (Page, Hong) suggest productive friction often outperforms harmony, especially on novel and complex problems. Sherwood asserts harmony from anecdote (Nelson, Coopers & Lybrand) without engaging this counter-evidence. Apply with awareness that the "harmony beats diversity" premise is empirically contested.
- **"Energy pumping" metaphor is poetic but unfalsifiable**: there is no operational definition of how much energy, of what kind, applied how, produces what outcome. The metaphor cannot be tested. Treat it as a useful heuristic, not a measurable lever.
- **Misapplied to remote / distributed / async teams** (period limitation, 2002 publication): the book pre-dates the 2020 hybrid-work transition. Nelson's "years of shared dinners" model assumes co-presence; the same prescription delivered as Zoom calls and Slack threads produces fundamentally different dynamics. Sherwood has no advice for async mental-model convergence.
- **Consultant-rescue narrative arc** (BOOK_OVERVIEW): every Sherwood case ends with the workshop succeeding. There are no documented failed engagements where multi-perspective CLDs surfaced the conflict and the parties still refused to move, or where leaders invested continuous energy and the team still failed. The base-rate of success implied by the book is likely overstated for both protocols.
- **"Wise policy" hand-wave on genuine zero-sum**: when the underlying conflict is structurally zero-sum (M&A redundancy, layoff target distribution, fixed-pool bonus allocation), the language of "wise policy" can become a euphemism that obscures rather than resolves.
- **Manager-as-protagonist framing** (BOOK_OVERVIEW): the energy-pump role assumes a unitary leader with intervention authority. Flat orgs, holacracy, self-managed teams (Buurtzorg, Morning Star, Valve) operate without this role and sometimes succeed.

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **Negotiation / BATNA / Getting to Yes** (Fisher & Ury): focuses on interests-vs-positions distinction; Sherwood adds the structural-causal-belief layer below interests.
- **Soft Systems Methodology** (Checkland): much more developed multi-stakeholder framework with explicit power analysis; Sherwood's version is a managerial-craft subset.
- **Stakeholder analysis matrices** (Mendelow grid, power-interest): identifies stakeholders but does not surface their causal beliefs.
- **Tuckman's forming-storming-norming-performing**: stage model of team development; Sherwood adds the open-system / energy-throughput dynamic but inherits Tuckman's assumptions.
- **Patrick Lencioni's Five Dysfunctions of a Team**: trust-conflict-commitment-accountability-results pyramid. Overlaps but Lencioni leads with trust; Sherwood leads with mental-model alignment.
- **Amy Edmondson's psychological safety + teaming**: post-2010 empirically-grounded; directly challenges Sherwood's "harmony" framing. If choosing between models, Edmondson has stronger evidence base.
- **Senge's Fifth Discipline shared-vision practice**: Sherwood inherits from Senge; Senge's "shared vision" overlaps heavily with "mental-model harmony" but emphasizes future-state imagery over causal-belief alignment.
