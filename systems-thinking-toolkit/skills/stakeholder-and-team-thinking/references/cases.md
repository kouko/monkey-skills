# Stakeholder & Team Thinking — Past Application Cases

Five calibrating cases for the merged stakeholder-and-team-thinking
skill: three from sk09 (multi-perspective wise-policy, outward-facing
between-group conflict) and two from sk10 (mental-model harmony +
leadership-as-energy-pumping, inward-facing team sustainment). The
theater-vs-dinner couple case appears in both source skills and is
deliberately reused here at the n=2 boundary where outward conflict
and inward harmony collapse into the same artifact.

Read all five before applying the skill — they encode the
straddle-policy discovery pattern, the Nelson energy-pump exemplar,
the exhortation anti-pattern, and the n=2 interpersonal collapse case.

## Case 1 — TV "Talent Problem" Three-Perspective Workshop (c15)

- **Problem**: TV company stuck in a recurring fight between executives
  (cost pressure), star presenters (demanding more), and junior talent
  (frustrated by ceiling). Each previous reorg had failed.
- **Methodology applied**: Three separate CLDs drawn — one per
  stakeholder group. Each diagram surfaced a different binding loop:
  executive cost-spiral, star bargaining-leverage R-loop, junior
  career-ceiling B-loop.
- **Conclusion**: When all three diagrams were laid out together,
  "develop young talent" appeared as a node that broke executive
  dependence on stars, relieved junior frustration, and trumped star
  bargaining cards.
- **Outcome**: A policy invisible inside any single CLD — visible only
  when all three were on the same table. This is the gold-standard
  straddle case: the wise policy was *structurally* absent from every
  participant's individual mental model and emerged only from the
  overlay.

## Case 2 — Utility Outsourcing + Railtrack / Balfour Beatty (c20, c21)

- **Problem**: Buyer-contractor outsourcing relationships become
  adversarial — buyer squeezes price, contractor cuts corners, quality
  drops, buyer squeezes harder.
- **Methodology applied**: Draw the buyer's CLD (cost-control
  reinforcing loop) and the contractor's CLD (margin-protection
  reinforcing loop) separately. Both R-loops spin vicious in the same
  direction.
- **Conclusion**: The wise redefinition is to introduce a shared
  variable (joint long-term value, asset reliability) that converts
  both R-loops from vicious to virtuous mode.
- **Outcome**: Sherwood's pedagogical success; Railtrack / Balfour
  Beatty is the real-world failure case showing what happens when the
  redefinition is *not* made — fatal infrastructure consequences. The
  inverse case is the warning: a straddle policy that exists on paper
  but is never operationalized is no different from no straddle at all.

## Case 3 — Theater-vs-Dinner Couple (c17) — n=2 collapse case

- **Problem**: A couple argues every Friday — one wants theater, the
  other dinner. Compromise alternation satisfies neither.
- **Methodology applied (outward, sk09 frame)**: Each partner draws a
  CLD; surface reveals different underlying drivers (need for shared
  cultural experience vs need for intimate conversation time). The
  surface dispute is wrong-grained.
- **Methodology applied (inward, sk10 frame)**: Even at n=2, mental-
  model alignment is the binding constraint. Surface-level negotiation
  without CLD-level surfacing just rotates which partner is unhappy.
- **Conclusion**: A "dinner before theater" policy or "theater on
  weekends, intimate dinners weeknights" straddles both — neither side
  proposed it because their original CLD did not contain the time-axis
  split.
- **Outcome**: Interpersonal-scale validation of the same multi-
  perspective move. This case is deliberately the same artifact under
  both lenses — at n=2 the outward / inward boundary dissolves and the
  same diagrams serve both protocols.

## Case 4 — Nelson's "Band of Brothers" at Trafalgar (c18)

- **Problem**: Build a fleet whose captains can execute audacious
  tactical innovation without continuous orders from the flagship in
  the chaos of battle.
- **Methodology applied**: Nelson personally selected each captain,
  then invested years in shared dinners, shared briefings, repeated
  value-and-tactics conversations. Each captain knew not only *what*
  Nelson would do but *why*.
- **Conclusion**: At Trafalgar, the fleet executed unprecedented
  maneuver (breaking the line) with minimal signaling because mental
  models were aligned in advance.
- **Outcome**: Sherwood's gold-standard high-performing-team exemplar.
  Shared mental models as emergent property of sustained leadership-
  energy investment — *years* of continuous flow, not an offsite.

## Case 5 — Coopers & Lybrand 100-Partner Anti-Case (c19)

- **Problem**: Professional-services firm with 100 partners —
  individually excellent, collectively unable to coordinate
  strategically. Repeated exhortations about teamwork did not change
  behavior.
- **Methodology applied**: Sherwood's diagnosis — mental models had
  drifted independently; no continuous energy investment in surfacing
  or aligning them.
- **Conclusion**: Exhortation does not substitute for energy
  throughput. A pep talk is a one-shot input to an open system that
  needs continuous flow.
- **Outcome**: Cautionary anti-pattern. Sherwood explicitly contrasts
  with Nelson (Case 4) — same observable structure (group of high-
  performing individuals) but opposite outcome because the leadership-
  energy investment was missing. This is the case to cite when a
  senior leader proposes "one big all-hands" or "another values
  workshop" instead of sustained weekly+ practice.
