# Lens: Burke's Dramatistic Pentad

> **Source**: Kenneth Burke, *A Grammar of Motives* (Prentice-Hall, 1945; University of California Press expanded edition 1969). The dramatistic pentad is introduced in the Introduction. Burke's framing question per Introduction: "What is involved, when we say what people are doing and why they are doing it?" He argues that any human motive can be analyzed through five interlocking terms drawn from drama: **act**, **scene**, **agent**, **agency**, **purpose** (a sixth term, **attitude**, was added later in the 1969 expanded edition). The pentad is the first volume of Burke's planned trilogy with *A Rhetoric of Motives* and *A Symbolic of Motives*. (Single-source lens — no synthesis disclosure applies.)

## The five elements

| Element | Asks |
|---|---|
| **Act** | What was done? |
| **Scene** | Where / when did it happen? What's the context? |
| **Agent** | Who did it? Who's the actor? |
| **Agency** | How? With what means / tools / methods? |
| **Purpose** | Why? What's the ultimate goal? |

For arguments, translate to:

| Element | In an argument context |
|---|---|
| **Act** | What action / decision is being recommended? |
| **Scene** | What context (market / cultural moment / historical situation) frames the recommendation? |
| **Agent** | Who is the actor — "we" / a specific company / an industry / a generation? |
| **Agency** | What method / strategy / tool implements the act? |
| **Purpose** | What ultimate goal does the act serve? |

## Pentad ratios — the critical move

After identifying all 5 elements, examine **which two dominate**.
The dominant pair is the **ratio**, and it reveals the underlying
motive structure:

| Ratio | Pattern | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Scene-Act** | "The circumstances forced this action" | Situational determinism — agent has no real choice |
| **Agent-Act** | "Who you are determines what you do" | Character-driven — action expresses identity |
| **Agent-Agency** | "Who you are determines how" | Identity-via-method — credibility through process |
| **Act-Purpose** | "The action *is* the goal" | Process-driven — doing is the end, not means |
| **Agency-Purpose** | "The means determine the ends" | Instrumental — methods constrain outcomes |
| **Scene-Agent** | "Setting determines who you become" | Structural — agent is a product of context |
| **Purpose-Act** | "The goal determines the action" | Teleological — ends justify means |
| **Agency-Act** | "The tools determine action" | Tool-driven — capability sets agenda |

Burke's claim: most arguments hide behind a **claimed ratio** while
operating on a **different actual ratio**. Surfacing the discrepancy
is the deconstruction.

## How to detect the ratio

### Step 1: Surface emphasis

Read the argument once. Which two pentad elements get the most
attention? That's the **claimed ratio**.

Signals:
- "We must act because the situation demands it" → **Scene-Act**
- "As the leading firm in our space, we believe…" → **Agent-Act**
- "Our methodology drives our recommendations" → **Agency-Agent / Agency-Act**

### Step 2: Deeper structure

Read again, looking for what *carries logical weight* (not just
attention). Which two elements would the argument fail without?
That's the **actual ratio**.

Signals:
- The argument cites situational evidence (market data, cultural moment) → Scene is doing real work
- The argument cites agent credentials (track record, identity) → Agent is doing real work
- The argument cites methodology in detail (process, framework) → Agency is doing real work

### Step 3: Discrepancy

If claimed ≠ actual, the argument is doing **motive laundering** —
presenting one motive structure to evoke a particular response, while
operating on a different motive structure beneath.

Common laundering patterns:

| Claimed | Actual | What's hidden |
|---|---|---|
| Agent-Act ("we believe") | Scene-Act ("the moment forces us") | Identity is a cover for situational pressure |
| Agency-Act ("our methodology") | Authority-Act (Agent dominance) | Method dressing for "trust us" |
| Act-Purpose ("doing this matters") | Agency-Purpose | Process is the actual goal, not the stated end |

## Worked example 1: Tech company DEI speech

> "After 2020, we knew we had to lead. Our purpose-driven culture
> means we don't just respond to crises — we shape them. That's why
> we're committing to hiring 500 underrepresented engineers, using
> targeted university partnerships and sponsorship programs, to build
> the most diverse engineering team in industry."

### Pentad layout

- **Act**: Hire 500 underrepresented engineers
- **Scene**: Post-2020 racial reckoning
- **Agent**: "Our company" with "purpose-driven culture"
- **Agency**: Targeted university partnerships + sponsorship programs
- **Purpose**: Build most diverse engineering team

### Ratio analysis

- **Claimed ratio**: **Agent-Act** ("our purpose-driven culture means we don't just respond to crises") — character-driven framing
- **Actual ratio**: **Scene-Act** ("After 2020, we knew we had to lead") — circumstance is doing the actual work

### Discrepancy

The speech *presents* character-driven commitment (we are this kind of
company, so we do this) but *operates on* circumstance-driven response
(the moment demanded it, so we do this).

This isn't necessarily bad — but the speech style misleads about the
underlying motive. A more honest version would acknowledge: "The 2020
moment forced this issue onto every company's agenda; we are responding
because the cost of *not* responding has risen."

## Worked example 2: VC pitch memo

> "The cloud-native database market is fragmenting. We see three
> camps: Postgres-loyalists, NoSQL evangelists, and emerging
> multi-model platforms. Our team — three ex-Snowflake engineers and a
> former Stripe payments lead — is building the platform that unifies
> these camps via a typed query layer. We've grown from 0 to $2M ARR
> in 11 months, with 40% MoM retention."

### Pentad layout

- **Act**: Build typed query layer / unified database platform
- **Scene**: Cloud-native DB market fragmentation
- **Agent**: Founder team (ex-Snowflake, ex-Stripe)
- **Agency**: Typed query layer (technical methodology)
- **Purpose**: Unify DB camps / capture market

### Ratio analysis

- **Claimed ratio**: **Agency-Act** ("typed query layer is the method that unifies") — methodology-driven
- **Actual ratio**: **Agent-Act** (founder credentials are doing most of the persuasive work; "we are ex-Snowflake / ex-Stripe so we will execute")

### Discrepancy

The pitch *positions* itself as method-driven (here's the technical
solution) but *succeeds or fails* on agent credibility (these
specific founders). This is honest VC math — early-stage investing
*is* mostly Agent-Act — but it's worth surfacing for the reader.

## Worked example 3: Op-ed on AI regulation

> "Tech regulation is failing because Congress is too old to
> understand AI. We should require minimum AI literacy for all
> members of relevant committees."

### Pentad layout

- **Act**: Require AI literacy for committee members
- **Scene**: Failing tech regulation
- **Agent**: Congress (too old, too technically illiterate)
- **Agency**: Mandatory training / credentials
- **Purpose**: Successful AI regulation

### Ratio analysis

- **Claimed ratio**: **Agent-Act** (focus on Congress as the cause of regulatory failure)
- **Actual ratio**: **Scene-Act** (the *novelty of AI* is the actual scene-driver, but the op-ed displaces blame onto agent)

### Discrepancy

By framing regulatory failure as Agent (Congress) rather than Scene
(unprecedented technological speed), the op-ed makes the solution
seem like agent-replacement (training / credentialing) rather than
scene-adaptation (different regulatory tools / faster cycles).
Recognizing this opens a different solution space.

```markdown
- **Claimed ratio**: <pair> (signals: ...)
- **Actual ratio**: <pair> (signals: ...)

<What does the discrepancy reveal? What alternative framing does it open?>
```

## Pitfalls

- **Naming pentad without ratio**: just listing 5 elements is
  bookkeeping. The ratio is where insight lives.
- **Forcing all 5 elements**: not every argument has all 5 in equal
  weight. Note when an element is implicit / weak / absent.
- **Treating claimed ratio as actual**: read twice — surface emphasis
  is rarely the deepest motive structure.
- **Confusing pentad with Toulmin**: pentad is for *narrative motive*
  analysis; Toulmin is for *logical structure*. Use both, but don't
  confuse "agent" (pentad) with "claim" (Toulmin) etc.
