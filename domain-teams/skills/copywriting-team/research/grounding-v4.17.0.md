## Cluster A: Intertextuality and Literary Allusion Theory

### A-1. Kristeva — Intertextuality (1966/1969)

- **Kristeva, J.** (1969) *Séméiôtikè: recherches pour une sémanalyse*.
  Seuil. The term "intertextualité" first appears in "Le Texte clos"
  (1966–7) and is elaborated in "Word, Dialogue, and Novel."
- **Canonical definition**: "any text is constructed of a mosaic of
  quotations; any text is the absorption and transformation of another."
- Replaces "influence" and "source" with a semiotic model: meaning is
  mediated by codes from other texts, not transferred writer→reader.
- **Grounding role**: foundational concept anchoring the entire source
  taxonomy. All neta injection — meme or literary — operates within
  Kristeva's intertextual framework.
- **Confidence**: HIGH (foundational, universally cited).

### A-2. Genette — Transtextuality (1982)

- **Genette, G.** (1982) *Palimpsestes: La littérature au second degré*.
  Seuil. English trans.: *Palimpsests: Literature in the Second Degree*
  (1997, Univ. of Nebraska Press).
- **5 types of transtextuality**:

  | Type | Definition | Neta mapping |
  |------|-----------|--------------|
  | **Intertextuality** | Actual presence of one text in another (quotation, plagiarism, allusion) | Direct: neta injection = intertextuality in Genette's sense |
  | **Paratextuality** | Framing signals (titles, prefaces, covers) | Not applicable to neta |
  | **Metatextuality** | Critical commentary on another text | Not applicable (neta is not critique) |
  | **Architextuality** | Genre/type classification | Background only (genre affects which neta sources fit) |
  | **Hypertextuality** | Text B grafted on earlier text A (not as commentary) | Strongest match: neta injection creates a hypertext (new copy) from hypotext (source reference) |

- **Grounding role**: provides the taxonomic vocabulary. Neta injection
  is primarily **intertextuality** (allusion) and **hypertextuality**
  (transformation of source into new text).
- **Confidence**: HIGH.

### A-3. Ben-Porat — Literary Allusion (1976)

- **Ben-Porat, Z.** (1976) "The Poetics of Literary Allusion." *PTL:
  A Journal for Descriptive Poetics and Theory of Literature*, 1,
  105–128.
- Deploys a formal notion of allusive signification. Allusion requires:
  (1) a recognizable marker in the alluding text, (2) activation of the
  source text in the reader's memory, (3) interpretive mapping between
  the two texts.
- **Grounding role**: formalizes the "recognition" condition already in
  the neta system. Ben-Porat's marker → activation → mapping parallels
  our Recognition → Compression → Relevance.
- **Confidence**: HIGH (widely cited in allusion studies).

### A-4. McQuarrie & Mick — Advertising Rhetoric Bridge

- **McQuarrie, E. F. & Mick, D. G.** (1996) "Figures of Rhetoric in
  Advertising Language." *JCR*, 22(4), 424–438. Already cited in
  `neta-injection-techniques.md`.
- **McQuarrie, E. F. & Mick, D. G.** (1999) "Visual Rhetoric in
  Advertising." *JCR*, 26(1), 37–54.
- Rhetorical figures = "artful deviations" relative to audience
  expectation. Links rhetorical/literary theory (Genette, classical
  rhetoric) to consumer response.
- **Bridge role**: connects Cluster A's literary theory to the
  advertising context the copywriting-team operates in. McQuarrie &
  Mick explicitly draw on semiotic + rhetorical traditions.

### A-5. Drift Corrections

- **Drift #23**: Kristeva's intertextuality is NOT limited to
  intentional allusion. It covers all text-to-text relations including
  unconscious. For neta injection, we use only the intentional subset
  (closer to Genette's "intertextuality" and Ben-Porat's "allusion").
  Do not cite Kristeva as defining "allusion" — cite her as the
  foundational framework within which allusion operates.

## Cluster B: 本歌取り (Honkadori) — Classical JP Literary Allusion

### B-1. Definition

**本歌取り** (honkadori, lit. "taking from the original poem") is a
classical Japanese waka technique: deliberately incorporating
recognizable elements (words, imagery, phrasing) from a well-known
earlier poem (本歌) into a new poem to create layered meaning. The
reader simultaneously perceives the new poem's surface meaning AND the
resonance with the original.

English academic term: **"allusive variation"** (Brower & Miner 1961).

### B-2. Codification — 藤原定家 (c. 1209)

- **藤原定家** (Fujiwara no Teika, 1162–1241) codified 本歌取り rules
  in《近代秀歌》(*Kindai Shūka*, c. 1209) and《毎月抄》(*Maigetsushō*,
  c. 1219).

  | Rule | Content |
  |------|---------|
  | Word limit | Borrow at most 2–3 ku (phrases) of 5. Taking 4+ ku = 盗作 (plagiarism). |
  | Topic shift | New poem's 心 (kokoro / meaning) MUST differ from original's. Same-topic = plagiarism. |
  | Source restriction | 本歌 must be well-known canonical poem (三代集 era). Obscure poems fail recognition. |
  | Cross-category | Cross seasonal/topic categories preferred (春→秋). Same-category = imitation. |

- **Confidence**: HIGH.

### B-3. English-Language Academic Sources

- **Brower, R. H. & Miner, E.** (1961) *Japanese Court Poetry*.
  Stanford UP. Canonical English-language treatment; coins "allusive
  variation."
- **Konishi Jin'ichi** (trans. Brower, Miner, Gatten) *A History of
  Japanese Literature* (1984–1991). Princeton UP.
- **Shirane, H.** (ed.) (2007) *Traditional Japanese Literature*.
  Columbia UP. Annotated honkadori examples.

### B-4. Structural Mapping to Neta 3 Conditions

| Neta Condition | 本歌取り Equivalent | Assessment |
|----------------|---------------------|------------|
| **Recognition** | 本歌 must be canonical, well-known (三代集). If audience doesn't know it, allusion fails. | DIRECT MATCH |
| **Compression** | 2–3 borrowed ku carry full emotional weight of original poem into new context. Literal paraphrase would require an essay. | DIRECT MATCH |
| **Relevance** | Topic-shift rule: borrowed material must map onto new meaning. Same-topic borrowing = plagiarism. | DIRECT MATCH |

Assessment: **structurally isomorphic**, not merely analogical. 本歌取り
is an 800-year-old codified precedent for the 3-condition framework.

### B-5. 本歌取り vs 盗作 (Plagiarism) Boundary

| Dimension | 本歌取り (allusion) | 盗作 (plagiarism) |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Word borrowing | 2–3 of 5 ku | 4+ ku |
| Topic (心) | Must differ | Same = copying |
| Source awareness | Invites recognition | Hides source |
| New meaning | Creates semantic layer | Adds nothing |

Parallels fair-use transformative doctrine (Campbell v. Acuff-Rose 1994)
and 著作権法 32条 主従関係 doctrine already documented in
`neta-safety-gate.md`.

### B-6. JP Rhetoric Framework

- **佐藤信夫** (1992/1978) *『レトリック感覚』*. 講談社学術文庫.
  Foundational JP rhetoric text. Covers 引喩 (allusion) with 本歌取り
  as canonical Japanese example alongside 掛詞, 枕詞, 縁語, 見立て.
- **佐藤信夫** (1992/1981) *『レトリック認識』*. 講談社学術文庫.
  Companion volume on rhetoric as cognition.
- CiNii confirms: **『広告コピーのレトリック : その発想と表現技術』**
  (BN13300771) — advertising copy rhetoric book.

### B-7. Gaps

- **Direct 本歌取り × 広告 academic paper**: not confirmed via CiNii
  search. 佐藤信夫 covers 引喩 broadly; advertising-specific 本歌取り
  application is practitioner-level, not deeply academic.
- **Analytical synthesis disclaimer**: The mapping of 本歌取り to the
  neta injection 3-condition framework is this team's interpretive
  synthesis. Cite Brower & Miner + Teika's rules as primary sources;
  cite the mapping itself as team synthesis.

## Cluster C: Cultural Capital (Bourdieu) vs Subcultural Capital (Thornton)

### C-1. Bourdieu — Cultural Capital (1984)

- **Bourdieu, P.** (1984/1979) *Distinction: A Social Critique of the
  Judgement of Taste*. Harvard UP. Already cited in
  `neta-injection-techniques.md` (line 74) but under-used.
- **3 forms**: embodied (dispositions, tastes), objectified (cultural
  goods), institutionalized (credentials).
- Cultural capital reproduces class distinctions. "Highbrow" taste
  signals education and social position. Recognition is broadly
  legitimized by institutions (universities, museums, literary canon).

### C-2. Thornton vs Bourdieu — The Theoretical Fork

| Dimension | Bourdieu Cultural Capital | Thornton Subcultural Capital |
|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| Legitimizing authority | Institutions (academy, museum) | In-group peers, niche media |
| Scope | Broad social hierarchy | Narrow scene/community |
| Acquisition | Education, family | Participation, immersion |
| Signal content | "I am educated / cultivated" | "I am in the know / authentic" |
| Decay rate | Slow (classics stay canonical) | Fast (trends cycle, memes expire) |
| Convertibility | High (→ economic/social capital) | Low outside subculture |

Thornton's key innovation: subcultural capital is **media-sensitive**.
Niche media confer it; mass media destroy it. This maps directly to
meme lifecycle — a meme loses subcultural capital when it becomes
mainstream.

### C-3. Peterson & Kern — Cultural Omnivore (1996)

- **Peterson, R. A. & Kern, R. M.** (1996) "Changing Highbrow Taste:
  From Snob to Omnivore." *American Sociological Review*, 61(5),
  900–907.
- High-status consumers increasingly consume across highbrow/lowbrow
  boundary. The "snob" → "omnivore" shift.
- **Implication for neta**: literary allusion and meme reference
  audiences may substantially overlap. A university-educated 30-something
  may recognize both Kafka and niche Reddit memes. The two signaling
  axes are **complementary, not contradictory**.
- **Confidence**: HIGH (1200+ citations by 2015; canonical sociology).

### C-4. Differential Signaling in Advertising

| Dimension | Literary Allusion (Bourdieu) | Meme Reference (Thornton) |
|-----------|------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Signal | Education, sophistication, brand authority | Trend-awareness, in-group membership |
| Brand positioning | Premium, heritage, intellectual | Relatable, current, community-native |
| Recognition window | Years to decades | Days to months |
| Failure mode (cringe) | **Pretentiousness** — reader feels talked-down-to | **Out-of-touch** — brand uses dead meme |
| Recovery | Easier (misattribution correctable) | Harder (trust damaged) |

### C-5. JP Context

- **教養** (kyōyō = cultivation / general education) is the operative
  JP concept for literary sophistication signaling — more commonly used
  in JP advertising discourse than Bourdieu's terminology.
- **片岡栄美** (Kataoka Emi, 関東学院大学) publishes on cultural capital
  and consumption in Japan.
- JP omnivore parallel: **雑食的文化消費** (zasshoku-teki bunka shōhi).
- **Confidence**: MEDIUM for JP-specific application.
