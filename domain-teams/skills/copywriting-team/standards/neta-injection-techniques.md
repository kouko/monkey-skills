# Neta Injection Techniques

Tier 3 standard: fully self-contained. Codifies four copywriting
operations that inject pop culture, subculture, or meme references
into advertising copy. Each technique is identified by its **English
academic anchor** (the primary label used in this skill), with
industry vernacular and Japanese practitioner terms documented as
aliases:

| # | Academic label (primary) | EN industry vernacular | JP academic / industry |
|---|--------------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| 1 | **Reversal** | Subvertising / Culture Jamming | 逆転 (gyakuten) / 逆転の発想 / サブバータイジング |
| 2 | **Substitution** | Snowclone / Meme Template | 置換 (chikan) / スノークローン / 大喜利 (oogiri) culture |
| 3 | **Subcultural Capital** | Tribal Signal / Community Cipher | サブカルチャー的資本 / 界隈消費 (kaiwai shōhi) |
| 4 | **Cross-domain Mapping** | Conceptual Simplification / Analogy | 領域間写像 / 次元降下的比喩 (jigen kōka; SF origin) / 概念の構造化・簡略化 (formal) |

All four labels + mechanisms are grounded in primary-source rhetoric
+ humor theory + subcultural sociology canon. Industry vernacular
and JP terms are documented so workers can adapt output language to
audience context without losing the mechanism.

This standard does NOT replace other form-specific canon (short-form-
catchcopy, long-form-pasona, light-action-frameworks). Neta injection
is an **optional post-production layer** applied to copy already
structured by another framework. See `copy-neta-injection.md` for
execution protocol and `neta-safety-gate.md` for safety verification.

## Primary Sources

### Canonical rhetoric + advertising

- **Lakoff, G. & Johnson, M.** (1980) *Metaphors We Live By*.
  University of Chicago Press. **Foundational.** Conceptual-metaphor
  theory: metaphor is cross-domain mapping, not ornament. Anchors
  Technique 4 (Cross-domain Mapping).
- **McQuarrie, E. F. & Mick, D. G.** (1996) "Figures of Rhetoric in
  Advertising Language." *Journal of Consumer Research*, 22(4),
  424–438. **Canonical.** Four rhetorical operations: repetition /
  **reversal** / **substitution** / destabilization. Anchors
  Technique 1 (Reversal) and Technique 2 (Substitution).
- **Ott, B. L. & Walter, C.** (2000) "Intertextuality: Interpretive
  Practice and Textual Strategy." *Critical Studies in Media
  Communication*, 17(4), 429–446. Three strategies: parodic allusion
  / creative appropriation / self-reflexive reference. Explicit claim
  that parody depends on reader's prior knowledge — anchors
  Technique 3 (Subcultural Capital).
- **Dery, M.** (1991) "Subvertising: The Billboard Liberation Front's
  Hit and Run Media Art." Documented rhetorical operation.

### Humor theory anchors

- **Suls, J.** (1972) "A two-stage model for the appreciation of
  jokes and cartoons." In Goldstein & McGhee (eds.) *The Psychology
  of Humor*. Academic Press. **Canonical** incongruity-resolution
  model.
- **Raskin, V.** (1985) *Semantic Mechanisms of Humor*.
  Reidel/Springer. Script-based Semantic Theory of Humor (SSTH).
- **McGraw, A. P. & Warren, C.** (2010) "Benign Violations: Making
  Immoral Behavior Funny." *Psychological Science*, 21(8),
  1141–1149. Three conditions: violation + benign + simultaneous.

### Subcultural capital anchor

- **Thornton, S.** (1995) *Club Cultures: Music, Media and
  Subcultural Capital*. Wesleyan UP. Bourdieu extension. "Being in
  the know" as cultural capital. Anchors Technique 3 (Subcultural
  Capital).
- **Bourdieu, P.** (1984/1979) *Distinction*. Harvard UP. Foundation
  for cultural capital.
- **Pullum, G. K.** (2003) / **Whitman, G.** (2004) "snowclone"
  (phrasal-template linguistics). Anchors Technique 2 (Substitution)
  template logic.
- **Dery, M.** (1993) *Culture Jamming: Hacking, Slashing, and
  Sniping in the Empire of Signs*. Open Magazine Pamphlet #25.
  (Coinage of "culture jamming" as industry term; "subvertising"
  attributed to Billboard Liberation Front ~1977 and popularized
  through Adbusters.) EN industry vernacular for Technique 1
  (Reversal).

### JP academic + CM precedent

- **呂晶 (北海道大学)「日本語広告表現の語用論的研究」**— JP ad
  language pragmatic study.
- **「日英語の広告言語表現に関する認知言語学的分析」**(東洋大学) —
  cognitive-linguistic analysis.
- **東京家政大学「広告における『しゃれ』・『だじゃれ』の多用に関する

- **Walter Dill Scott** (1903) *The Theory of Advertising* — JP
  広告心理学 chair tradition foundation.

## Critical Attribution Corrections

- **Drift #20: "Hitchon 1991 Adaptation of Lakoff and Johnson..."
  is NOT a standalone published work.** The actually-published 1991
  ACR paper is **Hitchon, J. C. (1991) "Effects of metaphorical vs.
  literal headlines on advertising persuasion." *Advances in
  Consumer Research*, 18, 752–753** (short proceedings paper). The
  "Adaptation..." title is informal / dissertation-phrasing and
  should not be cited as a discrete publication.

- **Drift #21: 「次元降下 (jigen kōka)」is a Liu Cixin SF-origin
  JP vernacular, NOT rhetoric-canon terminology.** The JP
  creative-industry term 次元降下 borrows from Liu Cixin's *The
  Three-Body Problem* (三体, 2008) where 降維打撃 is a physics-SF
  concept. It has gained traction as a frontline JP vernacular for
  Technique 4 (Cross-domain Mapping) among post-三体 creative/
  technical communities. Formal writing should use **概念の構造化・
  簡略化** as a safer substitute. The underlying mechanism is
  Lakoff & Johnson 1980 conceptual-metaphor theory. Cite Lakoff &
  Johnson as academic anchor; cite 次元降下 as JP industry
  vernacular with SF-origin disclosure, not as rhetoric canon.

- **Drift #22: McQuarrie & Mick's "operations" are about rhetorical
  figures, NOT "neta injection."** This team's mapping (reversal
  → Technique 1, substitution → Technique 2) is **interpretive
  application**, not a direct quote from the paper. Cite McQuarrie
  & Mick as mechanism grounding, not as "McQuarrie & Mick's neta
  taxonomy."

## Source Types vs Transformation Techniques

This standard defines 4 **transformation techniques (轉化技法)** — the
rhetorical operations applied to source material. Source types (取材類型)
— WHERE the reference comes from — are defined in
`neta-source-taxonomy.md`. The two axes are orthogonal: any technique
can operate on any source type, with varying effectiveness documented
in the taxonomy standard's compatibility matrix.

## Framing — the neta injection zone

### What counts as neta

**Neta (ネタ)** in JP copywriting discourse refers to externally-sourced
cultural references embedded in copy: pop culture / subculture / meme /
流行語 / 固有名詞, as well as literary allusion, classical quotation,
and famous phrases. Source types range from fast-moving SNS memes to
evergreen classical literature (see `neta-source-taxonomy.md` for the
full taxonomy). Neta injection is NOT decoration — it is a **cognitive
shortcut** exploiting the reader's prior knowledge to deliver compressed
meaning.

Three conditions must hold for a neta injection to work:
1. **Recognition**: the target audience recognizes the reference
2. **Compression**: the reference carries meaning the literal
   paraphrase would take 3-10× more words to convey
3. **Relevance**: the reference maps onto the product/message
   (not merely decorative)

Failure on any of the three makes the neta misfire (cringe, confusion,
or in-group offense).

### Label convention

The four techniques are identified by their **English academic
anchors** (Reversal / Substitution / Subcultural Capital /
Cross-domain Mapping). EN industry vernacular and JP practitioner
terms are documented per technique so workers can adapt output
language to audience. Workers should cite the academic anchor in
formal documentation and switch to industry/JP vernacular only in
audience-facing copy where the vernacular adds authenticity.

## Technique 1: Reversal

### Terminology

- **Academic anchor (primary)**: Reversal (McQuarrie & Mick 1996;
  Ott & Walter 2000 parodic allusion)
- **EN industry vernacular**: Subvertising / Culture Jamming
  (Dery 1993; Billboard Liberation Front ~1977; Adbusters)
- **JP academic / industry**: 逆転 (gyakuten) / 「逆転の発想」
  (common phrase in JP ad circles) / サブバータイジング

### Mechanism

Subvert a widely-known saying, quote, or trope by inverting one
expected element. Maps to McQuarrie & Mick's **reversal** operation
(1996, 22(4), 424–438) and Ott & Walter's **parodic allusion**
(2000, 17(4), 429–446).

### Psychological mechanism

Incongruity-resolution (Suls 1972) + benign violation (McGraw &
Warren 2010). The reader recognizes the original, encounters the
subverted version, experiences mild incongruity, resolves it by
mapping subversion onto product meaning. Benign condition requires
that the subversion does not mock the original beyond recovery.

### Structure formula

```
[Canonical reference] → [one expected element inverted] = [product message]
```

### JP precedent (canonical)

- **富士フイルム「美しい人はより美しく、そうでない方はそれなりに
  写ります」**(1980, 樹木希林) — 反転 of beauty-advertising trope
  that promises transformation.

### When to use

- The original reference is recognizable to ≥80% of target audience
- The subversion carries product meaning (not merely cleverness)
- The tone is benign (not mocking the original)

### When NOT to use

- Reader recognition is uncertain (risks confusion)
- The subversion relies on mocking the original's values (benign
  violation fails)
- The product message could be delivered equally clearly without
  the reference (reference is decorative, not load-bearing)

## Technique 2: Substitution

### Terminology

- **Academic anchor (primary)**: Substitution (McQuarrie & Mick 1996)
- **EN industry vernacular**: Snowclone (Pullum 2003 / Whitman
  2004 phrasal-template linguistics) / Meme Template
- **JP academic / industry**: 置換 (chikan) / スノークローン /
  大喜利 (oogiri) — JP SNS culture (X/TikTok) of creative filling
  within a fixed frame; structurally equivalent to Substitution
  on a template

### Mechanism

Replace parameters in a famous structure/template with product-
specific content. Maps to McQuarrie & Mick's **substitution**
operation (1996) and the **snowclone** linguistic pattern (Pullum
2003, Whitman 2004). The JP 大喜利 cultural practice on SNS
produces structurally identical output — a fixed frame accepting
creative slot-fills — and indicates that JP audiences have strong
prior familiarity with this operation.

### Psychological mechanism

Incongruity between template and inserted content (Raskin 1985
script theory). The reader recognizes the template, encounters
unexpected parameters, resolves the incongruity by recognizing
that the template's rhetorical force now serves the product.

### Structure formula

```
[Famous template: "[X] is [Y] in [Z]"] → replace X/Y/Z with product
parameters = [product message wearing the template's rhetorical force]
```

Example patterns (English snowclone linguistics):
- "X is the new Y" (substitutable X, Y)
- "Have X, will travel" (substitutable X)
- "In space, no one can hear you X"

### JP precedent

- **オロナミンC「元気ハツラツ！」**(大塚製薬) — structural-
  replacement slogan where the template "[state]ハツラツ" entered
  vernacular.

### When to use

- Template is recognizable to target audience
- Product parameters fit the template's slot-grammar naturally
- The template's rhetorical energy enhances the product message

### When NOT to use

- Template is obscure to audience (misses recognition)
- Parameters require forced / awkward fit into template slots
- The template has become overused (e.g., "Got X?" was distinctive
  in 1993; by 2010 it was cliché — see Timeliness in
  `neta-safety-gate.md`)

## Technique 3: Subcultural Capital

### Terminology

- **Academic anchor (primary)**: Subcultural Capital (Thornton
  1995; Bourdieu 1984 cultural capital foundation)
- **EN industry vernacular**: Tribal Signal / Community Cipher
- **JP academic / industry**: サブカルチャー的資本 (academic
  translation) / 界隈消費 (kaiwai shōhi) — current JP frontline
  term capturing "consumption motivation arising from recognizing
  an in-group's shared code (共通言語)." 界隈消費 is the strongest
  contemporary JP practitioner term for this technique.

### Mechanism

Reference recognizable only to a specific in-group (subcultural /
generational / professional / fandom). Maps to Thornton's
**subcultural capital** (1995) — "being in the know" as cultural
capital that signals in-group membership. Requires the reader's
prior knowledge (Ott & Walter 2000 claim about parody). The JP
**界隈消費 (kaiwai shōhi)** discourse captures the
consumption-motivation side of the same mechanism: a consumer
becomes motivated specifically *because* they recognize the
in-group code.

### Psychological mechanism

In-group signaling (Spence 1973 signaling theory) + benign violation
with psychological distance (McGraw & Warren 2010 condition: the
violation must feel benign to the specific in-group, not to a
universal audience). Recognition itself becomes a reward — the
reader feels "this brand knows us."

### Structure formula

```
[In-group cultural token: terminology / catchphrase / inside joke]
→ embedded naturally in copy = [product message + in-group
belonging signal]
```

### Key constraint

**The reference must be legible as a code to the in-group AND
acceptable-if-unrecognized to outsiders.** Copy that fails to be
parseable by outsiders is not "insider code" — it's exclusionary
messaging that damages brand reach.

### When to use

- Target audience has well-defined in-group boundaries
- The reference signals "this brand is one of us" without alienating
  adjacent audiences
- Subcultural capital (Thornton 1995) is earned, not performed —
  brand must have genuine in-group position or risk accusations of
  appropriation

### When NOT to use

- Target audience is broad (in-group reference reads as
  exclusionary)
- Brand has no genuine in-group connection (performative use reads
  as appropriation)
- The reference requires decoding time that exceeds attention window
  (キャッチコピー with insider code fails 3秒ルール)
- The in-group has history of being commodified — reference reads
  as exploitation

### Note on vernacular terms

"Tribal Signal" and "Community Cipher" are EN industry vernacular
circulating in digital-marketing practitioner writing; they map onto
the Thornton/Bourdieu sociological mechanism but are not academic
terms. 界隈消費 (kaiwai shōhi) is currently the sharpest JP
practitioner framing.

## Technique 4: Cross-domain Mapping

### Terminology

- **Academic anchor (primary)**: Cross-domain Mapping (Lakoff &
  Johnson 1980 conceptual metaphor theory)
- **EN industry vernacular**: Conceptual Simplification / Analogy
- **JP academic / industry**: 領域間写像 (ryōiki-kan shazō;
  academic translation of cross-domain mapping) / 次元降下的比喩
  (jigen kōka no hiyu; SF-origin vernacular popular among post-三体
  JP creative/technical communities — see drift #21) / 概念の
  構造化・簡略化 (formal alternative for business/academic contexts)

### Mechanism

Use an unexpected low-complexity analog to explain a high-complexity
concept. Maps to Lakoff & Johnson's **conceptual metaphor theory**
(1980) — cross-domain mapping where source domain is typically
concrete/familiar and target domain is abstract/unfamiliar.

**Vernacular caveat (drift #21)**: 次元降下 borrows from Liu Cixin's
*The Three-Body Problem* (三体, 2008) SF concept of 降維打撃. It has
become frontline JP vernacular among creative/technical communities
post-三体. Suitable for audience-facing copy targeting those
communities; for formal/business contexts, prefer 概念の構造化・
簡略化. The underlying mechanism is always Lakoff & Johnson 1980.

### Psychological mechanism

Conceptual metaphor cognitive shortcut (Lakoff & Johnson 1980) +
mild incongruity from unexpectedness (Raskin 1985). The reader
grasps the abstract concept through the concrete analog faster than
through literal explanation.

### Structure formula

```
[Abstract / high-complexity concept] → [concrete / low-complexity
analog that maps onto it] = [compressed comprehension]
```

### Examples of pattern

- "[Complex financial concept] is like [everyday household behavior]"
- "[Abstract AI capability] is like [children's game]"
- "[Enterprise security threat] is like [common home intrusion]"

### When to use

- The target concept is genuinely abstract or high-complexity
- The analog is universally graspable
- The mapping preserves meaningful structural correspondence (not
  just surface similarity)

### When NOT to use

- The target concept is already simple (no dimension to reduce —
  metaphor becomes patronizing)
- The analog introduces misleading mappings (correspondence breaks
  down in ways that matter)
- The analog carries unwanted connotations (e.g., trivializing,
  child-like when audience is senior executive)

## Integration with other copywriting-team standards

### With `short-form-catchcopy-canon.md`

Short-form キャッチコピー (7-15 chars) can use **Reversal** and
**Substitution** effectively; **Subcultural Capital** works if
in-group overlap with audience is tight; **Cross-domain Mapping**
rarely fits due to character budget.

### With `write-short-form-copy.md`

Neta injection is an **optional post-production layer** after the
5 切入点 + voice + 3秒ルール structure is set. See
`copy-neta-injection.md` protocol.

### With `long-form-pasona-canon.md` / `long-form-extended-frameworks.md`

Long-form copy uses neta injection sparingly, typically at the
Problem stage (**Reversal** of assumed-truth opener), the Benefit
stage (**Cross-domain Mapping** of outcome), or the Narrow-down
stage (**Subcultural Capital** signaling audience fit).

### With `light-action-frameworks.md`

CREMA's Evidence stage can use **Cross-domain Mapping** to compress
a technical proof point into a concrete analog. **Subcultural
Capital** (tribal signal) in CREMA's Conclusion stage suits
SNS-native audiences — the JP 界隈消費 framing applies.

### With `sns-evolution-aisas-ulssas.md`

ULSSAS-era copy leans heavily on neta injection because UGC-
triggering content is typically **Reversal** or **Substitution**
that invites remixing. The JP 大喜利 culture is the on-platform
native expression of Substitution and indicates high audience
fluency. AISAS-era copy uses neta for Search-survival (memorable
references survive search).

### With `jp-copy-craft-lineage.md`

眞木準 voice (掛詞 tradition) naturally produces **Substitution**
and **Cross-domain Mapping** outputs. 糸井 voice (意図的な曖昧さ)
can absorb **Subcultural Capital** but not obvious **Reversal**
(too direct for 糸井's open-ended tone). 岩崎 voice (observational
stillness) typically avoids neta injection entirely.

### With `persuasion-psychology-anchor.md`

Cialdini's **liking** principle (affinity via in-group recognition)
aligns with **Subcultural Capital**. **Consistency** principle
does not map directly to neta injection — neta exploits
incongruity, which is the opposite cognitive direction.

## Ethics boundary

**All neta injection must pass `neta-safety-gate.md`** (SHOULD gate).
The gate covers:
- Copyright / trademark risk (fair use / 著作権法 32条 compliance)
- 景表法 ステマ告示 2023 risk (brand-influence + identifiability)
- Cringe index (subjective but scored)
- In-group (Subcultural Capital) match — appropriateness
- Timeliness (meme currency)

**Hard legal vetoes**: any unmitigated Red on copyright/trademark
OR ステマ risk blocks delivery regardless of other gate outcomes.

Cross-reference: `persuasion-ethics.md` §Dark Patterns for
manipulation considerations.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Citing "Hitchon 1991 Adaptation of Lakoff and Johnson..." as
  a standalone paper.** Canonical cite is Hitchon (1991) "Effects
  of metaphorical vs. literal headlines on advertising persuasion"
  *ACR* 18, 752–753 (drift #20).
- **Presenting「次元降下 (jigen kōka)」as rhetoric-canon
  terminology.** Term is JP vernacular borrowing from Liu Cixin *三体*
  (2008); mechanism is Lakoff & Johnson 1980 Cross-domain Mapping
  (drift #21). Use 次元降下 only as audience-facing JP vernacular
  for creative/technical communities; for formal contexts use
  概念の構造化・簡略化 or the academic "cross-domain mapping."
- **Presenting McQuarrie & Mick's four operations as "McQuarrie &
  Mick's neta injection taxonomy."** The paper's operations
  (repetition / reversal / substitution / destabilization) are
  rhetorical figures; this team's mapping (reversal → Technique 1,
  substitution → Technique 2) is interpretive application (drift
  #22).
- **Using neta injection decoratively.** If the copy works equally
  well without the reference, the reference is decorative and should
  be removed. Neta must be load-bearing (compression + relevance).
- **Stacking multiple techniques in one short-form piece.** 眞木
  準's「ダジャレではなく、オシャレです」principle applies: density
  greater than one technique per キャッチコピー produces cringe
  rather than elegance (`jp-copy-craft-lineage.md` §眞木 LLM
  reproduction gap mitigation).
- **Using Subcultural Capital (tribal signal / 界隈消費) for
  broad-audience campaigns.** In-group codes alienate adjacent
  audiences. Match technique to audience breadth.
- **Mistaking phonetic ダジャレ (puns) for Substitution.** Wordplay
  lacking template-swap structure is 眞木-tradition 掛詞 (see
  `jp-copy-craft-lineage.md` §眞木), not Substitution /
  snowclone / 大喜利-style templating.
- **Claiming "neta is always cringe-risky, so avoid it."** Neta
  that meets the 3 framing conditions (recognition + compression +
  relevance) and passes `neta-safety-gate.md` is a valid advanced
  technique. Wholesale avoidance is as much an error as reckless use.
- **Relying on fresh memes for evergreen copy.** Meme half-life is
  ≈4-6 months (Shifman 2014 + Cambridge *Humor 2.0* Ch. 16). Copy
  intended for >6-month shelf life must use evergreen references
  (classics, structural patterns) not recent viral content.
- **Injecting neta before the base framework is set.** Neta
  injection is a post-production layer. Apply after PASONA / BEAF /
  PREP / CREMA / キャッチコピー structure is solid, not during
  initial drafting.
