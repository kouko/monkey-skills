# Source Quality and Evidence

Canonical anchor for the primary / secondary / tertiary source taxonomy
and the journalistic verification discipline that research-team uses
when judging whether a cited artifact is load-bearing. Tier 1: LLMs
recover the taxonomy well from training data; the body stays compact
and points at authoritative library-science and journalism anchors
rather than re-spelling them.

## Primary Sources

- James Madison University Libraries — "Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Sources". https://guides.lib.jmu.edu/sources. Discipline-by-discipline worked examples of what counts as primary / secondary / tertiary in humanities, sciences, and social sciences.
- Cornell University Library — "Tertiary Sources". https://guides.library.cornell.edu/sources/tertiary. Operational definition of tertiary as "sources that compile, analyze, and digest secondary sources" — encyclopedias, textbooks, handbooks, databases, fact books.
- Association of College and Research Libraries (2016) *Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education*. https://www.ala.org/acrl/standards/ilframework. The six frames (Authority is Constructed and Contextual / Information Creation as Process / Information has Value / Research as Inquiry / Scholarship as Conversation / Searching as Strategic Exploration) are the field consensus on how to judge authority and when to trust a source.
- Bill Kovach & Tom Rosenstiel (2021) *The Elements of Journalism: What Newspeople Should Know and the Public Should Expect*, 4th ed. Crown. Ch.4 "Journalism of Verification" defines the discipline of verification that research-team borrows for factual claims about current events.
- Society of Professional Journalists (2014) *Code of Ethics*. https://www.spj.org/spj-code-of-ethics/. "Seek Truth and Report It" + "Minimize Harm" + "Act Independently" + "Be Accountable and Transparent" — the four pillars that anchor ethical sourcing.

## The Primary / Secondary / Tertiary Taxonomy

Library and information science draws a three-level distinction that
research-team adopts verbatim. The classification is **not** a property
of the document itself — it is a function of **how the document is
being used** for a given research question.

| Level | Definition | How it is used |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Original, first-hand record of an event, experiment, observation, creative work, or raw data. Produced by a witness or participant. | Direct evidence for a claim. The researcher draws their own inference from it. |
| **Secondary** | Analysis, interpretation, or synthesis of primary sources, one step removed from the event. | Interpretive framing, contextualization, literature review. |
| **Tertiary** | Compilation or digest of secondary sources — encyclopedias, textbooks, handbooks, fact books, databases, popular summaries. | Orientation and navigation — a starting point for finding primary and secondary sources; **not** a load-bearing citation. |

### Discipline-specific examples (JMU / Cornell)

Different disciplines produce different primary sources. Memorise the
discipline-specific examples, not just the abstract definitions.

| Discipline | Primary | Secondary | Tertiary |
|---|---|---|---|
| Humanities / History | Letters, diaries, photographs, treaties, original manuscripts, interviews | Biographies, historical monographs, scholarly essays | Encyclopedias, textbooks, chronologies |
| Sciences | Lab notebooks, experimental datasets, peer-reviewed research articles, patents, conference proceedings | Review articles, meta-analyses, scientific monographs | Textbooks, handbooks, fact books, field guides |
| Social Sciences | Raw survey data, interview transcripts, fieldwork notes, government statistics | Scholarly analysis of survey data, review articles | Handbooks, encyclopedias, statistical digests |
| Law | Statutes, court opinions, treaties, regulations | Law review articles, treatises | Legal encyclopedias, restatements, annotated codes |
| Journalism | On-the-record interviews, court filings, press releases, primary documents obtained via FOIA | Wire service stories, long-form analysis, editorials | News summaries, compendia, "year in review" pieces |

### "Usage determines classification"

The same artifact can be primary in one research question and secondary
in another. A 1960s textbook is a **tertiary** source if you want to
learn what quantum mechanics says today, but a **primary** source if
you are studying how quantum mechanics was taught in 1960s American
universities. Research-team workers MUST state what the research
question is before judging a source's level, not the other way around.

## Journalism of Verification Discipline (Kovach & Rosenstiel 2021)

For factual claims about current events, research-team borrows the
verification discipline from Kovach & Rosenstiel 2021 Ch.4. The
canonical rules:

- **Never add** anything that was not there in the source record.
- **Never deceive** the audience about how you obtained the information.
- **Be transparent** about your methods, motives, and source quality.
- **Rely on your own original reporting** when possible; treat
  second-hand retellings as leads that still need verification.
- **Exercise humility** — be prepared to be wrong and to correct.

Cross-reference: research-team's fact / analysis / speculation
taxonomy (`citation-standards.md`) is the operational expression of
Kovach & Rosenstiel's verification discipline applied to research
output.

## Anti-Patterns

- Citing a Wikipedia article (tertiary) as load-bearing evidence for a
  factual claim. Wikipedia is a starting point for finding primary
  sources, not itself a primary or secondary source.
- Citing a textbook as a primary source for a framework that originated
  in a peer-reviewed paper — cite the paper, not the textbook that
  repeats it.
- Treating a retelling (blog post summarizing a paper) as the source
  when the actual paper is accessible. Cite the paper.
