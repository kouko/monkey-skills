# Strategic Frameworks

Anchor for the four canonical business-strategy frameworks research-team
invokes when a deliverable requires competitive, market, business-model,
or brand analysis: Porter's Five Forces, Blue Ocean Strategy, the
Business Model Canvas, and Aaker's Brand Equity model. Tier 1: LLMs
recover all four well from training data — the body stays compact,
anchors the primary sources, and supplies a decision rule for which
framework to pick.

## Primary Sources

- Michael E. Porter (1980) *Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing Industries and Competitors*. Free Press. Ch.1 introduces the Five Forces as the canonical industry-structure analysis and is the single authoritative source for the framework.
- Michael E. Porter (2008) "The Five Competitive Forces That Shape Strategy". *Harvard Business Review* 86(1): 25-40. https://hbr.org/2008/01/the-five-competitive-forces-that-shape-strategy. Porter's own 2008 update and clarification of the 1980 model — cite this for the current, author-approved formulation.
- W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne (2015) *Blue Ocean Strategy*, Expanded Edition. Harvard Business Review Press. The expanded edition updates the 2005 original with the ERRC Grid and the Strategy Canvas tool; Ch.2 grounds the red-ocean vs blue-ocean distinction.
- Alexander Osterwalder & Yves Pigneur (2010) *Business Model Generation*. Wiley. The canonical source for the 9-block Business Model Canvas (BMC); Ch.1 introduces all 9 blocks; Ch.5 grounds the canvas as a visual thinking tool.
- David A. Aaker (1991) *Managing Brand Equity*. Free Press. The original source for the 5-component brand equity model (brand awareness, perceived quality, brand loyalty, brand associations, other proprietary brand assets).

## Porter's Five Forces

The five forces that together determine industry profitability:

- **Threat of new entrants** — barriers to entry (capital, regulation,
  network effects, switching costs).
- **Bargaining power of suppliers** — concentration, substitute inputs,
  supplier switching costs.
- **Bargaining power of buyers** — concentration, price sensitivity,
  buyer switching costs, backward integration threat.
- **Threat of substitute products or services** — substitutes from
  outside the industry that meet the same buyer need.
- **Rivalry among existing competitors** — industry growth rate,
  differentiation, exit barriers, fixed-cost structure.

Porter 2008 emphasizes: the goal is **not** to beat the five forces
but to **position the firm** where the forces are weakest, or to
reshape the forces in the firm's favor.

## Blue Ocean Strategy — Red Ocean vs Blue Ocean

- **Red oceans** are existing industries where the boundaries are
  defined and the competitive rules are known. Firms try to
  outperform rivals to grab market share; competition turns the
  water red.
- **Blue oceans** are untapped market space where demand is created
  rather than fought over. The strategic move is **value innovation**
  — simultaneously pursuing differentiation and low cost — rather than
  positioning on the existing cost / differentiation trade-off curve.

**ERRC Grid** (Eliminate / Reduce / Raise / Create): the four-question
tool for reconstructing buyer value without inheriting industry
assumptions. Which factors should be **eliminated** outright? Which
**reduced** below the industry standard? Which **raised** above? Which
**created** that the industry has never offered?

## Business Model Canvas — the 9 Blocks (Osterwalder & Pigneur 2010)

A single-page visual of a business model, composed of 9 building
blocks arranged in a canonical layout:

```
┌──────────────┬──────────────┬──────────────┬──────────────┬──────────────┐
│  Key         │  Key         │    Value     │  Customer    │   Customer   │
│  Partners    │  Activities  │ Propositions │ Relationships│   Segments   │
│              ├──────────────┤              ├──────────────┤              │
│              │  Key         │              │   Channels   │              │
│              │  Resources   │              │              │              │
├──────────────┴──────────────┴──────────────┴──────────────┴──────────────┤
│       Cost Structure          │         Revenue Streams                  │
└───────────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The right half of the canvas answers "who is the customer and what
value do we deliver?" (**Customer Segments**, **Value Propositions**,
**Channels**, **Customer Relationships**, **Revenue Streams**). The
left half answers "what infrastructure is needed to deliver that
value?" (**Key Activities**, **Key Resources**, **Key Partners**, **Cost
Structure**). The **Value Propositions** block sits at the center as
the bridge between demand-side and supply-side.

## Aaker's Brand Equity — 5 Components

Aaker 1991 decomposes brand equity into five value-generating
components:

1. **Brand Awareness** — the degree to which consumers recognize and
   recall the brand in relevant purchase contexts.
2. **Perceived Quality** — the overall quality judgment consumers
   attach to the brand, independent of objective specifications.
3. **Brand Loyalty** — the degree to which consumers stick with the
   brand in the face of competitive offers and price differences.
4. **Brand Associations** — the network of attributes, emotions, and
   memories consumers link to the brand (user imagery, usage imagery,
   celebrity associations, symbols).
5. **Other Proprietary Brand Assets** — trademarks, patents, channel
   relationships that competitors cannot easily replicate.

Brand equity is the strategic asset that outlives any single product
or campaign; Aaker's model is how research-team assesses the defensive
moat of a consumer brand.

## When to Use Which Framework — Decision Rule

| Research question | Framework |
|---|---|
| "Is this industry attractive?" / "Where is profitability coming from?" | **Porter Five Forces** |
| "Is there a non-obvious market space we are missing?" / "Can we reconstruct the value curve?" | **Blue Ocean / ERRC Grid** |
| "How does this company actually make money?" / "What is the model?" | **Business Model Canvas** |
| "What is the defensive value of this brand?" / "Why can consumers not be won back?" | **Aaker Brand Equity** |

Picking the wrong framework is a bigger error than picking the right
framework and filling it in sloppily — Porter applied to a nascent
market where there are no competitors yet will mislead; Blue Ocean
applied to a mature commodity industry will not surface the real
problem.

## Anti-Patterns

- Running Porter's Five Forces on a pre-product company with no
  industry to analyze yet.
- Using the Business Model Canvas as a substitute for a financial
  model — BMC is a visual reasoning tool, not a P&L.
- Treating Blue Ocean as "find a blank space and go there" — value
  innovation requires simultaneously reducing cost and raising
  differentiation, not just finding a gap.
- Mixing Aaker brand equity components with random other "brand
  metrics" from marketing vendor decks. Use Aaker's 5 components
  verbatim or cite a different model explicitly.
