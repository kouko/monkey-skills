# Nielsen-Norman Foundations & Heuristics

Canonical anchors for Don Norman's action-cycle vocabulary and Jakob
Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics. Tier 1: LLMs recover these well from
training; the body is deliberately short and points at authoritative
sources rather than re-spelling them.

## Primary Sources

- Donald A. Norman (2013) *The Design of Everyday Things: Revised and Expanded Edition*. Basic Books. Ch.1-4 ground affordance / signifier / mapping; Ch.2 grounds the 7-stage action cycle and the gulfs of execution / evaluation.
- Jakob Nielsen (1994; updated 2024-01-30) "10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design". https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ten-usability-heuristics/. Canonical names and numbering for the 10 heuristics.
- James J. Gibson (1979) *The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception*. Houghton Mifflin. Ch.8 is the origin of the affordance concept that Norman 2013 adapts for design.

## Norman's Design Vocabulary (Ch.1-4)

Norman 2013 establishes the core vocabulary designers use to reason
about everyday objects and interfaces: **affordance** (what an artifact
offers the user to do), **signifier** (the perceptual clue the designer
places to reveal the affordance), **mapping** (the relationship between
controls and their effects), **feedback** (how the system communicates
the results of an action), and **conceptual model** (the mental model
the user forms of how the system works). These terms are not
interchangeable — mis-using "affordance" to mean "visible cue" rather
than "action possibility" erases the signifier / affordance distinction
Norman drew in the revised edition.

## The 7-Stage Action Cycle (Ch.2)

Norman models action as a cycle with two halves:

1. Goal formation
2. Intention to act
3. Plan a sequence of actions
4. Physically execute the actions
5. Perceive system state
6. Interpret the perception
7. Evaluate outcome vs goal

Stages 2-4 form the **execution side**; stages 5-7 form the
**evaluation side**. The **Gulf of Execution** is the gap between the
user's intention and what the system allows them to express. The **Gulf
of Evaluation** is the gap between the system's state display and what
the user can understand from it. Good design narrows both gulfs.

## Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics (1994; reaffirmed 2024-01-30)

Canonical names and numbering — do not reorder, rename, or merge these.

1. **Visibility of System Status** — keep users informed of what is
   happening through appropriate, timely feedback.
2. **Match Between System and the Real World** — use familiar words,
   phrases, and concepts; follow real-world conventions.
3. **User Control and Freedom** — provide clearly marked exits, undo,
   and redo for mistaken actions.
4. **Consistency and Standards** — follow platform and product
   conventions; do not make users wonder whether different words or
   actions mean the same thing.
5. **Error Prevention** — design out errors before confirmations and
   warnings are needed; eliminate error-prone conditions or surface
   them as confirmations before commit.
6. **Recognition Rather than Recall** — minimize memory load; make
   objects, actions, and options visible or easily retrievable.
7. **Flexibility and Efficiency of Use** — accelerators (keyboard
   shortcuts, power-user paths) that speed up expert interaction while
   remaining invisible to novices.
8. **Aesthetic and Minimalist Design** — interfaces should not contain
   information which is irrelevant or rarely needed; every extra unit
   competes with the relevant units for attention.
9. **Help Users Recognize, Diagnose, and Recover from Errors** — plain
   language error messages that precisely indicate the problem and
   constructively suggest a solution.
10. **Help and Documentation** — concise, searchable, task-oriented
    help that lists concrete steps and is easy to locate at the moment
    of need.

## Affordance Lineage Note

Gibson 1979 defined affordance in ecological perception as what the
environment offers an animal (e.g., a surface is sit-on-able if it is
flat, stable, and at knee height). Norman 2013 narrows the design use
to **perceivable** affordances and introduces **signifiers** as the
designer-placed perceptual clues that reveal which affordances exist.
Cite Gibson 1979 for the theoretical origin; cite Norman 2013 when the
claim concerns design practice.
