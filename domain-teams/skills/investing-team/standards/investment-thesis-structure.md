# Investment — Thesis Structure

Fully self-contained Tier 2 standard covering the **thesis construction
layer** of investing-team's work: the structured argument a worker must
build before any position sizing, verdict, or recommendation can be
issued. This file is the SSOT for thesis anatomy, variant perception,
inversion, and second-level thinking as applied to individual securities
or instruments.

Tier 2 because the core concepts (second-level thinking, pre-mortem,
conviction vs. sizing) are moderately parametric in LLM training data,
but the specific integration — how variant perception, inversion,
catalysts, and invalidators assemble into a single coherent thesis
unit — is routinely collapsed, misordered, or conflated with mere
"analysis." The body spells out the exact 5-element anatomy and the
decision sequence that links it to downstream standards.

**Scope**: Thesis construction for a single security or instrument.
Macro regime diagnosis belongs to `investment-macro-regime.md`; sector
and industry analysis belongs to `investment-sector-industry.md`;
valuation mechanics belong to `investment-security-valuation.md`;
position sizing and risk belong to `investment-position-sizing.md`;
the final buy/sell/hold verdict belongs to
`decision-framework-and-verdict.md`.

## Critical Attribution Corrections

### Variant perception is not contrarianism

LLM drafts routinely equate "variant perception" with "being a
contrarian" (i.e., taking the opposite of the consensus view). This
is wrong. Variant perception, as defined by Drobny 2006's practitioner
interviews, is a **specific, measurable, articulable divergence from
what the market is pricing** — it may or may not be contrarian in
direction. A consensus-agreeing view that consensus is *right for the
wrong reasons* can still be a valid variant perception. The load-bearing
test is not "do I disagree?" but "can I state precisely what I believe
that the current market price does not reflect, and why my information
or framework is more accurate than the aggregated market view?" Do NOT
conflate variant perception with contrarianism.

### Pre-mortem is prospective, not retrospective

LLMs and popular-press usage conflate pre-mortem with post-mortem.
They are different in timing and purpose. The **pre-mortem** (Mauboussin
2012; Klein 1989 antecedent) is a **prospective** technique performed
*before* the position is taken: "Imagine it is 12 months from now and
this thesis was completely wrong — what was the most likely cause?"
The post-mortem is a **retrospective** review of what actually happened
after the fact. Inverting a pre-mortem into a post-mortem is a
discipline failure — the pre-mortem's value is precisely that it forces
identification of failure modes before anchoring bias sets in. Do NOT
use the terms interchangeably.

### Fisher's scuttlebutt method is qualitative fieldwork, not social media sentiment

LLMs regularly modernize Fisher's "scuttlebutt method" (Fisher 1958
Ch.3) into "gathering information from social media, Reddit, or
Twitter/X." This is a category error. Scuttlebutt is a structured
program of **direct conversations** with the company's customers,
competitors, suppliers, former employees, and industry participants —
practitioners who have operational knowledge of the business's
competitive position. The purpose is to surface durable competitive
information that does not appear in financial statements or sell-side
research. Social media sentiment is a noise signal measuring
short-term opinion, not a structural competitive assessment. Do NOT
substitute sentiment data for scuttlebutt fieldwork.

## The Thesis as a Structured Argument

A thesis is not analysis. Analysis produces facts; a thesis produces
an argument — a structured claim that a security is **mispriced
relative to intrinsic value** (or relative to probable future price),
that the worker can identify **why** the mispricing exists, and that
the worker can specify **what would change** the thesis. Without these
three elements, what exists is analysis but not a thesis.

The standard unit of output from this layer is a **thesis document**
that a third party can read and disagree with. A thesis that no
reasonable person could disagree with is too vague to act on.

## 1. Variant Perception — The Load-Bearing Question

Variant perception is the foundation of every investable thesis. It
is defined as a **specific, articulable divergence between your view
of value or future state and what the current market price implies**.

The load-bearing question (Drobny 2006):

> "What do I believe that consensus doesn't believe, and why am I
> right about it?"

This question must be answered before the thesis is complete. If the
answer is "nothing" — the worker agrees with consensus on all material
points — then there is no variant perception, no thesis, and no
rationale for taking a position. A thesis that agrees with consensus
cannot generate alpha, even if the analysis is correct (Marks 2011
Ch.4: the market has already priced in what consensus believes).

### How to Surface Variant Perception

The practical method is a three-step contrast:

1. **State what your analysis produces**: e.g., "My DCF values the
   equity at $85/share at a 5-year horizon using 12% WACC and 3%
   terminal growth."

2. **State what the market is pricing in**: reverse-engineer the
   implied assumptions from the current price. If the stock trades at
   $55, the market is pricing in either a higher discount rate, lower
   terminal growth, or near-term earnings shortfall. Be specific: "At
   $55, the market implies either terminal growth of −1% or WACC of
   16% — both of which are inconsistent with the company's contract
   backlog and asset life."

3. **Articulate the gap and your reason for it**: "I believe the
   market is applying a distressed discount because of the Q2 miss,
   which was driven by a one-time supply chain disruption now resolved.
   My scuttlebutt (Fisher 1958) from three industry conversations
   confirms the disruption was non-recurring. The consensus has not
   updated."

The variant perception is the **gap** plus the **reason for it**. The
gap alone is not sufficient — every mispriced security has a gap; the
thesis must explain why the market is wrong and the worker is right.

### Variant Perception Validity Tests

A well-formed variant perception passes all three tests:

- **Measurable**: can be stated as a specific difference in a
  valuation parameter, timeline, probability, or magnitude (not
  "the company is underappreciated")
- **Falsifiable**: the worker can specify what evidence would prove
  the variant perception wrong
- **Not already in the price**: check sell-side consensus estimates,
  options-implied distributions, and recent management guidance;
  if the market already reflects your view, it is no longer variant

## 2. Thesis Anatomy — Five Required Elements

A complete thesis document contains exactly five elements. A document
missing any one is incomplete and cannot proceed to verdict.

### Element 1: Operative Claim

One sentence in the form: "I believe **[security/instrument]** is
**[overvalued / undervalued / positioned to move in direction X]**
because **[primary reason grounded in variant perception]**."

Example: "I believe Acme Corp. common shares are undervalued by
approximately 35% because the market is pricing in permanent margin
compression from the 2024 cost spike, while my analysis of their
Q3 contract win signals a structural input-cost normalization the
Street has not modeled."

The operative claim is not a conclusion — it is the assertion to be
supported by the rest of the thesis.

### Element 2: Variant Perception Delta

The specific, measurable way the worker's view diverges from consensus.
State it quantitatively where possible.

Format: "Consensus implies [X]; I believe [Y]; the delta is [Z] and
it arises from [reason]."

Example: "Consensus sell-side median implies 5% EBIT margin recovery
in FY2026; my model implies 11% based on the contracted input-price
fix signed in October. The delta of 6 margin points flows through to
approximately $1.2B additional EBIT and $0.80 additional EPS."

### Element 3: Catalysts

The 1–3 **falsifiable events or conditions** that will cause the
market to update toward the worker's view, with approximate timeline.
Catalysts must be observable by third parties — they cannot be
subjective interpretations.

Requirements:
- Specific: a scheduled earnings release, a regulatory decision, a
  contract announcement — not "the market will eventually realize"
- Timely: attach a rough window (next quarter, 6–18 months, etc.)
- Falsifiable: at the catalyst date, it will be clear whether the
  catalyst fired and whether the market responded

Example catalysts for the Acme thesis:
1. Q4 2025 earnings release (expected February): gross margin line
   will either confirm or deny input-cost normalization
2. FY2026 guidance: management will quantify the impact of the new
   contract on forward margins
3. Competitor earnings (March): will show industry-wide or
   company-specific dynamics

A thesis with no catalysts is a "value trap" thesis — the worker
may be correct on valuation but has no mechanism for when or why
the price converges to value.

### Element 4: Invalidators

The 1–3 **conditions that would prove the thesis wrong** (Fisher
1958 discipline: "what if I'm wrong?"). These must be specified
before the position is taken, not after the price moves against it.

Requirements:
- Specific: what observable fact or event would force a thesis
  revision
- Pre-committed: written down before entry, not rewritten after
  adverse price movement
- Distinct from normal noise: distinguish thesis-breaking events
  from normal price volatility

Example invalidators for the Acme thesis:
1. Q4 gross margin prints below 18% (would indicate the input-cost
   normalization thesis is wrong, not merely delayed)
2. Management revises the new contract terms materially downward
   at any point
3. Competitor announces equivalent input-cost fix at lower pricing
   (destroys the competitive-advantage component of the thesis)

**A thesis with no invalidators cannot be managed**. Without
pre-specified invalidators, the worker has no rational exit criteria
and is susceptible to anchoring — holding the position while
constructing post-hoc rationales after the thesis breaks. This is
the Bevelin (2007) "avoiding stupidity" discipline applied
operationally: enumerate failure modes before you commit.

### Element 5: Provenance Footer

The data sources used to construct the thesis, fetched dates, and
as-of dates for any time-sensitive inputs.

Required fields:
- Primary data sources (filings, earnings transcripts, industry data)
- Valuation model as-of date
- Consensus estimates source and as-of date (e.g., "FactSet consensus
  as of 2025-11-15")
- Any scuttlebutt conversations (date, role of source, no names
  required)
- Macro regime context used (cite `investment-macro-regime.md`
  framework if applicable)

The provenance footer enables the thesis to be stress-tested and
updated over time. A thesis with no provenance cannot be audited.

## 3. Inversion — Bevelin / Munger Discipline

Inversion is the practice of **listing the ways the thesis fails
before arguing why it succeeds**. It is the structural application of
Bevelin's (2007) "avoiding stupidity" principle: most catastrophic
investment outcomes come from ignoring obvious failure modes, not
from failing to identify obscure upside.

### The Inversion Prompt

Before completing any thesis, answer this question:

> "What would have to be true for this to be a terrible investment?"

Generate at least three answers. For each answer, check whether the
current analysis has addressed it. If an answer surfaces a risk the
thesis has not addressed, either address it or explicitly acknowledge
it as a known unresolved risk.

### The Pre-Mortem

The pre-mortem (Mauboussin 2012) is a structured inversion exercise
performed prospectively:

1. **Frame the failure**: "Imagine it is 12 months from now. We took
   this position and it was badly wrong — a 40% loss from entry."

2. **Name the most likely cause**: what single factor, if it
   materialized, would have caused this outcome? Do not list every
   possible risk — identify the one most likely, most impactful
   failure mode.

3. **Check whether it is addressable**: is the failure mode captured
   in the thesis invalidators? If not, add it.

The pre-mortem's value is forcing the worker to vivify a specific
failure before anchoring bias — generated by the work invested in
building the thesis — makes failure feel remote. Do this step
explicitly, not as a mental exercise.

### Common Inversion Outputs

The following failure modes recur across investment theses and should
be checked as a minimum baseline:

- **Thesis is correct but too early**: the variant perception is
  right but the catalyst timeline is wrong by 2–3x; position decays
  while waiting
- **Consensus is wrong in the right direction**: the thesis turns
  out correct, but the market re-rates for a different reason, which
  means the thesis's framework cannot be trusted for sizing the
  follow-on opportunity
- **Key assumption is a proxy, not the variable itself**: e.g.,
  using management commentary as a proxy for actual contract pricing
  when the contract is not disclosed
- **Second-order effects neglected**: the variant perception is
  correct for the company but the macro regime shifts in a way that
  offsets the stock-specific upside (see `investment-macro-regime.md`
  for regime-layer interaction)
- **Scuttlebutt sample is biased**: all industry conversations were
  with the same supply-chain segment; demand-side view was missing

## 4. Second-Level Thinking — Marks Discipline

Second-level thinking (Marks 2011 Ch.2) is the prerequisite for a
thesis that generates alpha. It is not a technique applied after
analysis — it is the **test applied to the analysis output** before
the thesis is considered complete.

### First Level vs. Second Level

| Level | Example statement | Why it fails as a thesis |
|---|---|---|
| **First-level** | "This is a good company with strong fundamentals." | Consensus agrees; price already reflects this. No alpha. |
| **First-level** | "Earnings will grow 15% next year." | If consensus estimates 15%, the price is already there. |
| **Second-level** | "The market prices in 5% terminal growth because of the sector de-rating in H1; I believe the de-rating was macro-driven and the company's structural moat is intact. My 8% terminal growth implies 30% upside." | Specific, measurable divergence from market pricing with a reason. |
| **Second-level** | "The market is pricing in permanent margin compression (12% EBIT). I believe Q3's contract win signals structural input normalization the Street has missed, re-rating the stock to 18% EBIT in 2 years." | Identifiable mechanism, quantified variant perception, catalyst specified. |

The thesis quality test: **could a reasonable, well-informed person
who read your thesis disagree with it?** If the answer is no — if
the thesis is merely a restatement of facts that every analyst in the
sector would accept — then it is first-level. A thesis that cannot
be disagreed with is not a thesis.

### The "So What?" Drill

For each analytical finding in the thesis, apply: "So what? Does the
market not know this?" If the answer is "the market already knows
this and it is priced in," the finding contributes to the background
but not to the variant perception. Only findings where the market
does not know, or has misweighted, contribute to a second-level
thesis.

## 5. Thesis Validity vs. Position Sizing

Thesis construction and position sizing are **separate decisions**
made sequentially, not simultaneously.

The thesis document answers: "Do I have a coherent, variant,
falsifiable argument for why this security is mispriced?"

Position sizing answers: "Given that thesis, how much risk should
I take?" Position sizing is a separate analytical step governed by
`investment-position-sizing.md` and interacts with portfolio
construction rules in `investment-portfolio-construction.md`.

**The separation matters because:**

- A structurally sound thesis can be sized small (or not at all) if
  the uncertainty around the variant perception is high, the catalyst
  timeline is too long for the portfolio's constraints, or the
  position would create unacceptable correlation with existing holdings
- A confident, well-constructed thesis can still be sized modestly
  when the security is illiquid, the sector concentration is already
  high, or the macro regime creates systematic risk that overrides
  the stock-specific thesis (Marks 2011 Ch.4 interplay)
- Rushing from thesis to position size without the intervening
  sizing framework is the most common structural failure in
  individual investor practice

The explicit output of this layer is a **thesis document**, not a
position. The output of `decision-framework-and-verdict.md` is the
verdict. Position sizing is a downstream step. Do not collapse these
three outputs into a single action.

## 6. Calibration — Mauboussin Discipline

Conviction must be calibrated to the quality of the evidence
underlying the thesis, not to the strength of the analytical work
invested. Mauboussin (2012) frames this as distinguishing
**skill-based signals** from **luck-based signals**.

### Skill-Based vs. Luck-Based Signals

| Signal type | Examples | Conviction contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Skill-based** (durable, repeatable) | Verified competitive moat, management capital allocation track record, structural cost advantage confirmed by scuttlebutt, long-term contract securing margin | High — these persist across quarters and are not noise-driven |
| **Luck-based** (noisy, mean-reverting) | Last quarter's earnings beat, short-term price momentum, recent analyst upgrade, one positive channel check | Low — these do not predict future outcomes at skill-level frequencies |

A thesis built primarily on luck-based signals has low calibrated
conviction, even if the argument is internally consistent. The
correct response is not to suppress the thesis but to **shrink the
confidence interval** and **shorten the catalyst horizon**: a
momentum-based thesis should have a near-term catalyst (next
earnings) and a tight invalidator (stop if the momentum reverses
within X weeks), not a 2-year holding thesis.

### Confidence Interval on Variant Perception

For each variant perception delta, state an explicit confidence
interval:

Format: "I am **[X]% confident** that the consensus is wrong by at
least **[Y%]** in the direction of **[my view]**, based on
**[evidence quality classification]**."

Example: "I am 65% confident that consensus is underestimating
FY2026 EBIT margin by at least 4 percentage points, based on: (a)
one high-quality scuttlebutt conversation with a direct supply-chain
participant, (b) public competitor disclosure confirming the
industry-wide input cost shift, and (c) management's Q3 language
that was consistent with margin normalization."

The confidence interval forces honesty about evidence quality before
the thesis is passed to the sizing step. A thesis with a 55%
confidence interval warrants smaller sizing than a thesis with an
80% confidence interval, even if both are structurally sound
(Mauboussin 2012 calibration principle).

### Evidence Quality Tiers

Use these tiers when classifying evidence in the provenance footer:

1. **Primary / direct**: scuttlebutt from supply-chain / demand-side
   participants, company's own disclosed data (contracts, filings),
   direct channel checks with product / service users
2. **Secondary / cross-checked**: sell-side research cross-verified
   against public data, multiple independent sources converging on
   the same finding
3. **Observational / single-source**: one analyst's estimate, one
   management statement, one channel check — informative but not
   independently verified
4. **Inferred**: logical extrapolation from analogs, historical
   base rates, or comparable company behavior — the weakest evidence
   tier; flag explicitly when used

A thesis relying primarily on Tier 4 evidence should declare a low
confidence interval regardless of how compelling the logical chain is.

## 7. Thesis Document Template

The following is the canonical template for a thesis document
produced at this layer. All five required elements must be present.

```
THESIS DOCUMENT
Security: [Ticker / ISIN / instrument description]
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Worker: [investing-team worker]

OPERATIVE CLAIM
I believe [security] is [overvalued / undervalued / positioned for X]
because [primary reason].

VARIANT PERCEPTION DELTA
Consensus implies: [specific metric / value]
My view: [specific metric / value]
Delta: [quantified gap]
Reason for delta: [why the market is wrong, grounded in evidence]
Confidence: [X]% that consensus is wrong by at least [Y]

CATALYSTS
1. [Event/condition] — expected [timeframe]
2. [Event/condition] — expected [timeframe]
(3. [optional third catalyst])

INVALIDATORS
1. [Specific observable fact/event that would prove the thesis wrong]
2. [Specific observable fact/event that would prove the thesis wrong]
(3. [optional third invalidator])

INVERSION / PRE-MORTEM
What would have to be true for this to be a terrible investment?
→ [Top failure mode]
→ [Second failure mode]
→ [Third failure mode]
Pre-mortem: If wrong in 12 months, most likely cause: [specific cause]

PROVENANCE
Data sources: [list]
Valuation model as-of: [date]
Consensus source and as-of: [source, date]
Scuttlebutt: [date, role — no names; note tier of evidence]
Macro regime context: [IC phase / GIP quad if applicable; cite
  investment-macro-regime.md]
```

## 8. Thesis Lifecycle

A thesis is a living document, not a one-time artifact. It requires
active management:

**Pre-entry**: thesis document complete with all five elements; inversion
and pre-mortem performed; confidence interval declared.

**On catalyst events**: update the thesis with the actual outcome of
each catalyst. Did the catalyst fire? Did the market respond as
expected? If the catalyst fired and the market did not respond, the
thesis may be wrong on mechanism (not just timing) — investigate
before reaffirming.

**On invalidator triggers**: if any invalidator materializes, the thesis
is broken. The correct response is thesis review, not price-based
hope. A broken thesis that continues to be held is no longer thesis-
driven investing — it is an unexamined loss.

**Thesis vs. position**: a thesis revision does not automatically require
position revision, but it requires explicit acknowledgment of what
changed and what the updated thesis is. "I held because the price
recovered" is not a thesis update.

## Cross-Layer Usage Notes

This thesis layer sits between the analysis layers and the verdict
layer:

- **Upstream inputs**: the thesis consumes outputs from
  `investment-macro-regime.md` (regime context for the operative
  claim), `investment-sector-industry.md` (sector / factor context
  for the variant perception), and `investment-security-valuation.md`
  (quantitative valuation output that the thesis uses to construct
  the variant perception delta)

- **Downstream consumers**: the completed thesis document is the
  **required input** to `decision-framework-and-verdict.md` — no
  verdict can be issued without a complete thesis. Position sizing
  (`investment-position-sizing.md`) is a further downstream step that
  consumes both the verdict and the confidence interval declared in
  the thesis

- **Bypass prohibition**: no worker may jump from analysis directly
  to verdict or sizing without producing a thesis document. The thesis
  document is the explicit checkpoint that ensures variant perception
  has been articulated and inversion has been performed before capital
  is committed.
