# Labor / 고용

---

## unemployment: 실업률 / Unemployment Rate

- **Series code**: K303 (ECOS-KEYSTAT)
- **Source**: Bank of Korea (Statistics Korea data) via FinanceDataReader
- **Unit**: Percent (%)
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: ~3 weeks after reference month (released mid-month)
- **History**: From 1999 (322 observations)

**What it measures**: The share of the labor force that is unemployed and
actively seeking work, based on the Economically Active Population Survey
(경제활동인구조사) conducted by Statistics Korea.

**How to interpret**:
- Rising → Labor market weakening. More people unable to find work. A
  lagging indicator — unemployment typically rises after GDP has already
  contracted. Sustained rises above 4% signal meaningful weakness.
- Falling → Labor market tightening. Can signal capacity constraints and
  potential wage-push inflation.

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐
Korea's headline unemployment rate is structurally low compared to Western
economies (typically 2.5-4.0%). While the headline number is closely
watched, market participants also track "extended unemployment" metrics
(including discouraged workers and involuntary part-time) for a fuller
picture.

**When to use**: Labor market slack assessment, BOK dual mandate tracking, consumption outlook gauge, structural unemployment analysis.

**Korea-specific context**:
- Korea's headline unemployment rate consistently understates labor market
  slack. This is due to:
  1. **Self-employment** (~25% of employment): Self-employed workers are
     classified as "employed" even during severe downturns when income drops.
  2. **Discouraged workers**: Those who stop actively searching are not
     counted as unemployed.
  3. **Underemployment**: Part-time workers seeking full-time work are
     counted as employed.
- Youth unemployment (15-29 age group) is structurally high (7-10%) due
  to the mismatch between university graduates and available jobs. This is
  a major social issue in Korea.
- Korea's labor market has a strong seasonal pattern: unemployment rises
  in January-February (post-Lunar New Year) and falls in March-April
  (spring hiring season).
- Korea's rapid aging (one of the fastest in the world) is shrinking the
  working-age population, creating structural labor shortages in certain
  sectors despite elevated youth unemployment.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Do not compare Korea's 3% unemployment directly with US 4% unemployment.
  Definitions and structural factors differ significantly.
- Seasonal adjustment matters. The unadjusted rate has strong seasonal
  patterns (winter peaks, summer troughs).
- Government employment programs (공공근로) can temporarily suppress the
  headline rate. These programs expanded significantly post-COVID.

## employment-rate: 고용률 / Employment Rate

- **Series code**: K304 (ECOS-KEYSTAT)
- **Source**: Bank of Korea (Statistics Korea data) via FinanceDataReader
- **Unit**: Percent (%)
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: Same as unemployment rate
- **History**: From 1999

**What it measures**: The ratio of employed persons to the working-age
population (15+). Unlike the unemployment rate, this is not affected by
labor force participation decisions.

**How to interpret**:
- Rising → More of the working-age population is employed. Positive signal
  for economic activity and household income.
- Falling → Fewer people working relative to population. Can reflect
  discouragement, retirement, or genuine job losses.

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐
Increasingly preferred over the unemployment rate by Korean policy makers
and analysts as a more honest measure of labor utilization. The employment
rate captures discouraged workers and labor force participation changes
that the unemployment rate misses.

**When to use**: True labor utilization measurement, structural labor market analysis, demographic impact tracking, cross-country labor comparison (preferred over unemployment rate).

**Korea-specific context**:
- Korea's employment rate (~62-63% for ages 15+) is lower than the OECD
  average (~68%), primarily due to:
  1. Low female labor force participation (improving but still below OECD avg)
  2. Long education period (most Koreans attend university, delaying entry)
  3. Early retirement from primary careers (~50-55) followed by
     self-employment or lower-paid work
- The Korean government has set explicit employment rate targets as part of
  its economic policy goals.
- Korea's aging demographics create a structural downward pressure on the
  employment rate as the 65+ population grows.

**Common pitfalls**:
- The denominator is working-age population (15+), which includes elderly
  who may not be expected to work. This depresses the rate compared to
  countries using 15-64 as the reference population.
- Rising employment rate during demographic aging may mask declining
  absolute number of workers.
