# Demographics / 인구

Korea faces among the sharpest demographic declines in the OECD.
Population peaked in 2020, fertility rate is the world's lowest, and
the aging ratio is rising at an unprecedented pace. These slow-moving
annual series provide long-horizon context for equity market
(KOSPI chaebol exposure to domestic demand), fixed income (JGB-style
demographic rate compression), and sector allocation (healthcare,
consumer discretionary de-rating).

Cross-market parallel: the closest analogue is Japan's demographic
profile from the 1990s — KR leads JP by ~15-20 years in timing.

---

## population: 추계인구 / Estimated Population

- **Series code**: K451 (ECOS-KEYSTAT)
- **Source**: Statistics Korea (통계청) via BOK ECOS
- **Unit**: Persons
- **Frequency**: Annual
- **Publication lag**: ~2-3 months after year-end
- **History**: From 1990

**What it measures**: Estimated total population of Korea as of
January 1 each year. Based on the Census (10-year cycle) plus mid-year
estimation from the 통계청 demographic model.

**How to interpret**:
- **Peak already reached (2020 at ~51.84M)** — Korea entered demographic
  decline in 2020, earlier than official government projections had
  assumed (previously 2028)
- Current trajectory: ~0.1-0.2% decline per year, accelerating
- Long-run projection: ~38M by 2070 (통계청 중위 시나리오)

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐
Slow-moving but structurally important. Drives terminal-value
assumptions for domestic-consumption-heavy sectors (KOSPI consumer
discretionary, staples, retail). Less relevant for export-heavy
chaebols (Samsung, Hyundai, SK Hynix) where demand is global.

**When to use**: DCF terminal growth assumption for domestic-focused
equity valuations, pension system solvency analysis, property demand
outlook.

**Korea-specific context**:
- Foreign population ratio is low (~4%) and unlikely to materially
  offset domestic decline without major immigration policy shifts.
- The 2020 peak was accelerated by COVID-era fertility drop —
  births fell from 327k (2018) to 230k (2024), with the 2024 fertility
  rate (0.72) being among the world's lowest ever recorded.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Annual frequency, not monthly — do not expect high-frequency signal.
- 통계청 demographic projections are revised every ~5 years; current
  projections may be stale.

## aging-ratio: 고령인구비율 / Elderly Population Ratio (≥65)

- **Series code**: K460 (ECOS-KEYSTAT)
- **Source**: Statistics Korea via BOK ECOS
- **Unit**: Percent (%)
- **Frequency**: Annual
- **Publication lag**: ~2-3 months after year-end
- **History**: From 1990

**What it measures**: Share of population aged 65 and over.
Korea crossed the 20% "super-aged society" threshold in 2025 and is
projected to reach 40% by 2050 (통계청 중위 시나리오) — the fastest
transition globally.

**How to interpret**:
- <14%: "aging society"
- 14-20%: "aged society"
- 20-28%: "super-aged society" (Korea entered this in 2025)
- >28%: "hyper-aged society" (Korea projected to enter ~2035)

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐
Long-term structural driver for:
- Healthcare / pharma sector (positive, secular demand growth)
- Financial sector (assets shift from working-age accumulation to
  retiree decumulation)
- Domestic consumption (headwind)
- Fiscal solvency (pension + health insurance deficit)

**When to use**: Sector rotation theses with long-horizon (5+ year)
views, bond duration allocation (demographic = secular disinflation).

**Korea-specific context**:
- Korea's aging is faster than Japan's — took JP 24 years to go from
  aged (14%) to super-aged (20%), KR took only 18 years.
- Policy response has been fragmented: pension reform debated since
  2007 without consensus; national health insurance premium hikes
  annually but deficit persists.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Annual data; don't confuse with quarterly or monthly economic stats.
- Ratio changes reflect both rising numerator (births) and falling
  denominator (total pop); pay attention to both levels.

## fertility-rate: 합계출산율 / Total Fertility Rate

- **Series code**: K461 (ECOS-KEYSTAT)
- **Source**: Statistics Korea via BOK ECOS
- **Unit**: Births per woman
- **Frequency**: Annual
- **Publication lag**: ~2-3 months after year-end
- **History**: From 1990

**What it measures**: Average number of children a woman is expected
to bear over her reproductive life at current age-specific fertility
rates. Replacement rate ≈ 2.1.

**How to interpret**:
- 2025 reading: ~0.72 (preliminary) — **world's lowest**, far below
  replacement
- Historical Korea trajectory: 4.5 (1970) → 1.5 (1990) → 1.0 (2018) → 0.72 (2025)
- Each 0.1 drop in TFR → ~25k fewer births/year → compounds over decades

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐
Very long-horizon but existentially important. Drives:
- Education sector (negative, shrinking cohorts)
- Housing demand (negative, fewer new households forming)
- Labor supply / wage trajectory (negative, skill scarcity premium)
- Automation / AI demand (positive, labor substitution)

**When to use**: 20-30 year DCF assumptions, structural sector theses
(education, baby products, housing starts).

**Korea-specific context**:
- Causes commonly cited: housing unaffordability (Seoul apartment
  price/income > 20x), work-life balance (longest OECD work hours
  until 2018), 사교육비 (private education costs),
  gender career penalty, delayed marriage (avg first marriage age
  ~33 for men, ~31 for women in 2024).
- Policy response: ~380 trillion KRW spent on fertility incentives
  since 2006 with minimal effect; consensus building that housing
  affordability + work culture are binding constraints.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Annual data, no forward-looking signal usable for short-term trades.
- TFR is not a forecast — it's a *hypothetical* assuming current
  age-specific rates hold, which they often don't. Projection models
  use 중위 (middle)/고위 (high)/저위 (low) scenarios that fan widely.
