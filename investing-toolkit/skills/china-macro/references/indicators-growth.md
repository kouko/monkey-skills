# Growth / 增长

---

## gdp-yoy: GDP 同比 / Gross Domestic Product YoY

- **Series code**: akshare:macro_china_gdp (column: 国内生产总值-同比增长)
- **Source**: 国家统计局 NBS via akshare
- **Unit**: Percent (%)
- **Frequency**: Quarterly
- **Publication lag**: ~20 days after quarter-end — among the fastest of any major economy
- **History**: From the 1990s (100+ quarterly observations)

**What it measures**: Year-over-year real GDP growth, constant prices.
The headline growth number that anchors Chinese policy discussions, the
"around 5%" annual growth target, and global EM growth assumptions.

**How to interpret**:
- Print above target (e.g., >5.0% when the target is "around 5%") →
  Economy running hot enough to meet ambitions; reduces urgency for
  stimulus; CNY typically firms.
- Print below target → Triggers speculation about stimulus scale —
  RRR cuts, LPR cuts, fiscal special bonds, property easing.
- YoY prints of 4% or below are politically significant; Chinese
  authorities have historically shown reluctance to accept sustained
  sub-4% growth.

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐⭐
The headline growth print moves CSI 300, Hang Seng, industrial metals,
AUD/USD (via commodity trade linkage), and global EM sentiment. More
market-relevant than most developed-market GDP prints because it frames
the entire Chinese policy reaction function.

**When to use**: Investment Clock growth axis, China policy-stimulus
probability, global EM allocation, commodity demand framing.

**China-specific context**:
- The annual GDP target is set at the March 两会 (Two Sessions) and
  typically phrased as "around X%" (约X%). Missing the target is
  politically sensitive and triggers second-half stimulus.
- China publishes GDP YoY as the headline. Quarter-on-quarter
  seasonally-adjusted (QoQ SA) figures are published but released with
  additional lag and receive less attention than in developed markets.
- NBS regularly revises historical GDP data upward during periodic
  benchmark revisions (e.g., following economic census updates). A
  historical chart pulled today may not match one pulled two years ago.
- The "GDP target vs. actual" gap is itself a policy signal — if actual
  GDP undershoots for two consecutive quarters, stimulus response is
  almost guaranteed.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Quarterly YoY readings compound base effects — Q1 2023 was extremely
  strong on China reopening, creating unfavourable base effects in
  Q1 2024 that persisted into 2025.
- Chinese GDP has long been suspected by foreign economists of being
  "too smooth" relative to volatile underlying series. Treat it as a
  political-economic signal as much as a measurement.
- The YoY construction means a recent-quarter weakening is not visible
  until several quarters later — cross-check with monthly industrial
  production, PMI, and retail sales for sequential momentum.

## industrial-yoy: 规模以上工业增加值同比 / Industrial Value-Added YoY

- **Series code**: akshare:macro_china_industrial_production_yoy (via investing.com calendar mirror)
- **Source**: 国家统计局 NBS via akshare (investing.com free calendar feed)
- **Unit**: Percent (%)
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: ~15 days after reference month
- **History**: Varies by mirror; generally 10+ years available

**What it measures**: Year-over-year growth in value-added of
"above-designated-size" industrial enterprises (规模以上, annual revenue
≥ 20 million CNY). Covers manufacturing, mining, and utilities. The
monthly headline for China's industrial sector momentum.

**How to interpret**:
- Rising → Factory activity accelerating. Supportive of commodity
  demand, industrial profits, and export recovery narratives.
- Falling → Industrial slowdown. Often leads GDP growth deceleration
  by one quarter. Consistent deceleration typically precedes policy easing.
- Readings above 6% are consistent with trend-growth-or-above industrial
  activity; sub-4% readings signal recession-like conditions in the
  industrial sector.

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐⭐
One of the three core monthly data points (alongside retail sales and
fixed-asset investment) that frame Chinese growth narratives. Moves
industrial metals (iron ore, copper), CSI 300 industrials, and shipping
equities.

**When to use**: Monthly growth-cycle tracking, China industrial cycle
analysis, commodity demand now-casting, GDP now-casting input.

**China-specific context**:
- "Above-designated-size" excludes small manufacturers (annual revenue
  below 20 million CNY) — the series is biased toward large SOEs and
  established private firms. Small-firm stress may not show up here.
- January and February readings are routinely reported as "Jan-Feb
  combined" due to Spring Festival distortion. The combined YoY is the
  cleanest signal for early-year reads.
- The series covers value-added at constant prices; it is not raw output
  and strips out pricing effects.
- Sub-sectors are published separately — autos (esp. EVs), electronics,
  chemicals, and black metals are the most-watched.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Per the sourcing caveat: the akshare route for this series pulls from
  investing.com's free calendar feed, which has been observed to lag
  actual NBS releases by up to ~8 months in stale periods. If recent
  data is critical, cross-check against the NBS English site when
  reachable.
- Single-month volatility is high; use 3-month moving averages for
  trend assessment.
- Export-heavy sectors (electronics) can distort the aggregate during
  global goods cycles — check sub-sector breakdowns.

## retail-yoy: 社会消费品零售总额同比 / Retail Sales YoY

- **Series code**: akshare:macro_china_consumer_goods_retail (column: 同比增长)
- **Source**: 国家统计局 NBS via akshare
- **Unit**: Percent (%)
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: ~15 days after reference month (released jointly with industrial production)
- **History**: 10+ years typically available

**What it measures**: Year-over-year growth in 社会消费品零售总额 — total
retail sales of consumer goods. Captures retail purchases by households,
government, and enterprises of consumer goods (60%) plus catering
(restaurants, ~10%), split across online and offline channels.

**How to interpret**:
- Rising → Domestic consumption recovering. Positive for consumer
  discretionary, staples, restaurants, travel-adjacent sectors.
- Falling → Consumption weakening. Often signals property-wealth-effect
  damage and household confidence issues. Sustained <5% YoY during
  the 2024-2025 window has been the headline symptom of Chinese
  demand weakness.
- Online segment (网上零售) has routinely grown 10%+ even as offline
  lags — the gap tracks channel shift and is watched separately.

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐⭐
Core indicator for the "Chinese consumer" narrative. Moves Chinese
consumer equities (Moutai, Midea, JD.com, etc.), luxury-goods-exposed
European stocks (LVMH, Hermes), and CNY via demand signals.

**When to use**: Chinese consumer-recovery tracking, luxury and tourism
sector analysis, stimulus-effectiveness assessment, property-wealth-effect
monitoring.

**China-specific context**:
- Critically, retail sales **does not include services spending** —
  hairdressing, tutoring, domestic travel, medical services, financial
  services. This is a major gap vs US retail sales which is already
  goods-focused but sits alongside a separate PCE services series.
  Chinese services consumption is measured separately in household
  surveys with much longer lags.
- Catering (餐饮收入) is the services-like component inside retail;
  it has been a bright spot during 2024-2025 even as goods retail lagged.
- Spring Festival distortion: Jan-Feb is always combined. Golden Week
  (October) and Singles' Day (Nov 11) cause seasonal spikes.
- Real (inflation-adjusted) retail sales is published alongside nominal;
  given near-zero CPI, the two series track closely as of Q1 2026.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Cross-country retail-sales comparisons are misleading because of the
  goods-only scope. Chinese "weak retail" partly reflects a scope issue
  as much as a demand issue.
- Online vs offline composition has shifted durably — trend reads should
  use full aggregate, not offline-only.
- Base effects from 2022 zero-covid lockdowns distorted 2023 readings;
  post-2024 readings cleaner but property drag persistent.
