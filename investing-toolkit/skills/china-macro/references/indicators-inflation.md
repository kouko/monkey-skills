# Inflation / 物价

---

## cpi-yoy: CPI 同比 / Consumer Price Index YoY

- **Series code**: akshare:macro_china_cpi (column: 全国-同比增长)
- **Source**: 国家统计局 NBS (National Bureau of Statistics) via akshare
- **Unit**: Percent (%)
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: ~10 days after reference month (released around the 9th-12th of the following month)
- **History**: From the mid-1980s (varies by akshare mirror); 400+ observations typically available

**What it measures**: Year-over-year change in China's headline Consumer
Price Index — the broadest official measure of consumer price inflation.
The basket is published by NBS and covers food, housing, transportation,
education, medical care, and other goods and services across urban and
rural households.

**How to interpret**:
- Rising → Inflation pressure building. Can reflect food price shocks
  (especially pork cycle), service-sector recovery, or imported inflation
  via a weaker CNY.
- Falling toward zero or negative → Disinflation or outright deflation.
  As of Q1 2026, China has flirted repeatedly with near-zero and negative
  CPI prints, fuelling "Japanification" debate.
- Prints near or below zero give PBOC cover to ease (RRR cuts, LPR cuts)
  without risking CNY pressure, but also signal weak domestic demand.

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐⭐
The primary inflation gauge for PBOC policy analysis. Moves CSI 300, CGB
yields, and CNY. Soft CPI prints are typically read as bullish for bonds
and for rate-sensitive growth sectors; sustained sub-zero prints amplify
deflation narratives and pressure cyclicals.

**When to use**: Investment Clock inflation axis, PBOC easing probability
assessment, deflation/Japanification regime monitoring, real-rate
calculations (LPR - CPI).

**China-specific context**:
- Food weight (~19%) within CPI is dominated by pork (~2-3% of the overall
  basket). The "pork cycle" (猪周期) swings headline CPI by 1-2 percentage
  points and often distorts the YoY trend.
- Chinese New Year (春节) falls in late January or February; NBS often
  reports January and February CPI as "Jan-Feb cumulative" or emphasizes
  the YoY comparison because month-to-month seasonality is severe.
- Core CPI (excluding food and energy) is published but less emphasized
  than headline. Core readings have trended in the 0.5-1.0% range during
  2023-2025, well below PBOC's implicit comfort zone.
- Services CPI and rents are not as weight-heavy as in the US; this is
  one reason Chinese CPI looks structurally low vs US CPI.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Do not read January or February prints in isolation — Spring Festival
  distortion is severe. Always compare to the combined Jan-Feb YoY.
- YoY readings can be depressed by base effects — the 2023-2024 pork
  price crash created favourable base effects that flattered 2024-2025
  readings in some months and hurt them in others.
- NBS has adjusted basket weights historically; long-horizon level
  comparisons need to account for these revisions.

## ppi-yoy: PPI 同比 / Producer Price Index YoY

- **Series code**: akshare:macro_china_ppi (column: 当月同比增长)
- **Source**: 国家统计局 NBS via akshare
- **Unit**: Percent (%)
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: ~10 days after reference month (released jointly with CPI)
- **History**: From the 1990s; 300+ observations typically available

**What it measures**: Year-over-year change in producer prices for
industrial goods at the factory gate (出厂价). Covers mining, raw
material processing, and manufacturing. Excludes services and excludes
the retail/consumer layer.

**How to interpret**:
- Positive and rising → Upstream reflation. Commodity prices, Chinese
  manufacturing capex, or global demand picking up. Typically supportive
  of industrial equity margins.
- Negative and deepening → Producer deflation. Oversupply (property
  chain, EV overcapacity), weak global goods demand, or falling commodity
  prices. Compresses industrial profits and signals weak nominal growth.
- Transition from negative to positive is a classic reflation signal
  watched by global commodity traders.

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐⭐
PPI is arguably more market-moving globally than Chinese CPI because it
anchors expectations for global manufactured-goods inflation. China is
the marginal producer for many goods; Chinese PPI deflation exports
disinflation to the rest of the world. Moves industrial metals, shipping
rates, and global goods inflation expectations.

**When to use**: Industrial profit cycle tracking, global goods-inflation
forecasting, China reflation trade timing, commodity demand assessment.

**China-specific context**:
- Chinese PPI has been in YoY deflation for most of 2023-2025, reflecting
  (1) the property chain unwind (steel, cement, glass), (2) EV and solar
  overcapacity driving output prices below cost, and (3) weak export
  pricing power. As of Q1 2026, the PPI deflation regime remains a core
  macro debate.
- The "PPI-CPI scissors" (剪刀差) — the gap between PPI and CPI YoY —
  is a watched metric. Negative PPI with positive CPI means upstream
  firms absorb margin compression while downstream firms hold pricing.
- Oil, coal, and iron ore are large PPI weights. Global commodity price
  moves pass through quickly to Chinese PPI.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Base effects dominate PPI readings in any given month. A "less negative"
  print can reflect nothing more than a weak month a year earlier falling
  out of the YoY window. Always check the MoM sequential print for real
  directional signal.
- PPI excludes services and excludes imported finished goods — it is not
  a proxy for overall inflation.
- NBS occasionally revises historical PPI when input price methodologies
  change; long-horizon charts may not reconcile exactly across releases.
