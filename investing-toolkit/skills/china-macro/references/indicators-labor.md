# Labor / 就业

---

## urban-unemployment: 城镇调查失业率 / Urban Surveyed Unemployment Rate

- **Series code**: akshare:macro_china_urban_unemployment
- **Source**: 国家统计局 NBS via akshare
- **Unit**: Percent (%)
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: ~15 days after reference month (released with monthly activity data)
- **History**: From 2018 when NBS adopted the surveyed methodology (100+ observations)

**What it measures**: The urban surveyed unemployment rate — the share
of the surveyed urban labor force that is unemployed and seeking work,
based on NBS's rolling sample household survey. Methodology is broadly
ILO-compatible. Covers urban residents only (including migrant workers
who have been in the city 6+ months).

**How to interpret**:
- Rising → Labor-market softening. Often precedes consumption weakness
  (via household income channel) and is a politically sensitive trigger
  for stimulus.
- Falling → Labor-market tightening. Supportive of wage growth and
  consumption narratives.
- The Chinese authorities target "around 5.5%" — actual prints have
  generally ranged 5.0-5.5% in 2024-2025, close to the target, but
  youth subsegments have been dramatically weaker.

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐
The headline rate itself is relatively stable and less market-moving
than US nonfarm payrolls. However, composition signals (especially
youth unemployment) drive meaningful narrative shifts about household
confidence and political stability.

**When to use**: Labor-market slack assessment, consumption-outlook
framing, youth-unemployment monitoring, stimulus-politics reading.

**China-specific context**:
- The series replaced older "registered urban unemployment" (城镇登记失业率)
  which only counted those who formally registered as unemployed —
  a structural undercount. The surveyed measure from 2018 is more
  internationally comparable.
- **Youth unemployment (16-24) methodology crisis**: NBS suspended
  monthly publication of youth (16-24) unemployment in June 2023 after
  it hit 21.3% — the highest on record. When NBS resumed publication
  in December 2023, it used a revised methodology that excludes
  currently-enrolled students. The restated readings remain elevated
  (14-17% range in 2024-2025) and the series is gappy — treat historical
  comparisons with care.
- Rural labor is not captured. The ~290 million migrant worker population
  is partially captured (those resident 6+ months in cities) but rural
  underemployment is invisible here.
- The surveyed rate is suspected by some foreign economists of being
  smoothed — compare with private survey indicators (Caixin, private
  job-board data) for sanity checks.

**Common pitfalls**:
- The series looks stable at ~5% but conceals enormous compositional
  stress. Always check the youth (16-24) and "31 major cities" breakdowns,
  which are published in the same NBS release.
- Methodology breaks: (1) 2018 switch from registered to surveyed;
  (2) 2023 youth-unemployment suspension and revised methodology —
  long-horizon charts misleading without annotation.
- The 6-month urban-residency threshold means migrant-worker unemployment
  from sudden factory closures may not be captured in the survey.
- "Around 5.5% target" is a soft target; exceeding it briefly has
  historically not triggered policy change, but sustained drift above
  6% would.
