# Trade / 对外贸易

---

## exports-yoy: 出口同比（美元计价）/ Exports YoY (USD)

- **Series code**: akshare:macro_china_exports_yoy (via investing.com calendar mirror)
- **Source**: 海关总署 GAC (General Administration of Customs) via akshare
- **Unit**: Percent (%), USD-denominated
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: ~15 days after reference month
- **History**: 10+ years typically available via mirror

**What it measures**: Year-over-year change in China's merchandise
exports, denominated in USD. Published by customs based on declared
export values. Covers goods exports only — services trade reported
separately by SAFE.

**How to interpret**:
- Rising → External demand strengthening for Chinese goods. Positive
  for exporters (electronics, machinery, EVs), port and shipping
  equities, and the CNY via trade-surplus channel.
- Falling → Export slowdown. Often signals global goods demand weakness
  or tariff/policy friction. Sustained negative prints compress
  industrial profits and pressure CNY.
- Surges in specific months often reflect front-loading ahead of expected
  tariff actions (notably observed ahead of US tariff hike windows).

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐⭐
China is the world's largest goods exporter. The monthly exports print
moves global shipping indices (SCFI, Baltic Dry), Korean and Taiwanese
export-proxy stocks, and global goods-cycle positioning. Especially
watched by macro PMs as a leading indicator of global trade health.

**When to use**: Global goods-cycle tracking, export-driven industry
analysis (EVs, solar, electronics), tariff-impact assessment, CNY
fundamental anchor.

**China-specific context**:
- China publishes trade in both USD and CNY. This skill uses the USD
  series for international comparability. The CNY print can diverge
  from USD print when CNY/USD moves sharply within a month.
- The "new three" (新三样) — EVs, lithium batteries, solar products —
  have been the outperforming export categories during 2024-2025 and
  are tracked separately. Traditional labor-intensive exports (apparel,
  furniture) have been weaker.
- Re-routing through ASEAN (Vietnam, Mexico transshipment) has been a
  theme; bilateral US-China trade is understated while China-ASEAN
  trade is overstated versus ultimate demand origin.
- Chinese New Year distorts January-February — customs data is
  typically reported as combined Jan-Feb.

**Common pitfalls**:
- The akshare mirror for this series pulls from investing.com's free
  calendar feed and can be **~8 months stale** during quiet maintenance
  windows. For the latest print, cross-check against the customs press
  release directly when non-China-blocked network access is available.
- Base effects from the 2023-2024 export weakness created favorable
  comparisons into 2025. A "strong" YoY print in 2025 may partly be
  base-effect recovery rather than genuine strength.
- USD-denominated exports reflect both volume and price — volume data
  (custodied separately) is more informative for real global-demand reads.

## imports-yoy: 进口同比（美元计价）/ Imports YoY (USD)

- **Series code**: akshare:macro_china_imports_yoy (via investing.com calendar mirror)
- **Source**: 海关总署 GAC via akshare
- **Unit**: Percent (%), USD-denominated
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: ~15 days after reference month (released jointly with exports)
- **History**: 10+ years typically available

**What it measures**: Year-over-year change in China's merchandise
imports in USD. Covers crude oil, iron ore, copper, soybeans,
semiconductors, machinery, and consumer goods.

**How to interpret**:
- Rising → Domestic demand recovering or commodity prices lifting.
  Supportive of commodity exporters (Australia, Brazil, Chile), as
  China is the marginal importer for many raw materials.
- Falling → Weak domestic demand or commodity deflation. Sustained
  negative prints during 2023-2025 reinforced the Chinese demand-
  weakness narrative.
- Imports weaker than exports → trade surplus widens (a persistent
  feature of 2024-2025) but signals weak internal demand even as
  external demand holds up.

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐⭐
Most important Chinese demand proxy for commodity markets. Moves
iron ore (via SGX), copper, Brent crude, AUD, BRL. The commodity
complex reads Chinese imports with the same intensity that equities
read US CPI.

**When to use**: Commodity demand analysis, global reflation trade
timing, China internal-demand tracking, commodity-exporter country
positioning (Australia, Brazil).

**China-specific context**:
- Commodity imports dominate the aggregate: crude oil (~15-20% of
  total imports by value), iron ore, copper, and soybeans collectively
  anchor the series. Price and volume contributions must be separated
  for clean signal.
- Semiconductor imports (~15-17% of total imports) reflect both
  consumer-electronics demand and domestic fab capex; the semi-specific
  import data is published separately and watched by foundry analysts.
- Chinese New Year distortion applies as with exports — use combined
  Jan-Feb YoY.
- "Processing trade" imports (processing-and-reexport) move in lockstep
  with export orders — weak processing imports lead export weakness by
  1-2 months.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Same staleness caveat as exports: the investing.com mirror via akshare
  can lag ~8 months during gaps. Cross-check with customs primary source
  when latest data is load-bearing.
- Commodity price effects dominate monthly moves. A "strong imports"
  print driven by oil price rebound does not imply demand recovery.
- Soybean imports are distorted by trade tensions and weather — these
  are often "noise" in aggregate imports.

## trade-balance: 贸易差额（美元）/ Trade Balance (USD)

- **Series code**: akshare:macro_china_trade_balance (via investing.com calendar mirror)
- **Source**: 海关总署 GAC via akshare
- **Unit**: 亿美元 (hundred-million USD) — typically reported as USD billions
- **Frequency**: Monthly
- **Publication lag**: ~15 days after reference month
- **History**: 10+ years typically available

**What it measures**: Monthly trade surplus or deficit in USD —
exports minus imports of goods. As of Q1 2026, China runs structural
trade surpluses of $60-90 billion per month (annualized near $1 trillion),
among the largest in absolute terms globally.

**How to interpret**:
- Widening surplus → Either strong exports or weak imports. Under
  2024-2025 conditions, widening surpluses reflected weak domestic
  demand as much as export strength — not unambiguously bullish.
- Narrowing surplus → Domestic demand recovery (higher imports) or
  export weakness. Context matters: narrowing from import recovery is
  bullish for commodities and CNY fundamentals; narrowing from export
  slowdown is bearish.
- Persistent very-large surpluses (>$80B monthly) fuel trade-friction
  narratives — US and EU have cited these as evidence of "Chinese
  overcapacity."

**Market significance**: ⭐⭐
Structural trade surpluses are a key CNY fundamental support — the
supply of export dollars anchors the CNY even during capital-outflow
episodes. Also a key input to current-account projections by FX
strategists.

**When to use**: CNY fundamental analysis, current-account projection,
trade-friction narrative construction, commodity-exporter positioning
(a wider surplus with weak imports is a bearish commodity signal).

**China-specific context**:
- The trade balance is published in both USD and CNY. This skill uses
  USD. The two can diverge on FX moves within a month.
- Distinct from the broader current-account surplus which includes
  services (where China runs a large deficit from outbound tourism
  and royalties) — the goods surplus overstates the external-demand
  contribution to GDP.
- The "mechanical surplus" phenomenon of 2023-2025: massive negative
  import volumes from property-chain collapse created surpluses that
  were symptoms of domestic weakness, not strength.
- SAFE publishes current-account data quarterly with larger lag — trade
  balance is the timely proxy.

**Common pitfalls**:
- Same ~8 month staleness caveat on the akshare/investing.com route.
- Units: akshare returns the value in USD hundred-millions (亿美元).
  Always verify the unit when doing cross-country comparisons —
  confusion with US-dollar-billion vs US-dollar-million is common.
- Monthly volatility is high; use 3-month moving averages to strip
  noise. Single-month spikes often reflect one-off resource shipments
  or timing effects.
- Chinese New Year can swing the monthly balance by $20-40 billion
  on timing alone.
