# 4DX Team XPS Evaluation — Audit a team's execution quality

## R — Reading

> "There are four components of XPS: 1. Establishing a cadence. 2. Fulfilling high-impact commitments. 3. Optimizing lead-measures performance. 4. Achieving lag-measure (WIG) results. The calculation of your XPS consists simply of identifying the score for each component and then adding them together. Since each component has a maximum score of 1, a perfect score would be 4."
>
> — McChesney et al., *Chapter 10: Sustaining 4DX Results and Engagement*

> "3.6 to 4.0 — High level of execution excellence. 3.2 to 3.59 — Good level of execution, but room for improvement. 2.5 to 3.19 — Fair level of execution, along with areas of concern. 0 to 2.49 — Significant level of concern… a team that in reality is not executing at all."
>
> — McChesney et al., *Chapter 10* (XPS interpretation table)

> "Note that each component of XPS has a maximum value of 1, so overperformance in any one area cannot mask underperformance in another."
>
> — McChesney et al., *Chapter 10* (the no-compensation rule)

> "Your XPS on 'Establishing a Cadence' for leaders of leaders should always be 100 percent… Your XPS standard for fulfilling commitments should be 90 percent (or higher)… you should hold frontline teams to the expectation of performing their lead measures at 90 percent (or more) of the target, every week."
>
> — McChesney et al., *Chapter 10* (component performance standards)

---

## I — Interpretation

XPS is the book's diagnostic instrument for answering one question: *is this team actually executing 4DX, or just going through the motions?* It exists because, as the authors observe, the whirlwind generates abundant metrics but a team's investment in breakthrough work has historically been invisible — leaving leaders to wonder whether teams are "really engaged or just going through the motions." XPS makes that engagement observable.

The structure is deliberately simple: four components × max 1 each = max 4.0. The simplicity is load-bearing — XPS is auditable in minutes, and the **no-compensation rule** ("overperformance in any one area cannot mask underperformance in another") means a team cannot hide a broken cadence behind impressive lag-measure results, nor a missing scoreboard behind heroic commitments. Each component must stand on its own.

Note what the book's XPS scores and what it does NOT score. It scores: (a) cadence — are weekly WIG sessions actually held? (b) commitments — are leaders making *and* fulfilling weekly commitments? (c) lead-measures — are the team's lead measures predictive, performed at the target level, and credibly tracked? (d) lag — is the WIG actually moving? It does NOT score Discipline 3 (the scoreboard) as a standalone component — the book treats the scoreboard as the substrate that makes (a) and (c) auditable. The agent should still check scoreboard health, but not as a fifth column.

The agent's role here is auditor / consultant, not coach-rescuer. The deliverable is a numeric XPS, a band diagnosis, and a single fix priority — almost always the **lowest-scoring component**, because compensation is forbidden. The book is explicit that low cadence creates "skepticism … that can lead to disengagement," so component-1 weakness is the most urgent regardless of total. The output is honest evaluation, not encouragement; refusing to inflate the score when execution is hollow is the skill's job.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Marriott — XPS as the operating heartbeat of a 70,000-leader rollout
- **Problem**: After initial 4DX rollout, leadership at Marriott needed a way to detect — across thousands of teams — which were actually executing and which were drifting back into pure-whirlwind operation. Without a single observable indicator, central leaders could only react to lag results, by which point the cycle was already lost.
- **Methodology applied**: XPS implemented as a real-time dashboard ("Executive Scoreboard") showing all four components for every team. Leaders of leaders used XPS not to punish low-scoring teams but to *target their own second-level commitments* at the teams where intervention had the highest leverage.
- **Conclusion**: A single 0-4 number, computed from observable behaviors (was the meeting held? was the commitment kept?), proved sufficient to operate a 70K-leader, two-continent execution culture for over a decade.
- **Outcome**: 7M+ weekly commitments across the company at a 97%+ follow-through rate; 4DX became "just the way we execute" rather than a methodology layer.

### Case 2: Susan / Bianca / Marcus — XPS-driven targeted intervention
- **Problem**: Susan (VP Sales) leads two frontline teams; East (Bianca) is on pace, West (Marcus) is behind. Without a structured audit lens, Susan would default to either generic exhortation ("try harder") or punitive escalation, both of which had failed her in the past.
- **Methodology applied**: Component-by-component check — Marcus's *cadence* held (he attends WIG sessions), but his *commitments* component failed (he abandoned last week's commitments to "just make prospecting calls myself"), and his *lead-measures* component was suspect (he himself questioned whether they were predictive). Lag was clearly behind.
- **Conclusion**: Marcus's weakest component was commitments-fulfillment + lead-measure quality, not effort. The fix priority was therefore not "work harder" but "use the five optimizing questions on your lead measures together this week."
- **Outcome**: Targeted fix — Susan + Marcus + Bianca scheduled a joint lead-measure review using the five optimizing questions; Bianca volunteered to share her new prospecting script. The intervention addressed the diagnosed weakest component, not the visible symptom.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. **Quarter-end / mid-cycle health check** — A team or team-of-teams has been running 4DX for 6-12 weeks and the leader wants an honest read on whether the practice is hollowing out before the lag measure proves it.
2. **Pre-mortem on a stalled WIG** — Lag measure isn't moving; before declaring the WIG wrong, audit whether the four execution practices were actually performed (often the WIG was fine; the execution was theatrical).
3. **External team / coaching engagement entry** — A coach or consultant inheriting a team that "is doing 4DX" wants a baseline before recommending changes.
4. **Self-audit by an honest team lead** — Lead suspects their own team's WIG sessions have drifted into status-update meetings or that their lead measures became "good to do" platitudes; wants the book's diagnostic lens applied to themselves.
5. **Post-rollout sustainability check** — Months after initial training enthusiasm faded, leadership wants to know whether the cadence survived the first whirlwind storm.

## E — Execution

The agent runs an audit walkthrough as an external evaluator. The voice is consultant-neutral — facts, scores, fix priority. No coaching, no encouragement, no excuses for low scores.

1. **Establish the audit subject.**
   - Ask: *"Which single team are we auditing? Who is the team leader, what is their WIG (in From-X-to-Y-by-When form), and how long has the team been running 4DX?"*
   - Completion criterion: one team identified, WIG stated, weeks-since-launch known. If user names multiple teams, audit one at a time — XPS does not aggregate across teams (book P-43).

2. **Component 1 audit — Establishing a Cadence (score 0.0-1.0).**
   - Ask: *"Of the last 8 weeks, how many WIG sessions were held? Did each run 20-30 minutes? Was the meeting strictly limited to WIG / lead-measure / commitment talk, or did the whirlwind seep in? Were sessions held even when the leader was absent (delegated), or were they cancelled?"*
   - Score: book standard for cadence is **100%** (every week, on time, whirlwind-excluded). Score = (sessions actually held / sessions expected) × meeting-discipline-multiplier (1.0 if scope held; 0.7-0.8 if whirlwind regularly seeped in; 0.5 if sessions were status updates in disguise).
   - Note any cancellation pattern, since the book flags cadence collapse as the leading indicator of execution decay.

3. **Component 2 audit — Fulfilling High-Impact Commitments (score 0.0-1.0).**
   - Ask: *"In the last 4 weeks, how many specific weekly commitments did each team member make? What percentage were fulfilled? Were they specific actions ('this week I will do X') or vague intent ('I'll try harder')? For leaders-of-leaders: were commitments second-level (working ON the system) or just frontline-equivalent (working IN the system)?"*
   - Score: book standard is **90%+ fulfillment**. Score = (commitments-fulfilled / commitments-made) × specificity-multiplier (1.0 for specific commitments; 0.6-0.8 if vague; 0.3 if "I'll try harder" was accepted as a commitment).
   - Flag if commitments are not being made at all — that's a 0 on this component, not a partial score.

4. **Component 3 audit — Optimizing Lead-Measures Performance (score 0.0-1.0).**
   - Walk the five optimizing questions from the book (Ch 10):
     1. *Is the lead measure directly predictive of impacting the lag measure?* (vs. "good to do")
     2. *Is the level of performance set high enough?* (or under-dosed)
     3. *Is the scorekeeping credible?* (or "casual estimation")
     4. *Is it a quality problem?* (going through the motions vs. doing it well)
     5. *Is it a team game or a leader's game?* (only the leader performs it)
   - Also probe scoreboard health here (since D3 isn't a standalone XPS component): *"Does a 5-second glance at the scoreboard tell you whether the team is winning or losing? Is it players-not-coach, visible during work, updated by the players themselves?"*
   - Score: book standard is **90%+ of the lead-measure target hit each week**. Score = (weekly target hit-rate over 4 weeks) × predictiveness-multiplier (1.0 if all five optimizing questions pass; 0.5-0.7 if one or two fail; 0.3 or below if the lead measure is fundamentally "good to do" not predictive).

5. **Component 4 audit — Achieving Lag-Measure (WIG) Results (score 0.0-1.0).**
   - Ask: *"What's the lag measure today vs. the From value vs. the target By-When value? What's the linear pace required and the actual pace?"*
   - Score: actual-progress / required-progress, capped at 1.0. If the team is 50% of the way to the target at 50% of the timeline, score = 1.0. If they're 30% of the way at 50% of the timeline, score = 0.6.
   - **Caution**: do NOT let a strong lag score paper over weak components 1-3. The book's no-compensation rule is explicit. Lag without execution-discipline is luck or whirlwind-tailwind, not 4DX.

6. **Compute total + assign band.**
   - Total = C1 + C2 + C3 + C4. Range 0.0-4.0.
   - **3.6-4.0**: High execution excellence — adoption is real, results are following.
   - **3.2-3.59**: Good execution, room for improvement — identify the lowest component as fix priority.
   - **2.5-3.19**: Fair execution, areas of concern — book flags this band as needing "concerned investigation, possibly intervention"; low execution corrodes team trust.
   - **0-2.49**: Significant concern — the team is "in reality not executing"; either overwhelmed by whirlwind or resisting 4DX. Recommend halt-and-reset, not optimization.

7. **Identify fix priority (1-3 actions).**
   - Default rule: **the lowest-scoring component is the fix priority**, regardless of total.
   - If C1 (cadence) is the lowest, no other fix matters until cadence is restored — fix C1 first.
   - If multiple components tie at the bottom, prioritize in book-order: C1 > C2 > C3 > C4 (foundation → trust → leverage → results).
   - Output 1-3 specific actions tied to the diagnosed weakness, drawn from the chapter's prescriptions (e.g. "non-negotiable cadence — schedule next 8 sessions and don't reschedule"; "tighten commitment language — every commitment must be specific enough to verify"; "run the five optimizing questions on your lead measures this week"; "make the scoreboard player-updated by next session").

8. **Deliver the XPS report (audit format).**
   - Format: `Component scores → Total → Band → Weakest component → Fix priority`.
   - Tone: consultant-neutral. Do not soften scores. Do not add encouragement. The score is the message.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Solo / personal-coach mode.** XPS is a *team* metric. A solo user whose personal 4DX has slipped should route to `4dx-sustain-momentum-rescue`, which is a recovery script, not an audit.
- **Live WIG-session running.** This skill audits past sessions; running this week's session is `4dx-d4-cadence`.
- **"Should we use 4DX?" decisions.** That's `4dx-meta-strategy-triage`. Auditing a methodology that doesn't fit is wasted time.
- **Cross-team head-to-head comparison.** XPS is an intra-team self-diagnostic; the book never frames it as a leaderboard. Using XPS to rank teams against each other corrupts its purpose (teams gaming the score) and is not what Chapter 10 prescribes.
- **Audit-without-action loops.** The book's strongest implicit warning is that XPS is a *lead measure* for leaders-of-leaders, meaning auditing without targeting second-level commitments at the diagnosed weakness wastes the audit. If the user has no authority or intention to act on the result, don't run the audit.

### Author-warned failure modes

- **CE-32 — No-compensation rule violated by averaging.** Some teams compute XPS as an average across components, allowing a 1.0 lag to mask a 0.3 cadence. The book is explicit: each component caps at 1.0; weakness in one cannot be masked. Auditor must enforce.
- **CE-33 — Cadence theater.** The team holds the meeting (cadence component looks fine) but the meeting has degenerated into status updates, whirlwind venting, or scoreboard glances without commitment-and-follow-through. Score the meeting-discipline multiplier honestly — held-but-hollow is not 1.0.
- **CE-34 — Lead-measure platitude.** "Be more attentive to customers" or "improve our quality" passes as a lead measure but fails the predictiveness test. The five optimizing questions exist precisely to catch this; auditor must run all five, not skip the awkward ones.
- **CE-35 — Lag-driven self-deception.** A team's WIG is moving (favorable market, whirlwind tailwind, reorg side effect) and they conclude their 4DX is healthy. The no-compensation rule is the antidote: score components 1-3 honestly even when component 4 is strong.
- **CE-36 — Audit as punishment.** When XPS is used to discipline low-scoring teams rather than target leader-of-leaders second-level commitments at them, teams game the score (overstate fulfillment, simplify lead measures to easy-to-hit ones). The book frames XPS as a *targeting* lens for the auditor's own commitments, not a club.
- **CE-37 — Engagement signals scored separately.** The chapter emphasizes "Holding Others Accountable With Respect" and "Recognizing High Performance" as additional leader-of-leaders practices. These are NOT separate XPS components; they are how the leader uses XPS. Auditor must not invent a fifth column.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations

- The book's XPS assumes a leader-of-leaders org structure with multiple frontline teams. For a single small team without that hierarchy, the second-level-commitment construct in C2 collapses — adapt by scoring all weekly commitments uniformly, not splitting into first/second level.
- The book references the 4DX app's "Executive Scoreboard" for real-time XPS automation. This skill operates without that app — the auditor reconstructs scores from interview / observation, which is more subjective than app-instrumented scoring; flag this in the report.
- US-anglophone-corporate origin: the book treats public peer commitment as universally motivating. In high-context cultures (JP, ZH, KR) the cadence component may run differently — written commitments, dyad cadences, or supervisor-mediated check-ins may substitute for full-team public verbal commitment without lowering the score; auditor should not penalize culturally-adapted formats.
- Chapter is written for sustained operation — the bands (3.6-4.0, etc.) assume a team that has been running 4DX long enough for the components to stabilize. For a team in week 1-4, premature XPS auditing produces noise; recommend re-audit after 6-8 weeks of operation.

### NOT for: cross-team performance ranking

NOT for: **cross-team performance ranking.** XPS is a team's mirror, not a leaderboard. The book uses XPS to help leaders-of-leaders decide where to invest their second-level commitments — a low XPS team gets *more* leader support, not less. Using XPS to rank, shame, or compete teams against each other inverts its purpose and reliably produces gamed scores (CE-36).

### NOT for: audit-without-action

NOT for: **audit-without-action.** A consultant-style "here's your XPS, good luck" delivery without a concrete fix priority for the lowest component is theater. The chapter is explicit that XPS is the *ideal lead measure for leaders of leaders' own focus* — the score exists to redirect leader behavior, not to rate teams in a vacuum.

### NOT for: substitute for actually doing 4DX

NOT for: **substitute for actually doing 4DX.** Auditing the four disciplines is not a discipline. A team that runs frequent XPS audits but never holds the WIG session, never writes specific commitments, and never tracks lead measures is performing audit theater on top of whirlwind reality. If activation reveals the team has not yet installed 4DX, route to D1-D4 skills first; come back to audit after 6-8 weeks of actual practice.

### Industry-experience addendum (XPS vs peer health frameworks)

Three older frameworks tackle similar diagnostic problems; XPS occupies a specific niche the auditor must respect. Citations in `references/industry-grounding.md`:

- **CMMI for Development v2.0** (CMMI Institute / ISACA, 2018) — five-level process-maturity model. CMMI **allows compensation** across practice areas (average up); XPS **does not** (CE-32 no-compensation rule). XPS reports the *weakest* lever, which is what makes it useful for leader-of-leaders targeting.
- **McKinsey Organizational Health Index** (Keller & Price, *Beyond Performance*, Wiley 2011) — nine outcomes spanning strategy, culture, capability, innovation. XPS is **narrower** — four execution components only. An org can have strong OHI and a 0.4 XPS, or vice versa. Don't stretch XPS to OHI territory.
- **Gallup Q12** (Buckingham & Coffman, *First, Break All the Rules*, Simon & Schuster 1999) — 12-item engagement survey. Q12 measures self-reported engagement; XPS measures observed cadence behavior. They can diverge — high Q12 + low XPS = warm climate without discipline; low Q12 + high XPS = compliance without commitment. Don't invent an engagement column inside XPS (CE-37).
