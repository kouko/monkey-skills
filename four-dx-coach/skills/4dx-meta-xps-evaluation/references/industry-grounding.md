# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-meta-xps-evaluation

Benchmarks the book's 4-component XPS (Execution Performance Score) against
three older organizational-health / process-maturity frameworks. Helps the
auditor distinguish what XPS is uniquely measuring from what these older
frameworks already cover, and clarifies where XPS's no-compensation rule
makes it stricter than its peers.

## Process-maturity benchmark: CMMI

- **CMMI Institute (an ISACA company), *CMMI for Development, Version
  2.0* (Pittsburgh: ISACA / CMMI Institute, March 2018)** — the
  industry-standard process-maturity model originally developed at the
  Software Engineering Institute, Carnegie Mellon (acquired by ISACA in
  March 2016). V2.0 merged the previously-separate CMMI-DEV, CMMI-SVC,
  and CMMI-ACQ into one unified model; the practice areas (PAs)
  replaced the older "process areas" terminology.
  ([CMMI Institute press release](https://www.isaca.org/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/2018/cmmi-institute-expands-cmmi-v2-to-include-services-and-supplier-management),
  [ISACA quick-reference guide](https://www.isaca.org/resources/reference-guide/cmmi-model-quick-reference-guide),
  [CMMI Institute home](https://cmmiinstitute.com/))

CMMI rates organizational maturity on a five-level scale: 1 Initial,
2 Managed, 3 Defined, 4 Quantitatively Managed, 5 Optimizing. Where
CMMI and 4DX-XPS overlap and differ:

- **Both** are diagnostic frameworks scored on a fixed scale used to
  target leader-of-leaders investment.
- **CMMI** allows compensation across practice areas (an org can be
  Level 3 overall while being Level 2 in one PA and Level 4 in
  another). **XPS does not** — components cap at 1.0 and weakness
  in one component cannot be averaged away by strength in another.
  This is the book's explicit no-compensation rule (CE-32).
- **CMMI** measures *process artifacts* (documented procedures,
  metric collection, configuration management). **XPS** measures
  *behavioral cadence* (do they hold the WIG Session, do they post
  scoreboards player-updated, do they make specific commitments).
  Process maturity ≠ execution discipline; an org can be CMMI-3
  with a near-zero XPS, and vice versa.

Implication for the auditor: don't import CMMI's average-across-PAs
habit. The XPS no-compensation rule is what makes it diagnostic of
*the weakest cadence component*, which is what leader-of-leaders
second-level commitments should target.

## Organizational-health benchmark: McKinsey OHI

- **Keller, S. & Price, C., *Beyond Performance: How Great
  Organizations Build Ultimate Competitive Advantage* (Hoboken, NJ:
  John Wiley & Sons, 2011, ISBN 978-1-118-08529-7; Wiley Online DOI
  10.1002/9781119202455)** — the McKinsey practitioner book that
  introduced the **Organizational Health Index (OHI)**, drawing on
  600,000+ executives/employees across 500+ orgs and ~900 academic
  sources.
  ([Wiley Online Books entry](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/book/10.1002/9781119202455),
  [McKinsey OHI article](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/the-hidden-value-of-organizational-health-and-how-to-capture-it),
  [McKinsey "five frames of performance and health"](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/a-better-way-to-lead-large-scale-change))

OHI measures **nine outcomes of organizational health** —
direction, leadership, culture & climate, accountability, coordination
& control, capabilities, motivation, external orientation, innovation
& learning — and the underlying ~37 management practices that produce
them. McKinsey's published claim is that OHI predicts long-term
financial performance better than performance metrics alone.

Where OHI and XPS differ:

- **Scope**: OHI covers nine outcomes spanning strategy, culture,
  capability, and innovation. **XPS is narrower** — four execution
  components (focus, leverage, engagement, accountability). XPS does
  not measure direction-quality (whether the WIG is the right one),
  capability-bench-strength, or innovation. Auditor must not stretch
  XPS to cover OHI territory.
- **Direction**: a healthy OHI is not a healthy XPS, and vice versa.
  An org can have strong OHI scores on direction and culture and
  still have a 0.4 XPS because the cadence has degenerated. An org
  can have a 1.0 XPS on a wrong-shaped Primary WIG (CE-35: lag-driven
  self-deception) and still be unhealthy.
- **Comparability**: OHI is benchmarked against a global database
  enabling peer comparison. **XPS is intra-team self-diagnostic** —
  the book never frames it as a leaderboard, and using it that way
  produces the gaming the book warns against (CE-36).

Implication: an XPS audit is not an OHI audit. If the underlying
question is "is the org healthy", that needs OHI or equivalent. XPS
answers a narrower, more actionable question: "is *this team*
executing the four disciplines, and which component is the weakest
lever for leader-of-leaders to target".

## Engagement benchmark: Gallup Q12

- **Buckingham, M. & Coffman, C., *First, Break All the Rules: What
  the World's Greatest Managers Do Differently* (New York: Simon &
  Schuster, 1999, ISBN 978-0-684-85286-7)** — the public introduction
  of the Gallup **Q12** employee-engagement instrument, derived from
  ~80,000 manager interviews across 25 years.
  ([Wikipedia overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First,_Break_All_the_Rules),
  [Amazon listing](https://www.amazon.com/First-Break-All-Rules-Differently/dp/0684852861),
  [Q12 summary](https://blog.hptbydts.com/in-a-nutshell-gallup-q12))

Q12 is a 12-question survey instrument measuring employee
engagement, anchored on Gallup's research linking engagement to
performance outcomes (productivity, retention, customer
satisfaction, profitability). Sample items: "I know what is expected
of me at work", "I have the materials and equipment I need", "In the
last seven days, I have received recognition or praise for doing
good work".

Where Q12 and XPS differ:

- **Q12 measures self-reported engagement**; **XPS measures observed
  cadence behavior**. A team can score high on Q12 (members feel
  engaged) and low on XPS (no scoreboard, no specific commitments,
  no peer accountability), or vice versa.
- **Q12 is a survey**; **XPS is an audit**. Survey-based engagement
  is gameable in different ways than observation-based cadence
  scoring is.
- The book is explicit (CE-37) that engagement signals like
  "Holding Others Accountable With Respect" and "Recognizing High
  Performance" are *how leaders use XPS*, not separate XPS
  components. Auditor must not invent a fifth column for engagement.

## What XPS auditors learn from these benchmarks

XPS occupies a specific niche the three peers do not fill:

1. **Stricter than CMMI** — no-compensation across components means
   XPS reports *the weakest cadence lever*, not an average. This is
   what makes it useful as a leader-of-leaders targeting tool.
2. **Narrower than OHI** — XPS measures execution-discipline only.
   For direction, capability, or culture, the auditor needs a
   different instrument; don't stretch XPS to cover them.
3. **Different from Q12** — XPS is observed-behavior, not
   self-reported engagement. They can disagree, and when they do,
   the auditor should treat the disagreement as diagnostic
   (high Q12 + low XPS = warm climate without execution
   discipline; low Q12 + high XPS = compliance without commitment).

The book's strongest implicit warning is that XPS is the *ideal lead
measure for the leader-of-leaders' own focus* — meaning the score
exists to redirect leader behavior, not to rate teams in a vacuum.
This is consistent with the OHI literature (Keller & Price argue
health metrics drive leader investment, not leaderboard ranking) and
inconsistent with naive applications of CMMI (which is sometimes
used for vendor qualification leaderboards). Auditor enforces the
book's framing.
