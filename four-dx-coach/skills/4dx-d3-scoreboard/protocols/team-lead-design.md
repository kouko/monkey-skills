# Protocol: Team-Leader — Facilitate a team-built public scoreboard

> Loaded by SKILL.md orchestrator when scope detection identifies a team
> leader (3-12 reports) facilitating *the team* to build *the team's*
> scoreboard. Voice = consultant-to-leader. The team designs and builds;
> the leader holds the four firm rules and uses veto-not-dictate.

## R — Reading (verbatim source quote)

> Let the team build the scoreboard. The greater their involvement, the better—they will take more ownership of it if they build it themselves.
>
> — McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 14 (Applying D3, Step 3 — Build the Scoreboard)

## I — Interpretation (mode-specific)

A team scoreboard is **not** a personal scoreboard scaled up, and it
is not a management dashboard scaled down. It is a third artifact
with its own constraints:

- **Public-by-design** — lives where every team member sees it during
  work. "Out of sight, out of mind" applies harder at team scale
  because no single person owns the ritual of looking.
- **Multi-stakeholder legibility** — engineers, ops, sales, support
  must all read it instantly. Anyone walking past — including a
  stranger — should answer "are we winning?" in 5 seconds.
- **Team-built, leader-vetoed** — the book's specific authority
  pattern. Team designs and builds; leader holds the WIG-anchor (no
  unrelated metrics), enforces simple / visible / lead+lag /
  5-second tests, uses veto-not-dictate when design drifts.
  Engagement is the point of letting them build it. A leader-authored
  board is technically cleaner and behaviorally dead.
- **Gestalt of the team's bet** — one view encodes Team WIG (lag,
  with target line) + each lead (with target line, individually if
  the lead requires per-person tracking). The whole "game" must be
  visible at once — not split across tabs or rotating dashboards.

The leader's job is therefore Socratic facilitation, not graphic
design. Hold the four firm rules; let the team own theme, layout,
materials, update ritual.

Voice contrast: agent is **consultant-to-leader** — does not address
the team directly; whispers to the leader. Output is leader-facing
prompts, decision rules, and a session plan the leader uses live in
the team-build session.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Serena's Event Management team — textbook three-component team scoreboard
- Five-star hotel team, Team WIG ($22M → $31M corporate-event
  revenue) with two leads (2 quality site visits / associate / week;
  upsell premium bar to 90% of events).
- Serena facilitated the team through the four steps (Theme →
  Design → Build → Keep Updated). WIG/lag with target line at top;
  Lead 1 with per-associate detail graph; Lead 2 as a 90%-target bar.
  **Team built it.**
- Outcome: scoreboard with exactly three components passed simple /
  visible / complete / 5-second tests. The book's canonical worked
  example.

### Case 2: Juice-bottling shifts — public visibility creates peer accountability
- Michigan plant; full-truckload counts known internally but
  invisible across shifts. A scoreboard placed where the next shift
  could see the previous shift's number — public, comparative, no
  other intervention.
- Workers chose to skip lunch breaks to overtake another shift's
  number. The cleanest evidence that public team visibility is
  upstream of engagement, not downstream.

### Case 3: Northrop Grumman — the blown-down stadium analogy
- Coast Guard cutter team had no visible execution scoreboard;
  engagement diffused into the whirlwind. Authors used the post-
  Katrina blown-down stadium image as a diagnostic: if the team is
  silent on the WIG, the scoreboard is the blown-down one.
- Outcome: visible public scoreboard installed; engagement followed.

## A2 — Future Trigger

When a team leader needs this protocol:

1. Leader has run D1 (Team WIG defined) and D2 (2-3 leads chosen)
   and asks: "Now how do we track this so the team actually sees
   it?"
2. Current "team scoreboard" is a Notion board / weekly metric
   email / BI dashboard nobody looks at outside the WIG Session.
3. Leader is about to roll out 4DX and wants the artifact ready
   before the first WIG Session.
4. Cross-functional team members read the existing board
   differently — some can't tell "are we winning?" in 5 seconds.
5. Leader designed the board solo and the team treats it as
   management's tracker, not theirs.

## E — Execution

Voice is **consultant-to-leader**. Run a Socratic facilitation
dialogue with the leader. Goal is a scoreboard spec the leader will
return to the *team* to build, plus a team-build session plan. Do
NOT design the scoreboard for the leader.

1. **Confirm Team WIG + lead measures are defined.**
   - Ask: "What's the Team WIG (From-X-to-Y-by-When form) and what
     are your 2-3 lead measures?"
   - Completion: leader produces both, in writing, both measurable.
   - Halt: Team WIG missing → redirect to D1 team skills. Leads
     missing → redirect to D2 team skills.

2. **Confirm the leader will let the team build it.**
   - Ask: "Will you facilitate the team to design and build this, or
     are you planning to author it yourself?" Surface book Step 3:
     team-built drives ownership; leader-authored boards die in
     week 4.
   - Completion: leader commits to facilitating, not authoring.
     (Exception per book: very small discretionary time — leader
     produces, team still chooses theme.)
   - Anti-pattern: "I'll just make it nice and show them" — name as
     engagement-killer; redirect.

3. **Lock the public display location.**
   - Ask: "Where will the team see this during work, not only in the
     WIG Session?" Examples: shared wall in team space, TV in team
     room, pinned channel post with shared image, 4DX app for
     dispersed teams.
   - Completion: leader names a specific surface where 100% of the
     team will pass within a working day. "I'll put it in
     SharePoint" without an open-it-daily ritual fails.

4. **Specify the four firm rules the leader must hold.** — see
   `../standards/players-vs-coaches-board.md`,
   `../standards/lead-lag-pacing-elements.md`,
   `../standards/5-second-test.md`
   The team owns layout, theme, materials. Leader vetoes when the
   board violates any of these:
   - Rule 1 — **Simple**: ≤4 visual elements total. Team WIG + lag
     trend + N lead trends. No supporting data, historical
     comparisons, projections, status updates.
   - Rule 2 — **Visible**: large fonts, glance-readable from across
     the team space, posted publicly.
   - Rule 3 — **Lead AND lag with target lines**: WIG/lag must show
     "where we should be by today" line; each lead must show its
     standard (e.g. 90% sustained line). Per-person breakdown if
     the lead requires individual tracking (per Serena Lead 1).
   - Rule 4 — **5-second test**: a teammate or stranger walking past
     must answer "are we winning?" in 5 seconds.
   - Completion: leader can recite the 4 rules and commits to hold
     them in the team-build session.

5. **Plan the team-build session.**
   - Ask: "When and how will you run the build session?" Recommend
     book's 4 steps (Theme → Design → Build → Keep Updated) as the
     session agenda. Leader's role: open with the WIG and leads;
     let team pick theme; ask the four rule-questions during design;
     veto if violated, otherwise hands off.
   - Add a **cross-role legibility check** before locking: each
     functional sub-group glances at the draft and reports what they
     see in 5 seconds. If answers diverge, decoration is doing the
     divergent work — cut it.
   - Completion: leader has date, attendees, agenda, and understands
     they are facilitator not author.

6. **Lock the update ritual.**
   - Per book: leader makes very clear (a) who is responsible for
     updating, (b) when it is posted, (c) how often (≥ weekly).
     Updates by the team, not by the leader, not by an automated
     feed alone — manual update is the engagement loop.
   - Completion: leader produces a one-line statement: "X updates
     the scoreboard every Y, posted at Z."

7. **Output: spec + team-build plan + leader's holding-the-line
   prompts.**
   - Write up: location, the 4 firm rules, agenda for the team
     build, update ritual, and 3-4 sentences the leader can use in
     the session to redirect when team starts adding extras (e.g.
     "that's data the weekly review needs, not a glance number —
     let's route it to the 1-pager log").
   - Suggest the leader hold the team-build session within 7 days.
     Inertia is the failure mode this protocol exists to prevent.

## B — Boundary (mode-specific)

### Do NOT use this protocol for:

- **Personal-only scoreboards** — load `protocols/personal-design.md`.
- **Member POV — reading a board, not designing one** — load
  `protocols/member-read.md`.
- **Enterprise-wide BI / multi-team aggregated dashboards** — board
  rolling up across 30 teams for executives is correctly complex
  (coach's scoreboard, drill-down). This protocol is single-team
  scope.
- **Before D1 / D2 are defined** — redirect upstream.
- **Stroke-of-the-pen team goals** ("complete the migration") —
  project plan, not scoreboard.
- **Reactive / on-call team contexts** — see
  `4dx-meta-strategy-triage`.
- **High-context-culture teams where public peer-comparison reads
  as face-loss** — soften person-vs-person framing to team-vs-target.

### Author-warned failure modes (CEs at team scale)
- **Coach's-as-players' substitution (CE-08)** — Power BI / Tableau /
  Notion DB / KPI dashboard with 15 panels. Fails simple + 5-second
  + single-purpose. Most common failure.
- **Hidden / out-of-sight scoreboard** — SharePoint folder / channel
  tab / app nobody opens. Fires harder at team scale because no
  individual owns looking.
- **Status-without-trajectory (CE-09)** — lag shown but no target
  line. Team can see status but not whether status = winning. Book
  is firm: target line MUST be visible.
- **Project plan as scoreboard (CE-18)** — Gantt / milestone list /
  Jira board. The two artifacts coexist; don't conflate.
- **Leader-authored board** — visually cleaner, ownership zero. Book
  Step 3 is explicit: let the team build it.
- **Leader updates the score, not the team** — removes engagement
  loop. Book: leader makes who-and-when clear; team performs the
  update.
- **Communication-board drift** — scoreboard becomes a place to post
  reports / status updates / announcements. Book Ch 14 bans
  explicitly.
- **Dashboard rotation / multi-tab** — 6 tabs rotating on a TV fails
  single-glance. Pick one view.
- **Dispersed-team excuse to skip the artifact** — "we're remote" →
  per book: dispersed teams use 4DX app or shared digital screen
  visible on every desktop / mobile.

### Author's blind spots
- **2021 edition leans on the 4DX app** without engaging
  attention-economy realities — physical wall still beats in-app for
  co-located teams.
- **Strongest exemplars are industrial** — knowledge-work teams
  (engineering, research, design) need softened version (team-vs-
  target, not person-vs-person leaderboard).
- **"Players' scoreboard" framing is sports** — for clinical /
  recovery / support / collaborative teams, reframe as "progress
  visible to everyone."
- **Multi-stakeholder legibility under-engaged** — boards designed
  by one functional sub-group often fail to read clearly to other
  functions. Run cross-role legibility check inside team-build.

### Easily-confused neighbours
- **OKR scorecards / KPI dashboards** — strategic-alignment + cross-
  team analytic; do NOT apply 5-second / ≤4-element rules there.
- **Agile burndown / velocity charts** — work-in-progress charts;
  not WIG scoreboards. Can complement but does not replace.
- **BSC (Balanced Scorecard) team boards** — strategic-alignment
  communication; coach's-view artifacts; do not pass 5-second by
  design.
