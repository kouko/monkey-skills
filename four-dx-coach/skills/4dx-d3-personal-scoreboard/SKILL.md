# Personal Compelling Scoreboard — designing a players' scoreboard for one

## R — Reading

> "If you can't tell within five seconds whether you're winning or losing,
> you haven't passed this test."
>
> — McChesney et al., *The 4 Disciplines of Execution*, Ch 4 (Characteristics of a Compelling Players' Scoreboard, criterion 4)

---

## I — Interpretation

A scoreboard is not a dashboard. The book draws a sharp line:

- A **coach's scoreboard** is data-rich and analytic. It exists to help a
  manager *decide*. Spreadsheets, Notion DBs, traffic dashboards, fitness
  apps with 50 metrics — all coach's scoreboards.
- A **players' scoreboard** is simple and emotional. It exists to make the
  player *want to win*. It must pass four tests: simple, visible, shows
  lead AND lag, and answers "am I winning?" in five seconds.

For a personal goal, you are simultaneously coach and player — but the
artifact you design daily-life around must be the *player's* one. The
coach's view is fine in a weekly review, terrible on the wall.

The non-obvious design element is the **where-you-should-be line** (the
"goat" in the book's mountain-store example). Without it, you can see
where you are but not whether that's winning. Status without trajectory
is data, not a scoreboard.

Side effect that justifies the work: the act of making the score
*visible during work* changes behavior before any other intervention —
the book reports a juice-bottling shift skipping lunch to overtake another
shift's number, simply because the number was on a wall.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Northrop Grumman — the blown-down stadium scoreboard
- **Problem**: Coast Guard cutter design team had no visible execution
  scoreboard; engagement diffused into the whirlwind.
- **Methodology applied**: A leader at the site told the authors about a
  local stadium scoreboard blown down by Hurricane Katrina — fans went
  silent, "no one could tell you what the score was." Authors used this
  as the diagnostic: if your team is silent on the WIG, your scoreboard
  is the blown-down one.
- **Conclusion**: Visible score is upstream of engagement, not downstream.
- **Outcome**: Used as the canonical 4DX teaching analogy; the team
  installed visible scoreboards and engagement followed.

### Case 2: Remote Canadian plant — 74 → 94 quality via shift-vs-shift boards
- **Problem**: Quality score stuck at 74; standard managerial pressure
  not moving it.
- **Methodology applied**: Replaced the management report with shift-level
  visible scoreboards posted where the next shift could see the previous
  shift's number. No new tools, no new training.
- **Conclusion**: Just turning the lights on (visible score + comparison)
  drove the change.
- **Outcome**: Quality climbed to 94. The book treats this as the
  cleanest demonstration of "scoreboard lift" without confounding
  interventions.

### Case 3: Fighter-plane developers — "a project plan is not a scoreboard"
- **Problem**: Aerospace engineering team insisted their Gantt-style
  project plan was their scoreboard.
- **Methodology applied**: Jim Stuart's challenge — a 14-page Gantt
  doesn't pass the 5-second test, doesn't show lead-vs-lag, and isn't
  visible during work. They built a separate one-glance scoreboard
  alongside the project plan.
- **Conclusion**: Even rocket scientists with sophisticated tracking
  need a different artifact for engagement vs. analysis.
- **Outcome**: Establishes the rule that complexity-of-work does not
  justify complexity-of-scoreboard. The two artifacts coexist.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. User has a personal WIG + lead measures already defined and asks
   "now how do I track this?" or "what app should I use?"
2. User reports a tracker that exists but isn't doing anything — Notion
   page they don't open, a habit-app they ignore, an Excel sheet they
   stopped updating, a fitness app with 50 metrics that doesn't motivate.
3. User describes their tracking artifact and it's clearly a coach's
   scoreboard: multi-tab spreadsheet, multi-metric dashboard, Gantt
   chart, project plan — and they ask why it's not driving action.
4. User wants the *visibility* of their goal increased — sticky note,
   wall calendar, lock-screen widget — but doesn't know what to put on
   it.
5. User attempted D1 + D2 (WIG and lead measures) and momentum is
   fading after 2-4 weeks. (Often a missing-scoreboard symptom, not a
   willpower problem.)

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- "I built a Notion dashboard / tracker but I don't actually look at it"
- "My fitness app shows me too many things, none of them motivate me"
- "I have a habit tracker but I keep forgetting to check it"
- "How do I make my progress visible?"
- "Should I use a sticky note / whiteboard / app for this?"
- "ダッシュボードが複雑すぎて見ない"
- "進捗が見えない / 進捗を可視化したい"
- "Notionで管理してるけど開かない"
- "我做了一個追蹤表，可是都沒在看"
- "怎麼讓進度看得見？"
- "我的追蹤工具太複雜了"

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`: that skill produces the *finish line*; this
  skill produces the *visible representation* of progress toward it.
  Redirect there if no WIG exists.
- vs. `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`: that skill produces *what to
  track*; this skill produces *how to display it*. Redirect there if
  lead measures are missing.
- vs. `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`: that skill produces a *weekly cadence* of
  reviewing the score and committing; this skill produces the *artifact*
  the cadence reviews. They compose, not compete.
- vs. generic dashboard-design / BI advice: those optimize for analytic
  insight (coach's scoreboard); this skill *rejects* that frame for
  personal engagement use.
- vs. `4dx-sustain-personal-momentum-rescue`: that skill diagnoses *why*
  execution is failing; if the failure is "I never look at my tracker,"
  this skill is the fix.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, run a Socratic design dialogue. The goal is a
scoreboard spec the user commits to displaying somewhere visible during
work, not a discussion of tracking philosophy.

1. **Confirm WIG + lead measures are defined.**
   - Ask: "What's the WIG (in From-X-to-Y-by-When form) and what are
     your one or two lead measures?"
   - Completion criterion: user produces both, in writing.
   - Halt condition: if WIG is missing → redirect to
     `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`. If lead measures are missing → redirect to
     `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`. Do NOT proceed without both.

2. **Choose a display medium based on user's actual day.**
   - Ask: "Where will your eyes naturally land 3-5 times during a
     working day, even on a bad day?" Examples to offer: sticky note on
     monitor, whiteboard above desk, fridge magnet, lock-screen widget,
     paper wall calendar near workstation, printed page taped to a door.
   - Completion criterion: user names a specific physical or digital
     surface, not "an app I'll check."
   - Anti-pattern: any answer of the form "I'll set up a Notion
     page / Obsidian dashboard / dedicated app" without an existing
     habit of opening it daily — flag and redirect to a more passive
     surface (one already in their visual field).

3. **Design the lead measure visualization.**
   - Pick one of: weekly bar chart, running streak (consecutive days),
     checkbox grid (e.g. 7 boxes per week), cumulative count (running
     tally).
   - Rule: must be drawable / updatable in <30 seconds, by the user, by
     hand if needed.
   - Completion criterion: user can sketch it on paper and say "I'd
     fill this in like this."

4. **Design the lag measure visualization with a where-you-should-be
   line.**
   - Pick one of: progress bar with target marker, line chart with
     pacing line, thermometer with weekly target ticks.
   - The pacing line / goat / target marker is mandatory. Without it,
     the user can see status but not whether status = winning.
   - Completion criterion: user can point to "where I am" AND "where I
     should be by today" on the design.

5. **Apply the 5-second test.**
   - Show or describe the design back to the user cold and ask: "If you
     hadn't built this and saw it for the first time, could you tell in
     five seconds whether you're winning?"
   - Completion criterion: user answers yes without hedging. If no →
     simplify (cut elements, enlarge winning indicator, add a color or
     position cue), then re-test.
   - Hard cap: ≤4 visual elements on the final scoreboard. If more,
     cut.

6. **Lock the display location and the update cadence.**
   - User commits to (a) where it lives, (b) who updates it (always
     the user, never an app's automation if avoidable — manual update
     is part of the engagement loop), (c) when (daily for lead, weekly
     for lag is the typical default).
   - Completion criterion: a one-line statement of the form "I will
     post this on [location] and update [lead] daily / [lag] weekly."

7. **Output: scoreboard spec and immediate physical action.**
   - Write up: medium, lead viz, lag viz with target line, update plan,
     5-second-test passing rationale.
   - Suggest the user perform one physical setup action *today* (print,
     stick the note, draw the bars on the whiteboard) — not "this
     weekend." Inertia is the failure mode this skill exists to prevent.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Org-wide BI / KPI dashboard design.** Coach's scoreboards are valid
  artifacts; they're just not what this skill produces. A team-of-30
  performance dashboard for management decisions is correctly complex.
- **Before D1/D2 are defined.** A scoreboard without a WIG and lead
  measures will visualize the wrong thing prettily. Redirect.
- **Stroke-of-the-pen goals.** If the goal is "buy a new desk," there's
  nothing to scoreboard — execute and done.
- **Reactive / on-call / firefighting domains** where the whirlwind IS
  the strategic work; a separate WIG scoreboard creates phantom guilt.
- **High-context-culture team contexts** where public peer score
  comparison reads as face-loss. (For *personal* use this is rarely a
  concern; flagged because the book's "shift-vs-shift" examples
  generalize poorly to JP / ZH / KR team settings.)

### Author-warned failure modes (CEs from the cluster)

- **Coach's-as-players' substitution (CE-08)**: the user's "scoreboard"
  is a Notion DB / Excel / Gantt / multi-metric dashboard. Fails the
  5-second test, fails the simple test. Treat as the most common
  failure mode in personal use.
- **Status-without-trajectory (CE-09)**: the scoreboard shows where you
  are but not where you should be. Common with progress bars without
  target ticks, fitness rings without weekly goals, habit grids without
  pacing lines. Add the where-you-should-be line.
- **Project plan as scoreboard (CE-18)**: Gantt chart, milestone list,
  Notion task DB. Useful for analysis, useless for engagement. The two
  artifacts coexist; don't conflate.
- **Hidden scoreboard**: lives in an app the user doesn't open, a tab
  in Excel, a folder in Notion. Out of sight = out of mind. The
  book's "out of sight, out of mind" rule (Ch 4 §Visible).
- **Manager / app updates the score, not the player**: e.g. fitness app
  auto-syncs all numbers; user has zero ritual of touching the score.
  Ownership is lost; engagement effect collapses. Manual update is the
  feature.
- **Effort hours instead of lead measure outcomes**: tracking "hours
  worked on goal" rather than the lead-measure action. Confuses input
  with leading indicator. (Cf. CE-21.)
- **Goodhart misuse / vanity metric drift**: scoreboard becomes a
  performance for self-image (showing it off, gaming the streak by
  redefining "counts"). Reframe as private accountability tool, not a
  trophy. Counter-pressure: keep it slightly ugly and not shareable.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations

- The book's strongest scoreboard examples (juice-bottling shifts,
  hotel front desk, retail store) are co-located industrial settings
  with peer-visible scoreboards. Distributed / remote / solo
  workers don't get the cross-shift comparison effect — the engagement
  comes only from the user's own act of looking, which is weaker.
- The 2021 edition leans on the 4DX app for digital scoreboards but
  doesn't engage seriously with attention-economy realities — a
  scoreboard inside another app competes with notifications, email,
  Slack. Physical / passive surfaces (sticky note, paper, lock screen)
  often beat in-app dashboards for personal use.
- "Players' scoreboard" psychology is grounded in sports / hockey-rink
  culture; for users for whom "winning" framing repels (creative work,
  recovery contexts, collaborative non-competitive goals), reframe as
  "progress visible to me" rather than "winning the game" — the
  visualization mechanism is the same, the affective frame is not.

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **OKR scorecards / KPI dashboards**: serve a different purpose
  (strategic-alignment communication). Don't apply 5-second-test or
  ≤4-element rule to those.
- **Habit trackers (e.g. Atomic Habits streak grids)**: closely related
  but not identical — habit trackers track *frequency* of one behavior;
  a 4DX scoreboard tracks *lead + lag together* with a pacing line. A
  pure streak grid satisfies part of D3 but is missing the lag-trend
  + where-you-should-be line.
- **Bullet journal / weekly review pages**: closer to a coach's view —
  they are reviewed periodically, not glanced at constantly.

### Industry-experience addendum (data-ink, dashboard purpose, pre-attentive processing)

The book's "5-second test" and "compelling player's scoreboard" heuristics
have a 40-year information-design literature behind them that names the
mechanisms — primary citations in `references/industry-grounding.md`:

- **Tufte, *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information* (Graphics Press, 1983 / 2nd ed. 2001; ISBN 978-0961392147)** — data-ink ratio + chartjunk. Decoration that lowers the ratio delays the eye's path to the trend by hundreds of milliseconds; this compounds over a 6-month cadence.
- **Few, *Information Dashboard Design* (O'Reilly, 2006; ISBN 978-0596100162; 2nd ed. Analytics Press 2013)** — dashboard (glance-monitoring, single screen) vs database (drill-down). Mixing them produces an artifact that fails both. Keep the scoreboard at ≤4 elements; route drill-down to a separate weekly log.
- **Ware, *Information Visualization: Perception for Design* (Morgan Kaufmann, 3rd ed. 2012; ISBN 978-0123814647)** — pre-attentive processing operates in ~200 ms via position / length / limited-palette hue. Numbers and text labels require focal attention and cannot be glanced.

Personal-coach implication: a scoreboard failing data-ink, single-purpose, or pre-attentive-encoding checks will be ignored by week 4 — the perception-science origin of the book's "compelling vs not compelling" criterion.
