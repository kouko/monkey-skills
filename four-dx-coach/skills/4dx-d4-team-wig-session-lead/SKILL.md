# 4DX D4 — Team WIG Session (Leader Facilitation)

## R — Reading

> Hold WIG Sessions to the same day at the same time in the same place
> every week (including electronically), regardless of the whirlwind.
> If you're gone, delegate leadership of the session to another team
> member.
>
> — McChesney, Covey & Huling, *Chapter 15: Applying Discipline 4*

> Team members are more likely to take ownership of commitments they
> come up with themselves. Still, the leader should make sure the
> commitments meet the following standards: Specific … Aligned to
> moving the scoreboard … Timely.
>
> — McChesney, Covey & Huling, *Chapter 15*

---

## I — Interpretation

A **team WIG Session** is a 20-30 minute weekly meeting with one job:
push the team's Wildly Important Goal forward against the daily
whirlwind. The leader **facilitates**; team members **propose** their
own commitments. The fixed three-part agenda — **Account** (each
member reports last week's commitments), **Review** (team reads lead
+ lag on the scoreboard), **Plan** (each member proposes 1-2 new
self-chosen commitments) — is non-negotiable.

What changes at team scope vs. personal scope is *who is doing what
to whom*:

1. **Sacred cadence is the leader's job to protect.** Same day, same
   time, same length, every week. If the leader is travelling, the
   session still runs (phone / video / delegated chair). Skip when
   busy and the team learns 4DX is optional — within 3-4 weeks the
   cadence collapses.
2. **Whirlwind is excluded by the leader.** When a member tries to
   bring an operational issue ("the projector broke at last week's
   event…"), the leader redirects: "park that for after the session
   — was the commitment kept?"
3. **Commitments come from members, not from the leader.** The
   leader's authority moves from *naming* the commitment to *vetoing*
   one that fails the standards. If the leader finds themselves
   saying "Alice, this week I want you to call five customers", they
   have stopped facilitating and started dictating — peer ownership
   evaporates.
4. **Specificity gate is applied to every commitment.** Two
   questions: (a) "What's the most important thing this week to move
   the lead?" (b) "Is it specific enough that you'd know on Saturday
   whether you did it?" Failures get bounced back for re-proposal.
5. **Veto-not-dictate.** When a proposed commitment is vague, won't
   move the lead, depends on others, or repeats the same rote
   commitment for the third week, the leader vetoes and asks the
   member to re-propose. The leader never substitutes their own
   commitment for the member's.
6. **Public accountability is structural.** Members report to *each
   other*, not to the leader in 1:1. Peer presence is the mechanism;
   the leader sets the format that lets that mechanism work.
7. **Recognition seeds engagement.** The leader catches members
   "doing something right" (kept commitment, lead-measure win,
   path-clearing) early in the next session. Low-cost; strong signal
   that the cadence matters.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Serena's Event Management team (Ch 15, canonical walkthrough)
- **Problem**: Hotel events team needed to lift corporate-event
  revenue from $22M → $31M; lead measures = quality site visits + bar
  package upsell.
- **Methodology applied**: Serena ran a Monday-morning WIG Session.
  She **opened with the scoreboard** (modeling the discipline
  herself), **reported her own commitments first** ("I committed to
  work with Kim and Karen for twenty minutes…"), then went round the
  room. Each member named what they kept, what the scoreboard showed
  for them, and proposed their own next-week commitment.
- **Conclusion**: The session shape works because the **leader models
  reporting and commitment-making first** — the team will not commit
  to a discipline the leader is unwilling to perform. Member
  commitments stayed specific, scoreboard-aligned, this-week-doable.
- **Outcome**: Lag measure ran $14M against a $10.4M target after
  three months — the cadence converted lead-measure activity into lag
  movement.

### Case 2: Jeff's missed commitment — "the moment that matters most" (Ch 15)
- **Problem**: A team member (Jeff) failed to keep his commitment,
  citing whirlwind (a major event ran into trouble; he firefought
  rather than executed his commitment).
- **Methodology applied**: Serena did NOT skip the moment. She ran a
  three-step response inside the session: (1) **Demonstrate respect**
  — acknowledge the whirlwind was real and the firefighting was
  valued; (2) **Reinforce accountability** — "when we make a
  commitment, we have to find a way to fulfill it no matter what";
  (3) **Encourage performance** — "can we count on you to catch up,
  by fulfilling last week's plus this week's?"
- **Conclusion**: When the whirlwind beats a commitment, the leader's
  response in front of the team sets the standard for the whole team.
  Skipping the moment teaches the team commitments are conditional;
  running the three-step preserves the standard without shaming the
  member.
- **Outcome**: Jeff carried both commitments the following week;
  team saw the standard hold; cadence preserved.

### Case 3: MICARE (Ch 5) — leader-modeled cadence at scale
- **Problem**: Mexican mining operation needed sustained behavior
  change across thousands of frontline workers (lost-time accidents,
  water use, output per worker).
- **Methodology applied**: Every department leader ran a Monday WIG
  Session, webinar-connected for remote sites. The CEO held the
  cadence himself for years — modeled non-negotiability from the top.
- **Conclusion**: The CEO credited "concentrated attention to the
  cadence of accountability" — the leader's protection of the
  *cadence itself* — as the major success factor, not any one
  decision made inside it.
- **Outcome (7 years)**: Lost-time accidents 700 → 60/yr; water use
  down two-thirds; output per worker 6,000 → 10,000 metric tons.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will a team leader need this skill?

1. **Leader has installed D1-D3 across the team** (Team WIG defined,
   lead measures picked, scoreboard built) and is asking how to run
   the weekly session that closes the loop.
2. **Leader is about to run their first WIG Session** and wants a
   walkthrough — what to say, what order, what to push back on.
3. **Leader's WIG Session has drifted into a status meeting** —
   members report whirlwind, no specific commitments are made, lead
   measures aren't moving.
4. **Leader is travelling / on PTO / sick** and is tempted to skip
   the session — the skill coaches them to run it remotely or
   delegate the chair, never skip.
5. **A member missed a commitment** and the leader doesn't know how
   to respond without either shaming the member or letting the
   standard erode.

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- "Run my team's weekly WIG Session."
- "Facilitate our team WIG meeting" / "Walk me through how to lead
  this week's session."
- "「チームの WIG Session を運営する」「うちの WIG ミーティングを進めたい」"
- "「帶我們團隊的 weekly WIG Session」「協助我主持團隊 WIG 會議」"
- "Help me lead the weekly check-in for our 4DX team."
- "Someone on my team didn't keep their commitment — what do I do?"
- "I'm travelling next Monday — should I cancel the WIG Session?"

### Non-trigger language (do NOT activate)

- "I want a weekly review for my own goal" → solo, not team — use
  `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`.
- "I'm preparing my commitment for tomorrow's WIG Session" → member
  prep, not facilitation — use `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`.
- "Help me pick our team WIG" → D1, not D4 — use a D1 team skill.
- "Plan our quarterly OKRs" / "run a team retro" → wrong artifact;
  WIG Session is a specific weekly format with three fixed segments.
- "Should our team adopt 4DX?" → pre-D1 triage — use
  `4dx-meta-team-strategy-triage`.

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`: solo user with no peer team;
  agent fills peer-witness role. This skill assumes ≥3 team members
  in the room and the leader running it.
- vs. `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`: a member preparing *for* the
  session (drafting their commitment, anticipating veto); this skill
  *runs* the session.
- vs. `4dx-sustain-personal-momentum-rescue`: that's the recovery
  skill for after a cadence has broken; this is the live-cadence
  facilitation skill.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent coaches the leader through
running one weekly session. The agent is **consultant-to-leader** —
it does not address the team directly; it whispers to the leader.
Output is leader-facing scripts, prompts, and decision rules the
leader uses live in the room.

1. **Pre-session check** (≤2 min, before the room opens)
   - Confirm: scoreboard updated? Last week's commitments logged and
     visible? Time-box set (20-30 min)? WIG stated the same way every
     week (verbatim banner)?
   - Coach prompt for leader: "Are you reporting *your own*
     commitments first? If not, the team won't commit to a standard
     you're not modeling."
   - Halt: if scoreboard not updated, don't run the session yet —
     "without the scoreboard, this is just another meeting" (Ch 15).

2. **Frame the session** (≤2 min)
   - Leader script: "Welcome to our weekly WIG Session. Our Team WIG
     is *[X]*. We have ~25 minutes. Three segments: Account, Review,
     Plan. Let's start with the scoreboard."
   - Completion criterion: WIG stated verbatim by the leader; agenda
     named; scoreboard physically/digitally present.

3. **Segment 1 — Account, leader first, then round-robin** (~7-10 min)
   - **Leader reports their own commitments first.** Coach reminder:
     "Model the standard. If you didn't keep yours, name it cleanly
     — no excuses — that gives the team permission to do the same."
   - Then round-robin each member: "Alice, last week you committed
     to *[Y]*. What happened?"
   - For each member, classify aloud: kept / partially kept / not
     kept. Listen, mirror, no problem-solve.
   - **The moment that matters most** — when a member did NOT keep
     their commitment, run the **three-step response** (Ch 15):
     (a) **Respect** — acknowledge the whirlwind was real;
     (b) **Accountability** — "when we make a commitment, we find a
     way to fulfill it no matter what";
     (c) **Encourage** — "can we count on you to catch up, by
     fulfilling last week's plus this week's?"
   - Coach veto: do NOT skip the moment, do NOT shame, do NOT let
     "I was busy" pass without the three-step.
   - Halt: if there were no prior commitments (first session), say
     so explicitly and skip to step 4.

4. **Segment 2 — Review the scoreboard** (~5-7 min)
   - Leader prompt to team: "What is the scoreboard telling us this
     week — lead and lag?"
   - Probe trend, not snapshot: "Are the leads trending up, flat, or
     down? Is the lag finally moving?"
   - Ask the team to interpret before the leader does: "What do you
     make of that?"
   - Celebrate kept commitments and lead-measure wins by name —
     "Bob, three site visits, 100% upsell conversation — that's the
     pattern we want."
   - If lead green + lag flat for ≥3 weeks, flag and route to
     post-session diagnostic (lead measure may need to change, not
     pushed harder).

5. **Segment 3 — Plan, member proposes, leader gates** (~8-10 min)
   - Canonical question, asked of each member in turn: "What are the
     **one or two** most important things you can do **this week** to
     move our **lead measures**?" (Note the precision — not "what's
     most important", not "what should we do".)
   - For each proposed commitment, the leader applies the
     **specificity gate** aloud:
     (a) Specific enough that we'd know on Saturday whether it was
     done?
     (b) Aligned to moving the scoreboard (the lead measure
     specifically)?
     (c) Within the member's own control (not "I'll get my boss
     to…")?
     (d) Different from a rote-repeated commitment (Ch 15: don't
     repeat the same one >2 consecutive weeks)?
   - **Veto-not-dictate**: if any gate fails, the leader says "that
     doesn't pass — re-propose" and waits. Coach reminder: "If you
     find yourself naming the commitment, stop. Ask: 'What do YOU
     think you can commit to that moves the lead?'"
   - Cap at 1-2 commitments per member. If a member proposes 3+,
     leader pushes back: "Pick the highest-leverage one or two — the
     rest is whirlwind in disguise."
   - Path-clearing: if a member needs help from another member,
     that help becomes a commitment for the helper too — logged with
     the same standard.

6. **Confirm out loud, then log** (~2 min)
   - Each member states their final 1-2 commitments aloud verbatim.
   - The leader (or chair) logs them where next week's Account
     segment can read them.
   - Completion criterion: every member has spoken at least one
     commitment that passed all four specificity gates.

7. **Close + confirm next session** (~1 min)
   - "Same time, same place next week."
   - If the leader is travelling: "I'll dial in / [member] will
     chair — we don't skip."
   - Coach reminder for the leader: "Catch one person doing
     something right early next session. That's the lowest-cost,
     highest-yield move you have."

**Whirlwind-creep redirect (any segment)**: If a member starts
narrating operational drama, the leader interrupts politely: "Park
that — let's keep this on the WIG. Was the commitment kept or not?"
Coach reminder: "Do this without apology. The rule is the rule."

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Solo-user contexts.** The mechanism is peer-to-peer
  accountability. With no peers in the room, use
  `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session` (agent serves as peer-witness).
- **Member-prep contexts.** A team member preparing their own
  commitment *before* attending the session uses
  `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`. This skill is for the leader
  running the room.
- **Daily standups, status meetings, 1:1s, project reviews.** WIG
  Session is a specific artifact: weekly cadence, three fixed
  segments, whirlwind-excluded, peer-accountability. Don't conflate.
- **Goal-discovery / WIG-selection conversations.** No WIG = nothing
  to anchor on; route to a D1 team skill first.
- **Annual / quarterly / monthly reviews.** Wrong cadence scope.
- **Reactive / on-call / emergency-response teams.** If the
  whirlwind *is* the strategic value (ER, on-call SRE,
  customer-success firefighting), the cadence misfits at the team
  level — see `4dx-meta-team-strategy-triage`.

### Author-warned failure modes (leader-specific)

- **Leader skips when busy / travelling (CE-11)**. Hell weeks are
  exactly when the team needs the cadence to hold. Book is firm:
  cancel-and-reschedule within the week, never skip; if leader is
  out, delegate the chair, never cancel. Skipped sessions = collapsed
  cadence within 3-4 weeks.
- **Leader dictates the commitment**. The fastest way to kill peer
  accountability. If the leader names what each member should do,
  members lose ownership and revert to compliance. Veto vague
  proposals; never substitute your own.
- **Leader doesn't report their own commitments first**. Sets the
  signal that the discipline is for the team, not for the leader.
  Members will mirror — they will report shallow, defer accountability,
  let things slip.
- **Whirlwind invasion (CE-10)**. Session drifts into "let me tell
  you how busy our week was". Defeats the cadence's entire purpose.
  Leader's job is to hold the agenda without apology.
- **Compliance commitments / vague intents (CE-12)**. "I'll try
  harder", "I'll focus on X", "I'll work on outreach". Fails
  specificity. Veto and request re-propose.
- **Commitment doesn't move the lead measure (CE-13)**. Member picks
  their most-urgent whirlwind task and labels it a commitment.
  Leader veto: "Does that move the lead, or just feel important?"
- **Repeated commitment ≥3 weeks** (Ch 15). Even a high-impact
  commitment becomes routine and stops generating learning. Veto and
  ask for a new lever.
- **Accepting "I was too busy" without the three-step** (Ch 15).
  Skipping the moment that matters most teaches the team that
  commitments are conditional. Run the
  Respect → Accountability → Encourage sequence every time.
- **Too many commitments per member**. Cap at 2. Five at 50% kept
  beats nothing; 1-2 at 95% kept moves the lead. Volume is the
  opposite of execution discipline.
- **Dependency commitments**. "I'll get marketing to approve X."
  Reframe to the member's own action: "I'll send marketing the brief
  with a Friday deadline."
- **Treating the session as report-out**. Center of gravity is
  **Plan**, not Account. Account ≈ 30%, Review ≈ 25%, Plan ≈ 40%.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations

- **Knowledge work with long feedback loops (CE-27)**. Some Team
  WIGs (drug discovery, novel research, multi-quarter B2B sales
  cycles) have feedback loops longer than a week — weekly
  accountability can become theatrical. Adapt: commit to specific
  *experiments / activities* (Cystic Fibrosis Center pattern, Ch 5),
  not to outcomes. The agenda holds; the question shifts from "did
  the lead move" to "did you run the activity".
- **High-context-culture face dynamics (CE-29)**. Public
  member-to-member accountability can read as face-threatening in
  JP / ZH / KR teams, especially around missed commitments. Adapt
  the three-step: keep Respect (extend it longer), make
  Accountability about the *team's* shared standard rather than the
  individual's failing, frame Encourage as offering help. The
  mechanism still works; the surface adapts.
- **Hybrid / fully-remote teams**. Book assumes co-located scoreboard
  and physical presence. Remote works (MICARE webinar precedent), but
  the leader has to compensate: deliberate eye contact via video,
  shared digital scoreboard always on screen, no one's camera off
  during their report.
- **Goodhart-style gaming on lead measures (CE-24)**. As members
  optimize for the weekly score, they may game the lead. Leader's
  periodic sanity check (off-session): is the lead still predictive
  of the lag, or has it become its own target?

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **Scrum / sprint review**: wrong cadence (~2 weeks), wrong format
  (story-points + demo, not commitment + scoreboard), no concept of
  WIG vs. whirlwind separation.
- **Daily stand-up**: wrong cadence (daily ≠ weekly), wrong question
  set (yesterday/today/blockers ≠ Account/Review/Plan).
- **OKR check-in**: similar weekly cadence but typically a
  status-update format with no specific peer-commitment-making
  segment; often manager-driven, not member-self-chosen.
- **Status meeting / project review**: report-out, no
  commitment-with-accountability mechanism, whirlwind not excluded.

### Industry-experience addendum (meeting design + team learning)

Two primary sources fill in what the book under-specifies about the
session's *shape* and the facilitator's *stance*. Citations in
`references/industry-grounding.md`:

- **Lencioni, *Death by Meeting*** (Jossey-Bass 2004) — the canonical
  meeting-type taxonomy: Daily Check-In / **Weekly Tactical** / Monthly
  Strategic / Quarterly Off-Site. The Weekly Tactical's three elements
  (lightning round / progress review / real-time agenda) map almost
  one-to-one to Account / Review / Plan. 4DX *adds* whirlwind-exclusion
  + commit-forward (not report-out) on top of the Lencioni shape. The
  "treating session as report-out" failure mode is exactly running a
  Weekly Tactical without the 4DX overlay.
- **Edmondson, *Teaming*** (Jossey-Bass 2012) — psychological safety
  is the precondition for members admitting slippage, proposing
  specifically, and pushing back on vague vetos. Without it, the
  cadence runs but produces performance-protective behavior — looks
  healthy on the scoreboard, dies underneath. The book's three-step
  Respect → Accountability → Encourage protocol (Ch 15) is, in
  Edmondson's terms, a safety-preserving accountability ritual:
  separates the act from the person. Skipping Respect or Encourage
  collapses safety in 2-3 weeks.
