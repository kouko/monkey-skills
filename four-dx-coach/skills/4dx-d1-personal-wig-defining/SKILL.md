# 4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining — Define Your Wildly Important Goal

## R — Reading

> All WIGs must have a finish line in the form of *From X to Y by When*. Every WIG must contain a clearly measurable result, as well as the date by which that result must be achieved.
>
> — McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 2 (Rule 4)

---

## I — Interpretation

D1 says: most stated goals never get executed because they are too vague,
too many, or too "important-sounding" rather than truly breakthrough-shaped.
WIG-defining is the protocol that converts a fuzzy aspiration into a single
testable finish line.

The protocol has three moves:

1. **Narrow to one.** Saying yes to one breakthrough goal means saying no to
   every other "good idea." For an individual, the cap is **one WIG** (max
   two only if they are independent and non-conflicting). Three or more is
   self-deception — the whirlwind will eat them.

2. **Pick the WIG by leverage, not by importance.** Ask "where would a
   *breakthrough* most transform my situation?", not "what's most important?"
   Most-important things are often already running adequately on autopilot
   (airline safety, paying rent). The WIG is where current performance is
   the gap.

3. **Write it as *From X to Y by When*.** X is the lag-measurable starting
   state in numbers. Y is the same lag-measure target. *When* is a fixed
   calendar deadline. Verb-led, specific, simple. If it can't be written
   this way, it isn't a WIG yet — it's an aspiration.

For project-shaped goals (fixed end-state, no recurring behavior), the
variant is *outcomes-list-with-deadline* — define "done" as a list of
deliverables on a date, not "% complete."

The output of this skill is one written WIG, OR an honest re-classification
("this is a habit", "this is a project", "this is a war that needs to be
decomposed into battles", "this is stroke-of-pen — just decide").

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: NASA moon shot (Ch. 2)
- **Problem**: NASA had dozens of worthy aerospace objectives in 1961, no breakthrough mandate.
- **Methodology applied**: JFK said "land a man on the moon and return him safely to the earth before this decade is out." X = earthbound, Y = moon-and-back, When = Dec 31 1969. He explicitly named other worthy goals being declined.
- **Conclusion**: A single, measurable, deadline-bound WIG threw the "Game on!" switch.
- **Outcome**: Apollo 11 landed July 1969 — five months under deadline — despite NASA having no operational technology for the three core battles (navigation, propulsion, life support) when the WIG was announced.

### Case 2: Tim Cook's Apple — "say no to good ideas" (Ch. 2 / Ch. 6)
- **Problem**: Apple post-1997 had a sprawling product line; Steve Jobs / Tim Cook reframed leadership as the discipline of refusal.
- **Methodology applied**: Cut to a handful of products; treat "saying no to a thousand things" as the act of strategic focus.
- **Conclusion**: Discipline 1 is fundamentally a *no* discipline — saying no to good ideas, even great ones — to protect the WIG.
- **Outcome**: Concentration on a small number of WIG-shaped products underwrote Apple's execution turnaround.

### Case 3: Mountain Land Rehab — 12-year stalled training project (Ch. 9)
- **Problem**: An organization-wide training initiative had been "in progress" for 12 years; "% complete" wasn't moving.
- **Methodology applied**: Reframed the project as a WIG with a deliverables-list and a date — instead of "complete training program", listed the specific outputs required and assigned a deadline (project-as-WIG variant).
- **Conclusion**: Percent-complete is a poor lag measure for project WIGs; defined deliverables on a date is the correct form.
- **Outcome**: The project shipped within a constrained timeframe once it was given a WIG-shaped finish line.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. The user states a goal but it is verb-less, number-less, or deadline-less ("I want to be healthier", "grow my business", "be a better parent", "write more").
2. The user lists 3+ "top priorities" simultaneously and can't commit ("this quarter I want to launch the product, write a book, learn Korean, and run a marathon").
3. The user has a project — not a habit — but is tracking it as "% done" and progress is stalled.
4. The user wants to "use 4DX" but hasn't yet expressed any goal in *From X to Y by When* form.
5. The user expresses an *activity* ("exercise more", "study Japanese daily") rather than an *outcome*.

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- EN: "I want to be healthier" / "grow my business" / "I have too many priorities" / "where should I focus?" / "help me set a goal"
- JP: 「目標がぼんやりしている」「優先順位が多すぎる」「やりたいことが多すぎて決められない」「どこに集中すべきか」「目標の立て方を教えて」
- zh-TW: 「目標太模糊」「想做的事太多」「不知道要專注哪個」「幫我訂一個目標」「我有太多優先事項」

### Non-trigger phrasings (do NOT activate)

- "Should I buy a sit-stand desk?" → stroke-of-pen, just decide
- "I want to lose 10kg by Dec 31, currently 78kg" → already a well-formed WIG; route to D2 lead-measure-discovery instead
- "Help me build a daily meditation habit" → habit design, not breakthrough goal
- "How do I run my weekly review?" → route to `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`
- "Is 4DX even right for this?" → route to `4dx-meta-personal-strategy-triage` first

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-meta-personal-strategy-triage`: triage decides whether 4DX *applies at all* (stroke-of-pen / whirlwind / behavioral-change). This skill assumes triage already concluded "behavioral-change."
- vs. `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`: D2 starts *after* this skill produces a well-formed WIG. If the WIG is still vague, you cannot find lead measures.
- vs. `4dx-d1-personal-whirlwind-triage`: whirlwind-triage is about capacity (~80/20 split, designing strategic work within the operator's gray-week). This skill is about goal *form*, not capacity.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent runs a Socratic coaching dialogue. **Do not write a WIG for the user — extract it from them.**

1. **Capture the raw goal.**
   - Ask: "What's the goal you want to use 4DX for? Say it however it feels natural."
   - Completion criterion: user states a goal in their own words; agent records verbatim.

2. **Force lag-measure surfacing.**
   - Ask: "If we said this goal was achieved one year from now, what would be measurably different about your life or work? What number would have changed?"
   - Completion criterion: at least one quantifiable downstream lag (kg / revenue / chapters / clients / minutes / hours / count).
   - Halt condition: if user cannot name any measurable difference, the goal is purely aspirational — jump to step 7 and re-classify as habit / value / unmeasurable.

3. **Pin X (starting state).**
   - Ask: "Where are you on that number right now, today?"
   - Completion criterion: a numeric current value (or honest "I don't know — let me check" with a commitment to baseline).

4. **Pin Y (target state).**
   - Ask: "Where do you want to be on that same number?"
   - Completion criterion: a numeric target in the same units as X.

5. **Pin *When*.**
   - Ask: "By what calendar date?"
   - Completion criterion: a fixed date (not "soon", "this year", "Q3-ish").

6. **Wildly-important test (transformation vs incremental).**
   - Ask: "If you hit X→Y by that date, would your situation be *transformed*, or just *improved*?"
   - Halt condition: if only incremental, this is whirlwind work, not a WIG. Tell the user honestly: "This is a maintenance goal, not a breakthrough goal. 4DX is overkill — just track it." Stop.

7. **Count test.**
   - Ask: "How many WIGs are you trying to hold right now?"
   - Push back if >2: "The book is firm — one WIG per individual, max two only if they are independent and non-conflicting. Three or more is self-deception. Which one would create the biggest transformation if you actually hit it?"
   - Completion criterion: user has narrowed to one (or named one as primary, with at most one independent secondary).

8. **Battle-vs-war test.**
   - Ask: "Could this WIG be won as a single battle, or is it a war that depends on winning several smaller battles?"
   - Halt condition: if war (composite), help user pick the *smallest* set of sub-WIGs (battles) needed to ensure winning, then re-run steps 2-7 on each. Do not push forward until each battle has its own *From X to Y by When*.

9. **Project-as-WIG check (Ch. 9 caveat).**
   - Ask: "Is this a recurring behavior change, or a one-time project with a fixed end-state?"
   - If project: rewrite finish line as a *list of deliverables on a date*, not a percent-complete. Reject "% complete" as a lag measure.

10. **Output the WIG.**
    - Write the final form: **"From [X] to [Y] by [date]"** (or for projects: deliverables list + date).
    - Show it back: "Read this aloud. Does it pass — could a stranger tell on the deadline whether you won or lost?"
    - Completion criterion: user confirms the written WIG is unambiguous and binary (won / lost on the date).

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Stroke-of-pen decisions** ("Should I switch CRMs? Buy a treadmill? Hire a VA?"). 4DX is not for changes executable by authority/money/decision alone. Route to a normal decision protocol.
- **Pure habit design** ("Help me meditate daily"). Habits are recurring behaviors with no fixed lag-measurable end-state. WIG-shape forces a finish line that habits don't have.
- **Already-well-formed numeric goals** with X / Y / When already explicit. Skip to D2.
- **Reactive / on-call domains** where the whirlwind *is* the strategic work (SRE incident response, ER medicine, customer support). Manufacturing a parallel breakthrough WIG competes destructively with the work that matters.
- **Goals expressed as activities, not outcomes** ("exercise more", "study Japanese") — the skill *redirects* these into outcome form, but if the user resists outcome framing on principle (process-only orientation), do not force a WIG; route to habit-design instead.

### Author-warned failure modes

- **Multi-WIG drift (CE-03)**: User claims 3-5 WIGs simultaneously. Book is unambiguous: this kills all of them. Push back hard.
- **Whole-workload-as-WIG (CE-04)**: WIG = "do my job better." Useless. WIG must be *separable from* and *narrower than* the whirlwind.
- **Most-important confusion (CE-05)**: User picks the WIG by ranking life areas (family > health > work). The book's selection rule is *where breakthrough has biggest leverage* — often not the highest-ranked area.
- **Aspirational but unmeasurable (CE-07)**: "Be more present", "be a great parent", "find purpose". No lag measure → not a WIG.
- **Percent-complete as project lag (CE-19)**: Long-running projects tracked as "% done" stall indefinitely. Replace with deliverables-list-with-deadline.
- **Activity-as-outcome confusion**: "Exercise 3x/week" is a *lead measure*, not a WIG. The WIG is the lag it predicts ("from 78kg to 70kg by Dec 31").

### Author's blind spots / period limitations (apply with care)

- **Goal-discovery vs goal-execution**: 4DX assumes the right WIG is already discoverable. In genuinely uncertain domains (early-stage research, novel creative work) the right WIG may not be knowable yet. The OKR / lean-startup tradition would say: run small experiments before committing to a WIG. If the user's situation is exploratory, don't force a single-WIG commitment — flag the mismatch.
- **Winning-as-master-motivator (CE-31)**: 4DX assumes "winning" energizes. Self-Determination Theory suggests autonomy and mastery are the deeper drivers. If the user is repelled by competitive / scoreboard framing, reframe the WIG as a mastery commitment without losing the *From X to Y by When* form.
- **Selection bias**: All FranklinCovey case studies are paying clients; failed WIGs are rarely analyzed. Don't pre-commit to a WIG before the count test (step 7) and battle-vs-war test (step 8).

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **SMART goals**: similar surface form, but SMART permits "achievable" goals that are merely incremental. WIG demands *breakthrough* (transformation, not improvement) plus *separability* from the whirlwind plus the *one-only* rule.
- **OKRs**: O+KRs allow 3-5 objectives × 3-5 key results = up to 25 simultaneous metrics. WIG protocol caps at 1 per individual, max 2. Do not let "OKR experience" smuggle in multi-objective drift.
- **Habit stacks / atomic habits**: process-oriented; no fixed finish line. WIG is outcome-and-deadline-oriented.

### Industry-experience addendum (single-bet downside, exploit-vs-explore, mindset framing)

The book argues for the upside of focus without engaging the well-documented
downside of single-bet over-commitment. Three literatures fill the gap —
primary citations in `references/industry-grounding.md`:

- **Christensen, *The Innovator's Dilemma* (HBS Press, 1997; ISBN 978-0875845852)** — flawless execution of an obsoleting goal. A WIG correct under current rules can become wrong as the underlying situation shifts (new job, new family phase, new diagnosis). Add a stability check before locking 6 months.
- **March, "Exploration and Exploitation" (*Organization Science* 2(1), 1991; DOI 10.1287/orsc.2.1.71)** — 4DX is pure exploitation. When the user is in an exploration phase, single-WIG commitment is the wrong tool; run small experiments first.
- **Dweck, *Mindset* (Random House, 2006; ISBN 978-1400062751)** — the X-to-Y form is form-correct but its *framing* affects setback robustness. Reword fixed-mindset / identity-locked targets toward growth-mindset / process-framed wording without losing measurability.

Personal-coach implication: pair the book's selection rules with Christensen-stability, March-confidence, and Dweck-mindset checks. Each catches a single-bet failure mode that pure focus discipline does not name.
