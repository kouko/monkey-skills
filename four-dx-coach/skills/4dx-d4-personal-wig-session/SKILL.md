# 4DX D4 — WIG Session (Weekly Cadence of Accountability)

## R — Reading

> The WIG Session is sacred—it takes place every week, even if the
> leader can't attend… The whirlwind is never allowed into a WIG
> Session.
>
> — McChesney, Covey & Huling, *Chapter 5: Discipline 4 — Create a Cadence of Accountability*

---

## I — Interpretation

A **WIG Session** is a 20-30 minute weekly meeting whose only job is to
push one Wildly Important Goal forward against the daily whirlwind. It
has a fixed three-part agenda — **Account** (report on last week's
commitments), **Review** (look at lead + lag measures on the
scoreboard), **Plan** (make 1-2 new self-chosen commitments for the
coming week). Three rules are non-negotiable:

1. **Cadence is sacred** — same time, same length, every week, never
   skipped (cancel-and-reschedule, never skip).
2. **Whirlwind is excluded** — no operational topics, no "let me tell
   you how busy my week was"; only the WIG.
3. **Commitments come from the participant, not the leader** — peer
   accountability beats boss accountability because ownership flows
   from voice in commitment-making.

Each commitment must pass two filters: (a) it is a **specific
deliverable** ("call three customers Tue + Thu"), not a vague intent
("focus on outreach"); (b) it **directly moves the lead measure**, not
just any whirlwind task that happens to feel important.

For a **solo user with no peer team**, the agent fills the
peer-witness role: prompts via Socratic questions, never dictates the
commitment, holds the user accountable to specificity, and keeps the
cadence sacred even on hell weeks.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: MICARE (Mexican coal mining, Ch 5)
- **Problem**: Large mining operation needed sustained behavioral
  change across thousands of frontline workers — lost-time accidents,
  water use, output per worker.
- **Methodology applied**: Monday-morning WIG Sessions in every
  department, webinar-connected for remote sites, every manager's
  scoreboard visible. Three-part agenda each week, sustained for years.
- **Conclusion**: The CEO credited "concentrated attention to the
  cadence of accountability" — the *cadence itself*, not any one
  decision — as the major success factor.
- **Outcome (7 years)**: Lost-time accidents 700 → 60/yr; water use
  down two-thirds; coal output per worker 6,000 → 10,000 metric tons.

### Case 2: Cystic Fibrosis Center (clinical knowledge work, Ch 5)
- **Problem**: Long feedback loops in clinical care — body-weight as a
  lead measure for lung function in CF infants — risked never being
  acted on.
- **Methodology applied**: Doctors held weekly WIG Sessions to review
  observations, commit to specific experiments (mist tents, massage
  vests, supplemental feedings), report results back the following
  week.
- **Conclusion**: Even ambiguous, multi-week-feedback-loop work can be
  driven by a weekly cadence if commitments are specific
  experiments-with-followup.
- **Outcome**: Center had not lost a patient to cystic fibrosis in
  many years.

### Case 3: Store 334 (Ch 11) — D4 as the missing piece
- **Problem**: A grocery store ranked 250th of 250. D1-D3 had been
  installed; lead measures were tracked; no breakthrough.
- **Methodology applied**: Adding the weekly WIG Session was the only
  missing piece.
- **Conclusion**: D1-D3 alone *die* without D4's accountability cadence
  — the four disciplines are a matched set.
- **Outcome**: Store 334 turned into a top-performing store. D4 was
  the active ingredient.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. **User has a goal but no rhythm**: working on a meaningful goal
   (dissertation, weight loss, business launch, marathon training,
   side project) but feels they "drift" between weeks of intense
   effort and weeks of drift — momentum keeps dying.
2. **User asks for a weekly review template** and wants something
   firmer than journaling — they want commitments held against past
   commitments, not just reflection.
3. **User has lead measures (from D2) and a scoreboard (from D3)** but
   keeps not acting on them — the data is being looked at but no
   *commitment* is being extracted.
4. **User installed all of 4DX D1-D3 personally** (defined a WIG, picked
   lead measures, built a scoreboard) and is asking what closes the
   loop — the answer is this skill.
5. **User asks how to keep a goal alive when life gets chaotic** —
   their default is to skip the review when busy, and the goal dies.

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- "I want a weekly review for my [goal / WIG / project]."
- "Help me stay on track with my goal each week."
- "「毎週の振り返りをしたい」「ゴールへの進捗を毎週チェックしたい」"
- "「想要每週固定 review 維持目標進度」「每週檢查 WIG 進度」"
- "I keep losing momentum on my goal — how do I keep it alive?"
- "Run my WIG Session with me." / "Walk me through this week's check-in."
- "I have a lead measure but I'm not acting on it — what now?"

### Non-trigger language (do NOT activate)

- "Plan my week" / "help me time-block" → general productivity, not
  goal-cadence; use a planning skill instead.
- "Reflect on this year" / "annual retrospective" → wrong cadence
  scope; the WIG Session is *weekly*, not yearly.
- "Help me figure out what my goal should be" → use
  `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining` first; this skill assumes a WIG already
  exists.
- "Find my lead measure" → use `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`; this
  skill consumes the lead measure, doesn't discover it.
- "I run a team — facilitate our team meeting" → this skill is for
  **solo personal use**; team facilitation is the book's chapters 5
  + 15 directly, not this skill.

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`: D1 picks the goal; D4 runs the weekly
  cadence on it.
- vs. `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`: D2 finds the leverage metric;
  D4 turns it into weekly commitments.
- vs. `4dx-d3-personal-scoreboard`: D3 builds the visual; D4 *reads*
  the scoreboard during segment 2 and acts on what it shows.
- vs. `4dx-sustain-personal-momentum-rescue`: that skill is for *recovering*
  after the cadence has broken or engagement died; this skill is for
  *running* the live cadence.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent runs a single weekly session
following these steps. The agent acts as **peer-witness**, not as
boss, coach, or status-checker.

1. **Frame & open the session** (≤2 min)
   - Greet, state the WIG aloud verbatim, state the time-box.
     Example: "Welcome to your weekly WIG Session. Your WIG is *[X]*.
     We have ~25 minutes. Three segments: Account, Review, Plan."
   - Completion criterion: WIG stated verbatim by the agent;
     time-box explicitly named; user confirms readiness.

2. **Segment 1 — Account** (~5-7 min)
   - Ask: "Last week you committed to *[Y]*. What happened?"
   - For each prior commitment, classify: **kept / partially kept /
     not kept**. Listen, mirror, no judgment, no fix.
   - If "didn't keep", ask once: "What got in the way?" Note pattern
     (whirlwind invasion / wrong commitment / over-committed) but do
     not problem-solve mid-segment.
   - Completion criterion: every prior commitment has been spoken to
     and classified; agent has logged kept/not-kept counts.
   - Halt condition: if there were no prior commitments (first
     session ever), skip to step 3 with a note: "First session — no
     account segment yet."

3. **Segment 2 — Review the scoreboard** (~5-7 min)
   - Ask: "What does your scoreboard show this week — lead and lag?"
   - Probe trend, not snapshot: "Is the lead trending up, flat, or
     down? Is the lag finally moving?"
   - Ask user to interpret before agent does: "What do you make of
     that?"
   - If lead green + lag flat for ≥3 weeks: flag P-50 ("change the
     leads, don't push harder") and route to
     `4dx-sustain-personal-momentum-rescue` — this is a diagnostic boundary,
     not a within-session fix.
   - Completion criterion: user has stated lead trend, lag trend, and
     their own interpretation aloud.

4. **Segment 3 — Plan** (~8-10 min)
   - Ask the canonical question: "What are the **one or two** most
     important things you can do **this week** to move your **lead
     measure**?" (Note the precision — not "what's most important",
     not "what should I do".)
   - For each candidate commitment, apply the **two-question filter**:
     (a) Is it a specific deliverable (someone could verify it was
     done)? (b) Does it directly move the lead measure?
   - If either fails, push back Socratically: "How would you know on
     Saturday whether you did that?" / "Does that move the lead, or
     does it move something else?"
   - Cap at 1-2 commitments. If user proposes 3+, push back: "Pick
     the highest-leverage one or two — the rest is whirlwind in
     disguise."
   - Reject commitments that depend on other people's actions:
     "What's the part of that that's within your control?"
   - Completion criterion: 1-2 commitments stated, each passes both
     filters, each is fully within user's control.

5. **Confirm & log** (~1-2 min)
   - User states the 1-2 commitments aloud (or types them) verbatim.
   - Agent echoes them back and logs them for next week's Account
     segment.
   - Completion criterion: commitments captured in a form the next
     session can reference.

6. **Close & schedule** (~1 min)
   - "Same time next week."
   - If user signals they want to skip next week: hold the line —
     "WIG Sessions are sacred. Cancel-and-reschedule, but don't
     skip. When this week, what time?"
   - Completion criterion: next session is on the calendar.

**Whirlwind-creep redirect (any segment)**: If the user starts
narrating operational drama ("you wouldn't believe how crazy work was
this week…"), interrupt politely: "Hold that — let's keep the WIG
Session on the WIG. Was that commitment kept or not?" Do this without
apology; the rule is the rule.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Team facilitation contexts.** This skill is for one solo user.
  For team WIG Sessions, the leader runs the meeting per the book's
  chapters 5 + 15 directly — not via this agent.
- **Daily standups, status meetings, 1:1s, project reviews.** WIG
  Session is a specific artifact: weekly cadence, three-part agenda,
  whirlwind-excluded, peer-accountability. Don't conflate.
- **Goal-discovery conversations.** If the user doesn't have a WIG yet,
  this skill has nothing to anchor on — route to
  `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`.
- **Annual retrospectives / quarterly reviews.** Wrong cadence scope.
- **Reactive / on-call / emergency-response work.** When the
  whirlwind *is* the strategic value (SRE on-call, ER medicine,
  customer-success firefighting), the 4DX cadence model misfits — see
  `4dx-meta-personal-strategy-triage`.

### Author-warned failure modes

- **Compliance commitments / vague intents (CE-12)**. "I'll try to do
  better", "I'll focus on outreach", "I'll work on the project."
  Fails specificity test. Push back until commitment is verifiable
  on the next Saturday morning.
- **Whirlwind invasion (CE-10)**. Session drifts into "let me tell
  you how busy my week was". Defeats the cadence's entire purpose.
  Hold the agenda.
- **Skipping when busy (CE-11)**. Hell weeks are exactly when the
  session matters most. Book is firm: cancel-and-reschedule within
  the week, never skip. Skipped sessions = collapsed cadence within
  3-4 weeks.
- **Commitment doesn't move the lead measure (CE-13)**. User picks
  the most-urgent whirlwind task, calls it a commitment. Reject —
  ask "does that move *the lead*, or just feel important?"
- **Boss-assigned (or self-dictated) commitments (CE-14)**. Even in
  solo coaching, agent must NOT dictate the commitment — that
  destroys the autonomy that makes commitment-keeping work. Agent
  prompts; user proposes.
- **Too many commitments**. 5 commitments at 50% kept beats 0
  outcomes; 1-2 commitments at 95% kept moves the lead. Cap at 2.
- **Dependency commitments**. "I'll get my boss to approve X." Not
  within user's control. Reframe to user's own action: "I'll send
  the proposal to my boss with a Friday deadline."
- **Treating the session as report-out**. The session's center of
  gravity is the **Plan** segment, not the Account. Account is
  ~25%, Plan is ~40% of time.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations

- **Solo cadence with no peer (CE-28)**. The book's mechanism assumes
  a peer team — public commitment + social pressure. The book never
  validates the dynamic for true solo users. This skill compensates
  by having the agent serve as peer-witness, but the compensation is
  partial; users who fall off entirely should consider a human
  accountability partner (coach, friend, public log).
- **High-context-culture face-loss (CE-29)**. Public peer
  accountability can read as face-threatening in JP / ZH / KR
  cultures. For solo agent use this is muted, but if user externalizes
  to a real human peer, prefer private-log alternatives.
- **Knowledge work with long feedback loops (CE-27)**. Some WIGs
  (drug discovery, novel research) have 6-month feedback loops —
  weekly accountability may become theatrical. The CF Center pattern
  (commit to specific *experiments*, not outcomes) is the
  workaround, but the skill must adapt the question from "did the
  lead move" to "did you run the experiment".
- **Goodhart-style gaming on lead measures (CE-24)**. The more
  committed user gets to "winning" the weekly score, the more they
  may game the lead. Periodic sanity check: is the lead still
  predictive of the lag, or has it become its own target?

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **GTD weekly review** (Allen): broader scope (all projects, all
  contexts); no concept of WIG, lead measure, or cadence-as-sacred.
  WIG Session is narrower and stricter.
- **OKR check-in**: similar weekly cadence but typically a
  status-update format, no commitment-making segment, often
  manager-driven not self-chosen.
- **Bullet-journal monthly migration**: reflection-heavy, no
  commitment-with-accountability mechanism.
- **Stand-up / scrum daily**: wrong cadence (daily ≠ weekly), wrong
  scope (sprint task ≠ WIG lead measure), wrong format (status
  three-questions ≠ Account/Review/Plan).

### Industry-experience addendum (meeting science + cadence theory)

The book asserts cadence is sacred on practitioner authority. Meeting
science and flow theory give the mechanism — and one structural caveat
the book under-serves. Primary citations in
`references/industry-grounding.md`:

- **Rogelberg, *Surprising Science of Meetings* (Oxford UP, 2019)** —
  empirical synthesis: short + agenda-tight + weekly is the meeting
  shape that survives scrutiny. Agenda discipline is the largest
  predictor of perceived effectiveness.
- **Lencioni, *Death by Meeting* (Jossey-Bass, 2004)** — four-meeting
  taxonomy; WIG Session is a Weekly Tactical (45-90 min) compressed to
  20-30 min, single-WIG focus. Account/Review/Plan ≈
  lightning-round/progress-review/real-time-agenda.
- **Reinertsen, *Principles of Product Development Flow* (Celeritas,
  2009, F6)** — fixed cadence dampens variability accumulation. *But*
  it's overhead-positive only when batch size and feedback latency
  match. A writer with a 12-month novel WIG running weekly sessions on
  lead-measure compliance is theatre, not 4DX. CF Center's workaround
  (commit to *experiments*, not outcomes) is the principled adaptation;
  if experiment-cycles are also sub-weekly impossible, lengthen cadence.
