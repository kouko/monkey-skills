# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-audit

The 4DX book is written from a leader-as-coach POV: a leader (or a
coach helping a leader) runs Socratic dialogue from scratch, one
discipline at a time. The audit role this skill performs —
**read all artifacts the user already has, diagnose against the
4DX framework, prescribe sequenced next moves** — is consultant-craft,
not 4DX-craft. The book's chapter rules supply the *what* of each
layer's well-formedness, but the *posture* (synthesizer-from-artifacts
rather than dialogue-coach-from-zero) is grounded in classical
consulting and analytical-reading literature. The four sources below
fill that gap.

## Underlying mechanism

A consultant audit is a **discovery + feedback cycle**: read the
client's existing artifacts and self-description, diagnose against
a framework, render a structured finding, and route the client to
the right next action. This differs from coaching (which generates
content via dialogue) in that the client has already done the
artifact-creation work — the consultant's contribution is **synthesis,
diagnosis, and sequencing** rather than co-creation. The sources
below ground each leg of that cycle: discovery (Schein), feedback
+ contracting (Block), positioning vs the coach role (Maister), and
the analytical-reading discipline that underwrites "read everything
before diagnosing" (Adler & Van Doren).

## Key industry sources

### Source 1: Block, *Flawless Consulting* (3rd ed., 2011)

**Citation**: Peter Block, *Flawless Consulting: A Guide to Getting
Your Expertise Used* (3rd ed., Pfeiffer/Wiley, 2011, ISBN
978-0-470-62074-8). 1st ed. 1981; 2nd ed. 1999.

**Applicable to**: audit posture; discovery → feedback flow; how to
deliver findings without triggering client defensiveness.

Block's central claim is that consulting fails not on technical
expertise but on the relationship the consultant builds during
discovery and feedback. The five-phase model (entry & contracting →
discovery & dialogue → analysis & decision → engagement & implementation
→ extension/recycle/termination) maps almost directly to this skill's
5-step protocol: inventory artifacts (entry), map to 5 layers
(discovery), diagnose per layer (analysis), prescribe + route (decision
→ engagement). Block's "authentic behavior" rule — say what is true
about the work even when uncomfortable — anchors the audit's
willingness to label a layer `malformed` or `wrong-shape` rather than
politely rounding up to "good start."

The "feedback meeting" chapter directly informs the audit's output
format: lead with the data the client provided, present diagnosis as
findings (not judgments), and end with the client's choice of next
step — which is exactly why this skill ends every audit with "pick
the highest-priority gap and we deep-dive, or run them in sequence."

### Source 2: Schein, *Process Consultation* (1969) and *Helping* (2009)

**Citation**: Edgar H. Schein, *Process Consultation: Its Role in
Organization Development* (Addison-Wesley, 1969; Addison-Wesley OD
Series). Companion: *Helping: How to Offer, Give, and Receive Help*
(Berrett-Koehler, 2009, ISBN 978-1-57675-863-2). Updated as *Process
Consultation Revisited: Building the Helping Relationship*
(Addison-Wesley, 1999, ISBN 0-201-34596-X).

**Applicable to**: discovery phase posture; helper–helpee role
calibration; not-doing-the-work-for-the-client.

Schein distinguishes three consulting models: (1) purchase-of-expertise
(client knows what they need, buys an answer), (2) doctor-patient
(consultant diagnoses + prescribes unilaterally), and (3) **process
consultation** (consultant helps the client see their own situation
clearly and make their own choice). 4dx-audit deliberately blends
modes 2 and 3: the diagnostic step is doctor-patient (the 4DX
framework provides the diagnostic standard), but the prescription
step is process — the audit ends with "pick the gap that matters
most to you," not "do this first." Schein's helper-helpee model
also explains why this skill explicitly **routes to deep-dive coach
skills rather than executing them inside the audit**: the audit is
the helper saying "here's what I see"; the deep-dive coaching is
where the client and the framework do the work together.

Schein's *Helping* (2009) further argues that the helper must
**establish the help-relationship before offering content** — which
in this skill's protocol surfaces as Step 1 (inventory) preceding
Step 2-5 (diagnosis + prescription). Skipping the inventory step
collapses the help-relationship into doctor-patient and triggers
client defensiveness against the diagnosis.

Links:
[Process Consultation Revisited (archive.org)](https://archive.org/details/processconsultat0000sche),
[Schein's Ten Principles (umass)](https://digitalcommons.umassglobal.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?filename=7&article=1538&context=immersion&type=additional).

### Source 3: Maister, Green & Galford, *The Trusted Advisor* (Free Press, 2000)

**Citation**: David H. Maister, Charles H. Green, Robert M. Galford,
*The Trusted Advisor* (Free Press, 2000, ISBN 978-0-7432-1234-2;
20th Anniversary Edition, Touchstone, 2021, ISBN 978-1-9821-5710-4).
Companion: David H. Maister, *Managing the Professional Service Firm*
(Free Press, 1993, ISBN 978-0-684-83431-7).

**Applicable to**: positioning the consultant-mode skill against the
coach-mode D-skills; trust-equation as a reason to lead with audit
before prescription.

Maister-Green-Galford's Trust Equation:

> Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-orientation

…is the core reason this skill's audit comes before the route to
deep-dive coach skills. A consultant who jumps to prescription
("just go run d1-wig-formulation") without first showing they read
the client's artifacts has zero **Credibility** (no demonstrated
expertise on the client's specific situation) and zero **Intimacy**
(no demonstrated care for the specific context). The audit's
Step 1 (inventory) and Step 2 (map to layers) are pure
trust-building — they show the consultant has actually read what the
client gave them — which is what makes the Step 5 prescription
land instead of bouncing off as generic advice.

Maister also draws the line between the **expert** (sells answers),
the **consultant** (sells diagnosis + recommendation), and the
**trusted advisor** (sells judgment about what matters most). This
skill aims for the second tier: it does not pretend to know the
client's situation better than they do (advisor-tier), and it does
not just hand over a 4DX cheatsheet (expert-tier). It diagnoses
against the framework and recommends sequenced next moves — which
is consultant-tier work.

Links:
[Trusted Advisor Associates](https://trustedadvisor.com/books/the-trusted-advisor),
[Free Press / Simon & Schuster](https://www.amazon.com/Trusted-Advisor-David-H-Maister/dp/0743212347).

### Source 4: Adler & Van Doren, *How to Read a Book* (rev. ed., 1972)

**Citation**: Mortimer J. Adler and Charles Van Doren, *How to Read
a Book: The Classic Guide to Intelligent Reading* (revised and
updated edition, Touchstone/Simon & Schuster, 1972, ISBN
978-0-671-21209-4). 1st ed. by Adler alone, 1940.

**Applicable to**: Step 1 (inventory) and Step 2 (map content to
layers); the discipline of "read everything before judging anything."

Adler & Van Doren's four levels of reading — **elementary** (decode),
**inspectional** (skim to learn what kind of book it is and its
structure), **analytical** (read for full understanding within its
own terms), **syntopical** (read multiple sources comparatively
against a question) — map directly onto this skill's reading discipline.
A 4DX audit on a multi-artifact pile is a **syntopical read against
the 4DX framework**: the auditor is not asking "is this strategy doc
good?" (analytical read of one artifact); they are asking "across all
provided artifacts, what does this organization's 4DX implementation
look like?" — which requires reading each artifact analytically first,
then synthesizing across them.

The most operationally useful Adler-Van Doren rule for this skill is
**inspectional reading must precede analytical reading**: skim the
whole stack first to see what's there, what's missing, and what shape
each piece takes, before diagnosing any single layer. Step 1
(inventory) of the audit protocol is exactly this rule applied — list
what's present before judging what's well-formed. Skipping inventory
and jumping to "your WIG is malformed" is the analytical-read
mistake the book warns against in Chapter 5.

### Source 5: Cindrich, "5 Things Leaders Get Wrong with FranklinCovey's 4 Disciplines of Execution" (LinkedIn Pulse)

**Citation**: Andy Cindrich, "5 Things Leaders Get Wrong with FranklinCovey's
4 Disciplines of Execution," LinkedIn Pulse article, c. 2018-2019. Author
self-describes: "I was on the team that developed them back in the early
2000s." Cindrich is a credentialed FranklinCovey practitioner-author writing
post-hoc on observed implementation failures across the consulting practice.
Companion: same five mistakes adapted to education context in "5 Things
Educators Get Wrong with Discipline" on leaderinme.org (Leader-In-Me, FranklinCovey
education arm).

**Applicable to**: cross-layer audit (this skill); supplementary to topic-skill
audit-modes when the rule-check matrix needs failure-pattern anchoring beyond
book-internal rule citations.

Cindrich names five canonical 4DX implementation failure modes from inside
the consulting practice that developed the framework. The five (verbatim
short-form):

1. **Failure to use 4DX as your operating system** — treating it as a
   one-off training program; "results will be fleeting." Counter: 4DX
   is the team's OS, not an initiative.
2. **Failure to pick a strategic goal** — choosing a job-already-being-done
   as a WIG. Verbatim: *"Hitting your sales number is your job — it is
   not a WIG."* This is the operationalization of book Ch 6's "WIG must
   be breakthrough not BAU" but with surgical phrasing.
3. **Failure to get to small-outcome lead measures** — using only
   "leveraged behaviors" (do X activity Y times/week) which become
   boring; cognitive engagement requires small-outcome lead measures
   that allow multiple paths.
4. **Using scoreboards to name and shame** — scoreboards weaponized as
   public embarrassment; cites Brené Brown that shame "crushes our
   tolerance for vulnerability, thereby killing engagement, innovation,
   creativity, productivity, and trust."
5. **Getting accountability all wrong** — leaders treat accountability
   as control rather than personal commitment. Cindrich's diagnostic
   equation: **Goal Clarity + Commitment + Cadence = Results**.

The companion Leader-In-Me education adaptation provides the same five
modes plus four verbatim micro-WIG-and-lead exemplars (third-grade SSR
20 min × 3/week; AP essays 75% scoring 4-5; basketball offensive rebounds
≥10/game; band sections mastering one movement weekly) that calibrate
what "small-outcome lead measure" looks like vs the boring "leveraged-
behavior" trap.

Direct application: this skill's cross-layer audit runs the user's
multi-artifact context against the 5 layers (WIG / Lead / Lag-Scoreboard
/ Cadence / Substrate). Cindrich's five modes give the audit a second
diagnostic axis — not "is the layer well-formed by book rule?" but "is
this one of the 5 named failure patterns FranklinCovey practitioners see
most often?" Use the 5-mode checklist after the layer-by-layer rule
check; surface mode-matches in the recommendation step. Cindrich's
diagnostic equation (Goal Clarity + Commitment + Cadence = Results)
gives the gap-naming heuristic when audit reveals that one of the
three is missing.

**Authority note**: Cindrich's article is a LinkedIn Pulse piece by an
acknowledged FranklinCovey insider, not a peer-reviewed publication.
Treat as **credentialed-author meta-reflection on practice** — equivalent
in grounding strength to an HBR practitioner essay or the book's own
McChesney-Covey-Huling chapter framings, but not equivalent to the
peer-reviewed academic sources elsewhere in this file.

Public URL: https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/5-things-leaders-get-wrong-franklincoveys-4-execution-andy-cindrich

## What 4DX consultant-auditors learn from these sources

The book's chapter rules tell the auditor *what* to look for in
each of the 5 layers — Block, Schein, Maister, and Adler-Van Doren
tell the auditor *how to look*: read everything before judging
anything (Adler), establish the help-relationship before offering
content (Schein), build trust through demonstrated reading before
prescribing (Maister), and contract every finding around the client's
own next-move choice rather than a unilateral mandate (Block). This
is the consultant-craft layer the 4DX book leaves implicit because
its primary author voice is a leader-coach, not a third-party
consultant. Without these sources, the audit easily collapses into
either (a) generic-prescription mode — "use 4DX, here's the
checklist" — which fails the trust equation, or (b) doctor-patient
mode — "your scoreboard is wrong, replace it with this one" —
which fails the help-relationship. With these sources, the audit
sits where it should: a process-consultant's diagnostic that hands
back to the client a clear map and a sequenced choice.
