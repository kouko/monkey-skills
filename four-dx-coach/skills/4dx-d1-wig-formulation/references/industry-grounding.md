# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-d1-wig-formulation (multi-mode)

Consolidated primary-source citations for the merged D1 WIG-formulation skill,
which spans three modes: **solo** (personal-WIG defining for a single user),
**team-leader** (team Primary-WIG selection on behalf of a team), and
**member** (comprehending and pressure-testing an inherited team WIG from the
member's seat). Each citation below names the source, the mode(s) it applies
to, and the mechanism it grounds.

The 4DX book frames WIG selection as a focus discipline — discipline 1 is
"focus on the wildly important" — but is leader-POV throughout chapters 2,
6, and 12 and engages neither the strategy literature on what counts as a
strategy at all (Porter, Rumelt, Hambrick & Fredrickson, Mintzberg) nor the
single-bet downside literatures (Christensen on disruption blindness, March
on exploitation-vs-exploration, Dweck on fixed-mindset framing) nor the
member-side ask-discipline literature (Edmondson, Grant, Meyer). The sources
below fill those three gaps in parallel — each is tagged for the mode(s)
that reference it.

---

## Underlying mechanism

A WIG is a single-bet commitment over a multi-month horizon. Single-bet
commitments concentrate variance — they win bigger when right and lose
harder when wrong. The 4DX book argues for the upside (focus produces
breakthrough) without engaging the downside (mis-bet produces 6 months of
efficient execution of the wrong goal). Three sets of mechanisms in the
industry literature address what the book under-engages:

1. **Strategy completeness** (team-leader mode) — Porter / Rumelt /
   Hambrick & Fredrickson / Mintzberg name the conditions under which a
   candidate WIG is actually a strategy rather than a goal masquerading as
   one. The book's 2x2 (Importance × Feasibility) plus four-trap checklist
   filters for measurability and non-broadness, but a candidate that clears
   all four traps can still be strategically empty.
2. **Single-bet downside** (solo mode) — Christensen / March / Dweck name
   the disruption-blindness, over-exploitation, and fixed-mindset framing
   traps that the book's pure-focus stance under-engages.
3. **Member-side ask discipline** (member mode) — Edmondson / Grant /
   Meyer name the psychological-safety, dissent-without-penalty, and
   cross-cultural-calibration moves that let a member surface "I cannot
   state this WIG verbatim" or "I think the lag measure is mis-targeted"
   without inviting the dissent penalty.

## Key industry sources

### Source 1: Christensen, *The Innovator's Dilemma* (HBS Press, 1997)

**Citation**: Clayton M. Christensen, *The Innovator's Dilemma: When New
Technologies Cause Great Firms to Fail* (Harvard Business School Press,
1997; ISBN 978-0875845852). [Harvard Business School book page](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=46)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: solo

Christensen's central finding: well-managed companies executing perfectly
on their current most-important goal still lose to disruptive entrants who
optimize a different metric on a different curve. The book's case studies
(disk drives, mechanical excavators, mini-mills) all show a pattern where
the incumbent's WIG was correct *under the dominant performance metric of
the prior period* and became wrong as the underlying market shifted. Pure
focus produced flawless execution of an obsoleting goal.

Translated to personal-coach scope: a WIG selected from current-state pain
("get to 70 kg by Dec 31") is correct under current assumptions about what
matters. If the user's underlying situation is shifting (new job, new family
phase, new diagnosis, new market), a 6-month single-WIG can lock execution
into an obsoleting target. The book's selection rules (where breakthrough
matters most, Battle-vs-War) help — but they do not prompt the user to
explicitly check "are the rules I'm optimizing under stable for 6 months?"
This is a Christensen-shaped pre-WIG check the agent should add.

### Source 2: March, "Exploration and Exploitation in Organizational Learning" (*Organization Science*, 1991)

**Citation**: James G. March, "Exploration and Exploitation in
Organizational Learning," *Organization Science*, vol. 2, no. 1
(February 1991), pp. 71–87. [INFORMS PubsOnline DOI 10.1287/orsc.2.1.71](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/orsc.2.1.71)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: solo

March formalizes the trade-off between **exploitation** (refining a known
bet) and **exploration** (sampling unknown alternatives). His core finding:
organizations that over-allocate to exploitation succeed in the short run
and fail in the long run; the optimum is a portfolio that allocates
~10–20% to exploration even when exploitation is currently winning.

Applied to WIG selection: 4DX is a pure-exploitation methodology. It is the
right tool when the user has enough evidence to commit to a single bet for
6 months. It is the wrong tool when the user is in an exploration phase
(early-career, post-pivot, novel domain). The WIG-defining mode should run
an explicit confidence check before locking in a single bet — *do you have
enough evidence that this is the right thing to commit 6 months to?* — and
flag the lean-startup / OKR alternative when confidence is low. The 4DX
book's selection bias (FranklinCovey clients had committed before signing
on) hides this failure mode systematically.

### Source 3: Dweck, *Mindset* (Random House, 2006)

**Citation**: Carol S. Dweck, *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success*
(Random House, 2006; ISBN 978-1400062751). [Penguin Random House book page](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/44330/mindset-by-carol-s-dweck-phd/)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: solo

Dweck's two-decade body of research distinguishes **fixed mindset**
(ability is innate; effort signals lack of talent) from **growth mindset**
(ability is developed; effort is the mechanism). The framing of a target
affects effort under setbacks: "I want to be a 70 kg person"
(identity-framed; fixed-mindset adjacent) produces sharper drop-off after a
setback than "I am training my body toward 70 kg by Dec 31"
(process-framed; growth-mindset adjacent), even though the X-to-Y form is
identical.

The 4DX *From X to Y by When* form is form-correct but does not specify
the *framing* of the X-to-Y. The WIG-defining mode should pass-check the
wording for fixed-mindset traps: identity-locked nouns ("be a writer"),
comparison language ("better than my colleague"), and innate-ability
language. A growth-mindset rewording preserves the X-to-Y measurability
and adds robustness against setback-induced abandonment in months 3–4 —
exactly when most personal WIGs collapse.

### Source 4: Porter, *Competitive Strategy* (Free Press, 1980) + "What Is Strategy?" (HBR, 1996)

**Citation**: Michael E. Porter, *Competitive Strategy: Techniques for
Analyzing Industries and Competitors* (Free Press, 1980; ISBN
0-02-925360-8). Companion: Michael E. Porter, "What Is Strategy?",
*Harvard Business Review*, vol. 74, no. 6 (November–December 1996),
pp. 61–78. [HBR article](https://hbr.org/1996/11/what-is-strategy)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: team-leader

Porter's foundational text on strategy as deliberate positioning. The
most-cited principle aligns precisely with the WIG focus discipline:
**"The essence of strategy is choosing what not to do."** A Primary WIG
is meaningful only because the team has explicitly de-prioritized the
parked Battles; if every high-importance candidate is "still being worked
on alongside" the Primary WIG, no actual strategic choice has been made.
The book's Trap 1 (≤2 Primary WIGs) and Trap 2 (not too broad) are both
forms of Porter's discipline.

Practical sharpening: the team-leader mode should require the leader to
name *which* parked Battles will receive demonstrably less attention as a
result of the chosen Primary WIG; if the answer is "none — we'll keep
doing all of it," the WIG selection is a wish list, not a strategy.

### Source 5: Rumelt, *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy* (Crown Business, 2011)

**Citation**: Richard P. Rumelt, *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy: The
Difference and Why It Matters* (Crown Business, 2011; ISBN
978-0-307-88623-1). Companion: Richard Rumelt, "The Perils of Bad
Strategy," *McKinsey Quarterly*, June 2011. [McKinsey article](https://www.mckinsey.com/business-functions/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-perils-of-bad-strategy)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: team-leader

Rumelt (Harry & Elsa Kunin Professor, UCLA Anderson) identifies the
**kernel of strategy** as three elements: (1) **Diagnosis** — identifying
the central challenge and what makes it hard; (2) **Guiding policy** —
the overall approach to overcome the obstacles named in the diagnosis;
(3) **Coherent action** — the coordinated steps that implement the
guiding policy. Rumelt's central warning: "the most common cause of bad
strategy is a weak diagnosis," and "the second most common cause is
confusing goals with strategy. Goals, ambitions, visions, missions,
values — none of these things are a strategy."

Applied as a WIG-test: a candidate Primary WIG that cannot be paired with
a one-sentence diagnosis ("the central challenge blocking us is X") is a
goal masquerading as a strategy. The 4DX 2x2 scores feasibility but never
asks for diagnosis; this gap explains why high-and-right candidates can
still be strategically empty.

### Source 6: Hambrick & Fredrickson, "Are You Sure You Have a Strategy?" (*AME*, 2001)

**Citation**: Donald C. Hambrick & James W. Fredrickson, "Are You Sure You
Have a Strategy?", *Academy of Management Executive*, vol. 15, no. 4
(November 2001), pp. 48–59. DOI 10.5465/AME.2001.5897655. [AOM DOI link](https://journals.aom.org/doi/abs/10.5465/ame.2001.5897655?journalCode=amp)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: team-leader

Peer-reviewed; introduces the **strategy diamond**: a strategy must
specify all five of (1) **Arenas** (where to compete), (2) **Vehicles**
(how to get there), (3) **Differentiators** (how to win), (4) **Staging**
(sequence and speed), (5) **Economic logic** (how returns are obtained).
A candidate Primary WIG that names a target outcome but doesn't specify
arena + vehicle + differentiator + staging + economic logic is, in the
authors' language, *not* a strategy.

Practical use as a WIG-test: for any high-and-right 2x2 candidate, the
leader should be able to answer all five in 2–3 sentences each. If
staging or economic logic is undefined, the WIG is premature — return to
brainstorm or reduce scope. This is a sharper completeness test than the
*From X to Y by When* check, which only forces measurability.

### Source 7: Mintzberg, *The Rise and Fall of Strategic Planning* (Free Press, 1994)

**Citation**: Henry Mintzberg, *The Rise and Fall of Strategic Planning:
Reconceiving Roles for Planning, Plans, Planners* (Free Press, 1994; ISBN
0-02-921605-2). Companion: Henry Mintzberg, "The Fall and Rise of
Strategic Planning," *Harvard Business Review*, January–February 1994.
[HBR article](https://hbr.org/1994/01/the-fall-and-rise-of-strategic-planning)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: team-leader

Distinguishes **deliberate strategy** (intentions fully realized) from
**emergent strategy** (realized patterns that weren't expressly intended).
Mintzberg's central critique: "strategic planning" is an oxymoron —
planning is analysis, strategy is synthesis. In genuinely uncertain
domains (early-stage product, novel R&D), the right Primary WIG may not be
knowable yet; running a 2x2 with speculative feasibility scores produces
false confidence. The book acknowledges this as a single-line caveat;
Mintzberg's framing is that this isn't an edge case — for many real
organizations, *all* strategy is at least partly emergent, and the WIG
should be re-tested every cycle rather than locked in for 1–2 years.

Applied to team-leader mode: Mintzberg layers a humility caveat over the
Porter / Rumelt / Hambrick-Fredrickson stack. In genuinely uncertain
domains, lock the WIG for shorter cycles (one quarter, not 1–2 years) and
treat selection as iterative-emergent rather than one-shot-deliberate.

### Source 8: Edmondson, *The Fearless Organization* (Wiley, 2018)

**Citation**: Amy C. Edmondson, *The Fearless Organization: Creating
Psychological Safety in the Workplace for Learning, Innovation, and
Growth* (Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley & Sons, 2018; ISBN 978-1-119-47724-2).

**Applicable to mode(s)**: member

Synthesizes ~20 years of psychological-safety research, including
Edmondson's seminal hospital-team studies showing that the *better* teams
reported *more* errors — not because they made more, but because members
felt safe enough to surface them.

Direct application: a member who cannot state the team WIG verbatim, or
who cannot articulate why this WIG was chosen, should treat that as a
*signal to ask* — not as a personal failure to absorb the announcement.
Edmondson frames the asking itself as a learning behavior, the absence of
which produces "silent execution failures" — exactly what 4DX line-of-
sight is meant to prevent. The book's framing reduces the social cost of
"I need to ask my leader to restate the WIG" by making the ask a
contribution to team learning rather than a confession of inattention.

### Source 9: Grant, *Originals* (Viking, 2016)

**Citation**: Adam Grant, *Originals: How Non-Conformists Move the World*
(New York: Viking, 2016; ISBN 978-0-525-42956-2). Chapter 3 ("Out on a
Limb: Speaking Up Without Getting Silenced") is the relevant section.

**Applicable to mode(s)**: member

Specifically addresses how subordinates can productively challenge an
inherited goal without burning credibility — including the distinction
between *idea-quality dissent* (productive) and *identity-position
dissent* (rejected by hierarchies).

Direct application: when a member receives a team WIG they suspect is
mis-targeted (wrong lag measure, wrong deadline, wrong scope), Grant's
research suggests framing the challenge as a question about evidence
rather than a verdict on the leader's choice — "what alternative did we
consider, and what made this one win?" rather than "I don't think this is
right." This is the exact phrasing the member-mode "surface the strategic
rationale" step prescribes, and Grant's evidence base explains *why* that
phrasing reliably succeeds where direct dissent fails.

---

### Source 10: Meyer, *The Culture Map* (PublicAffairs, 2014)

**Citation**: Erin Meyer, *The Culture Map: Breaking Through the Invisible
Boundaries of Global Business* (New York: PublicAffairs, 2014; ISBN
978-1-61039-250-1). Relevant dimensions: "Leading" (egalitarian vs
hierarchical) and "Disagreeing" (confrontational vs avoiding-confrontation).

**Applicable to mode(s)**: member

The "Leading" and "Disagreeing" dimensions directly determine whether a
member can comfortably ask "why this WIG?" — a question that reads as
collaborative in Dutch / Israeli contexts and as insubordinate in Korean
/ Japanese / Chinese contexts.

Direct application: the member-mode "ask your leader" homework defaults
to a direct-question script that fits low-power-distance, low-confrontation-
cost cultures. Members in high-power-distance, high-face-loss-cost cultures
need a softer route — written follow-up after the meeting, asking a
longer-tenured peer first, or framing the question through a future-task
scenario rather than a clarification-of-past-decision. Meyer's framework
gives the member a way to *calibrate* the homework to their cultural
register without dropping it entirely.

## What 4DX practitioners learn from these sources (per mode)

The ten sources above stack into three layered upgrades to the book's
WIG selection methodology — one per mode.

**For solo mode** — three additional checks beyond the book's
multi-WIG / aspirational-but-unmeasurable / activity-vs-outcome traps:
- **Christensen stability check** — are the rules I'm optimizing under
  stable for the 6-month horizon, or is my situation shifting? If
  shifting, shorten the horizon or convert to a learning-WIG.
- **March exploit-vs-explore check** — do I have enough evidence to
  commit to one bet for 6 months, or am I in an exploration phase that
  should run experiments first? If the latter, route to OKR-style
  quarterly resets, not single-WIG commitment.
- **Dweck mindset reword** — pass-check the X-to-Y wording for fixed-
  mindset framing and reword for growth-mindset robustness without
  losing the measurable form.

**For team-leader mode** — three layered tests on top of the 2x2 +
four-trap checklist:
- **Porter's NOT-do test** — name 2+ parked Battles that will receive
  demonstrably less team attention as a *result* of choosing this
  Primary WIG. If none, the WIG is a wish list, not a strategy.
- **Rumelt diagnosis test** — pair the WIG with a one-sentence
  diagnosis: "the central challenge that makes this hard is X." If the
  leader cannot articulate this, return to brainstorm — the WIG is a
  goal without a guiding policy.
- **Hambrick-Fredrickson five-element test** — does the team have
  working answers to arenas / vehicles / differentiators / staging /
  economic logic? Missing staging or economic logic means execution
  will drift even if the WIG is technically well-formed.
- *Mintzberg humility caveat*: in genuinely uncertain domains, lock the
  WIG for shorter cycles (one quarter, not 1–2 years).

**For member mode** — three legitimizing frames for the upward ask:
- **Edmondson safety frame** — "I need to ask my leader to restate the
  WIG" is a learning behavior the team needs, not a confession of
  inattention.
- **Grant dissent-without-penalty script** — frame challenges as
  questions about evidence ("what alternative did we consider?") rather
  than verdicts on the leader's choice.
- **Meyer cultural calibration** — calibrate the ask to the member's
  actual cultural register (direct vs written-follow-up vs peer-first
  vs scenario-framed) rather than dropping the ask discipline entirely.

Each addresses a documented failure mode that 4DX's pure-focus, leader-POV
stance under-engages, and together they turn the WIG-formulation discipline
from a single-bet gamble into a primary-source-grounded discipline that
adapts cleanly across solo, team-leader, and member seats.
