# Standard: WIG discipline — the focus cap

## Statement

The number of WIGs an actor (individual / team / org) holds at any one
time is firmly capped:

- **Per individual**: **one WIG**, max two only when independent and
  non-conflicting. Three or more is self-deception.
- **Per team**: **one Primary WIG (preferred)**; two acceptable only
  via Approach B with genuinely separate operating segments. Total
  team WIGs (Primary + Battle WIGs combined) ≤ 3.
- **Per organizational layer**: **1-3 WIGs**. Multi-WIG drift past
  this cap kills all of them through whirlwind dilution.

Saying yes to one WIG means saying *no* to every other "good idea." A
WIG selection without explicitly de-prioritized parked Battles is a
wish list, not a strategic choice.

## Source

> No leader should have more than two Wildly Important Goals at one
> time, no matter how big or how small the organization.
>
> — McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 2 (paraphrased — the cap)

> We're suggesting that your team narrow its focus to one or two WIGs.
> Total team WIGs should be 1, 2, or at most 3 — and 1-2 is preferred
> when the team is functionally unified.
>
> — *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 6 (P-26, Trap 1)

> The first lesson here is fundamental: the more you try to do, the
> less you actually accomplish.
>
> — *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 2

Porter's *Competitive Strategy* (Free Press, 1980) sharpens this in
strategy terms: "the essence of strategy is choosing what *not* to
do." A WIG is meaningful only because the actor has explicitly
de-prioritized the parked Battles.

## Why it matters

The cap is the load-bearing mechanism of D1:

1. **Whirlwind always wins capacity contests** — the daily operational
   demands consume ~60-80% of an individual's / team's bandwidth. Any
   strategic work fits into the remaining 20-40%. Trying to fit 5
   "WIGs" into 30% of capacity guarantees all 5 get half-done and the
   lag never moves.
2. **Trust currency runs on the cap** — when an individual or team
   names a WIG, the cap is what makes the commitment credible. "We
   have 7 priorities" means we have none.
3. **Multi-WIG drift (CE-03) is the most-warned failure mode in the
   book** — appears in Ch 2, Ch 6 (Trap 1), Ch 7, Ch 12. The book
   treats this as the single largest cause of D1 failure.
4. **The cap is calibrated to attention, not to ambition** — humans
   and teams can hold *attention* on at most 1-2 breakthrough goals
   simultaneously. Adding a 3rd doesn't add a 3rd lag-measure
   movement; it cuts attention to all three.

Approach B (twin Primary WIGs across genuinely separate segments) is
*not* an exception to the cap — it's a recognition that an org with
two structurally separate operating arms has, in effect, two teams,
each operating under one Primary WIG. From any single team's
perspective there is still only one WIG.

## Application across modes

| Mode | How this standard applies |
|---|---|
| **Personal-define** | Step 7 count test asks "how many WIGs are you trying to hold right now?" Push back hard if >2. The cap is the agent's authority to refuse to coach a 3-WIG slate — the user is asked to converge to one (or one + one independent secondary) before the protocol completes. |
| **Team-select** | Step 7 forces the Approach A (single Primary) vs B (twin Primary across segments) decision; B is structurally tempting because it "covers more" and must be justified by genuinely separate segments, not by inclusion-needs. Step 8 Trap 1 re-checks the cap (≤2 Primary WIGs total; ≤3 team WIGs ever). |
| **Member-comprehend** | Implicit. The member doesn't *set* the cap — but they should know it, so they can recognize when their team has over-committed (4+ "team WIGs" floating around) and either flag it as a clarifying question or treat all but the top 1-2 as deprioritized in their own slice work. |

Across all three scopes the cap is the same; what differs is *who
enforces it* (agent on user / leader on leadership team / member as
internal calibration) and *what the consequence of breach is* (refuse
to coach forward / return to step 6 / raise as clarifying question
upward).
