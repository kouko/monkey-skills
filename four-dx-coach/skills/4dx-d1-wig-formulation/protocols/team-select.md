# Protocol: Team-Select — Pick the team's Primary WIG via Battles 2x2

> Loaded by SKILL.md orchestrator when scope detection identifies a
> team leader picking the team's Primary WIG from candidate Battles.
> Agent voice = **consultant-to-leader**: facilitates the leader's
> own brainstorming + 2x2 + convergence; does not pick the WIG, does
> not address the team directly.

## R — Reading (verbatim source quote)

> The first question to ask in your brainstorming is "If every other
> area of our operation remained at its current level of performance,
> in which *one area* would we most want to achieve significant
> results?" … This exercise is very insightful because it forces your
> team to assess each candidate WIG on the two axes of "Impact of
> Failure" and "At Risk of Failure (Without Significant Change)."
>
> — McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 6 ("Identifying Your Breakthrough Result")

## I — Interpretation (mode-specific)

A Primary WIG is the team's central breakthrough — the one place where
disproportionate energy will be spent. Ch 6 frames the selection task
as: the leadership team brainstorms a list of **candidate Battles**
(possible breakthrough goals), then filters them through a 2x2 to
converge on one.

The book's canonical axes are Impact-of-Failure × At-Risk-of-Failure.
For a team-leader workshop the equivalent everyday framing is
**Importance** (would a breakthrough here transform the team's
situation?) × **Feasibility** (can the team meaningfully move this in
1-2 years through its own behavior change, not external authority?).
Both framings target the same selection logic: the WIG must be
*mission-critical* AND *at genuine risk without behavior change* —
neither stroke-of-the-pen nor already-handled-by-the-whirlwind.

The 2x2 segments candidates into four cells:

| | High Feasibility | Low Feasibility |
|---|---|---|
| **High Importance** | **Primary WIG candidates** (upper-right) | Parked — important but team can't move it; flag for leadership escalation |
| **Low Importance** | Whirlwind / nice-to-have — handle in normal ops, don't WIG | Drop |

Three structural patterns (Ch 6):

- **Approach A — Single Primary WIG** (default). Covenant Transport
  picked driver turnover (106→86%) over on-time / safety / shipments
  because turnover had the *ripple effect* of moving the others.
- **Approach B — Twin Primary WIGs across segments**. A pharma giant
  picked "molecule to market" + "increase access to drugs" because the
  org served two distinct segments. Use only when the team genuinely
  has two non-overlapping operational arms.
- **Approach C — Strategy-line as quasi-Primary-WIG**. TELUS
  International set 4 strategic objectives and let 400+ teams
  self-select. Use only at large scale; not appropriate for a single
  team.

Voice contrast: agent is **consultant-to-leader** — does not address
the team directly; whispers to the leader. Output is leader-facing
prompts, scoring frames, and decision rules. The leader runs the
brainstorming session with the leadership team.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Covenant Transport — driver turnover ripple effect (Ch 6, Approach A)
- Leadership team had multiple worthy candidates: on-time delivery, total shipments, traffic safety, driver turnover. Picking by importance alone produced a tie.
- Mapped candidates on Impact × At-Risk; selected driver turnover (106→86% by Dec 31) because fixing turnover had the *ripple effect* of cascading positively into the others. Every team could meaningfully contribute.
- Outcome: surpassed goal (reached 82% turnover — lowest in company history); produced best financial results in twelve years.
- Lesson: single Primary WIG (Approach A) wins when one candidate's victory cascades into the others.

### Case 2: Sydney accounting firm — "go smaller" rescue (Ch 6 follow-on)
- Firm set "total revenue" as Primary WIG. After a quarter of zero traction, the team felt the WIG was simultaneously vague, mission-disconnected, and contaminated by every stroke-of-pen + whirlwind activity. Trap #2 (too broad) had bitten.
- Re-ran the 2x2 with smaller-scope candidates. Demoted "total revenue" *out* of WIG slot and re-promoted it to a strategy-line. Picked a narrower Battle one level down (specific segment / service-line growth metric) the team could actually own and move.
- Outcome: re-scoped WIG produced traction within the next cycle; revenue continued as monitored metric without WIG-grade discipline.
- Lesson: when a Primary WIG is too broad, the rescue is *going smaller*, not adjusting time horizon.

### Case 3: Pharma giant — twin Primary WIGs across segments (Ch 6, Approach B)
- Twelve strategic imperatives across two structurally different segments (R&D pipeline + commercial access). Selecting a single Primary WIG would force one segment to wait.
- Acknowledged the two-segment structure. Picked two Primary WIGs: "molecule to market" (Transform WIG, R&D segment) + "increase access to their drugs" (Perform WIG, commercial segment). From any single team's perspective there was still only one WIG.
- Lesson: Approach B is justified only when an organization has genuinely separate operating segments.

### Case 4: Thrift-store chain — mission-alignment recovery (Ch 6 sidebar)
- New president inherited a baffling range of WIG candidates. Reframed the selection question against the mission ("promote self-reliance among workers with disabilities"). Converged on increasing the number of workers with disabilities placed in sustainable jobs *outside* the organization.
- Lesson: mission-alignment test (Trap #4) is the final filter — among 2x2 high-and-right candidates, prefer the one most directly serving mission.

## A2 — Future Trigger

When a team leader needs this protocol:

1. Team has not yet been handed a cascaded org WIG and the leader is choosing the team's own Primary WIG from scratch.
2. Leader has 5+ "important" strategic candidates and cannot converge.
3. Leader's current Primary WIG feels too broad ("total revenue", "operational excellence") and is producing no traction — needs to *go smaller*.
4. Leader is debating Approach A vs B (one Primary WIG vs two across segments) and needs the structural call.
5. Leadership team is comfortable ranking *importance* but skipping the *feasibility / at-risk* axis — needs forcing onto the 2x2.

## E — Execution

Agent runs a **consultant-to-leader dialogue**. Walk the leader through brainstorm → 2x2 placement → convergence. **Do not pick the WIG for them — facilitate the leadership team's pick.**

1. **Frame the team's mission and strategic context.**
   - Ask: "Briefly — what is your team's mission, and what's the strategic context for this WIG cycle? (1-2 sentences each.) Is there an org-level WIG above you, or are you the top of the chain for this team?"
   - Completion: mission stated; strategic context noted; cascade-vs-selection confirmed (if cascade, redirect to `4dx-d1-team-wig-cascade`).

2. **Brainstorm 5-10 candidate Battles.**
   - Ask: "If every other area of your team's operation stayed at its current level of performance, in which *one area* would a breakthrough matter most? Generate 5-10 candidates — go broad, no filtering yet. Each should be a possible breakthrough outcome, not an activity."
   - Completion: ≥5 written candidates, each phrased as a breakthrough outcome.
   - Halt: if user produces only 1-2 candidates, push back: "Generate at least 5. Premature convergence kills the 2x2."

3. **Score each candidate on Importance (1-5).**
   - Ask: "For each candidate: if you achieved a breakthrough on this, would your team's situation be *transformed* (5), *significantly improved* (3-4), or just *incrementally better* (1-2)? Score each 1-5 with a one-line justification."
   - Completion: every candidate has an Importance score.

4. **Score each candidate on Feasibility (1-5).**
   - Ask: "For each candidate: in 1-2 years, can the team meaningfully move the needle on this *through behavior change you control*, not via external authority or budget? 5 = team owns ≥80% of the lever. 1 = mostly external. Score 1-5."
   - Completion: every candidate has a Feasibility score. Forcing this axis is the value of the exercise — leaders skip it.

5. **Plot the 2x2 (text grid is fine).**
   - Render the 2x2 as a labeled text grid; place each candidate by (Importance, Feasibility). Example:
     ```
     High Imp │  [parked]    │  [WIG candidates]
              │              │
     Low Imp  │  [drop]      │  [whirlwind / nice-to-haves]
              └──────────────┴────────────────────────
                Low Feas       High Feas
     ```
   - Completion: visual placement done; user can see clusters.

6. **Filter to upper-right (WIG candidates).**
   - Ask: "Of the candidates in the upper-right cell (high importance × high feasibility), which one — if won alone — would have the greatest *ripple effect* on the others? (Covenant Transport: turnover lifted on-time delivery + safety + financials simultaneously.)"
   - Completion: one candidate identified as ripple-effect leader, OR honest acknowledgement that two distinct segments require Approach B.

7. **Single vs twin Primary WIG decision (Approach A vs B).** — see `../standards/wig-discipline.md`
   - Ask: "Does your team operate as one functionally-unified group, or does it have two structurally separate segments (e.g. R&D pipeline + commercial access; build-side + run-side)? Approach A = one Primary WIG (default). Approach B = two Primary WIGs, one per segment, only if segments are genuinely non-overlapping. Reject Approach B if you're tempted just to make people feel included."
   - Completion: Approach A or B chosen with rationale. Total team WIGs cap = ≤3, with 1 (Approach A) preferred and 2 (Approach B) acceptable for genuinely segmented teams.

8. **Four-trap checklist (Ch 6 hard gate).**
   - Run each candidate Primary WIG against:
     - Trap 1 — too many? (≤2 Primary WIGs total; ≤3 team WIGs ever)
     - Trap 2 — too broad? (no "total revenue", "operational excellence", "overall market share")
     - Trap 3 — aspirational but unmeasurable? (must pass *From X to Y by When*)
     - Trap 4 — disconnected from mission? (must serve mission stated in step 1)
   - Halt: any trap triggered → return to step 6 with narrower / more measurable / mission-aligned candidate.

9. **Ladder-up check.** — see `../standards/ladder-up-test.md`
   - Ask: "If there is an org WIG above this team, does the candidate Primary WIG demonstrably *serve* it? Could you draw a one-sentence line of sight from this team WIG up to the org WIG? If not, the candidate is misaligned upward — return to step 6."
   - If this team is at the top of the chain, the ladder-up check defers to mission alignment (Trap 4).

10. **Express in *From X to Y by When* form.** — see `../standards/from-x-to-y-by-when-grammar.md`
    - For each chosen Primary WIG, write the lag-measure finish line: **"From [X numeric current] to [Y numeric target] by [calendar date]"**.
    - Completion: each Primary WIG passes the *stranger test* — "could a stranger tell on the deadline whether the team won or lost?"

11. **Output: WIG + parked Battles list.**
    - Final output: (a) 1-2 Primary WIGs in *From X to Y by When* form; (b) parked Battles list — high-importance / low-feasibility candidates dropped from this cycle, kept visible; (c) explicit Approach declaration (A or B); (d) confirmation the four traps clear and the WIG is mission-aligned.
    - Hand-off: if leader now needs to translate this Primary WIG down to sub-team Battles → `4dx-d1-team-wig-cascade`. If they need lead measures next → D2.

## B — Boundary (mode-specific)

### Do NOT use this protocol for:
- **Solo / individual goal definition** — load `protocols/personal-define.md`. The 2x2 is a leadership-team artifact; running it alone collapses to ranking-by-vibes.
- **Team-member-level WIG comprehension** — load `protocols/member-comprehend.md`. This skill is for the leader making the pick, not the people receiving it.
- **Cascading an already-set org Primary WIG** — translation, not selection. Hand off to `4dx-d1-team-wig-cascade`.
- **Stroke-of-pen organizational decisions** — restructuring, vendor switch, hiring decision. Strategy Map's *left column* by definition does not need 4DX.
- **Reactive / on-call team domains** — SRE incident teams, ER medicine, customer support — where the whirlwind *is* the strategic work.
- **Pre-strategy organizations** — if the team has no defined mission/vision yet, Trap 4 cannot be evaluated. Define mission first; then return.

### Author-warned failure modes (Ch 6 four traps + verified-team additions)
- **Trap 1 — too many Primary WIGs (CE-03 / P-26)**: Most seductive, most destructive. Cap is 2 Primary WIGs (only via Approach B with genuinely separate segments).
- **Trap 2 — Primary WIG too broad (CE-04 / P-26)**: "Total revenue", "operational excellence", "overall market share". Rescue: go smaller (Sydney accounting case).
- **Trap 3 — aspirational but unmeasurable (CE-07 / P-26)**: "Become the preferred provider", "lead the industry in innovation". Must pass *From X to Y by When*.
- **Trap 4 — disconnected from mission (P-45 / P-26)**: Measurable + feasible + impactful but mission-disconnected = "achieves goals, loses soul." Always re-test against mission.
- **P-44 — clarity sacrificed for inclusion**: Broadening the Primary WIG so every member feels "included" undermines focus.
- **Most-important confusion (CE-05)**: Picking by ranking strategic categories ("growth > retention > efficiency") instead of asking "where would breakthrough have biggest leverage?"

### Author's blind spots
- **Selection bias (CE-30)**: All FranklinCovey case studies are paying clients; failed Primary WIG choices are rarely analyzed.
- **Goal-discovery vs goal-execution**: Ch 6 assumes the right Primary WIG is *discoverable*. In genuinely uncertain domains, run small experiments first.
- **High-and-right is not automatic**: Ch 6 explicitly warns: resources, ripple effect, and mission alignment can override 2x2 placement.
- **Approach B over-application**: structurally tempting because it "covers more." Justified only by genuinely separate segments.

### Easily-confused neighbours
- **OKRs** — allow 3-5 objectives × 3-5 KRs = up to 25 simultaneous metrics. WIG protocol caps at 1-2 Primary WIGs total per team.
- **Hoshin Kanri X-matrix** — similar deployment logic but tolerates broader objectives at the top. WIG demands the four-trap discipline (especially Trap 2).
- **BSC strategic objectives** — closer to Approach C than Approach A. 4DX collapses to fewer because focus dilution costs exceed perspective coverage benefits.
- **SMART goals (team variant)** — surface form looks identical but SMART permits incremental "achievable" goals.
