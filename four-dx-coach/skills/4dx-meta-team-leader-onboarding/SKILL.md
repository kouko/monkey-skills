# 4DX Meta — Team Leader Onboarding (Earning Commitment, Not Compliance)

## R — Reading

> "Had the leaders of leaders spent their energy trying to convince or
> control the resistant leaders of the frontline teams, they would have
> received begrudging compliance at best and open defiance at worst.
> Instead, they were now on their way to the most powerful outcome of all:
> willing commitment."
>
> — McChesney et al., *Chapter 8: Getting Your Leaders on Board*

> "The key to influence is first to be influenced."
>
> — McChesney et al., *Chapter 8* (understanding mindset)

> "You can veto, but not dictate."
>
> — McChesney et al., *Chapter 8* (involvement mindset, citing Chapter 2)

---

## I — Interpretation

4DX is an operating system, not a directive. Its weekly cadence runs on
**self-chosen commitments** made out loud at the WIG Session — not on tasks
assigned downward. That mechanism is structurally incompatible with
compliance: a frontline-team leader who attends the cadence under duress
will produce empty or safe commitments, the lead measures will not move,
and the lag measure will stall. The book frames the leader-of-leaders' job
not as selling 4DX but as *creating the conditions under which their
direct-report leaders choose it*.

The book's three mindsets — **transparency** (share what was rejected and
what's still uncertain), **understanding** (be influenceable before you ask
to influence), **involvement** (final WIG belongs to you; Team WIG belongs
to them) — are the upstream conditions. The five-step process (ensure
understanding → answer clarifying questions → hear feedback → decide →
hand the Team WIG back to them) is the downstream choreography. Both fail
without the mindset; the mindset fails without the choreography.

For a coach engaging a single leader-of-leaders, the work is preparatory,
not a workshop facilitation. The leader has not yet had the conversation;
they are anticipating it, often with anxiety about specific named
direct reports who they expect to resist. This skill walks them through
naming those leaders, steel-manning each one's strongest objection,
distinguishing pilot-with-the-willing from hold-back-for-now, and
rehearsing the actual conversation for the single highest-leverage case.
The output is a plan they can act on this week — not a rollout document.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Marriott — 70K-leader rollout via voluntary pilot, not mandate
- **Problem**: Marriott considered rolling 4DX across its global property
  network of ~70,000 leaders. A top-down corporate mandate would have
  triggered exactly the "another corporate program" reflex the book warns
  about.
- **Methodology applied**: The rollout began at properties whose general
  managers volunteered (Spartanburg Marriott was among the early willing
  cases — already engaged in a behavioral-change-shaped guest-satisfaction
  problem). Other properties watched results before opting in. The leaders
  who moved first did so because *their property's* outcome required it,
  not because Bethesda HQ said to.
- **Conclusion**: Voluntary opt-in pilots produce evidence; evidence
  produces pull; pull beats push. Mandating 4DX across all properties at
  once would have produced compliance theater.
- **Outcome**: 4DX adoption diffused across Marriott's properties; case
  studies from the early willing sites became the recruiting argument for
  later sites. The book's "leader-of-leaders" chapters are largely
  retrospective reflection on this diffusion pattern.

### Case 2: Georgia child welfare — frontline leaders chose Team WIGs
- **Problem**: Beverly Walker (foreword author) led a department of ~20K
  caseworkers across regional offices. Each regional office had its own
  director — leaders of frontline teams from her vantage. Repeat
  child-abuse incidents needed to drop, and she could not personally
  dictate caseworker behavior at scale.
- **Methodology applied**: Walker named the Primary WIG at the agency
  level (reduce repeat incidents), let the regional directors choose their
  own Team WIGs aligned to it (e.g. specific home-visit cadence, specific
  follow-up protocols). She vetoed only when a Team WIG genuinely did not
  ladder up — and even then, sent it back for a second draft rather than
  rewriting it herself.
- **Conclusion**: When regional directors *chose* the Team WIG, the daily
  caseworker behavior change downstream had organizational legitimacy.
  When earlier reorgs had imposed protocols top-down, behavior reverted.
- **Outcome**: Repeat child-abuse incidents declined across the state.
  The pattern Walker describes — "We were partners, not subordinates" — is
  the book's clearest example of the involvement mindset producing
  structural results in a politically sensitive public-sector domain.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. **Pre-cascade anxiety** — User has decided 4DX is the right operating
   system for their org, drafted a Primary WIG and Key Battle WIGs, and
   now faces the conversation with their direct-report leaders. They are
   stuck on "how do I introduce this without it sounding like another
   corporate initiative?"
2. **Anticipated specific resister** — User can name 1-3 direct reports
   who they expect will push back, and wants to think through each one's
   objection before the meeting.
3. **Failed-prior-rollout rebound** — User has previously launched OKRs,
   balanced scorecards, or other frameworks that fizzled at the leader
   level. They suspect the failure was social, not technical, and wants
   to avoid the same trap.
4. **Pilot-vs-org-wide decision** — User is debating whether to mandate
   4DX across all teams simultaneously or start with a willing subset.
5. **Compliance-vs-commitment self-check** — User notices they are
   rehearsing arguments for why 4DX is the right answer and wants to
   pressure-test whether they are about to advocate when they should be
   inviting.

### Non-activation signals (do NOT trigger when…)

- User has not yet decided 4DX fits — route to
  `4dx-meta-strategy-triage` first.
- Buy-in is already secured and the user is asking *how to actually run
  the cascade meeting / draft Team WIGs* — route to
  `4dx-d1-wig-cascade` (the technical cascade).
- User is a frontline-team leader trying to get *their own team members*
  bought in — different scale; the book treats individual contributors
  via the Team WIG-choosing process at D1, not Chapter 8.
- User wants generic change-management coaching unrelated to 4DX — out of
  scope; recommend Kotter / ADKAR / similar.

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-meta-strategy-triage`: that skill asks *"is 4DX the right
  fit for our org?"*; this skill assumes fit-for-org is settled and asks
  *"how do I earn commitment from the specific humans who will run it?"*
- vs. `4dx-d1-wig-cascade`: that skill is the **technical** cascade
  (Primary WIG → Key Battles → Team WIGs as a structural exercise); this
  skill is the **social** precondition that makes the technical cascade
  produce commitment instead of compliance.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent is a **consultant to a single
leader-of-leaders**, walking them through buy-in preparation Socratically.
The deliverable is a per-leader plan plus one rehearsed conversation,
not a rollout doc.

1. **Confirm the precondition.**
   - Ask: *"Have you decided 4DX is the right operating system for your
     org? Do you have a draft Primary WIG and Key Battle WIGs you're
     willing to share with your direct reports?"*
   - Completion criterion: yes to both.
   - **Halt condition**: if no to fit-decision → route to
     `4dx-meta-strategy-triage`. If no to draft WIGs → tell the user
     they need a starting draft before this conversation can be useful;
     pause until they have one.

2. **Name the leaders, with skepticism level.**
   - Ask: *"Who specifically needs to be on board for this to work?
     List 3-5 by name. For each, give me a skepticism level: enthusiast /
     neutral / skeptic / hard-no. Don't strategize yet — just inventory."*
   - Completion criterion: a named list of 3-5 leaders, each tagged with
     a skepticism level. Vague answers ("all the regional managers") get
     a follow-up: *"Pick the three whose buy-in matters most."*

3. **For each leader: what do they specifically want?**
   - Ask, leader by leader: *"What does [Leader X] want for their team
     that 4DX could plausibly serve? Not what you want from them — what
     they would say their team's biggest unmet goal is."*
   - Completion criterion: a one-sentence outcome each leader cares about,
     framed in their voice. If the user can only describe what *they*
     want from the leader, surface this gap explicitly: *"You haven't
     described it from their angle yet — try again from their seat."*
   - **Heuristic**: if no leader has an outcome 4DX could plausibly serve,
     the issue may be upstream — flag it, but do not abandon the skill.

4. **Steel-man each leader's strongest objection.**
   - Ask, leader by leader: *"What's the strongest version of [Leader X]'s
     objection? Not the weakest — the one you'd find hardest to answer.
     'We already have OKRs', 'we're too busy', 'this is corporate flavor
     of the month', 'my team won't accept another framework' — pick the
     one that fits, or invent a stronger one."*
   - Completion criterion: each leader has a steel-manned objection in
     full sentences, not a label.
   - **Constraint**: do not let the user counter-argue yet. The point of
     this step is to prove they can articulate the objection well enough
     that the leader would recognize themselves in it. If the user
     immediately pivots to rebuttal, redirect: *"Steel-man first.
     Counter-arguments come later, if at all."*

5. **Pilot vs. hold-back classification.**
   - Walk the named leaders into three buckets:
     - **Voluntary pilot candidate** — enthusiast or neutral whose team
       has a 4DX-shaped problem (behavioral change required, not
       stroke-of-the-pen). Invite first; let early wins do the talking.
     - **Hold back for now** — skeptic or hard-no whose team's outcome
       does not yet visibly require 4DX. Do not recruit them. Let pilot
       evidence accumulate first.
     - **Engage but don't push** — neutral who is curious but
       unconvinced. Bring them into the clarifying-questions phase
       (book Step 2) without pressure to commit.
   - Completion criterion: every named leader is in exactly one bucket.
   - **Anti-pattern flag**: if the user puts a hard-no into the pilot
     bucket "to win them over", flag this — the book warns that
     conscripting resisters into pilots produces visible failure and
     poisons the rollout.

6. **Rehearse the conversation for ONE leader.**
   - Pick the highest-leverage case (usually a skeptic whose team has a
     clearly 4DX-shaped problem, or a neutral who anchors a peer group).
   - Walk the user through a script outline:
     - **Acknowledge the skepticism by name.** *"You've seen initiatives
       come and go. This one might or might not stick — let me show you
       what's different and you tell me if it lands."*
     - **Frame as their problem, not yours.** *"Your team's [outcome they
       care about] hasn't moved despite [previous attempt]. Here's an
       operating system for that specific gap."*
     - **Show the involvement boundary.** *"The Primary WIG is mine to
       finalize. The Team WIG is yours. I can veto, but I won't dictate."*
     - **Don't sell 4DX, sell the outcome.** *"What would it look like if
       your team hit [their outcome] by [horizon]? If that's compelling,
       we can talk about how. If it's not, we've found a different
       conversation to have."*
     - **Offer voluntary opt-in.** *"You don't have to decide today. Two
       other teams are piloting starting [date]. If you want to watch
       first and join later, that's a real option."*
   - Completion criterion: the user has spoken (or written) the script in
     their own voice, not just nodded at the outline. Ask them to say it
     out loud or type the exact words.

7. **Output a per-leader plan + rehearsed script.**
   - Plan structure:
     ```
     LEADER       SKEPTICISM   THEIR OUTCOME      STEEL-MAN OBJECTION   BUCKET
     [Name 1]     enthusiast   [outcome]          [objection]           pilot
     [Name 2]     skeptic      [outcome]          [objection]           hold-back
     ...
     ```
   - Plus the rehearsed conversation script for one leader, in the user's
     own words.
   - Completion criterion: both artifacts produced; user is ready to have
     the conversation this week.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Pre-fit-decision contexts.** If the user has not decided 4DX is the
  right operating system, this skill skips the structural triage and
  jumps straight to social rollout — wrong order. Route to
  `4dx-meta-strategy-triage` first.
- **Frontline-team-member buy-in.** This skill is leader-of-leaders →
  leaders-of-frontline-teams. For an individual contributor's buy-in,
  the book relies on the D1 Team WIG-choosing process, not Chapter 8.
- **Generic change management.** If the work is not 4DX-specific (e.g. a
  reorg, a tool migration, a culture shift), use Kotter / ADKAR / Bridges
  or a domain-team change-management skill.

### If you have positional authority and you're not actually trying for genuine commitment

This skill won't help. If the underlying intent is "I have the authority
to mandate this, I just want a softer-sounding script for the mandate",
then what you'll get is **compliance** — the leaders will attend WIG
Sessions, fill in scoreboards, and produce safe commitments — and 4DX
won't take root. The cadence is structurally dependent on self-chosen
commitments at the WIG Session; coerced commitments are empty by
construction. If you're not actually willing to let a hold-back leader
say "no, my team isn't ready", stop here and decide whether you want
4DX or you want compliance with 4DX-shaped paperwork. They are not the
same outcome.

### Author-warned failure modes

- **CE-32 — Advocacy disguised as transparency.** Leaders presenting
  draft WIGs while emphasizing "these are critical" and "the only
  effective option" produce the opposite of transparency. The book
  explicitly warns: don't let strong desire for unity slip into forced
  advocacy.
- **CE-33 — Skipping clarifying questions to save time.** Conflating
  Step 2 (clarifying questions) and Step 3 (feedback) shortcuts the
  process. The book reports that thorough Step 2 reduces Step 3 effort —
  the inverse of the intuition.
- **CE-34 — Premature enthusiasm expectation.** Expecting leaders of
  frontline teams to be enthusiastic immediately after the final WIG is
  presented. The book: "It's too early to expect or seek enthusiasm at
  this point. The leaders of frontline teams have a lot to think about."
- **CE-35 — Dictating a Team WIG.** Even subtle preference-signaling
  ("I think your team should focus on X") collapses the involvement
  mindset. "You can veto, but not dictate" is binary; near-dictation is
  dictation.
- **CE-36 — Conscripting resisters into pilots.** Adding a hard-no leader
  to the first pilot wave to "win them over" produces visible failure
  that poisons later recruitment. Pilot with the willing.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations

- The book's voluntary-opt-in pattern was developed in
  US-anglophone-corporate settings where direct disagreement is socially
  permissible. In high-context cultures (JP, ZH, KR), a "neutral"
  response often masks dissent; the agent should probe more carefully
  before classifying a leader as neutral.
- Chapter 8 assumes the leader-of-leaders has already done the
  intellectual work of Primary WIG drafting; it offers little for leaders
  who realize mid-conversation that their Primary WIG is wrong. In that
  case, the right move is to pause the rollout, not to push through.
- Remote / hybrid orgs lose much of the in-room small-group dynamic the
  book leans on for Steps 2 and 3. Asynchronous clarifying-question
  channels are an unaddressed gap.

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **Kotter 8-step / ADKAR** — broad change management; not 4DX-specific
  and lacks the cadence-of-accountability mechanism that makes Chapter 8
  load-bearing.
- **OKR rollout playbooks** (Doerr) — assume top-down quarterly cycle
  with consensus as the goal; 4DX explicitly rejects voting / consensus
  on Primary WIGs.
- **Servant leadership / Theory Y** — share the "be influenced first"
  mindset; lack the structural artifact (Team WIG ownership) that
  operationalizes it.

### NOT for: solo / personal goals

NOT for solo or personal goals. This skill assumes a leader-of-leaders
context with named direct-report leaders. For personal-scale 4DX
adoption, use the personal-coach skills (`4dx-meta-strategy-triage`
and downstream).

### Industry-experience addendum (why imposed WIGs collapse)

Chapter 8 describes the voluntary-opt-in pattern behaviorally; two primary
sources explain *why* the rules are load-bearing. Citations in
`references/industry-grounding.md`:

- **Self-Determination Theory** (Ryan & Deci 2017, Guilford) — three
  basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, relatedness. A
  dictated WIG thwarts autonomy directly; SDT predicts compliance
  rather than commitment. Skipping clarifying questions (CE-33)
  thwarts competence — the leader can't succeed without information
  and knows it. Public veto in front of peers thwarts relatedness in
  high-context cultures (face-loss).
- **Argyris, "Teaching Smart People How to Learn"** (*HBR* 1991) —
  highly skilled professionals fall into **defensive reasoning** when
  the frame itself is challenged; only **double-loop learning**
  (questioning the frame, not just correcting errors within it) opens
  genuine commitment. Step 2 (clarifying questions) is the
  double-loop opener. Skipping it pushes leaders into single-loop mode
  and produces shallow safe commitments that look like buy-in.

Implication: the book's "veto, not dictate" rule is an autonomy-preservation
rule; the clarifying-questions step is a double-loop opener. Both are
mechanism, not ceremony.
