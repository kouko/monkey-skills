# 4dx-d3-member-scoreboard-reading — Read the Team Scoreboard You've Been Handed

## R — Reading

> If you can't tell within five seconds whether you're winning or losing, you haven't passed this test.
>
> — McChesney et al., *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 4 (compelling players' scoreboard, criterion 4)

---

## I — Interpretation

The book's scoreboard rubric is written for the leader designing the artifact. The same four criteria — simple, visible, shows lead + lag with where-you-should-be, 5-second winning test — read perfectly well as a member's *diagnostic checklist* on a scoreboard they did not build.

A team member standing in front of an existing team scoreboard has a narrower job than the leader: not "is this the right scoreboard?" but **"can I extract signal from this scoreboard, this week, about whether my work is lifting the lead my team chose?"** That's a focused diagnostic, not a redesign exercise.

Five things have to be true at once for the read to work:

1. **The scoreboard passes the 5-second test for the member.** If a member glances and can't tell "we're winning or losing," the artifact is failing — and the failure is the leader's to fix, not the member's to compensate for. The member's job is to *notice* and surface.

2. **The member can locate their own contribution.** For each lead on the board, where does the member's daily work plug in? If the answer is "I have no idea," that's an upstream-D2 gap (route to influence-mapping), not a scoreboard problem.

3. **The member can read the trend.** Status alone isn't a read; status-vs-pacing-line is. If the lead bar moved last week and not this week, why? The member traces it back to *their own* per-lead activity in the same window.

4. **The member spots calibration drift.** Scoreboards die slowly. A week-three pacing line that was set against week-one assumptions can quietly become wrong. The member, who works the lead daily, often sees the drift before the leader does.

5. **The member surfaces breakage.** If the scoreboard fails the 5-second test for the *team* (not just for the member), or if the pacing line has clearly drifted, the member raises it — this is psychological-safety dependent and the protocol gives a non-confrontational script.

This skill is not "design my own scoreboard." It is "read the one you already have, weekly, in under 5 minutes, before any commitment-making." Small in scope by design — most of its use overlaps with d4-member skills which consume this read.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Younger Brothers — six observable safety leads, posted on-site (Ch. 13 / Ch. 14, member-side mirror)
- **Problem (leader-side as written)**: Six safety-compliance behaviors were displayed as a visible team scoreboard so any worker on any crew could see the running compliance rate per lead.
- **Member-side read**: A framer glancing at the board could tell within seconds whether the crew was winning on "scaffolding compliance" — and could trace yesterday's tick to a specific shift action ("I rebuilt that brace before lunch"). The member's read connects board state to personal contribution without needing the leader to interpret.
- **Conclusion**: A scoreboard that supports member-side contribution-tracing is doing real work; one that doesn't, isn't.
- **Outcome**: The 57 → 12 serious-incidents drop is leader-attributed in the book, but the daily mechanism — frontline workers checking the board, locating their own move, adjusting — is the symmetric member-side read this skill codifies.

### Case 2: Towne Park valet — retrieval-time board (Ch. 3 / Ch. 4)
- **Problem (leader-side as written)**: Retrieval time was the lead, posted at the valet stand for all on shift.
- **Member-side read**: A valet glancing at the board mid-shift could tell whether the team was on pace, and could ask themselves "did my last retrieval add or subtract from the running average?" That self-question is only possible because the board passes the 5-second test *and* the valet knows where their own work plugs in.
- **Conclusion**: One valet's identification of the wall-bottleneck shortcut came from reading the board against their own lived shift, not from leader prompting. The board enabled the read; the read enabled the move.
- **Outcome**: The case is told in the book as a lead-measure success and as a leader-side scoreboard-design success; the member-side reading — that the valet was doing a per-shift contribution audit against a visible board — is the implicit mechanism this skill makes explicit.

### Case 3: Northrop Grumman — the blown-down stadium analogy (Ch. 4)
- **Problem**: The author's diagnostic image — fans go silent when the stadium scoreboard blows down because no one can tell the score — applies symmetrically. A member on a team whose scoreboard has gone hidden, decorative, or stale is the silent fan.
- **Member-side read**: When the member can't read the score, the right move is not "guess harder." It is to surface back: "I came to read our scoreboard for prep and I couldn't tell if we're winning. Can we look at it together?"
- **Conclusion**: The member's inability to read a scoreboard is *signal about the scoreboard*, not a competence problem. This skill protects that signal.
- **Outcome**: The book uses the analogy to motivate leader-side design; the member-side use — as a diagnostic for "should I escalate?" — is the mirror move this skill installs.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. The user is on a 4DX team with a scoreboard already designed by the leader, and wants to know how to actually read it before a WIG Session, before writing a commitment, or as a weekly check-in.
2. The user looks at the team scoreboard and can't tell within 5 seconds whether the team is winning — and wants to know whether that's their own gap or a real scoreboard problem.
3. The user can read the scoreboard fine but doesn't know where their own work shows up on it — they want to locate their personal contribution to specific leads.
4. The user notices the scoreboard's pacing line / target line seems wrong relative to current reality (decorative, stale) and wants a structured way to confirm and surface.
5. The user is about to prep a D4 commitment but realizes they haven't read the scoreboard first — they want a quick scoreboard read before commitment-prep.
6. The user missed a commitment and wants to use the scoreboard to figure out what happened — but this is debrief territory, not pure reading. (See Boundary: route to d4-member-account-debrief.)

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d3-team-lead-scoreboard-design`: that skill produces a scoreboard; this skill reads one already produced.
- vs. `4dx-d3-personal-scoreboard`: that skill designs a solo scoreboard. This skill reads an inherited team scoreboard.
- vs. `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`: prep *uses* a fresh scoreboard read as input. This skill produces the read. They compose; if the user is preparing a commitment, route there and let it call this skill upstream if needed.
- vs. `4dx-d4-member-account-debrief`: debrief *uses* the scoreboard to diagnose a missed commitment. This skill produces a clean read of the board; debrief layers backward causal reasoning on top.
- vs. `4dx-d2-member-lead-measure-influence`: that skill maps personal influence per lead. This skill reads the scoreboard's display of those same leads. If the member doesn't yet know their per-lead influence, route there first — reading without an influence map produces a passive read.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, run a brief Socratic read — typically under 5 minutes for a known scoreboard. **Do not redesign the scoreboard.** If the artifact is broken, surface and route, do not patch.

1. **Comprehension gate — confirm the scoreboard exists and is accessible.**
   - Ask: "Where is your team's scoreboard, and can you see it right now? Wall, screen, doc, app, channel-pinned?"
   - Completion criterion: member names the medium and confirms current visibility.
   - Halt condition: if the team has *no* scoreboard, this is a leader-side gap. Output: "Your team is missing D3. Surface to your leader and route them to `4dx-d3-team-lead-scoreboard-design` (or share that skill name if appropriate). Member-side reading is impossible without an artifact." Halt skill.

2. **5-second test (member-side).**
   - Ask the member to glance at the scoreboard *now* and answer in five seconds: "Are we winning or losing on the team WIG?"
   - Completion criterion: clear yes/no answer, OR explicit "I can't tell."
   - Branch: if "I can't tell" → flag as **scoreboard-fails-5-second-test** and continue (the failure is data; do not abort).

3. **Locate-my-contribution per lead.**
   - For each lead displayed on the scoreboard, ask: "Where does *your* work plug into this lead? In one sentence."
   - Completion criterion: a one-line answer per lead, or an explicit "I don't know" per lead.
   - Halt condition: if the member can't locate themselves on *any* lead, this is a D2 influence-mapping gap. Route to `4dx-d2-member-lead-measure-influence` and stop. Reading without an influence map is passive.

4. **Trend read — week-over-week, per focus lead.**
   - For the member's 1-2 focus leads (from D2 influence mapping), read the displayed trend: "Did this lead move last week vs the prior week? Up, flat, or down? Is it on or off the pacing line / target line?"
   - Then: "What did *you* do on this lead in that window? Does your activity plausibly explain the movement?"
   - Completion criterion: per focus lead, a one-line trend statement plus a one-line member-activity statement.

5. **Calibration check — has the scoreboard drifted?**
   - Ask: "Looking at the pacing line / target / where-we-should-be marker — does it still match current reality? Was it set weeks ago against assumptions that no longer hold? (Examples: target was set before headcount changed; lag-measure baseline is from before the pricing change; lead-measure definition changed in practice but not on the board.)"
   - Completion criterion: explicit yes (still calibrated) / no (drifted, with a one-line why) / not sure.
   - If drifted or not sure: flag as **calibration-drift-suspected**. The member does not fix this — the leader does.

6. **Decide: reading complete, or escalate?**
   - If steps 2-5 produced clean answers (5-sec passes, contribution located, trend readable, calibration intact): the read is complete. Move to step 7.
   - If any flag fired (5-second-fails, calibration-drift, hidden-board, stale-board): move to step 6a.

   **6a. Escalation script (if a flag fired).**
   - Help the member draft a short, non-confrontational ask to the leader. Template:
     > "I came to read our scoreboard before [WIG Session / commitment prep / weekly check-in]. I noticed [X — e.g., I can't tell from a glance whether we're winning / the pacing line looks stale relative to the headcount change / the lead-measure definition we're posting doesn't match what we're actually counting]. Can we look at it together for 10 minutes? I'd rather flag it than guess."
   - Completion criterion: member confirms they will send the ask (timing: before next WIG Session is the default).
   - Note: this step depends on psychological safety. If the member says "I can't surface this to my leader," that is itself a signal to capture in the output card — do not push.

7. **Output the read card.**
   - Write back to the user, in their own words:
     - **Scoreboard medium / location**: …
     - **5-second read**: winning / losing / can't tell
     - **My contribution per lead**: lead 1 → …, lead 2 → …
     - **Focus-lead trend** (last week vs prior): up / flat / down; on / off pace
     - **My activity in that window plausibly explains the trend?**: yes / no / unclear
     - **Calibration status**: intact / drifted / unsure
     - **Flags raised**: (5-sec-fails / calibration-drift / hidden / stale / none)
     - **Escalation drafted?**: yes / no / blocked-on-safety
   - Completion criterion: member confirms the card.
   - Hand-off: "Carry this card into `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep` (forward) or `4dx-d4-member-account-debrief` (backward). The read is the input, not the output."

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Scoreboard *design* contexts.** If the user is *building* a scoreboard, route: solo → `4dx-d3-personal-scoreboard`; leader-of-team → `4dx-d3-team-lead-scoreboard-design`. This skill only reads existing artifacts.
- **No-scoreboard contexts.** If the team has no scoreboard at all, halt at step 1 and route to `4dx-d3-team-lead-scoreboard-design` (via the leader). Do not invent a personal substitute — that confuses team and personal D3.
- **Forward commitment-prep contexts.** If the user is preparing a weekly commitment, route to `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`. That skill consumes a read; this skill is the read alone, and outside prep context the read alone is rarely the user's actual goal.
- **Backward debrief contexts.** If the user is debriefing a missed commitment, route to `4dx-d4-member-account-debrief`. That skill uses the scoreboard for backward causal reasoning; this skill produces a current-state read only.
- **D2 influence-mapping gap.** If the member can't locate their work on *any* lead, the upstream gap is influence-mapping, not reading. Route to `4dx-d2-member-lead-measure-influence` first.
- **BI / KPI dashboards / executive scorecards.** Coach's-scoreboards have a different purpose (analytic, leader-facing). The 5-second / locate-contribution / pacing-line frame doesn't apply.
- **As a substitute for talking to the leader.** If the scoreboard is broken, the fix is leader-side. The skill helps the member read and *surface*; it does not let the member redesign by stealth.

### Author-warned failure modes (member-side mirror)

- **Coach's-as-players' substitution (CE-08, member-side)**: the member is handed a multi-tab Notion / Tableau / 14-page Gantt and told "that's our scoreboard." Fails the 5-second test, fails simple. Reading produces fatigue, not signal. Flag as scoreboard-fails-5-sec and surface.
- **Status-without-trajectory (CE-09, member-side)**: the board shows where the team is but not where it should be — no pacing line, no target marker, no goat. The member sees a number but can't tell if the number is winning. Flag as missing-pacing-line and surface.
- **Hidden scoreboard**: the artifact lives in an app the team doesn't open daily, a tab in a sheet, a Slack pin nobody scrolls to. Out of sight = out of mind for the team, not just the member. The member's "I can't see it without effort" is signal.
- **Decorative / stale scoreboard**: posted weeks ago, hasn't been updated, pacing line set against superseded assumptions. Reads as "we're still here" when the team has moved. Surface as calibration-drift.
- **Scoreboard-as-trophy (vanity-metric drift)**: the board shows only what's working, hides leads that aren't moving, gets curated for visitors. The member's read becomes "what does my leader want me to think?" rather than "what's actually happening?" This is hard to surface without political cost — capture as a private signal even if escalation is blocked.
- **Member-substitution failure**: the member compensates for a broken team scoreboard by reading their personal tracker instead. The personal artifact answers "am *I* winning?" not "is the *team* winning on the team WIG?" — different question.
- **Reading-as-procrastination**: the member reads and re-reads the scoreboard instead of acting on the focus lead. Reading should take ≤5 minutes weekly. If it's eating the slot, the whirlwind is using D3 as cover.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations (apply with care)

- **Leader-POV authoring**: Ch. 4 + Ch. 14 codify scoreboard *design*, not *reading*. The four design criteria invert cleanly into read criteria, but the book never walks through a member doing the read. This skill fills that gap by symmetric inversion; treat conclusions accordingly.
- **Co-located visible-board assumption**: best examples (factory wall, valet stand, hotel lobby) assume in-person glance access. Distributed / remote members read scoreboards through screens — the 5-second test still applies but ambient visibility (the "I see it without trying" mechanism) is weaker. Member's "I keep forgetting to check" is partly a remote-context signal, not pure motivation.
- **Psychological-safety assumption**: step 6a's escalation script presumes the member can raise a scoreboard concern without political cost. In low-safety teams (Edmondson 2018) this assumption fails — capture the read as a private signal and do not pressure the surface step. Note this in the output card under "blocked-on-safety."
- **High-context-culture caveat**: in JP / ZH / KR team cultures, telling a leader their scoreboard is broken can read as face-loss for the leader. Step 6a's framing ("I'd rather flag it than guess") is calibrated to be Model-II / non-attributing (Argyris 1986); even so, route members in high-context contexts to surface via 1:1 rather than session-floor when possible.
- **No "stale calibration" protocol in book**: Ch. 4 covers initial design quality; Ch. 14 doesn't separately handle pacing-line drift over a 6-12 month WIG cycle. Step 5's calibration check is a member-side addition based on industry-information-design literature (Few / Tufte) about dashboards going stale.

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **OKR check-ins**: OKR weekly check-ins update KR confidence (often "yellow / green / red"). 4DX scoreboard-reading is more granular — lead trend, lag trend, pacing line, where my work plugs in. Don't reflexively translate OKR-checkin habits.
- **Generic "data review" / dashboard review**: BI dashboards optimize for analysis (drill-down, slicing). Scoreboard-reading optimizes for one question: "are we winning, and where do I plug in?" 5 minutes, not 30.
- **Project status reports**: status reports answer "where is each task?". Scoreboard reading answers "is the lead lifting the lag, and what did I do?". Different artifacts.
- **Burn-down / burn-up charts (Scrum)**: closer to scoreboards than dashboards but track sprint scope completion, not lead-against-lag with a pacing line on a behavior-change WIG. Habit-tracker vs scoreboard-tracker confusion (see personal-D3) generalizes here too.

### Industry-experience addendum (member-side reading literature)

The book's design rubric inverts into a read rubric, but three sources sharpen the member-side read where the book is silent — full citations in `references/industry-grounding.md`:

- **Tufte, *Visual Display* (Graphics Press, 1983 / 2nd ed. 2001)** — chartjunk and low data-ink ratio delay the eye's path to the trend; the 5-second criterion (step 2) has a perception-science basis. (Cross-ref `4dx-d3-personal-scoreboard`.)
- **Eurich, *Insight* (Crown Business, 2017)** — the 95/15 self-awareness gap shows up in step 4: members round up "my activity caused this trend" without external calibration; the "plausibly" hedge is a calibration intervention. (Cross-ref `4dx-d4-member-account-debrief`.)
- **Argyris, "Skilled Incompetence" (HBR 1986)** — step 6a's "I'd rather flag it than guess" is a direct Model-II move (own observation + joint inquiry, no attribution). (Complements Edmondson cross-ref in `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`.)

Practical implication: the 5-second criterion, trend-causation prompt, and escalation script sit on a documented evidence base, not just on book authority.
