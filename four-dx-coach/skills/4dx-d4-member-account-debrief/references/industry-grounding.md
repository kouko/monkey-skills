# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-d4-member-account-debrief

Supplements the book's leader-POV with member-side / self-awareness / failure-
taxonomy / over-promising literature where the book is thin (V1 ⚠️ partial).
The book's accountability material (Ch. 5, Ch. 10's Susan/Marcus dialogue,
Ch. 15) is written from the leader's seat — Susan asking, Marcus answering.
The mirror question — how a member should *self-assess* a kept-or-missed
commitment honestly, without sliding into excuse-mode, shame-mode, or
self-Goodharting — is left implicit. The sources below fill that gap from
self-awareness research, organizational-failure taxonomy, and accidental-
diminisher / over-promising literature.

## Underlying mechanism

The Account segment of a WIG Session takes ~30 seconds per person, but the
30 seconds carry asymmetric weight: they convey to the team whether the
member's *signal* is trustworthy (when they say "kept," do they mean kept?)
and whether the member's *self-knowledge* is calibrated (do they know what
went wrong, and can they redesign?). The book assumes members default to
honest self-account; research on self-awareness suggests the opposite — most
people perceive themselves as far more self-aware than they actually are, and
almost all failure debriefs default to story-mode (recap-the-week) rather than
to the four-line spine the cadence requires. The sources below give the
member a research-grounded discipline for resisting those defaults.

## Key industry sources

### Source 1: Eurich, *Insight* (Crown Business, 2017)

Tasha Eurich, *Insight: Why We're Not as Self-Aware as We Think, and How
Seeing Ourselves Clearly Helps Us Succeed at Work and in Life* (New York:
Crown Business, 2017; ISBN 978-0-451-49681-2). Eurich's research program
across ~5,000 participants found that while ~95% of people *believe* they are
self-aware, only ~10–15% actually meet a calibrated definition of self-
awareness — the "95/15 gap."

Direct application: a member doing the Q2 classification step (kept /
partial / missed) sits exactly inside the 95/15 gap. Their first instinct
will likely round up — "I'd call that mostly kept" — and they will believe
that judgment is accurate. Eurich's research justifies this skill's
explicit anti-rounding-up prompts ("the team needs a real signal, not a
count") not as a coaching mannerism but as a calibration intervention. Her
distinction between *internal* self-awareness (knowing your own values and
behavior) and *external* self-awareness (knowing how others see you) maps
onto the WIG Session's specific demand: the member must classify their
commitment outcome the way their peers will classify it, not the way they
feel about it.

### Source 2: Edmondson, "Strategies for Learning from Failure" (HBR April 2011)

Amy C. Edmondson, "Strategies for Learning from Failure," *Harvard Business
Review*, vol. 89, no. 4 (April 2011), pp. 48–55. Three-category failure
taxonomy: (a) preventable failures in predictable operations (deviations from
spec — these *are* blameworthy and the lesson is "follow the spec"); (b)
unavoidable failures in complex systems (system-level interactions — the
lesson is "redesign the system"); (c) intelligent failures at the frontier
(experiments that yielded information — the lesson is "the experiment was
the win").

Direct application: this skill's Q4 ("what did I learn?") branches by
classification — but the book does not give the member a taxonomy of *what
kind* of miss it was. Edmondson's three categories give exactly that: a
missed commitment because the member didn't follow their own plan is a Type
(a) preventable failure (lesson: tighten execution); a missed commitment
because the lead-measure mechanism didn't work as theorized is Type (b)
complex (lesson: redesign the lead measure, route to D2); a missed
commitment because the member tried a new approach that didn't yield is Type
(c) intelligent (lesson: keep the learning, change the bet). Without this
taxonomy, all three collapse into "I missed" — and the redesign decision in
Step 6 has no information to act on.

### Source 3: Wiseman & McKeown, *Multipliers* (HarperBusiness, 2010)

Liz Wiseman and Greg McKeown, *Multipliers: How the Best Leaders Make
Everyone Smarter* (New York: HarperBusiness, 2010; ISBN 978-0-06-196439-8).
The "accidental diminisher" patterns — well-intentioned behaviors that
suppress others' contributions — were documented further in Wiseman &
McKeown, "Are You an Accidental Diminisher?", *Ivey Business Journal*,
August 2010, and integrated into the revised edition. The "Rescuer" and
"Always-On" patterns specifically describe leaders who jump in to do work
others were going to do.

Direct application: the Marcus pattern in 4DX Ch. 10 (Marcus made the
prospecting calls *himself* instead of building the system his team needed)
is a rescuer/always-on accidental-diminisher pattern — and the Wiseman
research shows it is *systematic*, not idiosyncratic. The honest debrief
must surface "did I accidentally-diminish my own committed work by doing
someone else's urgent?" — and Wiseman's vocabulary gives the member a
non-shaming way to name the move. The book treats Marcus's pattern as a
single anecdote; the Wiseman research treats it as a documented behavioral
class with field prevalence — which legitimizes this skill's specific
introspection prompt in Step 4 ("did you pick up someone else's urgent over
your own committed?").

## What 4DX team-members learn from these sources

The book treats the honest self-account as a moral/character matter — Marcus
"owns it" because Marcus is well-integrated into a healthy culture. The
industry literature reframes the honest self-account as a *trainable
calibration discipline* against documented cognitive biases (Eurich's 95/15
gap), supported by a *failure-type taxonomy* that makes the lesson legible
(Edmondson's three categories), and equipped with *named behavioral patterns*
that the member can recognize in themselves without shame (Wiseman's
accidental-diminisher vocabulary). The practical takeaway for this skill:
the protocol's anti-rounding-up prompts (Step 3), branched-by-cause learning
prompts (Step 5), and Marcus-pattern naming (Step 4) are not rhetorical
moves — they are research-grounded interventions against the specific
cognitive failures that derail member self-account in the wild.
