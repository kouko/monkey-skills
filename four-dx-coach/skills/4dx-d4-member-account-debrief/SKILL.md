# 4dx-d4-member-account-debrief — Walk Into the Session Clean

## R — Reading

> I wasn't even able to keep my commitments for last week because I
> decided to just make prospecting calls myself to help make our numbers.
>
> — Marcus, in McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 10 — the rare
> first-person member voice doing an honest self-account

> No other action builds trust and respect faster than the simple act of
> doing what you say you will do, every time… Your XPS standard for
> fulfilling commitments should be 90 percent (or higher).
>
> — *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 10 (P-39, the quantitative bar)

> The commitment must represent a specific deliverable… The commitment
> must influence the lead measure.
>
> — *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 5 (P-22, the two-part gate that lets you tell
> kept from not-kept without flinching)

## I — Interpretation

The Account segment of a WIG Session is short. Maybe 30 seconds per
person. What you say in those 30 seconds is the trust-building act of
the entire week — bigger than what you actually shipped. The book is
firm: **doing-what-you-said is the trust currency**, but *honestly
naming what you did and didn't* is the integrity currency. They are
different currencies. You can fail on the first and still pass the
second, and that is how trust survives a miss.

A healthy member debrief is built around five questions, in order:

1. **What was the commitment?** — restated verbatim, not paraphrased.
2. **What happened?** — kept / partial / missed. One word, then
   evidence. No story-mode.
3. **If missed or partial: what blocked it?** — factual cause, not
   blame, not excuse-coaching.
4. **What did I learn?** — about the commitment design, about my week,
   about whether the lead measure mechanism actually works.
5. **What's next?** — re-commit (same scope, different week) OR
   re-design (smaller scope, different shape).

The book's accountability-with-respect frame (Ch. 10) cuts both ways:
**self-respect** — missing one commitment is a data point, not a
character verdict — and **respect for the team** — they need a clean
signal from you to plan their week, not a story. The five common
failure modes of member debriefs each violate one side of that:

- **Excuse mode** ("I would have, but the whirlwind / Bob / weather…")
  drains *team* trust — the team can't tell what's actually movable.
- **Shame mode** ("I'm the worst, I should have done it") blocks *your
  own* analysis — you can't learn from a verdict.
- **Vague mode** ("kind of, partially, sort of") provides no learning
  signal to anyone — fails P-22's specific-deliverable test.
- **No-mention mode** (try to skip past it) silently undermines D4 —
  the cadence is the trust mechanism; ducking it breaks the cadence.
- **Over-commit mode** ("I'll do it AND three more things this week")
  is the Marcus pattern (CE-23) reflexed inward — you panic-pile
  scope, miss again, deeper.

The honest middle is brief, factual, and forward. Marcus's own line —
*"I wasn't even able to keep my commitments… because I decided to just
make prospecting calls myself"* — is the model. Verb tense is past,
agency is owned, mechanism is named, no apology theatre, no wallowing.
Then he proposes a redesign. That's the shape.

A second discipline operates on top: **multi-miss reframing.** One
miss is data. Two misses is a pattern. Three misses in a row is no
longer a debrief problem — it is a **commitment-design problem**, and
the right move is not another debrief but a redesign of the commitment
itself (smaller scope, different shape, route to
`4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`).

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Marcus's own honest self-account in Susan's WIG Session (Ch. 10)
- **Problem**: West Team falling behind Q2 sales WIG. Marcus, the team
  lead-as-member-in-VP-Susan's-session, has missed his own commitments
  and made prospecting calls himself instead of building the system.
- **Methodology applied**: When Susan asks for his account, Marcus
  doesn't story-mode it. One sentence: *"I wasn't even able to keep my
  commitments for last week because I decided to just make prospecting
  calls myself to help make our numbers."* Past tense, named agency,
  named mechanism (he chose it). No excuse, no wallowing.
- **Conclusion**: That sentence is what makes the rest of the dialogue
  productive. Susan can offer the five optimizing questions; Bianca
  can volunteer her prospecting script. The honest account *unlocks*
  the help.
- **Outcome (book)**: Full leadership-team realignment. Canonical
  exemplar for member-POV honest self-account inside the cadence.

### Case 2: Yolanda's clean back-room report-back (Ch. 11, derived)
- **Problem**: Store 334 — worst of 250. Yolanda commits to clean the
  back-room storage area as her week's commitment.
- **Methodology applied**: At the next WIG Session, Yolanda reports
  back specifically — not "I worked on it", but the deliverable was
  delivered, observable, photo-able. Specific because P-22's first
  test (specific deliverable) was passed at commitment-time.
- **Conclusion**: When the *commitment* was specific, the *account* is
  trivially specific. Most debrief failure starts at commitment-design
  (vague intent), not at debrief-time. Clean commitments make clean
  debriefs.
- **Outcome**: Department score 13 → 38 / 50 in 10 weeks. The store
  reversed because the cadence's report-backs became real signal.

### Case 3: Mike Crisafulli — refusing favourable results without understanding (The Missing Ingredient)
- **Problem**: SVP of IT got *favourable* early results across 46
  teams but didn't understand the mechanism.
- **Methodology applied**: Refused to accept the favourable account.
  Demanded each team report individually on results AND lessons.
  *Humility* applied to a "kept" commitment — not just "did we hit it"
  but "do I understand why we hit it".
- **Conclusion**: A clean debrief isn't only for misses. *Kept*
  commitments deserve the same honest mechanism-naming, or you ride
  unexamined wins into a future miss you can't diagnose. The debrief
  question "what did I learn about the lead-measure mechanism" applies
  to wins too.
- **Outcome (book)**: 3× the original goal by year-end.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the member need this skill?

1. **Member missed last week's commitment and the team's WIG Session
   is tomorrow** (or this morning) and they are sitting with the
   question "what do I say?". Most common trigger.
2. **Member half-did the commitment** ("I kind of did it, partially,
   ish") and is unsure how to classify it for the Account segment.
3. **Member kept the commitment but with a quality problem** ("I
   shipped, but with a regression / Friday not Thursday") and wants to
   know whether to call it a kept or a miss.
4. **Member has dressed-up the miss in their head** ("technically I
   made progress on it") and a quiet voice is telling them the team
   will see through it. They want help saying it straight.
5. **Member is dreading the session** because of the miss and is
   tempted to no-mention or excuse — wants help walking in clean.

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- "I missed my commitment last week — how do I show up at the session?"
- "WIG Session is tomorrow and I didn't finish. What do I say?"
- "Help me debrief last week's commitment honestly."
- "I half-did it. Is that a kept or a miss?"
- 「先週のコミットメント果たせなかった、どう振り返る？」
- 「セッション明日で、何て言えばいい？」
- 「コミットメント部分達成、どう報告すればいい？」
- 「上週 commitment 沒達成，這次 session 怎麼面對？」
- 「半完成的承諾要怎麼講？」
- "I shipped Friday instead of Thursday with a regression — kept or not?"
- "I want to walk into the session clean about last week."

### Non-trigger language (do NOT activate)

- "What should I commit to this week?" → forward-looking, picking the
  next commitment. Use `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`.
- "I've missed three weeks in a row" → that's a commitment-design
  failure, not a debrief problem. Route to
  `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep` for redesign, optionally
  `4dx-sustain-personal-momentum-rescue` if engagement has collapsed.
- "I don't have a team / I'm working on this solo" → no session to
  prepare for. Use `4dx-sustain-personal-momentum-rescue` for solo
  lapse.
- "Run my weekly review with me" (solo) → use
  `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`; this skill is the *member-side
  pre-process* for a *team* session, not a session itself.
- "How do I run the team's session?" → leader-side facilitation; out
  of scope for this skill (book chapters 5 + 15 directly, leader-led).
- "I want to talk about my year / quarter / month" → wrong cadence
  scope; this skill is one-week debrief.

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep` (sibling, forward-facing):
  commitment-prep picks the NEXT commitment for next week;
  account-debrief processes the PAST one for this week's session.
  Often used in sequence — debrief first (close the past), then prep
  (open the future).
- vs. `4dx-sustain-personal-momentum-rescue`: rescue handles
  multi-week breakdown / collapsed engagement. Account-debrief is one
  single-commitment outcome — keeps cadence alive, doesn't rebuild it.
- vs. `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`: that runs the live Account /
  Review / Plan agenda for a *solo* user. This skill is specifically
  for *team* members preparing what they'll *say* in someone else's
  facilitated session.
- vs. `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`: comprehension is when
  you joined and don't know what the WIG is. Debrief is when you've
  been on the team a while and have a commitment to account for.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent runs a brief pre-session debrief.
Voice is **personal-coach to the member**, non-judgmental, locker-room
brief — turn "I failed" into "here's what I learned, here's what I'll
say". Output target is a prepared **30-second statement** the member
can use verbatim or as a stable spine in tomorrow's Account segment.

1. **Open & set the frame** (≤30 sec)
   - "Walk into the session clean. We have ~5 minutes. Five questions,
     then a script you can say in 30 seconds. No shame, no excuse —
     just straight."
   - Completion criterion: member confirms the goal (script for the
     session, not a therapy session).

2. **Q1 — Restate the commitment verbatim**
   - Ask: "What was your commitment last week — exactly the words?"
   - If the member paraphrases ("something about outreach…"), push:
     "What did you actually say in last week's session? The exact
     deliverable."
   - If they cannot recall the exact words: that itself is data — the
     commitment was probably vague at commitment-time (P-22 violation
     upstream). Note for the redesign decision.
   - Completion criterion: a single concrete deliverable sentence is
     captured (or the absence of one is named).

3. **Q2 — Classify the outcome straight**
   - Ask: "Kept, partial, or missed? — one word."
   - Apply the **two-part P-22 test** to the answer:
     - (a) Specific deliverable: was there a concrete output someone
       else could verify? If no, classify as **missed** regardless of
       how much "effort" went in. Effort isn't the unit; deliverable
       is.
     - (b) Did it happen on the committed window (day, week)? Late by
       a day, or late by a day with a regression, is *not* a kept —
       it's a partial at best. Don't allow the user to round up.
   - Anti-CE-24 (Goodhart self-deception): if the user is reaching for
     "I'll count that as a yes", name it once, gently — "be careful;
     the team needs a real signal, not a count" — and re-ask.
   - Completion criterion: a single word — kept / partial / missed —
     plus a one-sentence factual evidence line.

4. **Q3 — If missed or partial: what blocked it?** (skip if kept)
   - Ask: "What blocked it? — factual, not blame."
   - Listen for excuse-mode signals ("the whirlwind / Bob / weather /
     I would have"). Mirror without coaching the excuse: "OK, that's
     the situation. What's the *part of that that was within your
     control* that you didn't do?" — the question gently reclaims
     agency without shaming.
   - Listen for the **Marcus pattern self-applied** (CE-23): did the
     member spend last week doing teammates' urgent tasks instead of
     their own committed deliverable? If yes, name it: "That's the
     Marcus move — picking up someone else's urgent over your own
     committed." This is a specific, book-anchored introspection
     prompt, not a generic "were you distracted?".
   - Listen for **shame-mode** ("I'm just bad at this"). Interrupt
     once: "Stop. One miss is data, not a verdict. Stay with the
     facts."
   - Completion criterion: one factual cause, owned in first person.

5. **Q4 — What did I learn?**
   - Ask one of the following, depending on Q2's classification:
     - If **missed**: "What does this tell you about the *commitment
       design*? — was it specific (P-22a), within your control, scoped
       to one week?"
     - If **partial**: "What does this tell you about your *week's
       capacity*? — was the commitment too big, or was the week
       eaten?"
     - If **kept**: "What did you learn about the *lead-measure
       mechanism* — did doing this actually move the lead? (Crisafulli
       discipline — don't just count it; understand it.)"
   - Completion criterion: a one-sentence learning, not a moral.

6. **Q5 — Re-commit or redesign?**
   - Decision rule:
     - 1 miss in isolation, with a clean cause → **re-commit** same
       scope, different week.
     - 2 misses in a row OR a partial-with-quality-problem → **resize
       down** — same shape, smaller deliverable.
     - 3+ misses in a row → **stop**. This is no longer a debrief
       problem. Hand off: "Three misses in a row is a commitment-design
       problem, not a willpower problem. Use
       `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep` to redesign the shape — different
       lead measure approach, smaller scope, or different mechanism."
   - **Anti-over-commit guard (CE-23 reflexed)**: if the member
     proposes "I'll do it AND three more things this week" to make up
     for the miss, reject: "No. The lead measure won't move from
     piling. Pick one. Same one, smaller version, or different shape."
   - Completion criterion: a clear next-step direction (re-commit
     same / resize down / hand off to redesign).

7. **Compose the 30-second script**
   - Build the member's spoken Account-segment statement using this
     spine, in their own register:
     ```
     [Verbatim commitment]. [Kept / partial / missed].
     [If miss/partial: one-sentence factual cause, owned].
     [What I learned: one sentence].
     [Next: re-commit / resize / I'll bring a redesign].
     ```
   - Check the script against the five failure modes one last time:
     - Excuse mode? → cut.
     - Shame mode? → cut.
     - Vague mode? → tighten to specific deliverable.
     - No-mention? → must include the commitment by name.
     - Over-commit? → cap to one next step.
   - Read it back to the member. Time it — should be ~30 seconds
     spoken aloud. If longer, cut.
   - Completion criterion: a single short paragraph (or 3-5 short
     sentences) the member can deliver verbatim or as a stable spine.

8. **Close**
   - "That's your Account. The team needs the clean signal. Go in,
     deliver it, sit down. Plan segment is where you'll re-commit or
     bring the redesign — not the Account segment."
   - Completion criterion: member has a usable script in front of them
     and knows what *not* to do at the session (extend the apology,
     pile on commitments, narrate the week).

**Multilingual register cue (E step output)**:
- EN: blunt, locker-room brief, past tense, owned agency. ("Committed
  X. Missed. Took on Bob's urgent. Re-committing same scope Wed.")
- JP: 過去形 + 主語明確、「〜してしまった」「〜できませんでした」を
  含めて responsibility は受けるが over-apologize しない。「先週は X
  をコミットしました。果たせませんでした。同窓のタスクを優先してしま
  ったのが原因です。今週は同じスコープで水曜までに再コミットします」
- zh-TW: 直白、過去式、認帳但不過度道歉。「上週我承諾 X。沒達成。
  原因是把同事的緊急任務優先了。這週同樣 scope，週三前重新 commit。」

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Solo / no-team contexts.** This skill prepares a member for a
  *team* session. If there is no team session — solo user with no
  weekly cadence partner — there is no Account segment to prepare for.
  Use `4dx-sustain-personal-momentum-rescue` for solo lapse.
- **Chronic underperformance (3+ misses in a row).** That is no longer
  a debrief problem; it is a commitment-design problem. The right move
  is to *stop* trying to debrief the same shape and route to
  `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep` to redesign the commitment itself
  (smaller, different mechanism, different lead measure approach).
- **Picking the next commitment.** This skill closes the past
  commitment; it does not open the next one. Use
  `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep` for forward-looking commitment
  selection.
- **Leader-side facilitation.** If the user is the leader running the
  session and asking how to *receive* a member's account, that is the
  Susan-side dialogue (Ch. 10) — out of scope here. The member is the
  audience.
- **Annual / quarterly retrospectives.** Wrong cadence scope. This
  skill is one-week one-commitment.
- **Therapy / shame work.** If the dominant register is "I'm a failure
  / I'm worthless", the work is upstream of 4DX. Briefly normalize
  ("one miss is data, not a verdict"), then if the shame doesn't move,
  decline the rest of the protocol and surface the boundary explicitly.

### Author-warned failure modes (cited)

- **Excuse mode (CE-22 self-applied — vague / formulaic recognition,
  inverted to vague / formulaic excuse).** "Whirlwind / Bob / weather"
  framings drain team trust because they signal *the speaker doesn't
  know which part was within their control*. Reclaim agency with one
  question: "What's the part that was within your control that you
  didn't do?"
- **Marcus pattern self-applied (CE-23).** Member did teammates'
  urgent work instead of their own committed deliverable. Specific
  introspection prompt — "did you pick up someone else's urgent over
  your own committed?" — that's missing from generic weekly retros.
  Name it when present.
- **Goodhart self-deception (CE-24).** "I'll count that as a yes"
  when the deliverable wasn't actually delivered (late, partial,
  regressed). Name it once, gently. The team needs real signal.
- **Pile-on response.** Compensating for a miss by committing to MORE
  next week (the Marcus pattern reflexed inward) — "I'll do it AND
  three more things." Reject in step 6.
- **Story-mode / narrative-mode in the script.** A 30-second Account
  is *not* a recap of the week. It is: commitment → outcome → cause
  → learning → next. Cut everything else.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations

- **Book is leader-POV.** Almost all of Ch. 5/10/15 is written for
  Susan, not Marcus. Member-side debrief protocol is the symmetric
  inverse of leader-side accountability-with-respect — coherent with
  P-22, P-39, CE-22/23/24, but not directly authored. V1 ⚠️ partial.
- **Book assumes commitments were well-designed at commitment-time.**
  In practice, ~half of member misses trace to vague-at-source
  commitments (P-22a violation). The "did I learn the commitment was
  badly designed?" branch in step 5 compensates, but the cleanest fix
  is upstream — `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep` enforces P-22 at
  commitment-time so debriefs become trivially clean.
- **High-context-culture face-loss (CE-29 family).** Public miss-
  reporting in JP / ZH / KR contexts can read as face-threatening to
  team and self. The protocol's brief / past-tense / owned-agency
  shape *helps* (no over-apology, no wallow), but if the member is
  unwilling to say it aloud at the session, prefer a written
  Slack/Teams Account submitted in advance — same content, lower
  social cost.
- **Quality-problem classification ambiguity.** Book treats kept-with-
  quality-issue as a partial in spirit (P-22a "specific deliverable"
  test) but doesn't define a sharp rule. This skill's working rule:
  shipped-but-with-regression / shipped-late = partial. Member may
  push back; that pushback is itself useful learning data ("you wanted
  to round up — why?").

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **Agile retrospective (sprint retro).** Team-level, multi-topic,
  what-went-well / what-didn't / what-to-try. WIG Account is
  individual-level, single-commitment, three-line. Don't conflate;
  agile retros are noisy enough to allow story-mode.
- **OKR check-in.** Score-based (0.0-1.0), no honest-account
  discipline, often manager-driven. Reading an OKR check-in as a WIG
  Account loses the trust-currency mechanism.
- **Performance review / 1:1.** Different frame entirely (multi-week,
  evaluative, asymmetric). WIG Account is peer-witnessed, weekly,
  symmetric, deliverable-specific. Don't import the apology-and-
  improvement-plan format from performance reviews into a WIG session.
- **Therapy / coaching reflection.** Work in the feeling layer
  ("how does the miss make you feel?") is upstream and out of scope.
  WIG debrief is in the deliverable layer ("what did you say, what
  did you ship, was there a gap").

### Industry-experience addendum (member-side literature)

The book is leader-POV; member-side honest self-account is supplemented from self-awareness, failure-taxonomy, and accidental-diminisher research that grounds the protocol's anti-rounding / branched-learning / Marcus-pattern prompts.

- **Eurich, *Insight* (Crown Business, 2017)** — the 95/15 self-awareness gap: most people *believe* they classify themselves accurately and don't. Justifies the anti-rounding-up prompts in Step 3 as calibration intervention.
- **Edmondson, "Strategies for Learning from Failure" (HBR April 2011)** — three-category failure taxonomy (preventable / complex / intelligent) gives Step 5 the missing classification needed for redesign decisions.
- **Wiseman & McKeown, *Multipliers* (HarperBusiness, 2010)** — accidental-diminisher / rescuer pattern legitimizes the Marcus-pattern introspection in Step 4 as a documented behavioral class, not a one-off anecdote.

See `references/industry-grounding.md` for full citations and mechanism notes. Practical implication: the protocol's specific prompts are research-grounded interventions against documented cognitive failures, not coaching mannerisms.
