# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-meta-strategy-triage (multi-scope)

Consolidated primary-source citations across both protocols (personal-mode
+ team-mode). Each section names the source, the protocol(s) that
reference it, and a 2-3 sentence summary of the mechanism it grounds.

The book is leader-POV throughout Ch 1 + Ch 6 and treats the
stroke-of-pen / whirlwind / behavioral-change taxonomy as self-contained.
The sources below fill the gaps where the book is thin — solo-scale
anti-patterns and exploration mode (Kotter / Heath / March for personal
mode); team change-readiness preconditions (Kotter / Galbraith / Schein
for team mode).

---

## Source 1: John P. Kotter, *Leading Change* (HBS Press, 1996; rev. 2012)

**Citation**: John P. Kotter, *Leading Change* (Harvard Business School
Press, 1996; 10th-anniversary update HBR Press 2012, ISBN 978-1-4221-8643-5;
foundational HBR article 1995).

**Used by**: `personal-mode.md` (Step 1 + Step 5 implicit), `team-mode.md`
(Step 7 — urgency-symmetry gate).

**Mechanism**: Eight-step model — establish urgency, build a coalition,
develop vision, communicate, empower action, generate short-term wins,
consolidate gains, anchor — synthesized from a multi-year HBR study of
~100 large-scale change efforts. ~70% of transformations fail, with most
failures in **Stage 1 (Urgency)** or **Stage 2 (Guiding Coalition)**
*before* any methodology installs. Step 1 is the direct upstream of 4DX's
triage move: if the actor cannot articulate the cost of inaction in one
sentence, no D1-D4 discipline rescues a change with no urgency anchor —
the WIG installed on a non-urgent actor / team becomes Friday ceremony
within 3 weeks. For team mode the gate is sharper: the *team*'s urgency
must be ≥ the leader's; otherwise top-down installation produces surface
compliance + zero behavior change.

Links: [HBR — "Leading Change: Why Transformation Efforts Fail" (1995)](https://hbr.org/1995/05/leading-change-why-transformation-efforts-fail-2),
[HBS Press product page](https://store.hbr.org/product/leading-change-with-a-new-preface-by-the-author/9781422186435).

## Source 2: Chip Heath & Dan Heath, *Switch: How to Change Things When Change Is Hard* (Crown / Broadway Books, 2010)

**Citation**: Chip Heath & Dan Heath, *Switch* (Crown / Broadway Books, 2010,
ISBN 978-0-385-52875-7).

**Used by**: `personal-mode.md` (Step 5 environmental-compatibility layer
under time-sovereignty); referenced from `team-mode.md` Step 7 implicitly.

**Mechanism**: Three-layer behavior-change model — the **Rider** (rational
mind — needs direction), the **Elephant** (emotional mind — needs
motivation), and the **Path** (situation — needs to be shaped to make the
right behavior easier). Behavior-change strategies fail when they address
only one layer. Mapped to 4DX: the book is overwhelmingly Rider-focused
(causal chains, two-axis tests, scoreboards as cognitive aids); addresses
the Elephant only indirectly via "winning" framing (CE-31); barely
addresses the Path — the book assumes the user's environment already
permits the new behavior. For a personal-coach scenario where the user is
fighting an unsupportive Path (open-plan office, partner who interrupts,
phone always within reach), running 4DX without first shaping the Path
produces theatrical compliance. The triage should flag the Path question
explicitly: *is the environment compatible with the WIG, or do we need to
shape the Path first?* If the answer is no, environmental redesign
precedes 4DX.

Links: [Penguin Random House book page](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/77696/switch-by-chip-heath-and-dan-heath/).

## Source 3: James G. March, "Exploration and Exploitation in Organizational Learning" (*Organization Science* 2(1), Feb 1991)

**Citation**: James G. March, "Exploration and Exploitation in Organizational
Learning," *Organization Science*, 2(1), 71–87, February 1991. DOI
10.1287/orsc.2.1.71.

**Used by**: `personal-mode.md` (Step 4 portfolio-bet anti-pattern; Step 7
matched-set capacity check — exploration phase incompatible with
single-bet commitment).

**Mechanism**: Foundational paper formalizes the trade-off between
**exploitation** (refining and executing a known bet) and **exploration**
(sampling unknown alternatives). Organizations and individuals that
over-allocate to exploitation optimize the wrong thing; those who
over-allocate to exploration never compound. The optimum is a portfolio.
4DX is a *pure-exploitation* instrument: pick one WIG, drive it, win.
This is correct when the user already knows what to bet on. It is
*systematically wrong* when the user is in an exploratory phase
(early-career, post-pivot, post-divorce, post-burnout, novel domain) —
committing to a single bet for 6 months locks in a wrong direction.
March's framing gives the triage a name for the failure mode the book
under-engages: **WIG-too-early**. When the user describes uncertainty
about direction itself ("I think I want to write but I'm not sure what
about"), the right move is not 4DX but an exploration loop (small
experiments, OKR-style quarterly resets, lean-startup hypothesis testing)
until enough evidence accrues to commit to a WIG. Maps directly to the
**portfolio-bet** verdict in the solo rubric.

Links: [INFORMS PubsOnline DOI 10.1287/orsc.2.1.71](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/orsc.2.1.71).

## Source 4: Jay R. Galbraith, STAR Model (*Designing Organizations*, Jossey-Bass, 2002 / 3rd ed. 2014)

**Citation**: Jay R. Galbraith, *Designing Your Organization: Using the
STAR Model to Solve 5 Critical Design Challenges* (Jossey-Bass, 2007 with
Amy Kates, ISBN 978-0-7879-9494-5); broader STAR model originally
*Designing Complex Organizations* (Addison-Wesley, 1973); restated in
*Designing Organizations* (Jossey-Bass, 2002 / 3rd ed. 2014).

**Used by**: `team-mode.md` (Step 7 — reward-system gate).

**Mechanism**: Five alignment levers — **Strategy → Structure → Processes
→ Rewards → People** — built on decades of organizational-design field
work. Empirical claim: any *one* misaligned lever degrades performance
regardless of how disciplined the others are. The book covers Strategy
(goal-shape via Ch 1 + Ch 6) and partially Structure (size 3-12, leader
authority); Galbraith adds Processes (does the team's existing operating
cadence already conflict with weekly WIG Sessions?), Rewards (do
compensation and recognition systems reward whirlwind firefighting over
WIG progress?), and People (F-28 Models / Not-Yets / Nevers covers part
of People). Practical addition for triage: ask whether existing
performance reviews + bonus structures contradict the WIG before
installation — if yes, the WIG loses to the incentive system within a
quarter regardless of the four traps clearing. This is the **Reward**
gate in TEAM-NOT-YET-READY.

Links: [Jossey-Bass / Wiley product page](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Designing+Your+Organization%3A+Using+the+STAR+Model+to+Solve+5+Critical+Design+Challenges-p-9780787994945),
[Galbraith — *Journal of Organization Design* 3(1): 2-13 (2014), CEO/USC PDF](https://ceo.usc.edu/wp-content/uploads/2018/03/3_Org_Design_Challenges_Galbraith.pdf).

## Source 5: Edgar H. Schein, *Organizational Culture and Leadership* (Jossey-Bass, 1985 / 5th ed. 2017)

**Citation**: Edgar H. Schein, *Organizational Culture and Leadership*
(Jossey-Bass; 1st ed. 1985, 5th ed. 2017 with Peter A. Schein, ISBN
978-1-119-21204-1).

**Used by**: `team-mode.md` (Step 7 — assumption-conflict gate).

**Mechanism**: Three-level culture model — **Artifacts → Espoused Values
→ Underlying Assumptions** — anchored on MIT Sloan decades of fieldwork.
Relevant warning for methodology adoption: installing a methodology
touches only the Artifacts level (visible rituals: WIG Sessions,
scoreboards, weekly cadence). If the underlying assumptions contradict the
methodology — e.g. an assumption that "real work is what the boss puts on
your desk this morning, not what you committed to last Friday" — the
artifacts get performed but the assumptions win. High-context-culture
caveats around public peer-commitment (already flagged in book CE-29)
generalize through Schein's lens: *any* assumption-level conflict produces
ritual compliance + zero substrate change. Diagnostic ask: "If a team
member's Friday WIG commitment conflicts with their boss's Monday-morning
request, what actually wins this team?" — the honest answer reveals the
assumption layer. If boss-request always wins, the assumption is
"compliance over commitment" — change the assumption first.

Links: [5th ed. Wiley product page](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Organizational+Culture+and+Leadership%2C+5th+Edition-p-9781119212041),
[Gordon-Conwell library PDF (earlier ed.)](https://www.gordonconwell.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/19/2019/04/ScheinE.OrganizationalCultureandLeadership.pdf).

## Source 6: Edward M. Hallowell, *Crazy Busy* (Random House Digital, 2007) (book-endnote-anchor: Ch 5 endnote 14)

**Citation**: Edward M. Hallowell, *Crazy Busy: Overstretched, Overbooked, and About to Snap! Strategies for Coping in a World Gone ADD* (New York: Random House Digital, 2007), p 183.

**Used by**: `personal-mode.md` (whirlwind-psychology layer under Step 4 / Step 5), `team-mode.md` (whirlwind-substrate framing for urgency check).

**Mechanism**: Hallowell — Harvard Medical School psychiatrist, ADD/ADHD clinical researcher — documents the cognitive cost of urgency overload as a clinical syndrome rather than a productivity complaint. The book draws on Hallowell at Ch 5 to name *why* the whirlwind is a structural cognitive problem (working-memory exhaustion, attention fragmentation, inability to hold long-horizon goals in mind under sustained urgency load), not a moral failing of insufficient discipline. Triage application: when the user describes "I can't even think about the WIG, the day eats me alive," this is not a discipline failure to fix with stricter scheduling — it is the documented Hallowell condition, and the triage should route to whirlwind-reduction (delegation, batching, environmental redesign) before any 4DX install. Cite as the clinical anchor for "the whirlwind is real, not a metaphor."

## What practitioners learn from these sources combined

The 6-verdict goal-shape rubric catches *what kind of goal* fails the fit.
The five sources above add *what kind of context* fails the fit even when
the goal-shape is right. Three additional pre-checks before issuing
APPLICABLE / TEAM-APPLICABLE:

1. **Kotter urgency check** (both modes) — can the actor articulate the
   cost of inaction in one sentence? In team mode, is the *team*'s
   urgency ≥ the leader's? If no, fix urgency first.
2. **Heath Path check** (primarily personal mode) — is the environment
   compatible with the new behavior, or does the Path need shaping
   first? If the latter, environmental redesign precedes 4DX.
3. **Galbraith Reward + Schein Assumption checks** (team mode) — do
   incentives reward whirlwind over WIG? Does the assumption layer
   permit Friday commitments to beat Monday boss-requests? If either
   fails, return TEAM-NOT-YET-READY.

For solo additionally: **March exploration check** — is the user
committed enough to a direction that a 6-month single-bet makes sense, or
is this an exploration phase that should run small experiments first?
Maps to the portfolio-bet verdict.

Each check corresponds to a documented failure mode for behavior-change
initiatives that the 4DX taxonomy does not name directly. Adding them as
pre-triage gates catches the four to six false positives per ten "this is
behavioral change, run 4DX" verdicts.
