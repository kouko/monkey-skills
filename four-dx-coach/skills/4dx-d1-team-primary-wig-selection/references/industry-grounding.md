# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-d1-team-primary-wig-selection

Supplements the book's Importance × Feasibility 2x2 + four-trap checklist
with the broader strategy literature on **what counts as a strategy at all**
and **the risks of over-formalized goal selection**. The book treats Primary
WIG selection as a 2x2 plotting exercise; these sources add the discipline
of strategy-as-choice (Porter), the kernel-of-strategy test (Rumelt), and
the five-element strategy diamond (Hambrick & Fredrickson) — each a
sharper test than "Importance × Feasibility" alone for whether a
candidate Primary WIG is actually a strategy or merely a goal.

## Strategy as choice — what NOT to do (Porter)

- **Michael E. Porter, *Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing
  Industries and Competitors* (Free Press, 1980, ISBN 0-02-925360-8)** —
  foundational text on strategy as deliberate positioning. Porter's most
  cited principle aligns precisely with the WIG focus discipline: **"The
  essence of strategy is choosing what not to do."** A Primary WIG is
  meaningful only because the team has explicitly de-prioritized the
  parked Battles; if every high-importance candidate is "still being
  worked on alongside" the Primary WIG, no actual strategic choice has
  been made. The book's Trap 1 (≤2 Primary WIGs) and Trap 2 (not too
  broad) are both forms of Porter's discipline. Practical sharpening:
  the agent should require the leader to name *which* parked Battles
  will receive demonstrably less attention as a result of the chosen
  Primary WIG; if the answer is "none — we'll keep doing all of it",
  the WIG selection is a wish list, not a strategy.
  ([Porter — "What is Strategy?", *Harvard Business Review*, Nov-Dec 1996](https://hbr.org/1996/11/what-is-strategy),
  [Octopus Intelligence summary of Porter's 1980 framework](https://www.octopusintelligence.com/what-is-competitive-strategy-according-to-michael-porter/))

## Kernel of strategy — diagnosis + guiding policy + coherent action (Rumelt)

- **Richard P. Rumelt, *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy: The Difference and
  Why It Matters* (Crown Business, 2011, ISBN 978-0-307-88623-1)** —
  Rumelt (Harry & Elsa Kunin Professor, UCLA Anderson) identifies the
  **kernel of strategy** as three elements: (1) **Diagnosis** —
  identifying the central challenge and what makes it hard; (2)
  **Guiding policy** — the overall approach to overcome the obstacles
  named in the diagnosis; (3) **Coherent action** — the coordinated
  steps that implement the guiding policy. Rumelt's central warning:
  "the most common cause of bad strategy is a weak diagnosis," and
  "the second most common cause is confusing goals with strategy.
  Goals, ambitions, visions, missions, values — none of these things
  are a strategy." Applied as a WIG-test: a candidate Primary WIG that
  cannot be paired with a one-sentence diagnosis ("the central challenge
  blocking us is X") is a goal masquerading as a strategy. The 4DX 2x2
  scores feasibility but never asks for diagnosis; this gap explains why
  high-and-right candidates can still be strategically empty (the
  Sydney "total revenue" failure case is exactly this).
  ([Rumelt — "The Perils of Bad Strategy", *McKinsey Quarterly*, June 2011](https://www.mckinsey.com/business-functions/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/the-perils-of-bad-strategy),
  [Lenny's Newsletter Q&A with Rumelt (2024)](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/good-strategy-bad-strategy-richard))

## Five-element completeness check — the strategy diamond

- **Donald C. Hambrick & James W. Fredrickson, "Are You Sure You Have a
  Strategy?", *Academy of Management Executive* 15(4): 48-59 (November
  2001), DOI 10.5465/AME.2001.5897655** — peer-reviewed; introduces
  the **strategy diamond**: a strategy must specify all five of (1)
  **Arenas** (where to compete), (2) **Vehicles** (how to get there),
  (3) **Differentiators** (how to win), (4) **Staging** (sequence and
  speed), (5) **Economic logic** (how returns are obtained). A
  candidate Primary WIG that names a target outcome but doesn't specify
  arena + vehicle + differentiator + staging + economic logic is, in
  the authors' language, *not* a strategy. Practical use as a WIG-test:
  for any high-and-right 2x2 candidate, the leader should be able to
  answer all five in 2-3 sentences each. If staging or economic logic
  is undefined, the WIG is premature — return to step 6 (ripple-effect
  filter) or reduce scope. This is a sharper completeness test than the
  *From X to Y by When* check, which only forces measurability.
  ([Academy of Management Perspectives DOI link](https://journals.aom.org/doi/abs/10.5465/ame.2001.5897655?journalCode=amp),
  [Penn State institutional repository — "Are You Sure You Have a Strategy?"](https://pure.psu.edu/en/publications/are-you-sure-you-have-a-strategy-2/))

## Mintzberg caveat — emergent vs deliberate (when 2x2 over-formalizes)

- **Henry Mintzberg, *The Rise and Fall of Strategic Planning: Reconceiving
  Roles for Planning, Plans, Planners* (Free Press, 1994, ISBN
  0-02-921605-2)** — distinguishes **deliberate strategy** (intentions
  fully realized) from **emergent strategy** (realized patterns that
  weren't expressly intended). Mintzberg's central critique: "strategic
  planning" is an oxymoron — planning is analysis, strategy is synthesis.
  In genuinely uncertain domains (early-stage product, novel R&D), the
  right Primary WIG may not be knowable yet; running a 2x2 with
  speculative feasibility scores produces false confidence. The book
  acknowledges this as a single-line caveat ("In genuinely uncertain
  domains… run small experiments first"); Mintzberg's framing is that
  this isn't an edge case — for many real organizations, *all* strategy
  is at least partly emergent, and the WIG should be re-tested every
  cycle rather than locked in for 1-2 years.
  ([HBR article — "The Fall and Rise of Strategic Planning" (1994)](https://hbr.org/1994/01/the-fall-and-rise-of-strategic-planning),
  [Internet Archive — book scan](https://archive.org/details/risefallofstrate0000mint))

## What leaders learn from these sources

The book's 2x2 + four-trap checklist filters for measurability + mission +
non-broadness, but a candidate that clears all four traps can still be
strategically empty. Three layered tests to apply on top of the 2x2:

1. **Porter's NOT-do test** — name 2+ parked Battles that will receive
   demonstrably less team attention as a *result* of choosing this
   Primary WIG. If none, the WIG is a wish list, not a strategy.
2. **Rumelt diagnosis test** — pair the WIG with a one-sentence
   diagnosis: "the central challenge that makes this hard is X." If
   the leader cannot articulate this, return to brainstorm — the WIG
   is a goal without a guiding policy.
3. **Hambrick-Fredrickson five-element test** — does the team have
   working answers to arenas / vehicles / differentiators / staging /
   economic logic? Missing staging or economic logic means execution
   will drift even if the WIG is technically well-formed.

Mintzberg layers a humility caveat over all of the above: in genuinely
uncertain domains, lock the WIG for shorter cycles (one quarter, not
1-2 years) and treat selection as iterative-emergent rather than
one-shot-deliberate. The book's 1-2 year horizon is a strong default for
operationally-mature organizations (Marriott, Covenant Transport) but
over-formalizes early-stage settings. For ambiguous-domain teams, the
Approach C strategy-line pattern (TELUS) plus shorter re-evaluation
cadence is the documented adaptation.
