# 4dx-d1-team-primary-wig-selection — Pick Your Team's Primary WIG

## R — Reading

> The first question to ask in your brainstorming is "If every other area of our operation remained at its current level of performance, in which *one area* would we most want to achieve significant results?" … This exercise is very insightful because it forces your team to assess each candidate WIG on the two axes of "Impact of Failure" and "At Risk of Failure (Without Significant Change)."
>
> — McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 6 ("Identifying Your Breakthrough Result")

---

## I — Interpretation

A Primary WIG is the team's central breakthrough — the one place where
disproportionate energy will be spent. Ch 6 frames the selection task as:
the leadership team brainstorms a list of **candidate Battles** (possible
breakthrough goals), then filters them through a 2x2 to converge on one.

The book's canonical axes are Impact-of-Failure × At-Risk-of-Failure
(F-17). For a team-leader workshop the equivalent everyday framing is
**Importance** (would a breakthrough here transform the team's situation?)
× **Feasibility** (can the team meaningfully move this in 1-2 years with
its own behavior change, not external authority?). Both framings target
the same selection logic: the WIG must be *mission-critical* AND *at
genuine risk without behavior change* — neither stroke-of-the-pen nor
already-handled-by-the-whirlwind.

The 2x2 segments candidates into four cells:

| | High Feasibility | Low Feasibility |
|---|---|---|
| **High Importance** | **Primary WIG candidates** (upper-right) | Parked — important but team can't move it; flag for leadership escalation |
| **Low Importance** | Whirlwind / nice-to-have — handle in normal ops, don't WIG | Drop |

Selection method (Ch 6, four moves):

1. **Brainstorm 5-10 candidate Battles.** Hold every other area constant
   and ask where the *one* breakthrough would matter most. Generate
   broadly; do not pre-filter.
2. **Score each candidate on Importance and Feasibility (1-5).** Force
   the uncomfortable Feasibility / risk axis — leaders skip it.
3. **Plot the 2x2 and look at the upper-right cell.** Upper-right = WIG
   candidates. The book is firm: high-and-right is *not* automatic — final
   choice still depends on resources, mission alignment, and ripple effect.
4. **Converge to ONE Primary WIG** (or, per Ch 6 Approach B, one Primary
   + at most one supporting Primary covering a different segment of the
   team). Total team WIGs ≤3, with **2 ideal and 1 preferred** when the
   team is functionally unified.

Three structural patterns from Ch 6:

- **Approach A — Single Primary WIG:** Covenant Transport chose driver
  turnover (106→86%) over on-time delivery / safety / shipments, because
  fixing turnover had the *ripple effect* of moving the others. Default
  to this pattern.
- **Approach B — Twin Primary WIGs across segments:** A pharma giant
  chose two — "molecule to market" (Transform WIG) + "increase access
  to drugs" (Perform WIG) — because the org served two distinct segments.
  Use only when the team genuinely has two non-overlapping operational
  arms.
- **Approach C — Strategy-line as quasi-Primary-WIG:** TELUS International
  set 4 strategic objectives instead of one Primary WIG, letting 400+
  teams self-select. Use only at large scale; not appropriate for a
  single team.

The output of this skill is one well-formed team Primary WIG written as
*From X to Y by When*, plus a parked-Battles list (importance acknowledged,
not currently funded with team energy).

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Covenant Transport — driver turnover ripple effect (Ch. 6, Approach A)
- **Problem**: Covenant's leadership team had multiple worthy candidates: on-time delivery, total shipments, traffic safety, driver turnover. Picking by importance alone produced a tie.
- **Methodology applied**: Mapped candidates on Impact × At-Risk; selected driver turnover (106→86% by Dec 31) because it had the *ripple effect* of cascading positively into the others — fixing turnover would also lift on-time delivery and safety, multiplying results. Every team could meaningfully contribute.
- **Conclusion**: Single Primary WIG (Approach A) wins when one candidate's victory cascades into the others. Pick by leverage, not by ranking importance.
- **Outcome**: Surpassed goal (reached 82% turnover — lowest in company history); produced best financial results in twelve years.

### Case 2: Sydney accounting firm — "go smaller" rescue (Ch. 6 follow-on; verified-team C-24)
- **Problem**: Firm had set "total revenue" as their Primary WIG. After a quarter of zero traction, the team felt the WIG was simultaneously vague, mission-disconnected, and contaminated by every stroke-of-pen + whirlwind activity. Trap #2 (too broad) had bitten.
- **Methodology applied**: Re-ran the 2x2 with smaller-scope candidates. Demoted "total revenue" *out* of WIG slot and re-promoted it to a strategy-line. Picked a narrower Battle one level down (a specific segment or service-line growth metric) that the team could actually own and move.
- **Conclusion**: When a Primary WIG is too broad, the rescue is *going smaller*, not adjusting time horizon. Importance feels high but feasibility is low because the team doesn't fully own the lever.
- **Outcome**: Re-scoped WIG produced traction within the next cycle; total revenue continued as monitored strategic metric without WIG-grade discipline.

### Case 3: Pharma giant — twin Primary WIGs across segments (Ch. 6, Approach B)
- **Problem**: Twelve strategic imperatives across two structurally different segments (R&D pipeline + commercial access). Selecting a single Primary WIG would force one segment to wait.
- **Methodology applied**: Acknowledged the two-segment structure. Selected two Primary WIGs: "molecule to market" (Transform WIG, R&D segment) + "increase access to their drugs" (Perform WIG, commercial segment, with two Key Battles — prescriber education + payer streamlining). From any single team's perspective, there was still only one WIG.
- **Conclusion**: Approach B is justified only when an organization has genuinely separate operating segments. Each segment still operates under one Primary WIG; the multiplicity is at the org level, not the team level.
- **Outcome**: Cited by McChesney et al. as a stable structure for sustained large-scale strategic investment.

### Case 4: Thrift-store chain — mission-alignment recovery (Ch. 6 sidebar)
- **Problem**: New president inherited an organization on solid financial footing but with a baffling range of WIG candidates: continue marketing/operations, hire more workers with disabilities, pursue growth.
- **Methodology applied**: Reframed the selection question against the mission ("promote self-reliance among workers with disabilities"). Asked: given solid financial/operational base, where would the highest-impact breakthrough be relative to mission? Converged on increasing the number of workers with disabilities placed in sustainable jobs *outside* the organization.
- **Conclusion**: The mission-alignment test (Trap #4) is a final filter: among 2x2 high-and-right candidates, prefer the one most directly serving mission. Financial WIGs that drift from mission cause organizations to "achieve their goals but lose their soul."
- **Outcome**: WIG transformed the organization while sustaining day-to-day operations.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will a team leader need this skill?

1. The team has not yet been handed a cascaded org WIG and the leader is choosing the team's own Primary WIG from scratch.
2. The leader has 5+ "important" strategic candidates and cannot converge.
3. The leader's current Primary WIG feels too broad ("total revenue", "operational excellence") and is producing no traction — needs to *go smaller*.
4. The leader is debating Approach A vs B (one Primary WIG vs two across segments) and needs the structural call.
5. The leadership team is comfortable ranking *importance* but skipping the *feasibility / at-risk* axis — needs forcing onto the 2x2.

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- EN: "Help me pick our team's Primary WIG" / "where should our team focus this year" / "we have too many strategic goals" / "what's our team's WIG" / "I'm leading a team and need to choose one breakthrough goal"
- JP: 「チームの Primary WIG をどう選ぶ？」「戦略候補が多すぎてチームの焦点が決まらない」「今年チームはどこに集中すべき？」「うちのチームの WIG は何にすべきか」
- zh-TW: 「幫我們團隊選 Primary WIG」「我們有太多戰略目標想做」「團隊今年該專注哪一個」「我帶團隊，要選一個突破目標」

### Non-trigger phrasings (do NOT activate)

- "I want to pick MY personal goal" → use `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining` (solo, no team)
- "My CTO already set our org WIG to X. What's my team WIG?" → use `4dx-d1-team-wig-cascade` (translation, not selection)
- "I'm a team member, not the lead, and I want to understand why we picked this WIG" → use `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension` (comprehension, not selection)
- "Should we adopt 4DX at all?" → route to `4dx-meta-personal-strategy-triage` (or team-equivalent if available) first
- "We already picked our Primary WIG and need lead measures" → route to D2

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`: that skill is for an individual contributor or solo operator with no team to align. This skill assumes the user is a team leader choosing a Primary WIG that the team will execute together. The 2x2 is collaborative, not introspective.
- vs. `4dx-d1-team-wig-cascade`: cascade *translates* an already-set org Primary WIG into a Team WIG using the ensure-not-support test. This skill *selects* the Primary WIG itself when no org WIG has been handed down (or the leader is the org-level decider).
- vs. member-comprehension skills: those are for team members whose leader picked the WIG and who want to understand the rationale. This skill is for the leader making the pick.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent runs a **consultant-to-leader dialogue** with the team leader. Walk them through brainstorm → 2x2 placement → convergence. **Do not pick the WIG for them — facilitate the leadership team's pick.**

1. **Frame the team's mission and strategic context.**
   - Ask: "Briefly — what is your team's mission, and what's the strategic context for this WIG cycle? (1-2 sentences each.) Is there an org-level WIG above you, or are you the top of the chain for this team?"
   - Completion criterion: mission stated; strategic context noted; cascade-vs-selection confirmed (if cascade, redirect to `4dx-d1-team-wig-cascade`).

2. **Brainstorm 5-10 candidate Battles.**
   - Ask: "If every other area of your team's operation stayed at its current level of performance, in which *one area* would a breakthrough matter most? Generate 5-10 candidates — go broad, no filtering yet. Each should be a possible breakthrough goal, not an activity."
   - Completion criterion: ≥5 written candidates, each phrased as a breakthrough outcome (not an activity, not a stroke-of-pen action).
   - Halt condition: if user produces only 1-2 candidates, push back: "Generate at least 5. Premature convergence kills the 2x2."

3. **Score each candidate on Importance (1-5).**
   - Ask: "For each candidate: if you achieved a breakthrough on this, would your team's situation be *transformed* (5), *significantly improved* (3-4), or just *incrementally better* (1-2)? Score each 1-5."
   - Completion criterion: every candidate has an Importance score with a one-line justification.

4. **Score each candidate on Feasibility (1-5).**
   - Ask: "For each candidate: in 1-2 years, can the team meaningfully move the needle on this *through behavior change you control*, not via external authority or budget? 5 = team owns ≥80% of the lever. 1 = mostly external. Score 1-5."
   - Completion criterion: every candidate has a Feasibility score with a one-line justification. Forcing this axis is the value of the exercise — leaders skip it.

5. **Plot the 2x2 (text grid is fine).**
   - Render the 2x2 as a labeled text grid with each candidate placed by its (Importance, Feasibility) coordinates. Example:
     ```
     High Imp │  [parked]    │  [WIG candidates]
              │              │
     Low Imp  │  [drop]      │  [whirlwind / nice-to-haves]
              └──────────────┴────────────────────────
                Low Feas       High Feas
     ```
   - Completion criterion: visual placement done; user can see clusters.

6. **Filter to upper-right (WIG candidates).**
   - Ask: "Of the candidates in the upper-right cell (high importance × high feasibility), which one — if won alone — would have the greatest *ripple effect* on the others? (Covenant Transport: turnover lifted on-time delivery + safety + financials simultaneously.)"
   - Completion criterion: one candidate identified as ripple-effect leader, OR honest acknowledgement that two distinct segments require Approach B.

7. **Single vs twin Primary WIG decision (Approach A vs B).**
   - Ask: "Does your team operate as one functionally-unified group, or does it have two structurally separate segments (e.g. R&D pipeline + commercial access; build-side + run-side)? Approach A = one Primary WIG (default). Approach B = two Primary WIGs, one per segment, only if segments are genuinely non-overlapping. Reject Approach B if you're tempted by it just to make people feel included."
   - Completion criterion: Approach A or B chosen with rationale. Total team WIGs cap = ≤3, with 1 (Approach A) preferred and 2 (Approach B) acceptable for genuinely segmented teams.

8. **Four-trap checklist (Ch 6 hard gate).**
   - Run each candidate-Primary-WIG against:
     - Trap 1 — too many? (≤2 Primary WIGs total; ≤3 team WIGs ever)
     - Trap 2 — too broad? (no "total revenue", "operational excellence", "overall market share")
     - Trap 3 — aspirational but unmeasurable? (must pass *From X to Y by When*)
     - Trap 4 — disconnected from mission? (must serve mission stated in step 1)
   - Halt condition: any trap triggered → return to step 6 with narrower / more measurable / mission-aligned candidate.

9. **Express in *From X to Y by When* form.**
   - For each chosen Primary WIG, write the lag-measure finish line: **"From [X numeric current] to [Y numeric target] by [calendar date]"**.
   - If the leader needs help on the formulation step, optionally hand off to `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining` (steps 2-5 of that skill's Execution section apply equally to a team WIG's lag measure) or run those steps inline.
   - Completion criterion: each Primary WIG passes the *stranger test* — "could a stranger tell on the deadline whether the team won or lost?"

10. **Output: WIG + parked Battles list.**
    - Final output: (a) 1-2 Primary WIGs in *From X to Y by When* form; (b) parked Battles list — the high-importance / low-feasibility candidates that were dropped from this cycle, kept visible for re-evaluation when feasibility shifts; (c) explicit Approach declaration (A or B); (d) confirmation that the four traps are clear and the WIG is mission-aligned.
    - Hand-off: if the leader now needs to translate this Primary WIG down to sub-team Battles, route to `4dx-d1-team-wig-cascade`. If they need lead measures next, route to D2.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Solo / individual goal definition** — use `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`. The 2x2 here is a leadership-team artifact; running it alone collapses to ranking-by-vibes. (NOT for solo / individual user — use `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`.)
- **Team-member-level WIG comprehension** — when a team member who didn't pick the WIG is trying to understand why their leader chose it, do not re-run selection. Use `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`. (NOT for team members who didn't pick the WIG — use `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`.)
- **Cascading an already-set org Primary WIG** — if the org WIG is fixed and the question is "what's my team's slice", that's translation, not selection. Use `4dx-d1-team-wig-cascade`.
- **Stroke-of-pen organizational decisions** — restructuring, vendor switch, hiring decision. The Strategy Map's *left column* by definition does not need 4DX.
- **Reactive / on-call team domains** — SRE incident teams, ER medicine, customer support — where the whirlwind *is* the strategic work. Manufacturing a parallel Primary WIG competes destructively with the work that matters.
- **Pre-strategy organizations** — if the team has no defined mission/vision yet, the four-trap checklist's Trap 4 cannot be evaluated. Define mission first; then return.

### Author-warned failure modes (Ch 6 four traps + verified-team additions)

- **Trap 1 — too many Primary WIGs (CE-03 / P-26)**: "We've decided to make everything a WIG." Most seductive, most destructive. Cap is 2 Primary WIGs (only via Approach B, only with genuinely separate segments).
- **Trap 2 — Primary WIG too broad (CE-04 / P-26)**: "Total revenue", "operational excellence", "overall market share". These encompass every stroke-of-pen + whirlwind activity. "Trying to hit a bullet with a bullet." Rescue: go smaller (Sydney accounting case).
- **Trap 3 — aspirational but unmeasurable (CE-07 / P-26)**: "Become the preferred provider", "lead the industry in innovation", "earn world-class loyalty." Must pass *From X to Y by When*. "Concepts stir the imagination, but targets drive performance."
- **Trap 4 — disconnected from mission (P-45 / P-26)**: A Primary WIG that's measurable + feasible + impactful but mission-disconnected produces an organization that "achieves its goals but loses its soul." Always re-test against mission (thrift-store case).
- **P-44 — clarity sacrificed for inclusion**: Broadening the Primary WIG so every team-member feels "included" undermines focus. Inclusion is a separate problem; do not solve it by polluting the WIG.
- **Most-important confusion (CE-05)**: Picking the WIG by ranking strategic categories ("growth > retention > efficiency") instead of asking "where would breakthrough have biggest leverage?" The book's selection rule is leverage, not category-rank.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations (apply with care)

- **Selection bias (CE-30)**: All FranklinCovey case studies are paying clients; failed Primary WIG choices are rarely analyzed. Don't auto-import another organization's Primary WIG — your team's importance × feasibility map is yours.
- **Goal-discovery vs goal-execution**: Ch 6 assumes the right Primary WIG is *discoverable* via 2x2 plus debate. In genuinely uncertain domains (early-stage product, novel R&D), the right WIG may not be knowable yet. Run small experiments first; don't lock into a Primary WIG via 2x2 if the inputs to feasibility scoring are themselves speculation.
- **High-and-right is not automatic**: Ch 6 explicitly warns: "We are not suggesting that your Primary WIG should always be chosen from the candidates that are 'high and right'." Resources, ripple effect, and mission alignment can override 2x2 placement.
- **Approach B over-application**: Approach B (twin Primary WIGs) is structurally tempting because it "covers more." Justified only by genuinely separate segments. If you can describe both proposed Primary WIGs as serving the same operational arm, you're really in Approach A and just haven't picked yet.

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **OKRs**: O+KR allows 3-5 objectives × 3-5 key results = up to 25 simultaneous metrics. WIG protocol caps at 1-2 Primary WIGs total per team. Do not let "OKR experience" smuggle in multi-objective drift.
- **Hoshin Kanri X-matrix**: similar deployment logic but tolerates broader strategic objectives at the top. WIG demands the four-trap discipline (especially Trap 2 — too broad).
- **BSC strategic objectives**: balanced scorecard's four-perspective model is closer to Approach C (multiple strategy lines) than Approach A (single Primary WIG). 4DX collapses to fewer because the cost of focus dilution exceeds the benefit of perspective coverage.
- **SMART goals (team variant)**: surface form looks identical but SMART permits incremental "achievable" goals; WIG demands *breakthrough* (transformation, not improvement) plus the four-trap filter.

### Industry-experience addendum (strategy literature beyond 2x2)

The 2x2 + four-trap filters for measurability and mission, but a candidate clearing all four can still be strategically empty (the Sydney "total revenue" failure). Three tests from the strategy canon. Primary citations in `references/industry-grounding.md`:

- **Porter, *Competitive Strategy* (Free Press, 1980)** — "The essence of strategy is choosing what *not* to do." Test: name 2+ parked Battles that receive demonstrably less attention as a *result* of this WIG. If none, it's a wish list.
- **Rumelt, *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy* (Crown, 2011)** — kernel = diagnosis + guiding policy + coherent action. Most bad strategy is weak diagnosis or goal-strategy confusion. Test: pair the WIG with a one-sentence diagnosis ("the central challenge is X"). If the leader cannot articulate it, return to brainstorm.
- **Hambrick & Fredrickson, "Are You Sure You Have a Strategy?" (*Acad Mgmt Exec* 15/4, 2001)** — strategy diamond: arenas / vehicles / differentiators / staging / economic logic. Test: working answers to all five. Missing staging or economic logic = WIG is premature.

**Mintzberg caveat (*Rise and Fall of Strategic Planning*, 1994)** — in genuinely uncertain domains, strategy is partly emergent; shorten WIG cycle to one quarter rather than lock for 1-2 years on speculative feasibility scores.
