# 4DX Discipline 2 — Lead Measure Discovery

## R — Reading

> "Lead measures track the critical activities that drive, or lead to, the lag
> measure. ... A good lead measure has two primary characteristics: It's
> predictive ... and it's influenceable."
>
> — McChesney et al., Chapter 3

---

## I — Interpretation

A goal (the lag measure: weight, revenue, finished novel) tells you whether
you've already won or lost. By the time it moves, the behavior that moved it
is in the past. Lead measures are the upstream behaviors you can act on TODAY
that the goal depends on.

The discovery rule has exactly two axes, and both must be true:

1. **Predictive** — when this measure moves, the lag measure later moves with
   it. There is a causal chain you can articulate in one sentence.
2. **Influenceable** — you, personally, with no permission required, can move
   this measure by doing something this week.

Most metrics fail one axis. Stock-market returns predict your portfolio P&L
but you can't influence them — fail. Drinking 2L water daily is influenceable
but doesn't predict whether you publish your novel — fail. Weekly weight is
neither lead nor influenceable — it's the lag on a shorter clock.

A lead measure is also typically counter-intuitive. The shoe-store study of
4,500 outlets found that *measuring children's feet* was the highest-leverage
behavior — not friendliness, not price, not stock width. The obvious answers
are usually wrong; the leverage point hides one layer below.

Aim for 2-3 lead measures total. One frequency-based ("do X N times per
week") and one quality-based ("ensure each X meets standard Y") is the
canonical pair. More than three and you've recreated the whirlwind.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Shoe retailer, 4,500 stores
- **Problem**: WIG was per-store sales growth. Hundreds of plausible lead
  candidates (friendliness, conversion, basket size, foot traffic, stock
  width).
- **Methodology applied**: Statistical leverage analysis across stores
  asking "which behavior, when present, predicts higher sales AND can a
  clerk control it daily?"
- **Conclusion**: *Measuring a child's feet for fit* — single
  highest-correlated behavior. Both predictive (correctly fitted shoes →
  parent buys + returns) and fully influenceable (any clerk can do it).
- **Outcome**: Counterintuitive lead became the new training standard;
  validated as leverage point across the chain.

### Case 2: Younger Brothers Construction — safety
- **Problem**: WIG was reducing serious safety incidents (lag = injuries).
  Tracking incidents only tells you after someone's hurt.
- **Methodology applied**: Distilled compliance to six observable
  behaviors (hard hats, eye protection, scaffolding, etc.); supervisors
  walked sites and physically logged compliance.
- **Conclusion**: Behavioral compliance both predicted incidents and was
  daily-influenceable by every worker.
- **Outcome**: Incidents fell from 57 to 12 per year. The lead was harder
  to track than the lag (P-11: pay the price), but tracking it was the
  whole point.

### Case 3: Towne Park valet — retrieval time
- **Problem**: Hotel guest-satisfaction WIG. Time-to-retrieve-car was a
  candidate lag, but daily behavior was unclear.
- **Methodology applied**: Identified retrieval time itself as the lead
  for satisfaction; built measurement infrastructure even though it was
  awkward to capture in real time.
- **Conclusion**: Predictive (faster retrieval → higher satisfaction
  scores) AND influenceable (running, route optimization, staging).
- **Outcome**: Engagement and satisfaction both rose; one team member
  even broke down a concrete wall on a Saturday because the new lead
  measure made the bottleneck visible and actionable.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. User has a clearly-defined WIG (From X to Y by When) but is staring at
   it not knowing what to do MONDAY.
2. User has been "working hard" on a goal for weeks but the lag measure
   isn't moving — they're acting on noise, not leverage.
3. User has named a "lead measure" that's actually the lag-on-a-shorter-
   clock (weekly weight, monthly revenue) or that they can't influence
   (market direction, competitor moves).
4. User is about to install a tracking dashboard with 20+ metrics and
   needs to be cut down to the 2-3 that actually drive the goal.
5. User asks "what's a leading indicator for X?" — generic answer would
   miss the influenceable axis.

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- "I have the goal but I don't know what to do every day."
- "What should I track to know I'm on the right path?"
- "What's a leading indicator for [goal]?"
- "I'm working hard but nothing's moving — what am I missing?"
- "How do I know if my daily actions are the right ones?"
- 「目標は決まったが、毎日何をすればいい？」
- 「先行指標って何を選べばいい？」
- 「頑張ってるのに数字が動かない、何が間違ってる？」
- 「我有目標了，但不知道每天該做什麼才會有結果。」
- 「該追蹤什麼指標才知道我走在對的路上？」
- 「我以為我在做對的事，但結果沒變化。」

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`: D1 produces the goal (lag measure in From X
  to Y by When form). D2 produces the daily behaviors that drive it.
  If the user can't yet state their WIG in one sentence, redirect to D1
  first — D2 with a vague WIG produces noise.
- vs. `4dx-d3-personal-scoreboard`: D3 is the visual artifact that DISPLAYS
  the lead+lag once chosen. D2 is the upstream choice of WHICH measures.
- vs. `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`: D4 is the weekly cadence that REVIEWS lead
  performance. D2 is the one-time discovery that picks them.
- vs. `4dx-sustain-personal-momentum-rescue`: If lead measures exist but aren't
  moving the lag after 4+ weeks, that skill diagnoses with the five
  optimizing questions. D2 is for first-time selection.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent runs the user through Socratic
discovery — never inventing leads on their behalf.

1. **WIG gate check**
   - Ask: "State your WIG in one sentence: from X to Y by when."
   - Completion criterion: user produces a single sentence with current
     state, target state, and deadline.
   - Halt condition: if the user can't produce this, STOP and redirect
     to `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`. Do NOT proceed — leads built on a vague
     WIG are noise.

2. **Brainstorm candidate behaviors (5-10)**
   - Prompt: "What daily or weekly behaviors do you BELIEVE would drive
     this WIG? Don't filter — list 5-10 candidates."
   - Push for counter-intuitive options: "What's something nobody else
     would think of? What's the equivalent of 'measure children's feet'
     for your goal?"
   - Completion criterion: ≥5 candidate behaviors written down.

3. **Two-axis scoring (1-5 × 1-5)**
   - For each candidate, ask the user to score:
     - **Predictive (1-5)**: "If you do this consistently, how confident
       are you the lag measure will move? Articulate the causal chain in
       one sentence."
     - **Influenceable (1-5)**: "Can you, alone, with no permission,
       move this measure THIS WEEK?"
   - Completion criterion: every candidate has both scores + a
     one-sentence causal chain for predictive.

4. **Drop anything <4 on either axis**
   - Anything scoring ≤3 on predictive: noise (e.g. "drink water").
   - Anything scoring ≤3 on influenceable: rainfall-class (e.g. "market
     conditions improve").
   - Completion criterion: only 4-5★ candidates remain on both axes.

5. **Select 2-3, prefer one frequency + one quality**
   - Prompt: "Pick 2-3 — at most. Ideally one frequency-based (do X N
     times per week) and one quality-based (each X meets standard Y)."
   - Halt condition: if user picks >3, force a cut. More than three
     recreates the whirlwind.
   - Completion criterion: 2-3 named lead measures.

6. **Operational definition**
   - For each selected lead measure, write:
     - What exactly counts as "done" (not vague — "30 min focused
       writing with phone in another room", not "write more").
     - How and when it gets logged.
     - Weekly target number.
   - Completion criterion: each lead has a definition another person
     could apply without asking clarifying questions.

7. **Predict the causal chain (in writing)**
   - User writes one paragraph: "If I hit my lead targets every week
     for 6 weeks, here's why I expect the lag to have moved by [Y]:
     ..."
   - This is a falsifiable forecast — anchor for D4 review.
   - Completion criterion: written paragraph linking lead activity →
     lag movement with explicit mechanism.

8. **Output**
   - 2-3 named lead measures, each with operational definition + weekly
     target + causal-chain rationale. Hand off to `4dx-d3-personal-
     scoreboard` for display and `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session` for weekly review.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **No defined WIG yet** — leads need a lag to be predictive of. Redirect
  to `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`.
- **Stroke-of-pen goals** — if the goal is achieved by a single
  decision/purchase (buy a sit-stand desk, sign a contract), there is
  no daily behavior to discover. Lead measures are for behavioral-change
  goals only.
- **Domains where the lag is structurally unmeasurable on a weekly clock**
  — true scientific R&D, novel-writing, drug discovery — the
  lead-to-lag feedback loop may run in months/years and weekly leads
  become theatrical.

### Author-warned failure modes (from cluster CEs)

- **Lag-masquerading-as-lead (CE-01)** — "weekly weight" tracked as a
  lead for "lose 10kg by year-end" is just the lag on a shorter clock,
  not a leverage point. Real leads: daily caloric deficit logged,
  weekly cardio sessions completed.
- **Predictive but not influenceable (CE-02)** — "rainfall predicts
  crop yield, but no farm worker can make it rain." Stock direction,
  industry tailwinds, "the economy" — disqualified regardless of
  predictive power.
- **Lead-data-too-hard-so-skipped (CE-17)** — lead data is almost
  always harder to collect than lag data. Author warning: if you give
  up on tracking the lead because it's awkward, you've abandoned D2 —
  you're back to staring at the lag (Towne Park paid the price; valet
  retrieval time was awkward to time, but they did it anyway).
- **Leader-only game (CE-20)** — the leader runs around personally
  doing the lead measure while the team watches. In personal use this
  shows up as "I'll just do it perfectly when I have time" — the
  lead must be doable in the user's actual normal week, not in an
  imagined ideal one.
- **Lead performed but not at sufficient quality (CE-21)** — user hits
  the frequency count but quality is hollow ("I made the 3 cold
  calls" — but didn't follow the script). Quality-based leads exist
  precisely to catch this.
- **Goodhart / measure-gaming (CE-24, book gap)** — once a lead
  measure becomes a target with visible reward, it gets gamed. Wells
  Fargo employees opened fake accounts because cross-sell was the
  lead. In personal use: "logged 30 min writing" might mean 30 min
  staring at the screen. Defend by pairing frequency + quality leads,
  and by checking the *causal chain* every 4 weeks: if leads are green
  but lag is flat, the lead is being gamed or was wrong.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations

- **Operational-work bias** — author's leverage examples are almost all
  operational (call centers, hotels, plants, retail). Knowledge-work
  and creative-work leads (where output is ambiguous and feedback loops
  are long) are under-explored. Apply the two-axis test rigorously
  rather than copying retail examples.
- **Survival-bias in the "any operation has a discoverable leverage
  point" claim** — for some goals (genuinely novel work) no validated
  lead may exist. The book doesn't say "if you can't find one in 60
  minutes, give up" — but for personal use, that's the honest
  fallback: revert to consistent execution of best-guess behaviors and
  re-examine in 4 weeks.

### Easily-confused neighboring concepts

- **"Leading indicator" (generic finance/ops usage)** — usually means
  "anything predictive." Lead measure (4DX usage) requires
  influenceable as well. Reject any candidate that's only predictive.
- **"Habit" or "routine"** — a habit is a behavior; a lead measure is
  a *measurement* of a behavior tied to a specific WIG. A daily
  meditation habit is not a lead measure unless you can articulate how
  it predicts a specific lag.
- **OKR Key Results** — KRs can be either lag or lead in 4DX terms;
  4DX is stricter. If the user has KRs already, ask whether each one
  is lag (the result) or lead (the behavior driving the result), and
  whether the latter passes both axes.

### Enterprise-misfire to avoid (re-anchor to personal scale)

In enterprise rollouts, leads sometimes get picked top-down by
executives without frontline input — the team then "complies" without
believing in the causal chain (CE-15 compliance-without-commitment).
The personal-scale equivalent: the user accepts a lead measure
suggested by the agent without internalizing why it should work. Step
7 (write the causal chain in your own words) exists specifically to
prevent this — if the user can't write the paragraph, they don't
own the lead, and it will be abandoned within 3 weeks.

### Industry-experience addendum (Goodhart failure cases)

The book treats lead measures as a clean methodology and gives
measure-gaming only CE-24. Real-world deployments that passed the
two-axis test cleanly still collapsed once stakes were attached. Three
canonical cases — primary citations in `references/industry-grounding.md`:

- **Wells Fargo cross-selling (2011–2016)** — "products per household"
  lead drove ~3.5M fake accounts; CFPB et al. fined $185M (Sep 8 2016).
- **Phoenix VA wait-time (exposed 2014)** — 14-day-scheduling lead drove
  unofficial wait lists hiding ~115-day real waits; VA OIG report
  14-02603-267 (Aug 26 2014) found 1,700+ patients never enrolled.
- **Atlanta Public Schools (2009–2015)** — year-over-year CRCT-score
  lead drove systematic answer-changing; 11 of 12 defendants convicted
  under Georgia RICO on Apr 1 2015 (longest trial in state history).

The personal-coach implication: when YOU attach high stakes to a
self-set lead measure ("books read per month", "30 min daily writing",
"5 cold calls per day"), watch for self-Goodharting — speed-reading
without comprehension, screen-staring counted as writing, voicemails
counted as calls. Always pair frequency with quality (Step 5) and
re-check the causal chain (Step 7) every 4 weeks: if leads are green
but the lag is flat, the lead is being gamed or was wrong.
