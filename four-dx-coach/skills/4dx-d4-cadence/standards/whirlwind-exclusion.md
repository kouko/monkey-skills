# Standard: Whirlwind exclusion — no operational discussion in session

## Statement

The WIG Session admits **only** the WIG and its lead measures. The
daily whirlwind — operational drama, urgent firefighting, status
updates on non-WIG work, "let me tell you how busy my week was" —
is excluded. When whirlwind invades, redirect politely without apology:
"Park that — let's keep this on the WIG. Was the commitment kept or
not?" The rule is the rule.

## Source

> The whirlwind is never allowed into a WIG Session.
>
> — McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 5

> If you are facing an issue that has come up in the whirlwind, that's a topic for another time and another type of meeting.
>
> — *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 15

## Why it matters

Whirlwind invasion (CE-10) is the single most common failure mode of
the WIG Session. It defeats the entire mechanism in three ways:

1. **Time leakage** — a 25-minute session that admits whirlwind
   becomes a 60-minute status meeting, then becomes a meeting people
   skip when busy.
2. **Attention dilution** — the lead measure is what the cadence is
   *for*. When operational drama crowds it out, the team stops
   thinking about the lead and starts thinking about whatever was
   loudest this week.
3. **Commit-forward collapse** — once the conversation drifts into
   reporting on the whirlwind, the Plan segment shrinks or vanishes
   and the session reverts to report-out shape (Lencioni's Weekly
   Tactical without the 4DX overlay = a status meeting).

The redirect must be done **without apology**. Apologetic redirects
("sorry, I know this is awkward, but…") signal the agenda is
optional. The clean form — "Park that. Was the commitment kept?" —
holds the agenda's authority without making it personal. Lencioni's
meeting taxonomy gives the redirect a destination: "let's take that
to our daily check-in" / "that's a Monthly Strategic question".

## Application across modes

| Mode | How this standard applies |
|---|---|
| **Solo** | Agent enforces. When user starts narrating operational drama, agent interrupts: "Hold that — let's keep the WIG Session on the WIG. Was that commitment kept or not?" No apology. The rule applies even though there's no peer in the room — the user is the one whose attention needs to be held to the WIG. |
| **Team-leader** | Leader enforces. Coach reminder: "Do this without apology. The rule is the rule." When a member tries to bring operational issues, leader redirects to a separate meeting type. Hardest variant: when leader themselves drifts (often via "by the way, the project I mentioned…") — coach must call this out as a precondition for the team to take the rule seriously. |
| **Member-prep** | Member prepares to *resist* whirlwind invasion in their own commitment. Filter #2 (lead-measure-moving) explicitly screens out whirlwind-disguised-as-commitment. Coach prompt: "the most urgent task this week is not automatically a WIG commitment". |
| **Member-debrief** | If the cause of a miss in Q3 is "the whirlwind ate my week", the protocol explicitly does NOT accept that as a final answer. Reclaim agency: "What's the part of that within your control that you didn't do?" — gently because the member is owning the miss, but firmly because excuse-mode drains team trust. |

In all four modes the rule is structurally identical: the WIG Session
(or the prep / debrief work that bookends it) operates at lead-measure
scope only. Whirlwind topics belong in a separate meeting type, a
separate conversation, or a separate work block. Co-mingling them
collapses the cadence within 3-4 weeks.
