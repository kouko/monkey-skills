# Standard: Commitment shape — specific + influenceable + binary

## Statement

A WIG Session commitment must pass five filters. Failing any one
disqualifies it; the shape must be repaired before it counts:

1. **Specific** — verifiable on Saturday morning. "Call three customers
   Tue + Thu" passes; "focus on outreach" fails.
2. **Lead-measure-moving** — directly moves the team's (or solo user's)
   lead measure. Most-urgent whirlwind task ≠ commitment.
3. **Within own control** — entirely within the user's power. "I'll get
   marketing to sign off" fails; "I'll send marketing the brief with a
   Friday-EOD ask" passes.
4. **Self-chosen** — proposed by the participant, not dictated by leader
   / agent / coach. Ownership is the load-bearing mechanism.
5. **One-or-two cap** — never 3+. Five at 50% kept ≪ 1-2 at 95% kept.

Output is **binary** at debrief time: kept / partial / missed.
Effort isn't the unit; deliverable is. Late-by-a-day or shipped-with-
regression = partial, not kept.

## Source

>
> — McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 5 (P-22)

> Your XPS standard for fulfilling commitments should be 90 percent (or higher).
>
> — *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 10 (P-39)

> Each week, every team member answers the question: "What are the one or two most important things I can do this week to impact the lead measures?"
>
> — *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 5

## Why it matters

The shape is what makes commitment-keeping work mechanically:

- **Specific** lets the participant verify themselves on Saturday and
  lets peers hold them accountable on Monday. Vague commitments never
  fail and never pass — they erode trust currency invisibly.
- **Lead-moving** keeps the cadence anchored to the lever, not the
  whirlwind. Without it, the WIG Session becomes a weekly to-do list
  and the lag never moves.
- **Own control** is what makes the commitment a real commitment. "I'll
  get Bob to approve" is a wish, not a promise.
- **Self-chosen** protects the autonomy that makes commitment-keeping
  intrinsically motivating. Boss-assigned commitments degrade to
  compliance and stop generating learning.
- **1-2 cap** is the empirical finding the book defends repeatedly:
  more commitments doesn't mean more output; it means more half-done
  and a collapsed signal.
- **Binary classification** is what gives the team a real signal each
  week. Rounding up ("I kind of did it") collapses the integrity
  currency and makes the next week's plan worse.

Cialdini's commitment-and-consistency principle (*Influence*, 2007)
explains why the shape matters at the moment of voicing: once said
publicly, the commitment is locked. A bad-shape commitment locks just
as tight as a good-shape one — so the only window to repair the shape
is *before* it gets said aloud.

## Application across modes

| Mode | How this standard applies |
|---|---|
| **Solo** | Agent runs the five filters in Step 4 (Plan). Pushes back Socratically on each failure ("how would you know on Saturday whether you did that?"). Cap at 1-2; reject dependencies; force self-chosen. Binary classification applied at next week's Account segment. |
| **Team-leader** | Leader applies the five filters as the **specificity gate** in Step 5. Veto-not-dictate: when a filter fails, leader says "that doesn't pass — re-propose" and waits. Adds a 6th implicit filter (Ch 15): not the same rote commitment for ≥3 consecutive weeks. |
| **Member-prep** | Step 4 is the **core** of this protocol — apply all five filters privately, before the room hears the words. Cialdini's commitment-lock means this is the only window where shape can still be tuned. Pfeffer adds a political-signal layer (legibility to leader); Drucker adds strengths-fit; both inside the five-filter band. |
| **Member-debrief** | P-22 binary test in Q2: did the commitment have a specific deliverable, and did it happen on the committed window? Anti-rounding-up prompts in Step 3 are calibrated against Eurich's 95/15 self-awareness gap. Q4 branches by which filter the commitment likely violated upstream (specificity / scope / capacity / mechanism). |

The standard is the same across all four modes; what differs is *who
applies it* (agent / leader / member to themselves) and *when in the
cycle* (commitment-time / prep / live session / debrief).
