# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-d4-cadence (multi-scope)

Consolidated primary-source citations across all four protocols (solo /
team-leader / member-prep / member-debrief). Each section names the
source, the protocol(s) that reference it, and a 2-3 sentence summary
of the mechanism it grounds.

The book is leader-POV throughout chapters 5/10/15. The sources below
fill the gaps where the book is thin — solo-user adaptation
(Reinertsen, Rogelberg, Lencioni); facilitator stance (Lencioni,
Edmondson); member-side commitment selection (Pfeffer, Drucker,
Cialdini); member-side honest self-account (Eurich, Edmondson,
Wiseman).

---

## Source 1: Rogelberg, *Surprising Science of Meetings* (Oxford UP, 2019)

**Citation**: Steven G. Rogelberg, *The Surprising Science of Meetings:
How You Can Lead Your Team to Peak Performance* (Oxford University
Press, 2019, ISBN 978-0-19-068921-6, 180 pp.).

**Used by**: `solo-session.md`, indirectly all protocols (cadence shape).

**Mechanism**: Synthesizes ~15 years of empirical meeting-science
research (field surveys, lab studies, planted-confederate experiments)
on >5,000 employees. Three findings bear directly on the WIG Session:
(1) shorter time-boxes raise per-minute effectiveness — 25-min default
beats 60-min default by Parkinson dynamics; (2) **agenda discipline
(single explicit purpose per meeting) is the single largest predictor
of perceived effectiveness**; (3) "meeting recovery syndrome" — most
workers need 30-60 min to re-enter focused work after a meeting —
argues for fewer, shorter, agenda-tight meetings. Won the Humboldt
Prize for meeting-science research. Empirical warrant for the WIG
Session's 20-30 min single-WIG shape.

Links: [OUP product page](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-surprising-science-of-meetings-9780190689216), [Personnel Psychology review](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/peps.12475)

## Source 2: Lencioni, *Death by Meeting* (Jossey-Bass, 2004)

**Citation**: Patrick Lencioni, *Death by Meeting: A Leadership Fable
About Solving the Most Painful Problem in Business* (Jossey-Bass, 2004,
ISBN 978-0-7879-6805-2).

**Used by**: `solo-session.md` (taxonomic warrant), `team-leader-session.md`
(meeting-type taxonomy + facilitation moves).

**Mechanism**: The canonical meeting-type taxonomy — Daily Check-In
(~5 min) / **Weekly Tactical** (45-90 min) / Monthly Strategic
(2-4 hr) / Quarterly Off-Site Review. The WIG Session is structurally
a Weekly Tactical with a stricter single-WIG focus and shorter 20-30
min budget. Lencioni's three Weekly-Tactical structural elements map
almost one-to-one to the WIG Session:

| Lencioni Weekly Tactical | 4DX WIG Session segment |
|---|---|
| Lightning round (each person 1 min, 2-3 priorities) | Account on last week's commitment |
| Progress review (critical metrics) | Review the scoreboard |
| Real-time agenda (priorities of the week) | Plan: make this week's commitment |

What 4DX *adds* on top of Lencioni: **whirlwind-exclusion rule**,
**peer commitment-making** (commit-forward instead of report-out), and
**45-90 min → 20-30 min compression** because whirlwind is excluded.
The "treating session as report-out" failure mode is exactly running a
Lencioni Weekly Tactical without the 4DX overlay.

Links: [Publisher product page (Table Group)](https://www.tablegroup.com/product/dbm/), ["Four Meetings" white paper PDF](https://ttg-wp.s3.amazonaws.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/14163149/Avoid-Death-by-Meetings.pdf), [O'Reilly excerpt](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/death-by-meeting/9780787968052/pr10.html)

## Source 3: Reinertsen, *Principles of Product Development Flow* (2009)

**Citation**: Donald G. Reinertsen, *The Principles of Product
Development Flow: Second Generation Lean Product Development*
(Celeritas Publishing, 2009, ISBN 978-1-935401-00-1).

**Used by**: `solo-session.md` (cadence-vs-flow tradeoff for solo
adaptation), `standards/sacred-cadence.md` (Cadence Principle F6).

**Mechanism**: Principle F6 (Cadence Principle): "Cadence is the use
of a regular predictable rhythm within a process." Reinertsen shows
mathematically that *fixed cadence transforms unpredictable events
into predictable ones* and *prevents variability from accumulating in
sequential processes* — the deep mechanism the book asserts but
doesn't explain. Reinertsen also flags the tradeoff the book ignores:
cadence is overhead-positive only when batch size and feedback latency
are reasonably matched. For domains with very long lag-measure
feedback loops (multi-month R&D, drug discovery, novel writing) the
weekly cadence becomes theatrical — Reinertsen's framework predicts
this exact failure mode. CF Center pattern (commit to *experiments*,
not outcomes) is the documented workaround.

Links: [Chapter 1 PDF (lpd2.com)](http://lpd2.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/06/ReinertsenFLOWChap1.pdf)

## Source 4: Edmondson, *Teaming* (Jossey-Bass, 2012)

**Citation**: Amy C. Edmondson, *Teaming: How Organizations Learn,
Innovate, and Compete in the Knowledge Economy* (Jossey-Bass, 2012,
ISBN 978-0-7879-7093-2).

**Used by**: `team-leader-session.md` (psychological safety as
precondition for the three-step Respect/Accountability/Encourage),
`standards/sacred-cadence.md` (cadence consistency as safety signal).

**Mechanism**: Defines **psychological safety** as the shared belief
that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — admitting a
mistake, asking a "stupid" question, proposing a half-formed idea,
pushing back on the leader without being punished or humiliated.
Necessary precondition for team learning; without it, defensive
routines prevent learning even when individuals are highly capable.
The book's three-step Respect → Accountability → Encourage protocol
(Ch 15) is, in Edmondson's terms, a **safety-preserving accountability
ritual**: separates the *act* (missed commitment) from the *person*
(still respected member). Skipping Respect or Encourage collapses the
protocol from safety-preserving to safety-eroding within 2-3 weeks.

Links: [HBS faculty record](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=42298), [Wiley listing](https://www.wiley.com/en-cn/Teaming:+How+Organizations+Learn,+Innovate,+and+Compete+in+the+Knowledge+Economy-p-9780787970932)

## Source 5: Edmondson, "Strategies for Learning from Failure" (HBR April 2011)

**Citation**: Amy C. Edmondson, "Strategies for Learning from Failure,"
*Harvard Business Review*, vol. 89, no. 4 (April 2011), pp. 48–55.

**Used by**: `member-debrief.md` (failure-type taxonomy in Q4 Step 5).

**Mechanism**: Three-category failure taxonomy: (a) **preventable
failures** in predictable operations (deviations from spec — *are*
blameworthy; lesson: "follow the spec"); (b) **unavoidable failures**
in complex systems (system-level interactions; lesson: "redesign the
system"); (c) **intelligent failures** at the frontier (experiments
that yielded information; lesson: "the experiment was the win"). The
book does not give the member a taxonomy of *what kind* of miss it
was. Edmondson's three categories give exactly that. Without this
taxonomy, all three collapse into "I missed" — and the redesign
decision in Step 6 has no information to act on.

## Source 6: Pfeffer, *Power* (HarperBusiness, 2010)

**Citation**: Jeffrey Pfeffer, *Power: Why Some People Have It — and
Others Don't* (HarperBusiness, 2010; ISBN 978-0-06-178908-3).

**Used by**: `member-prep.md` (political-signal layer).

**Mechanism**: Stanford GSB. Argues managing the relationship with
one's boss is at least as important as job performance, and visible
commitments are a primary relationship-management instrument. A
member's WIG commitment is *legible to the leader* in a way that
whirlwind work is not. A commitment too small signals timidity even
if technically lead-measure-moving; a commitment too big signals
either competence or recklessness depending on whether it's kept.
Pfeffer's research gives members a framework for picking commitments
that signal both team-loyalty (genuinely lead-measure-moving) and
personal capability (visibly ambitious within deliverable) — the
layer the book's "specific + within control + self-chosen" filters do
not address.

## Source 7: Drucker, "Managing Oneself" (HBR 1999)

**Citation**: Peter F. Drucker, "Managing Oneself," *Harvard Business
Review*, vol. 77, no. 2 (March–April 1999), pp. 64–74; reprint HBR
Classics R0501K (Jan 2005); book-form HBS Press 2008 (ISBN
978-1-4221-2312-6).

**Used by**: `member-prep.md` (strengths-fit selection layer).

**Mechanism**: "Concentrate on your strengths… place yourself where
your strengths can produce results." Among 1–3 candidate commitments
that all pass the book's five filters, the member should prefer the
strengths-aligned one over the development-stretch one. The book
treats all five-filter-passing commitments as equivalent; Drucker
argues they are not — strengths-aligned commitments are kept at far
higher rates and build cumulative capability. Development-stretch
commitments belong in a separate development conversation, not in the
weekly WIG cadence where the team is counting on the lead measure
moving.

## Source 8: Cialdini, *Influence* (1984; rev. ed. 2007)

**Citation**: Robert B. Cialdini, *Influence: The Psychology of
Persuasion* (rev. ed., HarperBusiness, 2007; ISBN 978-0-06-124189-5).

**Used by**: `member-prep.md` (commitment-and-consistency lock),
`standards/commitment-shape.md` (why prep window is the only window).

**Mechanism**: The commitment-and-consistency principle: humans have a
near-automatic drive to act consistently with what they have publicly
committed to, especially when the commitment is *active, public,
effortful, and freely chosen* — exactly the four properties of a
WIG-Session commitment. This is *why* the prep step matters: once a
member voices a commitment in front of peers, consistency pressure
binds them to it through the week. The same mechanism binds them to
a *bad* commitment just as tightly. The prep conversation is the only
window where the commitment can still be reshaped without paying the
inconsistency tax.

## Source 9: Eurich, *Insight* (Crown Business, 2017)

**Citation**: Tasha Eurich, *Insight: Why We're Not as Self-Aware as
We Think, and How Seeing Ourselves Clearly Helps Us Succeed at Work
and in Life* (Crown Business, 2017; ISBN 978-0-451-49681-2).

**Used by**: `member-debrief.md` (Step 3 anti-rounding-up prompts).

**Mechanism**: Research program across ~5,000 participants found that
while ~95% of people *believe* they are self-aware, only ~10–15%
actually meet a calibrated definition — the **"95/15 gap."** A member
doing the Q2 classification step (kept / partial / missed) sits exactly
inside that gap. Their first instinct will likely round up — "I'd
call that mostly kept" — and they will believe that judgment is
accurate. Eurich's research justifies the protocol's anti-rounding-up
prompts as a calibration intervention, not a coaching mannerism. Her
distinction between *internal* self-awareness (own values/behavior)
and *external* self-awareness (how others see you) maps onto the WIG
Session's specific demand: classify the way peers will classify, not
the way you feel about it.

## Source 10: Wiseman & McKeown, *Multipliers* (HarperBusiness, 2010)

**Citation**: Liz Wiseman and Greg McKeown, *Multipliers: How the Best
Leaders Make Everyone Smarter* (HarperBusiness, 2010; ISBN
978-0-06-196439-8). Companion: Wiseman & McKeown, "Are You an
Accidental Diminisher?", *Ivey Business Journal*, August 2010.

**Used by**: `member-debrief.md` (Step 4 Marcus-pattern self-applied).

**Mechanism**: The "accidental diminisher" patterns — well-intentioned
behaviors that suppress others' contributions. The "Rescuer" and
"Always-On" patterns specifically describe leaders who jump in to do
work others were going to do. The Marcus pattern in 4DX Ch. 10 (Marcus
made the prospecting calls *himself* instead of building the system
his team needed) is a rescuer/always-on accidental-diminisher
pattern — and Wiseman shows it is *systematic*, not idiosyncratic.
The honest debrief must surface "did I accidentally-diminish my own
committed work by doing someone else's urgent?" Wiseman's vocabulary
gives the member a non-shaming way to name the move. The book treats
Marcus's pattern as a single anecdote; Wiseman treats it as a
documented behavioral class with field prevalence.

## Source 11: Bravelab.io / Smenżyk — "The very first attempt to implement 4DX" (LinkedIn Pulse, 2021)

**Citation**: Mariusz Smenżyk (founder, Bravelab.io), "The very first
attempt to implement 4DX in Bravelab.io," LinkedIn Pulse article,
published 2021-04-20.

**Applicable to mode(s)**: audit-mode (any scope)

Self-disclosed real-world implementation report from a Polish digital
agency. Most useful as a verbatim anchor for two D4 failure modes the
book warns about (≤30-min ceiling; member-prep necessity) but doesn't
anchor with a named real-world case.

Verbatim D4 failure quotes:

> *"WIG Sessions required more than 30 minutes. Sometimes up to 1h."*

> *"Everyone has to be prepared before the WIG Session (everyone needs
> to know their current measurements. It's important to put them out
> on the scoreboard together)."*

> *"People who will be using 4DX should learn the basics of this method
> before we start."*

The team established the right cadence shape (every Monday at 1pm,
85% achievement threshold, accurate scoreboard data) but failed three
operational disciplines simultaneously: session creep (30 → 60 min),
unprepared members, and skipping pre-onboarding training. Each is a
distinct D4 failure mode that compounds — a 60-min unprepared session
with untrained members is qualitatively worse than any of the three
in isolation.

Direct application: when audit-mode receives a cadence log showing
sessions running past 30 min, members arriving without numbers, or
team-wide unfamiliarity with 4DX vocabulary in early sessions, cite
Bravelab as the verbatim anchor. The point is calibration — these
are the canonical operational-discipline failures, not exotic edge
cases.

**Authority note**: LinkedIn Pulse self-disclosure by company founder.
Treat as **credentialed self-disclosure** — single-org data point, but
verbatim and transparently honest about what failed.

Public URL: https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/very-first-attempt-implement-4dx-bravelabio-mariusz-smenzyk

## Cross-source synthesis

Across the ten sources:

- **Why short, agenda-tight, weekly works** — Rogelberg + Lencioni +
  Reinertsen.
- **Why facilitator stance > facilitator agenda** — Lencioni +
  Edmondson (Teaming).
- **Why prep matters before the room** — Cialdini + Pfeffer + Drucker.
- **Why honest self-account is trainable** — Eurich + Edmondson (HBR
  2011) + Wiseman.

The book asserts each of these on practitioner authority; the primary
sources give the mechanism, dosage, and the failure-mode prediction.
For solo users (where the book under-validates the mechanism), the
sources also flag the limits — Reinertsen's cadence-vs-flow tradeoff
is the most important safeguard the book under-serves.
