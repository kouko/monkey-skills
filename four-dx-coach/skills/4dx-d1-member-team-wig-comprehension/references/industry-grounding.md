# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension

Supplements the book's leader-POV with member-side / dissent / cross-cultural
literature where the book is thin (V1 ⚠️ partial). The book's two flagship cases
on team-WIG comprehension (Younger Brothers, hospital food-service) are written
from the leader's communication seat — what gets said *down*. The mirror
question — what a member should *do* when the WIG arrives unclear, unexplained,
or culturally hard to challenge — is left implicit. The sources below fill that
gap from organizational-psychology and cross-cultural-management research.

## Underlying mechanism

A team WIG, once announced, lands on members as a hierarchical instruction. The
book assumes members will then ask clarifying questions and surface
misunderstandings. In practice, three forces suppress that asking:
(1) low psychological safety — members fear the question itself looks like
dissent or incompetence; (2) authority gradient — in steeper hierarchies the
"interrupt the leader to ask" move is socially costly; (3) compliance reflex —
members default to looking aligned rather than being aligned. Industry research
documents these forces specifically, and offers prescriptions for surfacing
unclear / unfair WIGs without triggering the dissent penalty.

## Key industry sources

### Source 1: Edmondson, *The Fearless Organization* (Wiley, 2018)

Amy C. Edmondson, *The Fearless Organization: Creating Psychological Safety in
the Workplace for Learning, Innovation, and Growth* (Hoboken, NJ: John Wiley &
Sons, 2018; ISBN 978-1-119-47724-2). Synthesizes ~20 years of psychological-
safety research, including Edmondson's seminal hospital-team studies showing
that the *better* teams reported *more* errors — not because they made more,
but because members felt safe enough to surface them.

Direct application: a member who cannot state the team WIG verbatim, or who
cannot articulate why this WIG was chosen, should treat that as a *signal to
ask* — not as a personal failure to absorb the announcement. Edmondson frames
the asking itself as a learning behavior, the absence of which produces
"silent execution failures" — exactly what 4DX line-of-sight is meant to
prevent. The book's framing reduces the social cost of "I need to ask my
leader to restate the WIG" by making the ask a contribution to team learning
rather than a confession of inattention.

### Source 2: Grant, *Originals* (Viking, 2016)

Adam Grant, *Originals: How Non-Conformists Move the World* (New York: Viking,
2016; ISBN 978-0-525-42956-2). Chapter 3 ("Out on a Limb: Speaking Up Without
Getting Silenced") specifically addresses how subordinates can productively
challenge an inherited goal without burning credibility — including the
distinction between *idea-quality dissent* (productive) and *identity-position
dissent* (rejected by hierarchies).

Direct application: when a member receives a team WIG they suspect is
mis-targeted (wrong lag measure, wrong deadline, wrong scope), Grant's research
suggests framing the challenge as a question about evidence rather than a
verdict on the leader's choice — "what alternative did we consider, and what
made this one win?" rather than "I don't think this is right." This is the
exact phrasing this skill's Step 2 (surface the strategic rationale) prescribes,
and Grant's evidence base explains *why* that phrasing reliably succeeds where
direct dissent fails.

### Source 3: Meyer, *The Culture Map* (PublicAffairs, 2014)

Erin Meyer, *The Culture Map: Breaking Through the Invisible Boundaries of
Global Business* (New York: PublicAffairs, 2014; ISBN 978-1-61039-250-1). The
"Leading" dimension (egalitarian vs hierarchical) and "Disagreeing" dimension
(confrontational vs avoiding-confrontation) directly determine whether a member
can comfortably ask "why this WIG?" — a question that reads as collaborative in
Dutch / Israeli contexts and as insubordinate in Korean / Japanese / Chinese
contexts.

Direct application: this skill's "ask your leader" homework defaults to a
direct-question script that fits low-power-distance, low-confrontation-cost
cultures. Members in high-power-distance, high-face-loss-cost cultures need a
softer route — written follow-up after the meeting, asking a longer-tenured
peer first, or framing the question through a future-task scenario rather than
a clarification-of-past-decision. Meyer's framework gives the member a way to
*calibrate* the homework to their cultural register without dropping it
entirely.

## What 4DX team-members learn from these sources

The book treats inability-to-state-the-WIG as a leader-side communication
failure to be repaired downward. The industry literature reframes it as a
member-side *ask discipline* to be exercised upward — and gives the member the
psychological safety frame (Edmondson), the dissent-without-penalty script
(Grant), and the cross-cultural calibration (Meyer) needed to actually exercise
it. The practical takeaway for this skill: when the agent assigns "go ask your
leader" homework in steps 1, 2, or 3, the homework is not a fallback — it is
the central discipline. The three sources above legitimize the ask, script the
ask, and adapt the ask to the member's actual cultural context.
