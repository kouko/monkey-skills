# 4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension — Understand the Team WIG You've Been Handed

## R — Reading

> Everyone on the team must know the team WIG and how each person personally contributes. ... Without line of sight from the team WIG to the individual's daily work, execution will fragment.
>
> — McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 7 (paraphrased — line-of-sight principle)

> Each team member, every day, must be able to answer one question: *"What is the most important thing I can do today to advance my team's lead measure?"*
>
> — *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 12 (operating question for individuals on a 4DX team)

---

## I — Interpretation

The book is written for leaders. But every leader-side instruction has a
mirror-image responsibility on the member side: if the leader is told "make
sure every team member can state the WIG verbatim and name their own
contribution," then every team member is implicitly being told *"you must be
able to state it, and if you can't, that's a signal something is broken."*

This skill coaches the member side of that mirror.

A member of a 4DX team needs four things in their head:

1. **The team WIG verbatim, in *From X to Y by When* form.** If they can't
   state it, they cannot align to it. Vague paraphrases ("we're trying to
   improve customer satisfaction") are not WIGs and signal the leader's
   communication failed somewhere.

2. **The strategic rationale — why this WIG.** Knowing *what* without *why*
   produces compliance, not commitment. The book's leader-side guidance
   (Ch. 6) is to engage the team in selecting the WIG; if that didn't happen,
   the member needs to ask after the fact.

3. **The team's lead measures.** The lag (WIG) tells you whether you've
   already won. The lead is what you act on this week. A member who knows
   only the WIG but not the lead measures will default to whirlwind behavior.

4. **Their own personal slice.** The team WIG decomposes into individual
   contributions — different members move different lead-measure components.
   The member needs to identify *their* slice and translate it into a
   recurring "every day / week I will ___" commitment that bridges to D4
   (the WIG session).

Coaching is Socratic: the agent does not invent answers. Where the member's
own knowledge has gaps, the agent's job is to send them back to their leader
or peers with a precise question. Some answers can only come from the leader
who chose the WIG.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Younger Brothers Construction — frontline operators rallied to a single team WIG (Ch. 6 / Ch. 12, leader-side)
- **Problem (leader-side as written)**: A construction firm leader had to convert a corporate-level WIG into a frontline team WIG that every crew member could act on.
- **Member-side mirror**: The crew members below that leader needed to know precisely what the team WIG was, why it was chosen over other plausible site-level priorities, and what specific behavior on their own work moved it. Members who didn't get this context kept defaulting to the whirlwind (next ticket, next call-out).
- **Conclusion**: Once members could state the WIG verbatim and name their personal contribution, the daily question ("what's the most important thing I can do today to advance the team's lead measure?") replaced the whirlwind as the operating frame.
- **Outcome**: The team hit its WIG. The leader-side lesson is "communicate down"; the symmetric member-side lesson is "if it didn't get communicated to you, ask."

### Case 2: Hospital food-service team — line-of-sight as comprehension test (Ch. 7, leader-side)
- **Problem (leader-side as written)**: A hospital food-service leader was told to confirm every team member could draw a direct line from the team WIG to their own daily task.
- **Member-side mirror**: Each food-service worker had to be able to answer "what is the team trying to win, and what does my work today have to do with that win?" When workers couldn't, the leader treated it as a communication gap to repair, not as a worker problem.
- **Conclusion**: Inability to state the team WIG and one's own contribution is *diagnostic*, not blameworthy. The fix is a conversation, not self-flagellation.
- **Outcome**: Once the line-of-sight conversation happened, individual contribution became visible and trackable on the team scoreboard.

### Case 3: The daily question as member operating frame (Ch. 12)
- **Problem**: Without an operating frame, individual team members default to whatever screams loudest in the whirlwind — emails, escalations, last-minute requests.
- **Methodology applied**: Ch. 12 prescribes a single question to be carried by every team member each morning: *"What is the most important thing I can do today to advance my team's lead measure?"*
- **Conclusion**: The daily question is the member-level translation of D1 + D2. It presupposes the member knows the WIG, knows the lead measures, and knows their slice — which is exactly what this skill confirms.
- **Outcome**: Members who carry this question report that whirlwind tasks still get done, but strategic time gets carved out and protected — execution stops fragmenting.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. The user joined a team that's already running 4DX and they don't yet have the context the original team had when the WIG was set.
2. The user's leader announced a team WIG but the user can't recite it back in *From X to Y by When* form.
3. The user knows the team WIG verbatim but not why it was chosen — they're complying without understanding.
4. The user knows the WIG and the rationale but doesn't know the lead measures (or thinks the WIG itself is what they should "do daily").
5. The user knows the WIG and lead measures at the team level but doesn't know which slice is *their* personal contribution.
6. The user wants to bring the daily question ("what's the most important thing I can do today to advance the team's lead measure?") into their morning but can't fill in the blanks yet.

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- EN: "I joined a team running 4DX; how do I understand my role in their WIG?" / "My manager set a team WIG but I don't know what I'm supposed to do." / "What does my work have to do with the team's goal?"
- JP: 「チームの WIG が降りてきたけど自分の役割が分からない」「上司が WIG を決めたが自分が何をすればいいか分からない」「チームの目標と自分の仕事のつながりが見えない」
- zh-TW: 「主管定了 WIG 但我不知道我這個位置該怎麼接」「團隊有 WIG 但我不知道我該做什麼」「團隊目標跟我的工作有什麼關係」

### Non-trigger phrasings (do NOT activate)

- "I want to set my own personal goal this quarter." → solo case, route to `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`.
- "I'm a manager and I need to pick the team WIG." → leader case, route to `4dx-d1-team-primary-wig-selection`.
- "How do I run our team's weekly WIG session?" → cadence case, route to `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`.
- "My personal WIG is X→Y by Z; what should I do daily?" → already past comprehension, route to `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`.

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining`: that skill assumes the user *picks* their own WIG. This skill assumes the WIG was already picked *for them* (by their leader). Comprehension + alignment, not selection.
- vs. `4dx-d1-team-primary-wig-selection`: that skill is for the leader choosing the team WIG. This skill is for the people on the receiving end of that choice.
- vs. `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`: D2 starts after the user has clarity on their personal slice. If the user can't yet state the team WIG or their slice, lead-measure discovery is premature.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent runs a Socratic coaching dialogue. **Do not invent the team WIG or rationale for the user — extract what they actually know, surface what they don't, and send them to ask.**

1. **State the team WIG verbatim.**
   - Ask: "What is your team's WIG? Say it as precisely as you can — ideally in *From X to Y by When* form."
   - Completion criterion: user produces a sentence with a measurable starting state, a measurable target, and a deadline.
   - Halt condition: if user can only paraphrase ("I think it's about improving sales"), stop coaching and assign homework: *"Before we go further, ask your leader: 'Can you give me our team's WIG in From X to Y by When form?' Come back with the exact words."* Do not proceed.

2. **Surface the strategic rationale.**
   - Ask: "Why did your team pick this WIG? What was the alternative they declined?"
   - Completion criterion: user can articulate at least one reason and ideally one rejected alternative.
   - Halt condition: if user shrugs, assign homework: *"Ask your leader (or a longer-tenured peer): 'What made this the team WIG instead of [other plausible candidates]?' This is a fair question — don't apologize for it."*

3. **Surface the team's lead measures.**
   - Ask: "What are the team's lead measures — the weekly behaviors the team committed to that should drive the WIG?"
   - Completion criterion: user names at least one lead measure that is both predictive of the WIG and influenceable by the team.
   - Halt condition: if user names the WIG itself or a vague "work harder" answer, assign homework: *"Ask your leader: 'What are our team's lead measures? What are we doing each week that should move the WIG?'"*

4. **Locate the personal slice.**
   - Ask: "If the team WIG hits Y on the deadline, what specifically did *you* do that made it possible? What's the part of the lead measure that's yours?"
   - Completion criterion: user names a recurring, repeatable behavior they personally control that contributes to a team lead measure.
   - Halt condition: if user can't locate their slice, two possibilities — (a) the leader hasn't decomposed the lead measures by role yet (homework: ask), or (b) the team WIG genuinely doesn't depend on this person's role (in which case the alignment question is bigger than this skill).

5. **Translate into a daily/weekly commitment.**
   - Ask: "Translate that slice into the form: *'Every [day / week] I will [verb + measurable count]'.* Make it small enough that you can keep it for a quarter."
   - Completion criterion: user produces a verbalized commitment in that exact form.
   - Quality check: the commitment must be (a) numeric or binary-checkable, (b) doable inside their actual workweek given the whirlwind, and (c) directly tied to the team lead measure named in step 3.

6. **Install the daily question.**
   - Ask: "Each morning, can you carry this question — *'What is the most important thing I can do today to advance my team's lead measure?'* — and let it shape your first 30 minutes?"
   - Completion criterion: user agrees and names the trigger (calendar block / sticky note / morning ritual) where the question will live.

7. **Output the comprehension card.**
   - Write back to the user, in their own words:
     - **Team WIG (verbatim)**: …
     - **Why this WIG**: …
     - **Team lead measures**: …
     - **My personal slice**: …
     - **Every [day/week] I will**: …
     - **My daily question**: *What is the most important thing I can do today to advance my team's lead measure?*
   - Completion criterion: user confirms the card; if any field is "I need to ask my leader," the card is honest — that's the correct output. Do not paper over gaps.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Solo / individual goals**. If the user is picking their own WIG with no team context, the WIG isn't inherited — use `4dx-d1-personal-wig-defining` instead.
- **Leader-side WIG selection**. If the user is the manager *choosing* the team WIG, use `4dx-d1-team-primary-wig-selection`. This skill assumes the choice has already been made by someone else.
- **As a substitute for asking the leader directly**. Some questions — why this WIG, what the lead measures are, what the role decomposition is — can only be authoritatively answered by the leader. The skill's job is to surface the gaps and route the user to ask, not to invent plausible-sounding answers.
- **When the team isn't actually running 4DX**. If the team has a "goal" but no scoreboard, no lead measures, and no weekly cadence, this skill misroutes the user. Flag the mismatch — the team has a goal, not a WIG.
- **As an end-run around a misaligned WIG**. If the user fundamentally disagrees with the team WIG, this skill comprehends the WIG; it does not litigate the choice. Disagreement is a different conversation (1:1 with leader, not coaching).

### Author-warned failure modes (member-side mirror)

- **WIG-recall failure (member side of CE-12)**: The book's leader-side warning is "if any team member can't state the WIG, communication failed." The member-side mirror: if you can't state it, do not pretend you can. Go ask.
- **Compliance-without-commitment**: Member knows the words ("From 78% to 92% on-time delivery by Dec 31") but not the strategic reason. They will execute mechanically until the first conflict with the whirlwind, then drop the WIG. The "why" matters.
- **WIG-as-personal-task confusion**: Member treats the team WIG as their personal to-do ("OK I'll work on on-time delivery"). The team WIG is the team's lag measure; the member's work is on a *lead measure slice*, not on the WIG directly. This skill must keep that distinction sharp.
- **Whirlwind-eats-slice**: Member identifies the slice but doesn't carve calendar time for it. The daily question (step 6) is specifically the antidote — without it, slice work becomes "if I have time," which means never.
- **Inferred-rationale risk**: Because the book is leader-POV, member-side coaching is partially inferred. Be explicit: tell the user "this is a derived practice — the source book doesn't give you a member-side script, so I'm coaching the symmetric inverse of what leaders are told to do." Don't oversell certainty.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations (apply with care)

- **Member voice underrepresented**: All cited cases are leader-side. The book assumes if leaders communicate well, members will follow. Real-world member experience often includes leaders who *don't* communicate well — this skill exists precisely for that gap, but the book itself does not address it directly.
- **Hierarchical assumption**: 4DX assumes a leader-decides / team-executes structure. In flat, peer-driven, or self-managing teams, the "ask your leader" homework may not have a clear addressee. In that case, route the homework to "ask the team" or to a collective decision moment.
- **Single-WIG team assumption**: The book assumes one team WIG. In matrix orgs the user may be on multiple teams with multiple WIGs — this skill handles one team at a time and explicitly does not arbitrate between conflicting team WIGs (that's a meta-strategy / capacity conversation).

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **OKR cascading**: OKRs cascade Os and KRs through layers. 4DX is structurally different — the team WIG is the team's lag measure, and individual contribution is on the *lead* side, not on a smaller-O / smaller-KR. Don't reflexively translate OKR experience.
- **Generic "alignment" coaching**: Many leadership books say "align your team to the goal." 4DX is more specific — alignment means each person can state the WIG verbatim, name their lead-measure slice, and answer the daily question. Vague alignment talk doesn't satisfy this bar.
- **MBO (Management by Objectives)**: MBO assigns individuals their own objectives that roll up. 4DX assigns the team a single WIG and decomposes contribution on the lead side. The member's "thing" is a slice of a shared lead measure, not a personal objective.

### Industry-experience addendum (member-side literature)

The book is leader-POV; member-side comprehension discipline is supplemented from organizational-psychology research on psychological safety, dissent, and cross-cultural authority dynamics.

- **Edmondson, *The Fearless Organization* (Wiley, 2018)** — psychological safety reframes "I need to ask my leader to restate the WIG" as a learning behavior, not an admission of inattention.
- **Grant, *Originals* (Viking, 2016, Ch. 3)** — productive-dissent script: ask about evidence and rejected alternatives, not the leader's verdict. Maps directly to this skill's Step 2.
- **Meyer, *The Culture Map* (PublicAffairs, 2014)** — Leading + Disagreeing dimensions calibrate the "ask your leader" homework for high-power-distance / high-face-loss-cost contexts (written follow-up, ask a peer first).

See `references/industry-grounding.md` for full citations and mechanism notes. Practical implication: the agent's "go ask" homework is the central discipline, not a fallback — and members in steeper hierarchies need a softer route, not no route.
