# 4DX D4 — Member Commitment Prep (Walk-In Ready)

## R — Reading

> Each week, every team member answers the question: "What are the one
> or two most important things I can do this week to impact the lead
> measures?" The commitment must be specific, must move the lead
> measures, and must be within the team member's own control.
>
> — McChesney, Covey & Huling, *Chapter 5: Discipline 4 — Create a Cadence of Accountability*

---

## I — Interpretation

A WIG Session commitment is not a to-do list, not an attitude, and not
a status update. It is **one (max two) specific, verifiable action**
that the team member personally chooses to take this coming week,
which **directly moves the team's lead measure**, and which is
**entirely within their own control**.

Five filters every commitment must pass (Ch 5):

1. **Specific** — "If we played a tape of next Tuesday, would I know
   whether you did it?" No vague intents, no attitudes.
2. **Most important** — the single highest-leverage action this week,
   not a list of five things the member happens to be working on.
3. **Moves the lead measure** — not a whirlwind task, not a self-improvement task,
   not "the urgent thing on my plate". If it doesn't move the team's
   lead, it doesn't belong in the WIG Session.
4. **Within own control** — "I'll send the draft" beats "I'll get
   marketing to sign off". Dependencies on others' approvals,
   schedules, or decisions disqualify the commitment.
5. **Self-chosen** — not assigned by the leader, not "what do you
   think the boss wants me to say". Ownership flows from voice.

This skill is **the prep step before** walking into the team's WIG
Session. The agent acts as a **personal coach to the team member** —
private, supportive, allowed to push back on bad commitments before
they get said out loud at the meeting where backing down is harder.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Younger Brothers Construction (Ch 14, member-level commitments)
- **Problem**: A residential construction crew needed every framer,
  electrician, and plumber to make individual weekly commitments tied
  to a "build days" lead measure — not "I'll work hard".
- **Methodology applied**: Pre-session, each crew member wrote down
  one specific commitment that moved build-days (e.g., "Frame all
  three bedrooms by Thursday EOD"), reviewed it against the
  specificity + control filters before walking into the Friday
  session.
- **Conclusion**: Commitments prepped privately first were dramatically
  more specific than commitments improvised in the room. Crew members
  who walked in unprepared defaulted to vague/safe ("I'll keep
  framing").
- **Outcome**: Build-days lead measure rose; the company became one of
  the book's flagship D4 case studies.

### Case 2: Marriott housekeeping team (Ch 5)
- **Problem**: Housekeepers needed commitments that moved the
  guest-experience lead measure — but their default commitment was
  "I'll clean my rooms", which is just their job, not a WIG action.
- **Methodology applied**: Supervisors coached each housekeeper
  pre-session: "What's the ONE thing this week beyond your normal
  cleaning that would move the guest-satisfaction lead?" Members
  walked in with a sharpened commitment ("Personally check the
  shower-curtain hooks on every checkout room") instead of a duty
  restated.
- **Conclusion**: Distinguishing "my job" from "my WIG commitment" is
  hard in the moment; preparation surfaces it.
- **Outcome**: Member-level engagement scores rose alongside the lead
  measure.

### Case 3: Author warning — improvised commitments fail (Ch 5)
- **Problem**: Members walking into a WIG Session cold tend to
  produce one of five compliance commitments: "I'll try to do better"
  / "I'll do my best" / "I'll send the email if Bob approves" / "I'll
  work harder" / "I'll keep doing what I'm doing".
- **Methodology applied**: The book explicitly names these as
  failure-mode commitments and prescribes preparing the commitment
  *before* the session.
- **Conclusion**: Without prep, the social pressure of the room
  produces safe compliance, not specific deliverables. The prep
  conversation is where the commitment gets sharpened.
- **Outcome**: Teams with pre-session prep show measurably higher
  commitment-kept rates than teams that improvise in the room.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. **Member has a WIG Session today / tomorrow / this week** and wants
   to walk in with a sharpened commitment instead of improvising
   under social pressure.
2. **Member's last commitment was vague or didn't get kept** and they
   suspect the problem was the commitment itself, not their effort.
3. **Member doesn't want to look bad in front of peers/leader** and
   wants a private rehearsal of what they'll say.
4. **Member keeps committing to whirlwind tasks** that feel urgent but
   don't move the team's lead measure, and wants help separating the
   two.
5. **Member's leader recently pushed back on a commitment** ("that's
   not specific enough", "that depends on other people") and they
   want to fix it before next session.

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- "Help me prepare my commitment for next WIG Session."
- "What should I commit to at this week's WIG Session?"
- 「次の WIG Session の commitment を準備したい」
- 「来週のミーティングで何をコミットすべきか相談したい」
- 「我要為下次 WIG Session 準備 commitment」
- 「下週的 commitment 我想先想清楚再去開會」
- "My last commitment was too vague — help me sharpen this week's."
- "I want to walk in with a real commitment, not 'I'll try harder'."

### Non-trigger language (do NOT activate)

- "Run my weekly review with me" → solo user, no team session →
  use `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`.
- "Help me reflect on last week's commitment" → reflection AFTER
  the session → use `4dx-d4-member-account-debrief`.
- "Facilitate our team WIG Session" → leader running the meeting →
  the book chapters 5 + 15 directly, not this skill.
- "Help me figure out the team's lead measure" → upstream design
  question, not commitment prep → use the team D2 skill.
- "I don't know what my team's lead measure is" → comprehension gap;
  route to `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension` first.

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`: that skill **runs** the session
  for a solo user with no team; this skill **preps** a team member
  to walk into their team's existing session.
- vs. `4dx-d4-member-account-debrief`: that skill is **after** the
  session (did I keep what I committed to last week, what did I
  learn); this skill is **before** (what will I commit to this
  week).
- vs. `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`: that skill confirms the
  member knows what the team's WIG and lead measure even *are*; this
  skill assumes that's already known and turns it into a weekly
  commitment.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent runs a **private prep
conversation** with one team member, in personal-coach voice. The
agent's job is to surface a commitment the member will actually voice
at the session, sharpened against five filters, in 5-10 minutes.

1. **Gate — confirm the lead measure is known** (≤1 min)
   - Ask: "What's your team's lead measure right now? In one
     sentence."
   - If the member can't name it, or names a lag measure, or names
     the WIG itself: halt this skill and route to
     `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`. Don't try to prep a
     commitment against an unknown lead measure — it produces theatre.
   - Completion criterion: member states a specific lead measure
     verbatim.

2. **Look back at last week's commitment** (~1-2 min)
   - Ask: "What did you commit to last week, and did you keep it?"
   - If kept: note the pattern that worked, build on it. If not kept:
     ask once "what got in the way" — was it (a) the commitment was
     too big, (b) wrong commitment for the lead, (c) blocked by a
     dependency, or (d) whirlwind invasion?
   - Don't dwell — this is prep, not debrief. If the member needs to
     process last week, suggest `4dx-d4-member-account-debrief` and
     return here after.
   - Completion criterion: last-week pattern named (kept / partially /
     not kept) and one root cause if not.

3. **Generate candidate commitments** (~2-3 min)
   - Ask the canonical question, precisely: "Looking at the week
     ahead, what's the **ONE most important thing** you can do that
     **moves the team's lead measure** — something within your own
     control?"
   - Let the member produce 1-3 raw candidates. Don't filter yet;
     surface them.
   - If the member offers only one and it looks weak, ask once:
     "Anything else that would move the lead more?" — but don't push
     past two candidates.
   - Completion criterion: 1-3 raw candidate commitments on the
     table.

4. **Apply the five filters** (~3-4 min, the core of the skill)
   For each candidate, run all five tests aloud with the member.
   Reject candidates that fail; sharpen those that almost-pass.

   - **Specificity test**: "If we played a tape of next Tuesday,
     would I be able to see you doing this? Would your teammate
     know if you did it?" If "I'll focus on outreach" → push to
     "Call three customers Tuesday and Thursday." If "I'll work on
     the deck" → push to "Finish slides 1-10 by Wednesday EOD."
   - **Lead-measure test**: "Does this directly move the team's lead
     measure, or does it move something else that just feels
     important?" If it's a whirlwind task in disguise — even if
     urgent — flag it. The member can do the whirlwind task; it just
     can't be their WIG commitment.
   - **Control test**: "Is every part of this within your power, or
     does it require somebody else's approval, schedule, or
     decision?" If "I'll get marketing to sign off" → reframe to
     the part the member controls: "I'll send marketing the draft
     with a Friday-EOD ask." If "I'll send the email IF Bob
     approves" → strip the conditional: either commit to sending
     it, or commit to getting Bob's decision by a date.
   - **Self-chosen test**: "Are you proposing this because *you*
     think it'll move the lead, or because you think the leader
     wants to hear it?" Coach voice here matters: this is private,
     the member can be honest. If the answer is "what the boss
     wants", reset and re-ask the canonical question.
   - **Scope test (one-or-two cap)**: "Is this ONE thing, or are you
     trying to hide three things in one sentence?" "I'll call
     customers and update the CRM and prep the report" = three
     things. Cut to one (max two). Five at 50% kept beats nothing,
     but one at 95% kept moves the lead — and the book is firm: cap
     at 1-2.

   - Completion criterion: 1-2 commitments survive all five filters.

5. **Rehearse the wording** (~1 min)
   - Ask: "When the leader asks for your commitment in the session,
     what will you say — verbatim?"
   - Member states it aloud. Agent listens for any backsliding into
     vague language ("I'll try to…", "I'll work on…"). If detected,
     correct once: "Say it as a deliverable — what will be done by
     when."
   - Completion criterion: member has one or two sentences they can
     say verbatim at the session.

6. **Hand off to the session** (≤1 min)
   - "You're ready. Walk in, state these as your commitments, don't
     soften them under social pressure."
   - If the member expresses anxiety about saying it ("but my
     teammate will judge", "the leader will think it's too small"):
     hold the line — "Specific and small that gets done beats vague
     and big that doesn't. Your scoreboard at the end of the week
     decides, not the room."
   - After the session, suggest: "Come back for
     `4dx-d4-member-account-debrief` next week to close the loop."
   - Completion criterion: member confirms they'll voice the
     prepared commitment at the session.

**Coach-voice discipline throughout**: This is a private prep, not a
performance review. The agent is allowed to push back on bad
commitments — that's the value — but never to dictate. If the agent
finds itself proposing the commitment, stop and re-ask the canonical
question. Ownership is the mechanism; if the agent produces the
commitment, the mechanism is broken before the session even starts.

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Solo-user contexts (no team WIG Session exists).** The whole
  premise is preparing for a real meeting where the member will voice
  this commitment to peers. For solo users with no team, the
  commitment-making happens *inside* the session itself — use
  `4dx-d4-personal-wig-session`.
- **Leader-facilitating-the-meeting contexts.** Leaders propose their
  own commitments same as members (the book is firm: leaders are
  participants, not exempt), so a leader prepping their *own* commitment
  could in theory use this skill — BUT leaders also need facilitation
  guidance for the meeting itself, which this skill doesn't provide.
  For leaders, prefer the team-wig-session-lead skill (when shipped),
  which covers both their own commitment + facilitating others'.
- **After-the-session reflection.** This skill is BEFORE. For AFTER
  ("did I keep what I committed to last week, what did I learn"),
  use `4dx-d4-member-account-debrief`.
- **Goal/lead-measure discovery.** If the member doesn't know the
  team's lead measure, prep is impossible — route to
  `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension` first.
- **Daily-standup / scrum-task / OKR-check-in commitment writing.**
  Different artifact. WIG-Session commitments are weekly,
  lead-measure-moving, self-chosen, peer-witnessed. Don't conflate.

### Author-warned failure modes

- **Compliance commitments (CE-12)**. "I'll try to do better", "I'll
  do my best", "I'll work harder", "I'll keep doing what I'm doing".
  All five are explicitly named in Ch 5 as failure modes. Reject in
  the specificity test; do not let them out of prep.
- **Vague-scope commitments**. "I'll work on the project" / "I'll
  focus on outreach" — same family as compliance. Push to a verifiable
  deliverable with a who/what/when.
- **Dependency commitments**. "I'll get marketing to approve…" /
  "I'll send it IF Bob signs off." Reframe to the part the member
  controls; strip the conditional.
- **Whirlwind-disguised-as-commitment**. The most-urgent task this
  week is not automatically a WIG commitment. If it doesn't move the
  team's lead measure, it's whirlwind — let the member do it
  separately, but don't promote it into the session.
- **Three-things-in-one-sentence**. Cap at 1-2. The book is firm: 5
  commitments at 50% kept ≪ 1-2 at 95% kept.
- **Boss-pleasing commitments**. "What I think the leader wants to
  hear." Self-chosen is non-negotiable — re-ask the canonical
  question until the member's own voice produces the answer.
- **Agent dictates the commitment**. If the agent ends up proposing
  the wording rather than sharpening the member's wording, the
  ownership mechanism is destroyed before the session. Stop, reset,
  re-ask.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations

- **Members who don't know the lead measure (CE-32)**. The book
  assumes D1-D2 propagation worked — every member knows the team's
  WIG and lead measure. In practice many don't. This skill hard-gates
  on that and routes to comprehension first.
- **Members in low-psychological-safety teams (CE-33)**. The book
  assumes peer accountability is energizing; in face-loss-averse or
  blame-prone teams, members may produce safe compliance commitments
  to avoid being judged. Prep helps marginally (the agent is
  private), but the underlying culture problem is outside the skill's
  scope.
- **High-context-culture face-loss (CE-29)**. In JP / ZH / KR
  contexts, voicing a self-chosen specific commitment in front of a
  leader can feel presumptuous. The book doesn't address this. Prep
  conversations can sharpen the wording but can't lower the social
  cost of saying it.
- **Knowledge work with long feedback loops (CE-27)**. If the team's
  lead measure operates on a 3-month feedback loop (drug discovery,
  long-cycle research), weekly commitments may feel theatrical. The
  CF-Center pattern (commit to specific *experiments*, not outcomes)
  is the workaround.

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **OKR weekly check-in commitment**: typically status-update format
  ("on track / at risk / off track"), often manager-driven, not the
  self-chosen specific deliverable a WIG commitment requires.
- **Daily standup task**: wrong cadence (daily ≠ weekly), wrong scope
  (sprint task ≠ WIG-lead-measure action). A standup task isn't a
  WIG commitment.
- **GTD next-action**: scoped to any project (whirlwind included),
  no concept of lead measure or peer commitment. WIG commitment is
  narrower and stricter.
- **SMART goal**: SMART filters specificity/measurability/time but
  doesn't enforce **lead-measure-moving** or **self-chosen** — both
  load-bearing for the WIG commitment.

### Industry-experience addendum (member-side literature)

The book is leader-POV; member-side commitment selection is supplemented from organizational-power and self-management research on the political-signal, strengths-fit, and consistency-lock layers the five filters do not address.

- **Pfeffer, *Power* (HarperBusiness, 2010)** — managing-up: a commitment is *legible* to the leader; pick one that signals both team-loyalty and personal capability, not just lead-moving-ness.
- **Drucker, "Managing Oneself" (HBR 1999, repub. 2005)** — among five-filter-passing candidates, prefer the strengths-aligned one over the development-stretch one; keep-rates differ.
- **Cialdini, *Influence* (1984; rev. ed. 2007)** — commitment-and-consistency: once said publicly, the commitment is locked. Prep is the only window to shape it without paying the inconsistency tax.

See `references/industry-grounding.md` for full citations and mechanism notes. Practical implication: Steps 4 (filters) and 5 (rehearse) operate in the only window where signal-fit, strengths-fit, and consistency-lock can still be tuned.
