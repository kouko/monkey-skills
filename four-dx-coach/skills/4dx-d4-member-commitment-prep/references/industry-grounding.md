# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep

Supplements the book's leader-POV with member-side / managing-up / strengths-
based / commitment-psychology literature where the book is thin (V1 ⚠️
partial). The book's commitment guidance (Ch. 5, Ch. 15) is written for the
leader running the WIG Session — what to demand from members, what
compliance commitments to reject. The mirror question — how a member should
*choose* their commitment given the political, capability, and psychological
constraints they face — is left implicit. The sources below fill that gap from
organizational-power and self-management research.

## Underlying mechanism

A weekly WIG commitment performs three functions simultaneously: it (1) moves
the team's lead measure, (2) signals to peers and the leader what kind of
contributor the member is, and (3) binds the member's future self via public-
commitment psychology. The book centers (1) and treats (2) and (3) as
peripheral. Members in real organizations cannot — they pick commitments
inside a political economy where over-promising costs trust on missed weeks
and under-promising costs reputation in performance reviews, and where the
psychological lock-in of saying it aloud is a feature when the commitment is
right and a trap when it isn't. The sources below address each of those three
functions directly.

## Key industry sources

### Source 1: Pfeffer, *Power* (HarperBusiness, 2010)

Jeffrey Pfeffer, *Power: Why Some People Have It — and Others Don't* (New
York: HarperBusiness, 2010; ISBN 978-0-06-178908-3). Stanford GSB professor;
the book argues that managing the relationship with one's boss is at least as
important as job performance, and that visible commitments are a primary
relationship-management instrument.

Direct application: a member's WIG commitment is *legible to the leader* in a
way that whirlwind work is not. A commitment that is too small ("I'll send one
email") signals timidity and underperformance even if technically lead-measure-
moving; a commitment that is too big ("I'll close all five accounts") signals
either competence or recklessness depending on whether it gets kept. Pfeffer's
research gives members a framework for picking commitments that signal both
team-loyalty (genuinely lead-measure-moving) and personal capability (visibly
ambitious within what they can actually deliver). This is the layer the book's
"specific + within control + self-chosen" filters do not fully address.

### Source 2: Drucker, "Managing Oneself" (HBR 1999, repub. 2005)

Peter F. Drucker, "Managing Oneself," *Harvard Business Review*, vol. 77,
no. 2 (March–April 1999), pp. 64–74; reprinted as HBR Classics, January 2005
(reprint R0501K). Published in book form as *Managing Oneself* (Harvard
Business School Press, 2008; ISBN 978-1-4221-2312-6). The article's central
prescription: "concentrate on your strengths… place yourself where your
strengths can produce results."

Direct application: among 1–3 candidate commitments that all pass the book's
five filters, the member should prefer the one that plays to their existing
strengths over the one that stretches into a development area. The book treats
all five-filter-passing commitments as equivalent; Drucker's research argues
they are not — strengths-aligned commitments are kept at far higher rates and
build cumulative capability, while development-stretch commitments belong in a
separate development conversation, not in the weekly WIG cadence where the
team is counting on the lead measure moving.

### Source 3: Cialdini, *Influence* (1984; rev. ed. HarperBusiness, 2007)

Robert B. Cialdini, *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (originally New
York: William Morrow, 1984; revised edition: New York: HarperBusiness, 2007;
ISBN 978-0-06-124189-5). The commitment-and-consistency principle: humans
have a near-automatic drive to act consistently with what they have publicly
committed to, especially when the commitment is *active, public, effortful,
and freely chosen* — exactly the four properties of a WIG-Session commitment.

Direct application: Cialdini's research is *why* the prep step matters. Once a
member voices a commitment in front of peers, consistency pressure binds them
to it through the week — that is the mechanism the WIG Session exploits. But
the same mechanism binds them to a *bad* commitment just as tightly. The prep
conversation is the only window where the commitment can still be reshaped
without paying the inconsistency tax. This grounds the skill's "five filters
applied privately, before the room hears the words" prescription in
documented commitment psychology, not just in book authority.

## What 4DX team-members learn from these sources

The book's five filters are necessary but treat all five-filter-passing
commitments as fungible. They are not. Pfeffer adds the political-signal
filter (does this commitment build my standing, or is it invisible / too
timid / too reckless?); Drucker adds the strengths filter (does this play to
what I'm already good at, where keep-rates are highest?); Cialdini adds the
prep-window urgency (once said publicly, the commitment is locked — make it
the right one *before* speaking). The practical takeaway for this skill: when
the agent runs Step 4 (apply the five filters) and Step 5 (rehearse the
wording), the agent is operating in the only window where these three layers
can still be tuned. After the session begins, the consistency mechanism
takes over.
