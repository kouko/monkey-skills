# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-d2-lead-measures (multi-mode)

Consolidated primary-source citations for the merged D2 lead-measures skill,
which spans three modes: **solo** (personal lead-measure discovery for a
single user), **team-leader** (team lead-measure facilitation under the
two-axis test + veto-don't-dictate rule), and **member** (per-lead 0–5
influence audit and focus pick from the member's seat). Each citation below
names the source, the mode(s) it applies to, and the mechanism it grounds.

The 4DX book's chapters 3, 7, and 13 codify the methodology — the two-axis
test (predictive + influenceable) and the veto-don't-dictate rule — but
under-engage three layers: **(a)** the documented failure modes when stakes
are attached to a measure (Goodhart, Strathern, plus the Wells Fargo / Phoenix
VA / Atlanta APS case record); **(b)** the team-dynamics preconditions for
honest discovery and productive veto debate (Edmondson, Lencioni, Pfeffer &
Sutton); **(c)** the member-side decomposition of "influenceable" into
position, persuasion, and truth-telling layers (Pfeffer, Cialdini, Argyris)
plus the leader-influence layer (Drucker, Edmondson HBR). The sources below
fill all three gaps in parallel — each is tagged for the mode(s) that
reference it.

---

## Underlying mechanism

A team lead measure that survives the book's two-axis test (predictive +
influenceable) is, by construction, a property of the team collectively. In
practice, the methodology has three failure modes that the book under-
serves and that the literatures below name explicitly:

1. **Goodhart pressure** (all modes) — once visibility, incentives, or
   career stakes attach to the measure, humans optimize the metric not the
   underlying behavior. The mechanism (Goodhart 1975 / Strathern 1997) and
   the case record (Wells Fargo, Phoenix VA, Atlanta APS) are sharpest in
   the solo / personal mode where the user is also the one attaching the
   stakes; team modes inherit the same dynamics at scale.
2. **Voice + debate preconditions** (team-leader mode) — the brainstorm-
   wide step, the veto-don't-dictate rule, and the productive-conflict
   resolution all presuppose psychological safety (Edmondson) plus
   trust-conflict-commitment chain (Lencioni). Without those preconditions,
   the methodology runs and produces compliance-without-commitment leads
   that drift within 4 weeks. Imported case-study leads ("Stripe does
   this") additionally need the Pfeffer & Sutton evidence-based filter.
3. **Position / persuasion / truth-telling layers** (member mode) —
   "influenceable" decomposes at the member's seat into Pfeffer's power
   axes (formal authority, resources, allies, network, expertise),
   Cialdini's six-principle leverage map (reciprocity, social proof,
   authority, etc.), and Argyris's Model-II inquiry stance for surfacing
   the no-influence finding without inviting Model-I defensive routines.

## Key industry sources

### Source 1: Goodhart, "Problems of Monetary Management: The U.K. Experience" (1975)

**Citation**: Charles Goodhart, "Problems of Monetary Management: The U.K.
Experience" (1975), paper presented to the Money Study Group at the London
School of Economics; reprinted in *Papers in Monetary Economics 1*,
Reserve Bank of Australia. [EconBiz catalog record](https://www.econbiz.de/Record/problems-of-monetary-management-the-u-k-experience-goodhart-charles/10002525062)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: solo, team-leader, member

Original formulation: "Any observed statistical regularity will tend to
collapse once pressure is placed upon it for control purposes." This is
the underlying-mechanism citation for every documented lead-measure-as-
target failure case below. The book's two-axis test (predictive +
influenceable) is *necessary but not sufficient* for safe deployment;
Goodhart names the dynamic that breaks the test the moment stakes attach.

### Source 2: Strathern, "'Improving ratings': audit in the British University system" (*European Review*, 1997)

**Citation**: Marilyn Strathern, "'Improving ratings': audit in the
British University system," *European Review*, vol. 5, no. 3 (1997),
pp. 305–321 (Cambridge University Press). [Cambridge Core record](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/european-review/article/abs/improving-ratings-audit-in-the-british-university-system/FC2EE640C0C44E3DB87C29FB666E9AAB) | [open PDF on gwern.net](https://gwern.net/doc/statistics/decision/1997-strathern.pdf)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: solo, team-leader, member

Popularized form: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a
good measure." Strathern's restatement is the version most practitioners
know. Both formulations describe the same dynamic that 4DX leaders face
the moment they attach incentives, visibility, or career stakes to a
"predictive + influenceable" lead measure: humans optimize the metric,
not the underlying behavior the metric was supposed to proxy for.

### Source 3: Wells Fargo cross-selling — CFPB Consent Order (2016) + ancillary investigations

**Citation**: Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, *Consent Order:
Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.* (September 8, 2016). Ancillary primary records:
Congressional Research Service, "Wells Fargo: Timeline of Recent Consumer
Protection and Corporate Governance Scandals" (CRS IF11129); Stanford
Closer Look Series CGRI-CL-62, *The Wells Fargo Cross-Selling Scandal*.

- [CFPB Enforcement Action: Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2016)](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/enforcement/actions/wells-fargo-bank-2016/)
- [CRS IF11129](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF11129)
- [Stanford Closer Look CGRI-CL-62 (PDF)](https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/sites/gsb/files/publication-pdf/cgri-closer-look-62-wells-fargo-cross-selling-scandal.pdf)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: solo, team-leader, member (cross-referenced
across modes; primary documentation lives in solo-mode reference)

Wells Fargo set an internal cross-selling lead measure ("Gr-eight" — eight
products per household) and tied it to branch-level sales quotas, employee
performance reviews, and termination decisions. The measure passed the
book's two-axis test cleanly: *predictive* (more products per household
correlates with retention and lifetime value) and *influenceable* (a
personal banker can pitch products at each interaction). It then collapsed
under Goodhart pressure. CFPB / OCC / City and County of Los Angeles fines
totaled **$185 million**; initial findings cited **1,534,280 unauthorized
deposit accounts** and **565,433 credit-card accounts** (2011–2016); a
May 2017 expanded review revised the estimate to roughly **3.5 million**
fraudulent accounts. CEO John Stumpf resigned October 12, 2016.

### Source 4: Phoenix VA wait-time gaming — VA OIG Report 14-02603-267 (2014)

**Citation**: U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs Office of Inspector
General, *Review of Alleged Patient Deaths, Patient Wait Times, and
Scheduling Practices at the Phoenix VA Health Care System*, Report
14-02603-267 (August 26, 2014).

- [VA OIG Report 14-02603-267 (PDF)](https://www.vaoig.gov/sites/default/files/reports/2014-08/VAOIG-14-02603-267.pdf)
- [VA OIG Interim Report 14-02603-178 (PDF, May 28 2014)](https://www.vaoig.gov/sites/default/files/reports/2014-05/VAOIG-14-02603-178.pdf)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: solo, team-leader, member

The Veterans Health Administration adopted a 14-day (later 30-day) lead
measure for new-patient primary-care appointments, tied to senior-
executive performance bonuses. Both axes passed; Goodhart pressure broke
it. OIG documented schedulers maintaining unofficial "secret" wait lists
to make 14-day compliance appear achieved while real waits stretched to
**~115 days** for new primary care. OIG identified **over 3,500 veterans
on unofficial lists** (in addition to the 1,400 on the official list) and
**more than 1,700 patients** never enrolled on any waiting list. Among
patients reviewed: 28 negatively impacted by care delays (including 6
deaths); 17 additional patients with care deviating from expected
standards (including 14 deaths).

### Source 5: Atlanta Public Schools test-score cheating — GBI investigation (2011) + criminal trial (2015)

**Citation**: Georgia Bureau of Investigation, *Special Investigators'
Report to the Governor of Georgia* (Volumes 1–3, July 2011); criminal
trial outcome April 1, 2015 (RICO convictions for 11 of 12 defendants).

- [CNN — "Atlanta schools cheating scandal: 11 of 12 defendants convicted"](https://www.cnn.com/2015/04/01/us/atlanta-schools-cheating-scandal)
- [Education Week — "Atlanta Educators Convicted in Test-Cheating Trial"](https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/atlanta-educators-convicted-in-test-cheating-trial/2015/04)
- [Georgia Public Policy Foundation summary of GBI report](https://www.georgiapolicy.org/news/the-atlanta-public-schools-cheating-scandal/)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: solo, team-leader, member

Atlanta Public Schools under Superintendent Beverly Hall adopted year-
over-year improvement on the Georgia CRCT as a school-quality lead
measure, with bonuses, public rankings, and employment tied to gains.
Both axes passed. Goodhart pressure produced systematic answer-changing
parties after exams. GBI probe (July 2011) found cheating in **44 of 56
schools** and implicated **178 educators**. Thirty-five educators
indicted under Georgia's RICO Act; **eleven of twelve defendants
convicted** April 1, 2015 — the longest criminal trial in Georgia history
(8 months). Between 2002 and 2009, eighth-graders' Atlanta NAEP reading
scores had jumped 14 points — the highest of any urban district —
flagged by *Atlanta Journal-Constitution* statistical analyses as
implausibly large.

### Source 6: Edmondson, *Teaming* (Jossey-Bass, 2012) + "Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior" (*ASQ*, 1999)

**Citation**: Amy C. Edmondson, *Teaming: How Organizations Learn,
Innovate, and Compete in the Knowledge Economy* (San Francisco:
Jossey-Bass, 2012; ISBN 978-0-7879-7093-2). Foundational empirical:
Amy C. Edmondson, "Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work
Teams," *Administrative Science Quarterly*, vol. 44, no. 2 (1999),
pp. 350–383, DOI 10.2307/2666999.

- [HBS faculty publications page](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=42298)
- [ASQ paper indexed on SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.2307/2666999)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: team-leader

Edmondson's 1999 ASQ study found psychological safety predicted team
learning behavior independent of leader effectiveness. *Teaming* (2012)
extends this from stable teams to flexible cross-functional groupings —
relevant for product/platform teams that re-form per quarter.

Why this skill needs it: 4DX's brainstorm-wide step and two-axis scoring
step only produce honest output if team members feel safe proposing
counter-intuitive candidates and disagreeing publicly with the leader's
instincts. The book's silent-leader rule ("don't fill silence with your
own answer") presupposes safety; without it, silence becomes the team
waiting for the leader to take back the pen.

Diagnostic mapped to the skill: in the brainstorm step, count how many
team members propose at least one counter-intuitive candidate. If only
the 1–2 most senior voices speak, the bottleneck is psychological safety,
not facilitation skill. Run the methodology anyway, but expect the
resulting leads to inherit the safety gap.

### Source 7: Lencioni, *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* (Jossey-Bass, 2002)

**Citation**: Patrick Lencioni, *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team: A
Leadership Fable* (San Francisco: Jossey-Bass, 2002; ISBN 978-0-7879-6075-9).

- [Wiley product page](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/9780787960759)
- Reviewed in *Research in Hospitality Management* 11(3), 2021,
  [Tandfonline DOI 10.1080/22243534.2021.2006940](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/22243534.2021.2006940)

**Applicable to mode(s)**: team-leader

Canonical 5-stage model: absence of trust → fear of conflict → lack of
commitment → avoidance of accountability → inattention to results. The
team-leader-mode mediation step (when the team disagrees on
influenceability) and the veto step (leader vetoes a candidate without
authoring its replacement) both REQUIRE Stage 2 (productive conflict) to
function. If the team is at Stage 1 (no trust) or Stage 0 (unspoken
hostility), the mediation step collapses into either silence ("artificial
harmony" — Lencioni's term) or personalized argument. The veto-don't-
dictate move only produces commitment (Stage 3) downstream if conflict
(Stage 2) is healthy upstream.

Diagnostic: when the team is asked to score influenceability, watch the
highest-status quiet member. If they don't push back on the most-senior
voice's score, the team is operating below Stage 2 conflict — the lead
measure that emerges will be the most-senior voice's preference dressed
as consensus.

### Source 8: Pfeffer & Sutton, *Hard Facts, Dangerous Half-Truths, and Total Nonsense* (HBS Press, 2006)

**Citation**: Jeffrey Pfeffer & Robert I. Sutton, *Hard Facts, Dangerous
Half-Truths, and Total Nonsense: Profiting from Evidence-Based
Management* (Boston: Harvard Business School Press, 2006; ISBN
978-1-59139-862-2).

- [Stanford GSB faculty publications page](https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/books/hard-facts-dangerous-half-truths)
- [HBSP product page](https://hbsp.harvard.edu/product/8622-PDF-ENG)
- *Globe and Mail* best-business-book of 2006.

**Applicable to mode(s)**: team-leader

4DX's Ch 3 advises "look at what others who solved similar goals did
differently" (P-12, 80/20 leverage analysis). Naïvely applied, this
becomes "Wegmans does X, so let's do X" — importing a case-study lead
without testing it against THIS team's predictive chain. Pfeffer &
Sutton's central argument is that benchmarking-by-anecdote ignores
survivorship bias and base rates; evidence-based management requires
testing whether the imported practice is *causally* relevant in the local
context. Combined with the FranklinCovey selection bias in the source
book itself (its ~4,000 case studies are clients who paid for
implementation services), the implication is clear: every imported lead
candidate must clear the two-axis test against THIS team's WIG before
adoption.

Diagnostic: if a team member justifies a candidate primarily by external
precedent ("Stripe does this", "Toyota does this"), run the two-axis
test EXTRA rigorously and ask the team to articulate the causal chain in
their own context. External precedent is permissible as a search prompt
(broaden the candidate pool) but never as the predictive evidence (only
the local two-axis test counts).

### Source 9: Pfeffer, *Power* (HarperBusiness, 2010)

**Citation**: Jeffrey Pfeffer, *Power: Why Some People Have It — and
Others Don't* (New York: HarperBusiness, 2010; ISBN 978-0-06-178908-3).

**Applicable to mode(s)**: member

Stanford GSB; the book catalogues the sources of individual influence
inside organizations — formal authority, resources, allies, network
position, expertise, energy, focus — and argues these are unevenly
distributed even on small teams.

Direct application to member mode: the per-lead 0–5 influence audit is
sharper when the member can name *which* Pfeffer-axis a given score
depends on. A "5" on a customer-facing lead because of formal authority
differs from a "5" because of expertise or network access; the action
implications differ. A "1" because of structural position is a different
signal from a "1" because of low energy on the topic — only the
structural one warrants escalation. Pfeffer gives the member the
vocabulary to read their own scores honestly.

### Source 10: Cialdini, *Influence* (1984; rev. ed. 2007)

**Citation**: Robert B. Cialdini, *Influence: The Psychology of
Persuasion* (originally New York: William Morrow, 1984; revised edition:
HarperBusiness, 2007; ISBN 978-0-06-124189-5).

**Applicable to mode(s)**: member

The six universal principles — reciprocity, commitment-and-consistency,
social proof, authority, liking, scarcity — operate as the levers
through which one person moves another's behavior.

Direct application to member mode: when a member's seat does not supply
formal authority to move a lead directly, the six-principle map locates
*what other levers the seat does supply*. A back-office colleague on a
customer-facing lead set may move the lead via expert-authority cues to
the frontline ("I can move it indirectly by preparing better call lists
/ training the script"), via social proof ("when I share what's working,
peers adopt it"), or via reciprocity ("I'm the person colleagues come to
for support, so when I nudge a lead behavior they reciprocate"). Without
this lens, members under-score themselves on leads they actually can move
via non-positional levers.

### Source 11: Argyris, "Skilled Incompetence" (HBR, 1986)

**Citation**: Chris Argyris, "Skilled Incompetence," *Harvard Business
Review*, vol. 64, no. 5 (September–October 1986), pp. 74–79; reprint
86502. Companion: Chris Argyris, *Knowledge for Action* (Jossey-Bass, 1993).

**Applicable to mode(s)**: member

Argyris's classic finding: in well-functioning organizations, otherwise
highly competent employees systematically cannot raise honest concerns
about goals, role-fit, or strategy because the organization's Model-I
norms make the raising itself look like incompetence, disloyalty, or
trouble-making. The "skilled" part is that members become skilled *at*
not raising the concern.

Direct application to member mode: the no-influence escalation step is
the Argyris counter-move. When a member's per-lead influence audit comes
back genuinely low across all leads, the default Model-I script is
"pick one and pretend, because saying 'I have no real handle on any of
these' will read as slacking." Argyris's evidence base names that script
as the failure mode it is and reframes the surfacing of "I don't see a
daily-influence handle" as Model-II learning behavior — a contribution
the team needs to hear, not a member confession to suppress. The escalation
script — *"I want to be useful on the team WIG. I've mapped my position
against each lead and I don't see a daily-influence handle. Can we look
at this together?"* — implements Model-II inquiry stance: state what you
observe, share the reasoning, invite joint diagnosis.

## What 4DX practitioners learn from these sources (per mode)

The eleven sources above stack into three layered upgrades to the book's
lead-measure methodology — one per mode.

**For solo mode** — three Goodhart mitigations on top of the two-axis
test (the personal-scale corollary is *self-Goodharting*: when you attach
high stakes to your own lead measure like "I will read 4 books per
month," you will speed-read without comprehending):
- **Pair frequency-based leads with quality-based leads** so gaming the
  count is caught by the quality check (e.g. "1 chapter/day" + "write a
  one-paragraph summary per book").
- **Re-validate the causal chain on a 4–8 week cadence** — if leads are
  green but the lag stays flat, the lead is being gamed or was wrong.
- **Keep the falsifiable forecast written down** so drift between
  predicted and observed lag movement is visible rather than rationalized
  away.

**For team-leader mode** — three precondition checks before running the
two-axis test + brainstorm-narrow protocol:
- **Edmondson safety check** — does the brainstorm produce
  counter-intuitive candidates from non-senior voices? If only the 1–2
  most senior voices speak, expect compliance-without-commitment leads
  4 weeks later.
- **Lencioni Stage-2 conflict check** — does the highest-status quiet
  member push back on senior scoring? If not, the team is below
  productive-conflict — veto-don't-dictate produces seniority-dressed-as-
  consensus.
- **Pfeffer & Sutton evidence-based filter** — when a candidate is
  justified by external precedent, run the local two-axis test extra
  rigorously rather than letting precedent substitute for evidence.

**For member mode** — three layers for honest decomposition of
"influenceable" at the seat:
- **Pfeffer power-axis literacy** — score honesty depends on knowing
  *which* power axis (authority, resources, allies, network, expertise)
  is producing the score, so the action implication is correct.
- **Cialdini six-lever literacy** — non-positional levers (reciprocity,
  social proof, expert authority) score "indirect influence" honestly
  rather than collapsing to "I have no authority, score 1."
- **Argyris Model-II inquiry** — the willingness to surface "none of
  these are influenceable from my seat" is a trainable discipline against
  Model-I defensive routines, not a personality trait.

Each addresses a documented failure mode that 4DX's leader-POV
methodology under-engages, and together they turn the lead-measure
discipline from a methodology with hidden failure modes into one with
the failure modes named, dosed, and mitigated across solo, team-leader,
and member seats.
