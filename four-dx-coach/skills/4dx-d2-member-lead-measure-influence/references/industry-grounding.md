# Industry-experience grounding for 4dx-d2-member-lead-measure-influence

Supplements the book's leader-POV with member-side / organizational-power /
self-management / honest-dissent literature where the book is thin (V1 ⚠️
partial). Ch. 3 + Ch. 13 frame the two-axis test (predictive + influenceable)
as a property of the *team*. From the member's seat, "influenceable" is also a
property of *position* — political, structural, and self-perception layers
shape which inherited leads a member can actually move and whether they can
honestly surface the answer "none of them, from where I sit." The sources below
fill those three layers.

## Underlying mechanism

A team lead measure that survives leader-side selection is, by construction,
predictive of the WIG and influenceable by *the team collectively*. From the
individual member's seat, three additional forces determine whether the
inherited lead is influenceable by *them*:

1. **Position / power layer** — sources of influence (formal authority,
   resources, allies, network position, expertise) are unevenly distributed
   inside even a small team. A 0-5 influence score on a lead is partly a
   property of the lead and partly a property of the seat; members who don't
   read this honestly produce either inflated scores (compliance-without-
   commitment) or deflated scores (learned helplessness).

2. **Persuasion / leverage layer** — once a member identifies a lead they can
   influence, *how* they move it depends on what social levers their seat
   provides. A frontline rep moves a customer-facing lead via direct action; a
   peer in a non-frontline role may move the same lead via reciprocity, social
   proof, or expert-authority cues without direct customer access. The
   six-principle map clarifies which lever a given seat actually supplies.

3. **Truth-telling layer** — when the influence map comes back genuinely empty,
   the member faces an organizational-truthfulness problem documented since the
   1980s: smart people in well-functioning teams systematically cannot say "I
   have no real influence on the chosen leads from where I sit," because the
   admission reads as incompetence. Without a structured way to surface this,
   the member fakes a focus pick and the lead doesn't move.

The book centers the team-level "influenceable" property and treats all three
of these member-level layers implicitly. The sources below address each
directly.

## Key industry sources

### Source 1: Pfeffer, *Power* (HarperBusiness, 2010) — cross-reference

Jeffrey Pfeffer, *Power: Why Some People Have It — and Others Don't* (New
York: HarperBusiness, 2010; ISBN 978-0-06-178908-3). Stanford GSB; the book
catalogues the sources of individual influence inside organizations — formal
authority, resources, allies, network position, expertise, energy, focus —
and argues these are unevenly distributed even on small teams.

Direct application to this skill: step 3's per-lead 0-5 influence audit is
sharper when the member can name *which* Pfeffer-axis a given score depends
on. A "5" on a customer-facing lead because of formal authority differs from
a "5" because of expertise or network access; the action implications differ.
A "1" because of structural position is a different signal from a "1" because
of low energy on the topic — only the structural one warrants step-6
escalation. Pfeffer gives the member the vocabulary to read their own scores
honestly.

### Source 2: Cialdini, *Influence* (1984; rev. ed. HarperBusiness, 2007) — cross-reference

Robert B. Cialdini, *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (originally New
York: William Morrow, 1984; revised edition: New York: HarperBusiness, 2007;
ISBN 978-0-06-124189-5). The six universal principles — reciprocity,
commitment-and-consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity —
operate as the levers through which one person moves another's behavior.

Direct application to this skill: when a member's seat does not supply formal
authority to move a lead directly, the six-principle map locates *what other
levers the seat does supply*. A back-office colleague on a customer-facing
lead set may move the lead via expert-authority cues to the frontline (score
3 on "I can move it indirectly by preparing better call lists / training the
script"), via social proof ("when I share what's working, peers adopt it"),
or via reciprocity ("I'm the person colleagues come to for support, so when I
nudge a lead behavior they reciprocate"). Without this lens, members under-
score themselves on leads they actually can move via non-positional levers.

**Cross-reference**: this source is also cited in
`4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep/references/industry-grounding.md` (Source 3)
for commitment-and-consistency lock-in at session time. In this skill it
appears for the *six-principle leverage* lens, a different slice of the same
canonical work.

### Source 3: Argyris, "Skilled Incompetence" (HBR Sep–Oct 1986) — new

Chris Argyris, "Skilled Incompetence," *Harvard Business Review*, vol. 64,
no. 5 (September–October 1986), pp. 74–79; reprint 86502. Argyris's classic
finding: in well-functioning organizations, otherwise highly competent
employees systematically cannot raise honest concerns about goals, role-fit,
or strategy because the organization's Model-I norms make the raising itself
look like incompetence, disloyalty, or trouble-making. The "skilled" part is
that members become skilled *at* not raising the concern — they learn to
make alignment look genuine when it isn't.

Direct application to this skill: step 6 (no-influence escalation) is
specifically the Argyris counter-move. When a member's per-lead influence
audit comes back genuinely low across all leads, the default Model-I script
is "pick one and pretend, because saying 'I have no real handle on any of
these' will read as slacking." Argyris's evidence base names that script as
the failure mode it is and reframes the surfacing of "I don't see a daily-
influence handle" as Model-II learning behavior — a contribution the team
needs to hear, not a member confession to suppress.

The framing prescription in step 6 — *"I want to be useful on the team WIG.
I've mapped my position against each lead and I don't see a daily-influence
handle. Can we look at this together?"* — implements Argyris's Model-II
inquiry stance: state what you observe, share the reasoning, invite joint
diagnosis. This is the script-level operationalization of the Edmondson
psychological-safety frame already cited in
`/Users/kouko/GitHub/monkey-skills/four-dx-coach/skills/4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension/references/industry-grounding.md`,
applied here at the lead-influence layer rather than the WIG-comprehension
layer.

**Cross-reference**: complements Edmondson (cited in
`4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`) on psychological safety. Edmondson
gives the cultural / team-climate frame for "ask discipline"; Argyris gives
the individual-behavioral frame for *why* even safe-feeling teams still fail
to surface honest no-influence findings, and what Model-II inquiry stance
unlocks the surfacing.

## What 4DX team-members learn from these sources

The book's two-axis test treats "influenceable" as a binary property of the
team's lead set. The industry literature decomposes that property at the
member's seat into three layers: position-and-power (Pfeffer), persuasion-and-
leverage (Cialdini), and truth-telling-discipline (Argyris). The practical
takeaway for this skill: when the agent runs step 3 (per-lead 0-5 audit),
step 4 (interpret distribution), step 5 (focus pick), and step 6 (no-
influence escalation), the agent is operating across all three layers
simultaneously. Score honesty depends on Pfeffer-axis literacy; "indirect
influence" scoring depends on Cialdini-lever literacy; the willingness to say
"none of them" out loud depends on the Argyris Model-II reframe. Without
these three lenses, the influence map collapses into either compliance
inflation or learned helplessness, and the focus pick that follows is
unreliable.
