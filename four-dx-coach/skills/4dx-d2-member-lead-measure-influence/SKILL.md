# 4dx-d2-member-lead-measure-influence — Influence the Team Lead Measures You've Been Handed

## R — Reading

> A good lead measure has two primary characteristics: It's predictive ... and it's influenceable.
>
> — McChesney/Covey/Huling, *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 3

> Each team member, every day, must be able to answer one question: *"What is the most important thing I can do today to advance my team's lead measure?"*
>
> — *4DX* 2nd ed., Ch. 12 (operating question for individuals on a 4DX team)

---

## I — Interpretation

The book defines a lead measure as both *predictive* of the WIG and *influenceable* by the team. The leader-side use of the test is "did we pick a good lead measure?" The member-side use is the same test, applied one position narrower: **"of the leads my team picked, which can *I*, from my actual seat, actually move this week?"**

Most members on a 4DX team inherit lead measures they did not choose. Three things are then true at once:

1. **All team leads ladder to the team WIG.** That is what made them survive selection. The member doesn't need to re-litigate that — they need to understand the ladder so they can act on it instead of guessing.

2. **The member's personal sphere of influence on those leads is uneven.** A frontline rep can move a "discovery calls per week" lead directly; a back-office colleague on the same team can move it only indirectly (e.g., by preparing call lists faster). Some members can move all team leads; some can move only one; a few may genuinely have no daily-influence handle on any of them. That unevenness is not a flaw of the leads — it is the cost of a single team WIG with mixed-role members, and it has to be mapped, not denied.

3. **The member's job is to convert influence into focus, not into coverage.** Pick the 1-2 leads where personal influence is highest as the primary weekly focus. The remaining leads stay supported (the member doesn't sabotage them) but are not the member's anchor for D4 commitment-making. Trying to "contribute equally to all leads" recreates the whirlwind under a 4DX label.

When the influence map comes back genuinely empty — when a member's role does not, structurally, allow them to move *any* of the team's leads — that is *not* a member problem to solve alone. It is a signal to the leader that either (a) the lead set didn't decompose to this role, or (b) this role is on the wrong team for this WIG. The skill surfaces that escalation explicitly rather than letting the member fake influence to keep up appearances.

Coaching is Socratic: the agent does not invent the influence map. The agent walks the member down each lead, asks "what part of this can *you* move from where you sit, this week, with no permission required?", and protects the honest answer.

## A1 — Past Application

### Case 1: Younger Brothers Construction — frontline workers moving a safety-compliance lead they did not pick (Ch. 13, leader-side cited)
- **Problem (leader-side as written)**: The team's lead measures were six observable safety-compliance behaviors (hard hat, eye protection, scaffolding, etc.) selected by leadership against a serious-incidents lag. Crew members had no role in choosing them.
- **Member-side mirror**: Each crew member, regardless of trade, had to find their personal handle on those six. A framer's influence on "scaffolding compliance" looks different from an electrician's on "eye protection" — both leads ladder to the same WIG, but personal influence on each lead varies by role, and members had to map that themselves to know where to anchor weekly attention.
- **Conclusion**: Members who mapped their personal influence per-lead concentrated on the 1-2 they could move daily and supported the rest. Members who tried to "keep all six in mind every shift" defaulted to the whirlwind.
- **Outcome**: Annual serious incidents fell from 57 to 12. The leader-side lesson is "pick observable leads"; the symmetric member-side lesson is "map your influence on each lead and pick where you act."

### Case 2: Towne Park valet — retrieval-time lead acted on by individual valets (Ch. 3 / Ch. 13)
- **Problem (leader-side as written)**: The team chose retrieval-time as the lead measure for a guest-satisfaction WIG. The choice was leader-side; the daily move was valet-side.
- **Member-side mirror**: Each valet had to find their own handle on retrieval time — running the route, staging cars by departure window, breaking the concrete-wall bottleneck. The lead was the same for everyone; the *influence move* varied by station, shift, and individual judgment.
- **Conclusion**: An individual valet who saw the lead through their own influence map identified a concrete bottleneck (a wall blocking a shortcut) and removed it on a Saturday. That move was not in the leader's lead-selection brief — it was the member's own influence map producing action.
- **Outcome**: Retrieval times dropped, satisfaction rose, and the case became a flagship D2 example. The book frames it as a lead-measure success; the member-side reading is that the lead unlocked individual influence the leader couldn't have prescribed.

### Case 3: The daily question at the individual contributor seat (Ch. 12)
- **Problem**: Without a per-lead influence map, members default to whatever screams loudest in the whirlwind, even when they nominally know the team leads.
- **Methodology applied**: Ch. 12's daily question — *"What is the most important thing I can do today to advance my team's lead measure?"* — operates only when the member has already located their personal influence handle on the lead. Without that, the question collapses into "what feels urgent today?"
- **Conclusion**: The daily question presupposes the influence map. This skill builds the map; the daily question consumes it.
- **Outcome**: Members report that the question becomes operational — and weekly commitment-making (D4) becomes specific — only after the influence-on-each-lead mapping is honest.

## A2 — Future Trigger ★

### When will the user need this skill?

1. The user is on a 4DX team where the leader has already selected the team's lead measures, and the user wants to know how to engage with them rather than just "comply with" them.
2. The user can recite the team's lead measures verbatim but doesn't know which ones are *theirs* to move from where they sit.
3. The user has been "supporting all the team leads" and feels stretched thin — they need to concentrate on 1-2.
4. The user suspects their role doesn't actually have a handle on any of the current leads (e.g., back-office on a customer-facing lead set) and isn't sure whether that's their own gap or a real role-fit signal to surface.
5. The user wants to bring the daily question ("most important thing I can do today to advance the team's lead measure?") into their morning but doesn't yet know which lead to answer it against.
6. The user is about to write a D4 weekly commitment but isn't sure which lead the commitment should anchor on.

### Language signals (user phrasings that should activate)

- EN: "Our team's lead measures are set — how do I influence them?" / "I'm on a 4DX team, leads are picked, what do I actually do?" / "Which lead measure is *mine* to move?" / "I support all of them but feel stretched thin."
- JP: 「team の lead measure は決まったが、自分はどう関われる？」「自分の立場でどの lead を動かせるか分からない」「全部の lead を見てるが手応えがない」
- zh-TW: 「團隊的 lead measure 已經訂了，我怎麼影響？」「lead 已經選好但我不知道哪些跟我的位置有關」「我支援全部 lead 但完全被攤薄」

### Non-trigger phrasings (do NOT activate)

- "I want to discover lead measures for my own personal goal." → solo case, route to `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`.
- "I'm a leader and I need to facilitate the team picking lead measures." → leader case, route to `4dx-d2-team-lead-measure-facilitation`.
- "I don't even know what the team WIG / lead measures are yet." → comprehension upstream, route to `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`.
- "Help me prep my commitment for the next WIG Session." → downstream commitment-making, route to `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`.
- "How do I run our team's weekly WIG session?" → cadence facilitation, route to `4dx-d4-team-wig-session-lead`.

### Distinction from neighboring skills

- vs. `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`: that skill assumes the user *picks* their own leads against a personal WIG. This skill assumes leads were already picked *for the team* by someone else; the work is influence-mapping, not selection.
- vs. `4dx-d2-team-lead-measure-facilitation`: that skill is for the leader running a candidate-generation + two-axis-scoring session. This skill is for someone receiving the output of that session and figuring out their personal handle.
- vs. `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`: D1 confirms the member can state the team WIG, the rationale, and the lead measures verbatim. D2 (this skill) starts after that — the leads are known, now where is the personal influence?
- vs. `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`: D4 prep takes the influence map and converts it into a specific weekly commitment to voice at the WIG Session. This skill produces the map; D4 consumes it.

## E — Execution

When this skill activates, the agent runs a Socratic coaching dialogue with one team member. **Do not invent the leads, the influence map, or the focus pick — extract what the member actually knows, surface where personal influence lives, and protect the honest "no influence here" signal when it appears.**

1. **Comprehension gate — confirm the team leads are known.**
   - Ask: "List your team's lead measures, ideally verbatim. How many are there?"
   - Completion criterion: member names each team lead measure clearly, with operational definition (frequency / quality / standard) where they know it.
   - Halt condition: if member can't list the leads or names the WIG (lag) instead, halt this skill and route to `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`. Influence-mapping over an unknown lead set produces theatre, not focus.

2. **Ladder check — does each lead actually predict the team WIG?**
   - For each lead, ask: "Walk me through the causal chain — when this lead moves, why does the team WIG move? In one sentence."
   - Completion criterion: member can state a one-sentence causal chain for each lead, or explicitly flag the ones where they can't.
   - Note: if member can't articulate the chain for any lead, that's a comprehension gap — assign homework to ask the leader, but do not block the rest of this skill (mapping influence on leads-without-clear-chain is still useful and surfaces the gap).

3. **Per-lead influence audit — score 0–5 for each.**
   - For each team lead, ask the member to score their personal influence: "From where you sit, this week, with no permission required, can you move this measure? Score 0 (cannot touch it) → 5 (I am the primary mover)."
   - Push the member to be specific about *what they would actually do* for any lead they score ≥3. Vague "I'd help out" answers score 1, not 3.
   - Completion criterion: every team lead has a 0–5 score plus a one-line "what I'd do" for any score ≥3.

4. **Map check — interpret the distribution.**
   - Look at the score distribution and interpret aloud with the member:
     - **Multiple high scores (≥3 on most leads)**: typical for generalist roles; pick the 1-2 highest as primary focus, support the rest. Move to step 5.
     - **One or two high scores, rest low**: typical for specialist roles; the high scores are the focus. Move to step 5.
     - **All low (no score ≥3)**: this is the no-influence escalation case. Move to step 6 instead — do NOT force a focus pick out of low-influence leads.
   - Completion criterion: distribution interpreted; routing to step 5 or step 6 decided.

5. **Focus pick — name 1-2 primary-focus leads.**
   - Ask: "Of the leads where you scored ≥3, which 1-2 will be your primary focus? Where will weekly D4 commitments anchor?"
   - Push back on "I'll do all of them" — the cap is hard. Trying to act on 4+ leads is the whirlwind in 4DX clothing.
   - Push back on picking based on "what the leader expects" rather than where personal influence is highest. Self-chosen focus follows the influence map, not political optics — the leader will see results, not effort theatre.
   - Completion criterion: 1-2 leads named as primary focus, with a one-sentence "this is my anchor because…"

6. **No-influence escalation path (if step 4 routed here).**
   - Validate the signal: "If you genuinely cannot move any of these leads from where you sit, that is information, not a failure. Two possibilities — (a) the leads decomposed to roles your seat doesn't sit in, or (b) your seat is on the wrong team for this WIG."
   - Assign homework: "Schedule 15 minutes with your leader. Frame it as: *'I want to be useful on the team WIG. I've mapped my position against each lead and I don't see a daily-influence handle. Can we look at this together and either find one I'm missing, or rescope my contribution?'* This is a contribution to team learning, not a complaint."
   - Completion criterion: member commits to scheduling the conversation, with the framing rehearsed.

7. **Translate the focus into the daily question (forward to D4).**
   - Ask: "Each morning, can you carry the question — *'What is the most important thing I can do today to advance my team's lead measure?'* — and answer it against your 1-2 focus leads specifically?"
   - Quality check: member should be able to imagine answering it tomorrow morning with a concrete action, not a generic intent.
   - Completion criterion: member agrees and names where the question will live (calendar block / sticky / morning ritual).

8. **Output the influence card.**
   - Write back to the user, in their own words:
     - **Team lead measures** (as listed): …
     - **My influence scores (0–5)**: …
     - **My primary focus (1-2 leads)**: …
     - **What I'll actually do on each focus lead**: …
     - **Daily question, anchored**: *"Most important thing I can do today to advance [focus lead]?"*
     - **Open questions for my leader** (if any): …
   - Completion criterion: member confirms the card; if "open questions for leader" is non-empty, that's the correct output — do not paper over.
   - Hand-off: "Take this into `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep` next time you're prepping a WIG Session commitment — anchor on a focus lead, not on whirlwind."

## B — Boundary ★

### Do NOT use this skill in:

- **Solo / individual-goal contexts.** If the user is selecting their own leads against a personal WIG, the leads are not inherited — use `4dx-d2-personal-lead-measure-discovery`. This skill assumes someone else picked the leads.
- **Leader-facilitating-team-selection contexts.** If the user is the manager running a team session to brainstorm + score lead candidates, use `4dx-d2-team-lead-measure-facilitation`. This skill is for the people on the receiving end of that session, not the facilitator.
- **WIG / lead comprehension gap.** If the member can't yet name the team's lead measures, influence-mapping is premature. Route to `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension` first.
- **Weekly commitment writing.** This skill produces a focus map across leads. Converting a focus lead into a specific *weekly* commitment ("call three customers Tuesday and Thursday") is a different skill — `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`.
- **As a substitute for asking the leader directly.** Some answers — whether a lead's causal chain is real, whether the lead set decomposes to this member's role — can only authoritatively come from the leader. The skill surfaces those gaps and routes them; it does not invent plausible-sounding answers.
- **As an end-run around fundamental disagreement with the leads.** If the member thinks the team's leads are simply wrong (don't predict the WIG, weren't influenceable for *anyone*), this skill comprehends and maps; it does not litigate. The dissent conversation is `4dx-d1-team-primary-wig-selection` territory or a 1:1 with the leader, not coaching.

### Author-warned failure modes (member-side mirror)

- **Predictive-but-not-influenceable trap (CE-02, member-side)**: the leader-side warning is "don't pick a lead the team can't influence." The member-side mirror: don't pick a *focus lead* you personally can't influence. A score-2 lead the team can move collectively but you can't move from your seat is not your focus pick — even if it's "the most important" lead overall.
- **Coverage-instead-of-focus (member-side mirror of CE-03 too-many-WIGs)**: trying to act meaningfully on 4+ leads recreates the whirlwind. The book caps team WIGs at 1-2; this skill caps member focus leads at 1-2 by symmetry. Coverage feels safe; focus moves the lag.
- **Compliance-without-commitment (CE-15, member-side)**: a member accepts the lead set, scores everything 3, picks one randomly to "look engaged" — without having located genuine influence. The honest answer might be "I don't have an influence handle on any of these," and the right move is escalation (step 6), not faking a focus.
- **Lead-as-personal-task confusion**: the member treats a team lead measure as their personal to-do list ("OK so I'll do 'discovery calls per week'"). The team lead is a *team* measure; the member's contribution is a slice of the lead, not the whole lead. Step 3 (per-lead influence audit) explicitly asks "what would you actually do" to keep that distinction sharp.
- **Goodhart self-targeting (CE-24)**: once the member fixes a focus lead, the same self-Goodhart risks the personal-D2 skill warns about apply: hitting the count without the quality (called three customers but didn't follow the script; logged 30 min writing but stared at the screen). Re-check the causal chain (step 2) every 4 weeks against actual lag movement.
- **Whirlwind invasion of the focus**: the focus lead doesn't get acted on because the whirlwind eats the slot. Step 7 (anchor the daily question on the focus lead) is the first defense; D4 commitment prep is the second.

### Author's blind spots / period limitations (apply with care)

- **Member voice underrepresented in selection-side cases**: Ch. 3 + Ch. 13 are written from the leader's seat. The book assumes if leads are well-chosen, members will find their handle naturally. Real members on real teams often don't — this skill exists for that gap, but the book itself does not directly address inherited-lead influence-mapping.
- **Hierarchical assumption**: 4DX assumes a leader-decides / team-executes structure where the member's job is to find their slice of the chosen leads. In flat / peer-driven / self-managing teams, the "ask your leader" homework in step 6 may not have a clear addressee — route to "ask the team" or to a peer-collective decision moment instead.
- **Operational-work bias**: leverage examples are operational (retail, hotels, plants, construction) where individual influence on a chosen lead is usually visible. Knowledge-work / creative-work members on a team WIG often face leads where personal influence is murky or runs through long feedback loops. Apply step 3's "what would you actually do" test rigorously rather than copying retail patterns.
- **Single-team assumption**: 4DX assumes a member is on one 4DX team. In matrix orgs the member may be on multiple teams with multiple lead sets; this skill handles one team at a time and explicitly does not arbitrate between competing influence maps (that is a meta-strategy / capacity conversation).
- **Honest "no influence" rarely modeled**: the book's cases overwhelmingly show influence being *found*; it under-models the case where a member's role genuinely can't move any of the chosen leads. Step 6 corrects for that by giving the no-influence finding a legitimate escalation path rather than treating it as a member failure.

### Easily-confused neighboring methodologies

- **OKR cascading**: OKRs cascade Objectives + Key Results through layers; each level gets its own KRs. 4DX is structurally different — the team has one shared lead set, and members find their *slice of the same leads*, not a smaller-KR per person. Don't reflexively translate OKR experience.
- **Generic "ownership" coaching**: "take ownership of the goal" is too vague to act on. 4DX is more specific — ownership means a per-lead 0-5 influence score, a 1-2 lead focus pick, and a daily-question anchor. Vague ownership talk doesn't satisfy this bar.
- **MBO-style "your individual objectives"**: MBO assigns each person their own measurable objective. 4DX assigns the team a shared lead set and decomposes contribution on the lead side. The member's "thing" is influence on a shared lead, not a personal objective.
- **RACI matrix**: RACI assigns Responsible / Accountable / Consulted / Informed roles on tasks. Influence-mapping on lead measures is upstream of RACI — it locates *whether the member has a daily-influence handle at all*, before any role assignment.

### Industry-experience addendum (member-side influence literature)

The book's two-axis test treats "influenceable" as a team-level property. From the member's seat, influence is also a property of *position* — three layers shape what they can actually move:

- **Pfeffer, *Power* (HarperBusiness, 2010)** — influence sources (formal authority, resources, allies, network, expertise) are unevenly distributed inside a team. Step 3's 0-5 audit is sharper when the member knows which axis their score depends on. (Cross-ref: `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`.)
- **Cialdini, *Influence* (1984; rev. 2007)** — the six principles locate *non-positional* levers (reciprocity, social proof, expert-authority cues) a member without formal authority can still pull. (Cross-ref: `4dx-d4-member-commitment-prep`.)
- **Argyris, "Skilled Incompetence" (HBR 1986)** — classic finding: smart employees can't surface "I have no real influence here" honestly because the admission reads as incompetence. Step 6's escalation script is the Model-II counter-move. (Complements Edmondson in `4dx-d1-member-team-wig-comprehension`.)

See `references/industry-grounding.md` for full citations and mechanism notes. Practical implication: step 3's audit and step 6's escalation sit on a documented evidence base, not just on book authority.
