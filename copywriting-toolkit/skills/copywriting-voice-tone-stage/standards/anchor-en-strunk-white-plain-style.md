### Strunk & White — The Elements of Style (EN | Q1 toward-Q4, author duo)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Compress English prose to irreducible declarative force — vigorous, specific, active — so the reader grasps the thought without detour or decoration.

## Native critical read
- "Required reading" for anyone aspiring to write well (David Ogilvy, *Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.1, 1983)
- "Vigorous writing is concise" (Strunk, *Elements of Style*, Rule 17, 1918)
- "Write with nouns and verbs, not with adjectives and adverbs" (White, *Elements of Style*, "An Approach to Style", 1959)
- "The approach to style is by way of plainness, simplicity, orderliness, sincerity" (White, *Elements of Style*, "An Approach to Style" §1, 1959)
- "Do not overwrite… rich, ornate prose is hard to digest" (White, "An Approach to Style" §4, 1959)

## Prose mechanics
- Omit needless words: strike every word that does not serve the sentence's work (Rule 17). Cut: "the fact that", "owing to the fact that", "in a hasty manner", "this is a subject that"
- Use active voice as default (Rule 14). Subject acts on object; reserve passive only when actor is genuinely unknown or beside the point
- Put statements in positive form (Rule 15): "he usually came late" beats "he was not very often on time". Avoid "not un-" constructions
- Use definite, specific, concrete language (Rule 16). Replace abstractions ("a period of unfavorable weather") with observable nouns ("it rained every day for a week")
- Keep related words together (Rule 20). Place subject next to verb, modifier next to what it modifies; rearrange before adding commas
- Use nouns and verbs as load-bearing words; treat adjectives and adverbs as last resort, not first reach
- Do not explain too much. Trust the chosen verb; avoid adverbial reinforcement ("she grinned happily") and stacked dialogue tags
- Prefer standard idiomatic English; avoid fancy words, foreign phrases, and coinages where a plain word exists

## Examples
- "Omit needless words." (*Elements of Style*, Rule 17, Strunk 1918 / White 1959)
- "Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts." (Rule 17, 1918/1959)
- "Write with nouns and verbs, not with adjectives and adverbs." ("An Approach to Style" §4, White 1959)
- "Do not overstate." ("An Approach to Style" §9, White 1959)
- "Where Is Life Held Cheap? In Bombay." (*The New Yorker* "Comment", E.B. White, c.1940s)
- "The barn was pleasantly warm in winter when the animals spent most of their time indoors, and it was pleasantly cool in summer when the big doors stood wide open to the breeze." (*Charlotte's Web*, Ch.1, White 1952)
- "Summer is the time when one sheds one's tensions with one's clothes, and the right kind of day is jeweled balm for the battered spirit." (*One Man's Meat*, "Memorandum", White 1941)

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLM defaults to staccato three-word commands and fortune-cookie aphorisms, mistaking concision for chopped-up sentences and mistaking plainness for absence of rhythm
- **Mitigation** (14 words): "Vary sentence length; cut words, not clauses; keep one concrete noun per sentence"
