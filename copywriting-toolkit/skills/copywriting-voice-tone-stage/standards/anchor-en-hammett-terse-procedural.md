### Dashiell Hammett — terse procedural (EN | Q4 toward-Q3, author)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Record human action and appearance as if dictated by a neutral witness; withhold the narrator's interior entirely so the reader must infer motive, feeling, and judgment from observable surface.

**Native critical read**:
- "He was spare, frugal, hard-boiled, but he did over and over again what only the best writers can ever do at all. He wrote scenes that seemed never to have been written before." (Raymond Chandler, "The Simple Art of Murder", 1944)
- "Hammett gave murder back to the kind of people that commit it for reasons, not just to provide a corpse… He put these people down on paper as they are, and he made them talk and think in the language they customarily used for these purposes." (Chandler, same essay)
- "Hammett was the ace performer… He is said to have lacked heart, yet the story he thought most of himself is the record of a man's devotion to a friend." (Chandler, 1944)
- "Hammett wrote at first (and almost to the end) for people with a sharp, aggressive attitude to life. They were not afraid of the seamy side of things; they lived there." (Chandler, 1944)
- "Hammett's style… could say almost anything he had to say." (Chandler, 1944)

## Prose mechanics
- Third-person external only — report what a camera and microphone would record; no access to any character's thoughts, not even the protagonist's
- Physical description as enumerated catalogue: facial geometry, eye colour, jaw angle, hand position, garment condition — short declarative clauses with commas, no metaphor load
- Verbs concrete and transitive: "lit," "put," "poured," "said," "looked at," "took." Avoid abstract verbs of cognition/emotion in narration
- Adjective discipline: one or two per noun maximum, and observable ("thin," "blond," "crooked") not interpretive ("menacing," "sinister," "tragic")
- Dialogue tags stay at "said" and "asked"; almost never modified by adverbs. Action beats between lines carry tone ("Spade grinned. 'Yes.'")
- Short-to-mid sentences dominate; occasional longer sentence built by comma-chained parallel clauses, never subordination stacks
- Proper names repeat in full ("Spade," "the fat man") rather than varied via pronouns-plus-epithets. Flat repetition is a feature
- Scenes open with setting or posture, not mood. First sentences orient the reader to room, light, or body before any event
- No free indirect style; no narrator commentary. If a character is lying, prose does not hint — reports the words and the face

## Examples
- "Samuel Spade's jaw was long and bony, his chin a jutting v under the more flexible v of his mouth. His nostrils curved back to make another, smaller, v. His yellow-grey eyes were horizontal." (*The Maltese Falcon*, 1930, opening)
- "He looked rather pleasantly like a blond satan." (*The Maltese Falcon*, 1930)
- "I first heard Personville called Poisonville by a red-haired mucker named Hickey Dewey in the Big Ship in Butte." (*Red Harvest*, 1929, opening)
- "A man came around the corner at top speed. Another man was at his heels. The first man had a revolver in his right hand." (*Red Harvest*, 1929)
- "I was leaning against the bar in a speakeasy on Fifty-second Street, waiting for Nora to finish her Christmas shopping, when a girl got up from the table where she had been sitting with three other people and came over to me." (*The Thin Man*, 1934, opening)
- "'Who shot him?' I asked. The grey man scratched the back of his neck and said: 'Somebody with a gun.'" (*Red Harvest*, 1929)

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLMs asked for "Hammett" default to noir-atmosphere cliché — rain, neon, weary first-person interiority, world-weary metaphors ("the city was a tired animal"). This is Chandler-contaminated Hammett, not Hammett. Hammett's narrator has no weather mood and no similes of fatigue
- **Mitigation**: "Camera-microphone only. No interior access, no atmospheric similes, no weary narrator voice"
