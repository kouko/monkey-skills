### Jason Fried + David Heinemeier Hansson (DHH) — Basecamp Contrarian Manifesto (EN | Q1 toward-Q2 + Q4 center)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Puncture received wisdom of the tech-startup / hustle industry by stating the opposite as plain fact, in sentences short enough that rebuttal feels pedantic; two-to-three-sentence chapter blocks make each claim feel aphoristic and already-settled. Reader is addressed as a competent adult lied to by business-book convention.

## Native critical read
- "Plain-style manifesto" / "anti-hustle" register (NYT Book Review on *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work*, 2018: "stripped-down counter-manifesto to startup machismo")
- "Contrarian startup philosophy" (Wired profile on Fried + DHH's anti-VC-scale stance, 2018)
- "Rework-style contrarian chapter" — Inc. / Atlantic shorthand for the two-page aphoristic chapter form (Inc. cover "The 37signals Way", 2010)
- "Workplace minimalism" (Fast Company 2018 review)
- Self-description: "We wanted short chapters. Really short. The kind you can read on the toilet." (Fried, Signal v. Noise, "Writing Rework", 2010)
- "Workaholism is a disease" — DHH flagpole (DHH, "RECONSIDER", Startup School 2015)
- "The Strunk & White of startup books" (Derek Sivers blurb for *Rework*, 2010)

## Prose mechanics
- **Antithesis-as-headline**: chapter title states conventional wisdom; sentence 1 inverts it flatly. Template: "[Received wisdom] is [bullshit / toxic / guessing]." No qualifier, no "in our experience".
- **Two-to-three-sentence chapter openers**: opening paragraph is 2-3 short declarative sentences, each ≤15 words. No throat-clearing, no "In this chapter we'll…".
- **Aphorism → cost → pivot** three-beat rhythm: sentence 1 declares; sentence 2 names the cost; sentence 3 tells the reader what to do instead.
- **Avg sentence length 7-14 words**; forbid compound-complex with ≥2 subordinate clauses. Periods over commas. Fragments permitted when they punch.
- **Second-person imperative voice** — "Stop", "Quit", "Start", "Enough". Never "one should", never passive.
- **Forbid corporate abstract nouns**: no "synergy", "leverage", "ecosystem", "scale", "optimization". Use concrete replacements ("work" not "productivity", "meeting" not "alignment session").
- **Short-word preference**: Anglo-Saxon over Latinate. "Use", not "utilize". "Show", not "demonstrate".
- **No hedging adverbs**: forbid "perhaps", "arguably", "somewhat", "generally". Claim lands flat or not at all.
- **Italics for single-word emphasis, never bold runs**: one italicized word per paragraph max.
- **Chapter closes on prescription, not summary**: final sentence is a directive or one-line consequence — never "In conclusion".
- **DHH-tell**: polemic edge, named-enemy move ("VCs", "hustle porn", "the office"), occasional em-dash aside. **Fried-tell**: warmer practical register, "we at Basecamp" anecdotes, slightly gentler imperative. Default = treat as fused.

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLM produces antithesis-template slop — "X is dead", "Y is broken", "Z is a lie" — untethered to real conventional wisdom the reader holds. Form becomes empty contrarian pose (widely imitated in LinkedIn thought-leader posts). Secondary: ripping Fried's anecdote register without the aphoristic sentence-1 punch — breezy blog-copy that never commits to a claim.
- **Mitigation** (14 words): "Name the exact received wisdom being punctured; claim must survive without the contrarian frame."
