### Amy Hempel — compressed minimalism (EN | Q1 toward-Q4, author)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Render grief, dread, or intimacy at maximum compression — a sentence carries emotional weight by what it refuses to say. Surface is glassy, composed, slightly deadpan; the unsaid does the work.

## Native critical read
- "miniaturist" (widely applied in US review press — *NYT* on *Collected Stories*, 2006)
- "The Cliff Notes of stories" (Hempel self-description, *Paris Review*, The Art of Fiction No. 176, 2003)
- Gordon Lish "consecution" — sentence-by-sentence sonic/syntactic compression, acknowledged by Hempel (*Paris Review*); shared lineage with Carver, Robison, Hannah
- "lyric precision / prose so compressed it reads like poetry" (recurring review vocabulary for *Reasons to Live* 1985 and *At the Gates of the Animal Kingdom* 1990)
- "deadpan grief / comic bleakness" (critical shorthand in Lish-school reception; *BOMB Magazine* 1997 + *Paris Review*)

## Prose mechanics
- **Sentence length crashes unpredictably**: interleave short declaratives (3-7 words) with one longer clause; avoid uniform rhythm. Never two "medium" sentences in a row
- **Open on a deflected fact, not a scene**: first sentence delivers trivia, a rule, a list item, or an overheard line — never "It was a Tuesday when…" exposition
- **Concrete noun over abstract emotion**: replace any word in {grief, loss, fear, love, sadness} with a specific physical object or gesture. If the abstract noun survives, cut the sentence
- **One piece of found trivia per paragraph**: factoids (animal behavior, earthquake statistics, medical detail) metonymize feeling. Use one, never a cluster
- **Ellipsis via white space, not "…"**: end paragraphs on unfinished thought; let the line break carry silence. No dangling typographic ellipsis
- **Dialogue strips attribution to "I said" / "He said" — never adverbs** (Lish-school rule; no "she whispered sadly")
- **Humor is survival, not relief**: one dark joke per 500 words; deliver it in the same flat register as grief, no tonal shift
- **No summary sentences**: never write "That was the year everything changed"

## Examples
- "Tell me things I won't mind forgetting,' she said. 'Make it useless stuff or skip it.'" ("In the Cemetery Where Al Jolson Is Buried," *Reasons to Live*, 1985)
- "I think of the chimp, the one with the talking hands. / In the course of the experiment, that chimp had a baby. Imagine how her trainers must have thrilled when the mother, without prompting, began to sign to her newborn." ("In the Cemetery Where Al Jolson Is Buried," 1985)
- "The best thing about Christmas, Ted says, is pulling apart the wishbone." (*Reasons to Live*, 1985)
- "What Mrs. Lawton meant was, It is not as bad as you think." ("Going," *Reasons to Live*, 1985)
- "I have a superstition: if I tell you what I want, I won't get it." (*At the Gates of the Animal Kingdom*, 1990)
- "The Whartons brought their cat to us to keep while they went away. 'Nancy,' they said, 'we can't thank you enough.'" ("At the Gates of the Animal Kingdom," title story, 1990)
- "Just for now, I said, call it by its Spanish name. The name I gave the cat. *Gato*. It let me pretend I was somewhere else." (*At the Gates of the Animal Kingdom*, 1990 — attributed commonly, exact source TBD)

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLMs default to Hemingway-pastiche when asked for "minimalism" — short sentences about weather, men, whiskey with tough-guy dialogue tags. Hempel's register is opposite pole: domestic, animal-facts, nervous humor, feminine grief. A Hempel draft full of bars and rain is a failed draft
- **Mitigation**: "Replace any whiskey/weather/tough-dialogue with domestic object, animal-fact, or deflected joke"
