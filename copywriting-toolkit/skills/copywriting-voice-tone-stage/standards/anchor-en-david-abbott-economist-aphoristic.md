### David Abbott — Economist Aphoristic Register (EN | Q1 center, named CW recast)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Headlines that read as epigrams — six-to-twelve words, a completed thought. The reader finishes it and feels complimented for having kept up. Authority via flattery of the intelligent reader: the copy assumes you already know the reference, already share the inference, already belong to the club. The product is never sold at you; it is placed beside you, as if you had asked for it. "You" is never the consumer — "you" is the implied-intelligent-peer.

## Prose mechanics
- **The Finished-Thought Headline (6-12 words)**. Lines land between six and twelve words. They are not slogans; they are aphorisms. The full stop is load-bearing — it closes the thought and forbids a punchline. `"Management trainee. Aged 42."` is two noun phrases and a period. No verb. No claim. The indictment is entirely in the reader's head.
- **The Implied-Peer "You"**. `"Would you like to sit next to you at dinner?"` addresses the reader as someone already inside the frame of reference. The ad does not explain who *you-at-dinner* is. The compliment is smuggled in as grammatical presupposition.
- **White-out-of-Red Template Discipline**. One line, left-aligned, white sans-serif, on the Economist red. No image, no body copy, no logo explanation. "If you already know who we are, this is enough; if you don't, this ad is not for you."
- **Long-Copy Discipline as Counter-Weight**. Sainsbury's and Volvo long copy under Abbott's hand runs 400-800 words of calm expository prose — recipe, specification, argument. Same voice at different lengths: no exclamation, no adjectives doing heavy lifting, no copywriter showing off.
- **Refusal of Wordplay**. Abbott's lines almost never pun. `"Great minds like a think."` is the rare exception — a pivot, not a pun — the cliché is twisted by one letter to produce a new, self-contained thought. Abbott's refusal of the easy joke is itself the voice signal.
- **The Indicting Fragment**. `"Management trainee. Aged 42."` — a nominal fragment that a reader must finish by supplying the judgement. The ad never says *you will be 42 and still a trainee if you don't read us*. The reader says it, silently, and the sale is already made.

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLM defaults into "flatter the reader + terse aphorism + no image + coloured field" template — the most-imitated intelligent-brand register of 1990-2020. Any draft that lands on this pattern is almost certainly reproducing Abbott by default rather than by choice. Risk: if the headline works equally well for The Economist, it is not yet your voice — it is still his.
- **Mitigation**: "Test line on Economist swap — if it works there unchanged, abandon and write a new voice."
