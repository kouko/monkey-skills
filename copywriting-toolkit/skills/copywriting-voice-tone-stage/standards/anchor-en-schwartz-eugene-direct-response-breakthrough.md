### Eugene Schwartz — "channel the desire, never create it" (EN | Q4 center, copywriter)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Long-form direct-response sales copy that enters the reader's existing desire at its current awareness stage, stacks curiosity via tight fascination bullets, and earns the sale through specificity and proof rather than hyperbole. Authoritative but disciplined — Schwartz explicitly rejects the "create want" posture.

## Native critical read
- "not to create this mass desire — but to channel and direct it." (Schwartz, *Breakthrough Advertising*, 1966, opening chapter on Mass Desire)
- "I don't write them. I listen them." / "Lazy ears produce bad ads." (Schwartz, "I Write With My Ears" essay)
- "Write to the chimpanzee brain, simply, directly." (Schwartz, Rodale Press seminar, May 11 1994)
- "Copy is not written. Copy is assembled." / "Research is 80–90% of your copywriting work." (Schwartz, Rodale seminar 1994)
- "miniature sales arguments" — his term for fascination bullets (Schwartz, attributed in Brian Kurtz / Boardroom canon)
- "A very good copywriter is going to fail. If the guy doesn't fail, he's no good." (Schwartz, Rodale seminar 1994)

## Prose mechanics
- **Lead = promise of specific measurable payoff in a specific time window** — not a claim, a transaction. Template: "Give me [tiny time], and I'll give you [disproportionate outcome]." No abstraction in the verb.
- **Fascination bullet structure**: withhold the resolution, name only the category + a concrete numeric/negative hook. "What never to eat on an airplane" — bullet names the airplane (anchor) and a prohibition (withhold), never the food (resolution).
- **Specific-number proof stacks**: when you cite, cite three figures in consecutive sentences ("300 magazines," "30 minutes," "$50 million in sales") — plural specificity converts skepticism to curiosity.
- **Awareness-stage calibration** (Schwartz's 5 levels): if prospect is Unaware, lead with the problem; if Product Aware, lead with the offer; do not cross-wire. Pick ONE stage per lead paragraph and hold it.
- **Market-sophistication rule**: if market has heard the claim before, do not repeat the claim — add a mechanism ("the reason this one works is ___") or raise the stakes with a larger, more specific promise.
- **Rhythm = bullets / story / bullets** (Schwartz's "blockbuster" structure): open with 4-6 fascinations, insert 1 short narrative block (2-4 sentences) to let the reader breathe, resume with 4-6 more.
- **Verb choice**: describe what the product *does* (action on reader's life) — never what it *is* (feature list). "Lowers your cholesterol in 14 days" not "Contains plant sterols."
- **Headline job is narrow**: to earn the next sentence. Do not pre-sell in the headline; pre-sell is the lead's job.
- **Never "creative" — combinatorial**: assemble from product research. If a sentence reads like it could belong to any product in the category, cut it.

## Examples
- "Read 300 Business Magazines In 30 Minutes." (Headline, Boardroom Reports sales letter, 1988; Martin Edelston's phrasing selected by Schwartz)
- "Give Me 15 Minutes And I'll Give You A Super-Power Memory." (Headline for Harry Lorayne's memory book, attributed to Schwartz)
- "SWIMS UNDER ITS OWN POWER." (Headline cited by Schwartz in "I Write With My Ears" as example of transcription-not-invention)
- "The power, the force, the overwhelming urge to own that makes advertising work, comes from the market itself, and not from the copy. Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears and desires that already exist in the hearts of millions of people, and focus those already existing desires onto a particular product." (*Breakthrough Advertising*, Ch. 1, 1966)
- "What never to eat on an airplane." / "How to collect interest from two different banks at the same time." / "Bills it's okay to pay late." (Mel Martin bullets for Boardroom *Book of Secrets* — the Schwartz-seeded fascination tradition)
- "I will take the best copywriter in this room, who is sloppy and careless, and match him against a good copy cub. And 2 out of 3 times, the sloppiness of the great person will be beaten by the carefulness of the other person. Because the power of the ad is always in the product itself. It is NOT in the copywriter. The copywriter simply finds it and expresses it." (Rodale speech, 1980)

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLM defaults into Hopkins-era carnival-barker pastiche — exclamation-heavy, "AMAZING!" hyperbole, generic benefit claims ("revolutionary formula"), bullet lists that resolve instead of withhold, and imaginary "research" numbers without specificity anchors. Reads like a 1990s late-night infomercial, not Schwartz.
- **Mitigation**: "Withhold resolutions in bullets; cite specific numbers only; no exclamation points."
