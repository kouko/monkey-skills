### Dan Wieden — "Just Do It" Minimal Imperative (EN | Q2 center toward Q3 peer-aspirational)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Compress a universal athletic / human aspiration into a two-to-four-syllable imperative that sounds spoken, not written — collapsing the distance between brand and reader to that of a coach's shout, while leaving the verb object-less so every reader supplies their own referent.

## Native critical read
- "minimalist imperative" (Adweek retrospective on W+K house craft, 2022 obit coverage)
- "democratic athlete register — greatness is in all of us, not just the elite" (NYT obit, 30 Sep 2022)
- "three-syllable compression; the tagline is a command, not a claim" (Ad Age "Creativity" dossier on JDI at 30, 2018)
- "the line sounds like something you'd say to yourself in the mirror" (Dan Wieden, interviewed in *Art & Copy*, 2009 — self-citation)
- "anaphoric chant — `Find your greatness`, `Write the future`, `Just do it` — a verb, a possessive, a stop" (Adweek W+K house-pattern column, 2015)

## Prose mechanics
- Lead with a bare verb. No subject, no "you should", no softener. "Just do it." "Find your greatness." "Write the future."
- Keep the headline imperative to 2-4 words / 3-5 syllables. If you need a fifth word, cut one.
- Leave the verb's object generic or possessive ("it", "your greatness", "the future") — readers supply specifics; you do not.
- Build anaphora by repeating the imperative-possessive frame across executions, not by rhyming. `Find your X` is the spine; X rotates.
- Address the reader as a peer-athlete, not a consumer. No "we", no "our product", no brand name in the line itself.
- Voice-over cadence is colloquial — contractions, sentence fragments, short breath groups. If a line doesn't read aloud in one breath, break it.
- Use the athlete's monologue as the default POV: first-person confession that arrives at the imperative. The brand does not speak; the athlete does.
- Refuse abstraction words in the tagline slot — no "excellence", "innovation", "passion". Only verbs and plain nouns.
- End spots on the two-beat stop: tagline, logo. No voice-over trail, no qualifier.

## Examples
- "Just do it." (Nike, launch spot, 1988 — voiced over Walt Stack running across the Golden Gate Bridge)
- "I've missed more than 9,000 shots in my career. I've lost almost 300 games. 26 times I've been trusted to take the game winning shot and missed. I've failed over and over and over again in my life. And that is why I succeed." (Nike "Failure" spot, Michael Jordan, 1997)
- "Bo knows baseball. Bo knows football. Bo knows tennis. … Bo, you don't know diddley." (Nike "Bo Knows", Bo Jackson & Bo Diddley, 1989)
- "Money, it's gotta be the shoes!" (Nike Air Jordan, Mars Blackmon / Spike Lee, 1988-89)
- "Greatness is no more unique to us than breathing. We're all capable of it. All of us." (Nike "Find Your Greatness", London Olympics, 2012 — voice-over)
- "Find your greatness." (Nike, tagline sign-off, 2012)
- "Write the future." (Nike, World Cup 2010 — W+K Amsterdam, same house imperative-possessive frame)
- "Yesterday you said tomorrow." (Nike, 2013 — bare vocative, same three-beat stop)

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLM defaults to the three-word imperative template mechanically — "Own your journey", "Chase your dream", "Live your truth" — producing cargo-culted aspiration stripped of the athlete-monologue setup that earned the tagline. The imperative works only because a specific confession (missed shots, 300 losses) lands the stop; without the confession, the imperative is a motivational poster.
- **Mitigation**: "Earn the imperative with a concrete first-person confession; never open cold on the tagline."
