### Tim Delaney — Patek Philippe stewardship register (EN | Q2 toward-Q1, named CW recast)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Aphoristic brand-tagline register built on a single load-bearing sentence that reframes ownership as custodianship. The voice is declarative, patrician, emotionally restrained; it speaks from the position of an heirloom addressing its future owner rather than a product addressing a buyer. Long-copy variants extend the tagline into quiet, third-person vignettes about fathers, sons and inherited objects — sentiment earned through understatement, never through adjectives. Register sits at Q2 (craft / classical) pulling toward Q1 (sparse / iconic): almost no modifiers, no verbs of persuasion, no exclamation, no wit. Rhythm is iambic and closural; sentences land on nouns of duration ("generation", "lifetime", "son"). The reader is trusted to complete the emotion.

## Native critical read
- The 1996 line launch ("Generations") replaced product-feature advertising in haute horlogerie with a stewardship frame — you do not own the watch, you hold it in trust. This is the register-defining move.
- Intergenerational inheritance as structural device: the protagonist is never the wearer but the relationship between two wearers separated by time (father → son, and implicitly son → grandson).
- Quiet luxury before the term existed: the copy refuses to name price, mechanism, complication, or heritage dates in the headline. Prestige is signalled by what is withheld.
- Tagline-as-aphorism: the line is built to survive decoupled from image, which is why it has outlasted three decades of campaign photography and creative-director rotations at the brand.

## Prose mechanics
1. **Lead with a negation that reframes possession.** Open by denying the transactional verb ("own", "buy", "keep") before introducing the custodial one ("look after", "hold", "care for").
2. **Close the sentence on a human noun of succession.** Final word should be "son", "generation", "child" — not the product, not an adjective.
3. **Use "merely" or "only" as the pivot.** The concessive adverb does the persuasive work so no other modifier has to.
4. **Refuse product vocabulary in headline.** No "watch", no "timepiece", no "movement", no dates, no complications. The object is referred to by pronoun or ellipsis.
5. **Long copy in third-person vignette, present tense, declarative clauses.** Short sentences. No rhetorical questions. No second-person "you" except in tagline.
6. **One idea per sentence; one sentence per paragraph where possible.** White space carries the restraint.
7. **Verbs of duration over verbs of action** — "endures", "remains", "is passed", "is kept" — passive and stative constructions are permitted and preferred.

## Examples
1. "You never actually own a Patek Philippe. You merely look after it for the next generation." *(Patek Philippe, "Generations", 1996 — core line, Tim Delaney / Leagas Delaney)*
2. "Begin your own tradition." *(Patek Philippe, "Generations", sub-line on father-son executions)*
3. "Some of the world's most beautiful machines are to be found inside a Patek Philippe." *(Patek Philippe, "Generations" long-copy opener, men's Calatrava execution)*
4. "A Patek Philippe is made to last generations. It doesn't just tell you the time. It tells you something about yourself." *(Patek Philippe, "Generations" long copy)*
5. "Who will you be when you wear a Patek Philippe?" *(Patek Philippe, "Generations" women's long-copy variant)*
6. "The stitching on a Timberland boot will last longer than the boot itself." *(Timberland, Leagas Delaney 1990s, long-copy craft register — Delaney-attributed)*

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLMs copy the "You never actually own…" structure literally or near-paraphrase, producing instantly-parody luxury advertising; stack heritage signifiers (founding years, founder names) in headlines, violating the withholding discipline; make sentiment explicit with "love / bond / cherish / forever" adjectives that destroy the restraint; drift into second-person across long copy, collapsing the third-person vignette frame. Categories at highest risk: watches, jewellery, spirits, real estate — all 30-year imitators of this register.
- **Mitigation**: "Succession nouns carry emotion; withhold dates, price, adjectives; third-person vignette only."
