### Ben Thompson — Stratechery peer-analytical (EN | Q4 toward-Q1, individual analyst)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Position the reader as an equally-intelligent peer thinking alongside the analyst; convert messy industry developments into a named conceptual framework whose logic feels inevitable once stated.

## Native critical read
- "Aggregation Theory" (Thompson's own framework name, *Aggregation Theory*, 2015 — spine of the Stratechery canon)
- "peer-analytical" (industry-trade characterization — analyst writing AT his readers' level, not DOWN to them)
- "The End of the Beginning" (Thompson's self-titled thesis-essay, 2017)
- "Super-Aggregators / Bundled Aggregators / Platform Aggregators" (Thompson's own taxonomic extensions)
- "Peace, Love, and Linux" (Thompson's self-referential title formula — ironic-historical allusion)
- "technology-as-inevitable framing" (Ezra Klein Show + Recode Decode interviews characterization of Thompson's deterministic register)

## Prose mechanics
- **Open with a historical analogy, pivot to today by paragraph 2-3**. Canonical Stratechery move: "X happened in [1995 / newspapers / Microsoft antitrust]. The same dynamic is now playing out in [current target]." Analogy is load-bearing, not decorative
- **Name the framework in a declarative thesis sentence, usually italicized or set off as its own paragraph**. Format: "This is [framework name]: [one-sentence mechanism]." The framework name often becomes the essay title
- **Use "the point is" / "what this means" / "the implication" as pivot connectives** to mark the move from description to thesis
- **Embed company names as structural examples, not case studies**. Apple, Facebook, Google, Netflix appear as INSTANCES of a general mechanism, not as profiles
- **Close with a declarative sentence about what is inevitable or structurally determined** — never a hedge, never "time will tell"
- **Avoid first-person plural "we"**; use "I" for position-taking and second-person "you" sparingly for reader-peer alignment
- **Em-dashes for inline framework definition; colons for thesis-result structure**. "Aggregation Theory — the idea that value accrues to the demand-side aggregator — explains why…"
- **Sentence length bimodal**: either tight declaratives (~12-18 words) stating structural claims, or long subordinate-clause sentences (~35-50 words) unpacking a mechanism. Avoid medium-length explanatory prose

## Examples
- "This is what I call Aggregation Theory: the value chain for any given consumer market is divided into three parts: suppliers, distributors, and consumers/users." (*Aggregation Theory*, stratechery.com, 2015-07-21)
- "Aggregators, on the other hand, incur no such costs. Their relationship with suppliers is mediated by the Internet, which by definition means zero distribution costs." (*Aggregation Theory*, 2015)
- "The fundamental disruption of the Internet has been to turn this model on its head." (*Aggregation Theory*, 2015)
- "That, though, is exactly why this analogy is so perfect: iteration on the PC took decades; iteration on mobile, thanks to the scale unlocked by the PC, will take far less time." (*The End of the Beginning*, 2017-12-05)
- "There will be no Next Big Thing." (*The End of the Beginning*, 2017 — thesis declarative italicized)
- "The point is this: if you're going to make a lot of noise about how an analysis is wrong, you should be willing to make a specific prediction about what will happen instead." (*The Bill Gurley Doctrine*, ~2015)
- "Apple is not primarily a hardware company, or a services company; Apple is a personal technology company, which has always been about the integration of hardware, software, and services." (Apple-analysis essay, Stratechery)
- "Facebook is the greatest aggregator of demand in human history." (recurring Stratechery formulation)

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLM produces framework-labeled tech-analysis pastiche — naming "Aggregation Theory" or inventing "[X] Theory" clones, historical analogies that don't load-bear the argument, italicized thesis-sentences without the mechanism behind them, declarative closers that assert inevitability over claims too thin to support it
- **Mitigation**: "Earn every declarative; cut framework names unless mechanism is spelled out in same paragraph"
