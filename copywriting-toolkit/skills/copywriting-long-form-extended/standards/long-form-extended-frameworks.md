# Long-Form Extended Frameworks — QUEST / PASTOR

Tier 2 standard: references Tier 3 long-form-pasona-canon.md for the
PASONA-family baseline. This standard adds two Anglo-origin long-form
frameworks — QUEST and PASTOR — that complement the 神田 PASONA
lineage for English-language and international copy contexts. Both
frameworks are independently grounded on their respective canonical
publications and dual-verified through JP secondary sources.

This standard does NOT replace `long-form-pasona-canon.md`. It
extends the long-form routing matrix by offering alternative
structural choices when PASONA-family frameworks are not the best
fit (e.g., English-language copy, coaching/ministry verticals,
mountain-metaphor narrative arcs).

## Critical Attribution Corrections

- **Drift #6: QUEST is NOT by Clayton Makepeace.** Multiple EN
  secondary sources misattribute QUEST to Makepeace. Canonical
  author is **Michel Fortin** (blog post 2005-12-04). The
  "P.P.F.T formula" sometimes conflated with QUEST is
  unverifiable and should not be cited.
  Spelling: **Michel** (not Michael). JP transliteration:
  マイケル・フォーティン.

- **Drift #7: PASTOR's T = Transformation + Testimony (dual
  meaning).** JP blog-level secondary sources simplify T to
  "Testimony only." However, Edwards' original book and the
  JP ダイレクト出版 edition both preserve both meanings.
  Each P-A-S-T-O-R letter carries multiple dimensions (see
  stage definitions below). Simplifying any letter to a
  single meaning is a drift.

- **Drift #14: PASTOR is NOT by Dan Kennedy, Frank Kern, or
  others.** Secondary sources occasionally misattribute PASTOR
  to other direct-response marketers. Canonical author is
  **Ray Edwards**; the "shepherd" etymology is confirmed from
  Edwards' podcast and 4 corroborating sources.

## QUEST — Michel Fortin 2005

### Framework concept

QUEST is a guide, not a goal or a rule (Fortin's own framing).
The acronym maps to a mountain-climbing metaphor: ascent (Q+U+E),
summit (E+S transition), descent (S+T). Unlike AIDA, QUEST adds
a **pre-Attention filtering stage** (Qualify) and an **empathy
bridge** (Understand) before presenting the educational pitch.

QUEST complements AIDA — it does not replace it. Where AIDA
assumes the reader is already in-audience, QUEST explicitly
filters and builds rapport before educating.

### 5 stages

| Stage | Name | Mission |
|-------|------|---------|
| **Q** | Qualify | Filter the target reader. Open with a question, scenario, or identifier that makes the right reader self-select ("this is for me") and the wrong reader self-deselect. Pre-Attention filtering. |
| **U** | Understand | Build an empathy bridge. Demonstrate that you understand the reader's situation, pain, and aspirations — not by listing features, but by reflecting their experience back to them. |
| **E** | Educate | Present proof and information. Show why the solution works, citing evidence, case studies, or logical reasoning. The summit of the mountain — the reader's knowledge peak before the offer. |
| **S** | Stimulate | Sell the benefits. Translate Education into desire by painting the outcome picture — what life looks like after the solution. Emotional activation. |
| **T** | Transition | Close with a CTA. Move the reader from consideration to action. The descent — smooth, natural, inevitable from the summit. |

### Mountain metaphor flow

```
          E ← summit (knowledge peak)
         / \
        /   \
       U     S
      /       \
     Q         T
  (filter)  (CTA)
  ascent    descent
```

The ascent builds trust and knowledge; the descent converts that
trust into action. Rushing to the summit (skipping Q or U) creates
"who are you and why should I care?" resistance. Lingering at the
summit (over-educating without Stimulate) creates "interesting but
I won't act" inertia.

### Form and applicability

- **Optimal character count**: 3,000+ characters (long-form
  optimized). Adaptable to shorter forms by compressing E and S
  stages, but the Q and U stages lose their structural advantage
  below ~1,500 characters.
- **Best fit**: educational content marketing, coaching offers,
  expert-positioned products, webinar invitations, course sales
  pages — contexts where the writer's credibility and empathy
  matter as much as the offer itself.
- **AIDA complement**: Use QUEST when the audience needs
  qualification (niche products, high-ticket offers) or when
  empathy-building is critical. Use AIDA for broad-audience
  awareness campaigns.

## PASTOR — Ray Edwards 2016

### Framework concept

PASTOR uses the shepherd metaphor: the copywriter guides the
reader (flock) from pain to transformation. Each letter carries
**multiple semantic dimensions** — this multi-meaning design is
canonical and should not be flattened.

The shepherd etymology: Edwards explicitly frames the copywriter
as a pastor/shepherd who cares for and guides the reader, not a
manipulator who pushes the reader. This ethical framing is
intrinsic to the framework.

### 6 stages (with multi-meaning dimensions)

| Stage | Name(s) | Dimensions | Mission |
|-------|---------|------------|---------|
| **P** | Person, Problem, Pain | 3 dimensions | Identify the specific person you're writing to, articulate their problem, and name the pain it causes. All three dimensions in one stage — not just "problem." |
| **A** | Amplify, Aspirations | 2 dimensions | Amplify the consequences of inaction (what happens if the problem persists?) AND paint the aspirations (what does the reader want instead?). Dual-direction tension. |
| **S** | Story, Solution, System | 3 dimensions | Tell a relevant story (yours or a client's), present the solution principle, and describe the system/method. Narrative carries the persuasion. |
| **T** | Transformation, Testimony | 2 dimensions | Show the transformation (before/after picture) AND back it with testimony (social proof). **Both meanings are canonical** — do not simplify to testimony alone (drift #7). |
| **O** | Offer | 1 dimension | Present what the reader gets. Edwards suggests the Offer section should be ~20% of total copy length (Cooler Insights 2017 corroboration; Medium-High confidence). |
| **R** | Response | 1 dimension | Explicit CTA. Tell the reader exactly what to do next. |

### Narrative requirement

PASTOR's S stage (Story/Solution/System) requires genuine
narrative space — anecdotes, case studies, or founder stories.
This makes PASTOR inherently long-form (3,000+ characters).
Compressing S into a single sentence strips the framework of
its structural advantage.

### Form and applicability

- **Optimal character count**: 3,000+ characters (long-form only).
  The Story element requires narrative space that cannot be
  compressed below ~2,000 characters without losing the
  framework's core.
- **Best fit**: coaching/consulting offers, ministry/mission-driven
  products, personal-brand sales pages, course/program launches,
  membership pitches — contexts where the shepherd/guide
  relationship between writer and reader is natural.
- **PASONA complement**: Use PASTOR when the writer/brand has a
  strong personal story to tell and the reader relationship is
  guide-based. Use PASONA when the product/offer is the hero
  (not the writer) or when the audience is JP-primary.

### Selection criteria (extended)

- JP-primary audience, product-as-hero → PASONA family (see
  `long-form-pasona-canon.md` §Selection criteria)
- EN/international audience, education-first positioning → QUEST
- EN/international audience, personal-story/guide positioning → PASTOR
- Bilingual campaign (JP+EN) → PASONA family for JP version, QUEST
  or PASTOR for EN version (do not force-translate PASONA stages
  into English — the cultural resonance differs)
- High-ticket coaching with strong founder story → PASTOR
- Expert content marketing, webinar funnels → QUEST
- When uncertain → default to 新 PASONA (JP) or QUEST (EN) as
  safe general-purpose choices

## Cross-framework stage mapping

| Structural role | PASONA family | QUEST | PASTOR |
|-----------------|---------------|-------|--------|
| Target filtering | P (Problem) | **Q (Qualify)** ← explicit | P (Person/Problem/Pain) |
| Empathy / rapport | A (Affinity, 新+) | **U (Understand)** | A (Amplify + Aspirations) |
| Solution principle | S (Solution) | E (Educate) | S (Story/Solution/System) |
| Benefit / transformation | B (Benefit, PASBECONA) | S (Stimulate) | T (Transformation/Testimony) |
| Evidence | E (Evidence, PASBECONA) | within E | within T (Testimony) |
| Product detail | C (Contents, PASBECONA) / O (Offer) | within S+T | O (Offer) |
| Scarcity / urgency | N (Narrow down) | within T | within R |
| CTA | A (Action) | T (Transition) | R (Response) |

This mapping enables cross-pollination: a JP copywriter using
PASBECONA can borrow QUEST's explicit Qualify stage as a
pre-Problem filter. An EN copywriter using PASTOR can borrow
PASBECONA's B-E-C evidence depth between S and O.

## Ethics boundary

Same jurisdiction as `long-form-pasona-canon.md` §Ethics boundary.
QUEST and PASTOR both emphasize empathy over fear appeal (QUEST's
Understand stage, PASTOR's shepherd framing). Neither framework
endorses aggressive Agitation-style fear appeals.

PASTOR's Amplify stage includes "consequences of inaction" — this
is structurally similar to 旧 PASONA's Agitation but is balanced
by the Aspirations dimension in the same stage. The dual-direction
design (fear + hope in one stage) is canonical and should not be
split into fear-only amplification.

Expression restrictions under 景品表示法 / FTC Endorsement Guides
are handled by `persuasion-ethics.md`.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Attributing QUEST to Clayton Makepeace**. Canonical author is
  Michel Fortin (drift #6). Do not cite Makepeace or "P.P.F.T."
- **Simplifying PASTOR's T to "Testimony only"**. Canonical is
  Transformation + Testimony — both dimensions (drift #7).
- **Simplifying any PASTOR letter to a single meaning**. P has 3
  dimensions, A has 2, S has 3, T has 2. The multi-meaning design
  is the framework's structural feature.
- **Attributing PASTOR to Dan Kennedy or Frank Kern**. Canonical
  author is Ray Edwards (drift #14).
- **Using QUEST for short-form copy**. QUEST's Qualify and
  Understand stages need space to function; below ~1,500 characters,
  they collapse into generic openers.
- **Compressing PASTOR's Story stage into one sentence**. The
  narrative requirement is structural — without it, PASTOR becomes
  a generic problem-solution formula.
- **Force-translating PASONA stage names into English and calling
  it "the same as QUEST/PASTOR"**. The frameworks have different
  structural logics (PASONA: fear/empathy → offer → scarcity;
  QUEST: filter → empathy → educate → stimulate → close;
  PASTOR: person → amplify → story → transform → offer → respond).
- **Citing CREMA as a long-form framework**. CREMA (Conclusion-
  Reason-Evidence-Method-Action) is a JP web-marketing industry
  template for **short-to-mid form light-action copy** (email
  opt-in, newsletter subscribe, LP click-through, free download).
  It is documented in `light-action-frameworks.md` with appropriate
  primary-source handling (no canonical author, parallel to BEAF).
  Using CREMA for long-form contexts (sales letters, high-ticket
  LPs) is a **scope mismatch** — CREMA's Evidence is too compressed
  and its Action too direct to overcome deep purchase resistance.
  For long-form contexts, prefer QUEST / PASTOR / PASONA-family.
- **Treating the Offer 20% rule as absolute**. The ~20% proportion
  for PASTOR's Offer stage is a heuristic from Cooler Insights
  2017 corroboration, not Edwards' canonical prescription. Adjust
  based on product complexity and audience awareness level.
