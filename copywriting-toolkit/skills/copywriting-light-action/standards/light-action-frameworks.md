# Light-Action Frameworks — PREP 法 + CREMA 法

Tier 2 standard: pairs **PREP 法 (Point-Reason-Example-Point)** and
**CREMA 法 (Conclusion-Reason-Evidence-Method-Action)** as the
copywriting-team canon for **light-action** (micro-conversion) copy —
email opt-in, newsletter subscribe, free download, SNS share, LP
click-through, and similar low-commitment conversions. Both are
widely adopted in Japanese web-writing / SEO / copywriting practice
but lack canonical author attribution; they are positioned as
**"industry-standard framework"** parallel to BEAF's treatment in
`mid-form-beaf-canon.md`.

The **light-action vs heavy-action** distinction is grounded on
Avinash Kaushik's micro/macro conversion taxonomy (2007) and
Cialdini's commitment-and-consistency principle (1984), with
Freedman & Fraser's foot-in-the-door (1966) as the experimental
basis for why small commitments escalate. The framework-to-action-
weight mapping itself is an analytical synthesis over documented
framework characteristics — see § "Analytical synthesis disclaimer."

## Critical Attribution Corrections

- **Drift #15: PREP was NOT originated by Brian Tracy.** Attribution
  to Tracy appears in Medium articles and blog posts; no primary
  citation to any Tracy title. Do not cite Tracy as PREP's inventor.

- **Drift #16: PREP is NOT McKinsey-origin.** This attribution is
  conflation with Barbara Minto's *Pyramid Principle* (1987), likely
  via 照屋華子's McKinsey background leaking into PREP attribution
  in JP business-writing discourse. PREP and the Pyramid Principle
  are distinct frameworks.

- **Drift #17: PREP is NOT Toulmin's argumentation model.**
  Toulmin (1958) has 6 components (claim / grounds / warrant /
  backing / qualifier / rebuttal), structurally distinct from
  P-R-E-P's 4-stage repetition structure.

- **Drift #18: CREMA's name does NOT derive from espresso-foam
  「クレマ」.** Sources that address the name state it is simply the
  5 English head-word initialism (「頭文字をとった造語」). The
  espresso-foam connection is post-hoc reader interpretation, not
  documented etymology.

- **Drift #19: David Kolb did NOT invent CREMA.** Content Kessaku
  analogizes CREMA's flow to Kolb's experiential learning cycle —
  this is a comparative observation, not an attribution claim. Do
  not cite Kolb as CREMA's originator.

## Framing — the light-action zone

### Why a separate framework zone exists

Different copywriting frameworks suit different **action-commitment
weights**. Heavy-action copy (purchase, contract signing, high-
ticket commitment) needs:
- Extensive trust-building (PASONA Affinity / PASTOR Story)
- Evidence accumulation (PASBECONA B-E-C)
- Scarcity and urgency (PASONA Narrow down)
- Multiple thousands of characters to establish each

Light-action copy (email opt-in, newsletter subscribe, click-through)
needs:
- Fast logical payload delivery
- Immediate Action prompt
- Hundreds (not thousands) of characters
- Low reader cognitive cost — readers will not invest 10 minutes
  reading a subscribe page

Using PASONA/QUEST/PASTOR for light-action produces over-engineered
copy that suppresses conversion. Using CREMA/PREP for heavy-action
produces under-built copy that fails to overcome purchase
resistance. The frameworks are optimized for different commitment
zones.

### Theoretical grounding — Kaushik + Cialdini + FITD

The light-action / heavy-action distinction aligns with three
primary-source-grounded concepts:

1. **Kaushik 2007 micro/macro conversion**: macro = primary business
   outcome (purchase, qualified lead); micro = process milestones
   and secondary behaviors on the path to macro. Light-action copy
   targets micro-conversions; heavy-action copy targets macro.
2. **Freedman & Fraser 1966 foot-in-the-door**: small prior
   commitment raises compliance with later larger request (lawn-sign
   study: 76% vs 20%). Light-action conversions function as the
   "small prior commitment" that enables later heavy-action.
3. **Cialdini 1984 commitment-and-consistency**: small active public
   freely-chosen commitments escalate to larger compliance. This is
   the theoretical bridge linking today's email opt-in to next
   month's purchase.

Light-action copy is therefore not "lesser" copy — it is the
**commitment-escalation entry point** without which heavy-action
conversions degrade.

## PREP 法 — the 4-stage lightweight logical frame

### Stage definitions

| Stage | Name | Mission |
|-------|------|---------|
| **P** | **Point (結論)** | Open with the conclusion. "What I'm telling you is X." Front-load the payload. |
| **R** | **Reason (理由)** | Why is X true? One clear reason. Logical bridge, not emotional appeal. |
| **E** | **Example (具体例)** | Concrete instance that illustrates the reason. Makes abstract reason graspable. |
| **P** | **Point (結論再掲)** | Restate the conclusion. Rhetorical closure; reinforces memory. |

### Form and applicability

- **Optimal character count**: 50–300 characters per PREP block.
  Can be chained for longer content, but each block stays compact.
- **Best fit**: SNS posts, email subject lines + first sentences,
  blog post openers, video script hooks, presentation slide
  narration. Any context where a reader has <30 seconds of
  attention and you need to land a point cleanly.
- **Weakness**: no explicit action prompt. PREP ends on Point
  restatement, not Action. For click-through or subscribe contexts
  that require explicit CTA, CREMA is preferred.

### PREP chaining

For longer content (500–1500 characters), chain 2–3 PREP blocks,
one per sub-point. Each PREP block is independently self-contained;
a reader who scans only the first P of each block should grasp
the full argument structure.

## CREMA 法 — PREP extended with Method + Action

### Stage definitions

| Stage | Name | Mission |
|-------|------|---------|
| **C** | **Conclusion (結論)** | Open with the conclusion. Same as PREP's opening Point. |
| **R** | **Reason (理由)** | Why is the conclusion true. Same as PREP's R. |
| **E** | **Evidence (証拠 / 根拠)** | Backing for the reason — data, proof, testimonial, cited fact. Harder evidence than PREP's E (Example). |
| **M** | **Method (手段 / 方法)** | How the reader achieves the conclusion. Concrete steps or means. |
| **A** | **Action (行動)** | Explicit CTA. "Click here," "Subscribe below," "Download now." |

### PREP → CREMA structural extension

CREMA extends PREP along two axes:

- **Evidence instead of Example**: PREP's Example illustrates the
  reason; CREMA's Evidence proves it. Evidence raises persuasion
  depth for contexts where the reader may question the reason.
- **Method + Action appended**: PREP ends on Point; CREMA ends on
  Action. Method bridges from "why it's true" to "how the reader
  does it"; Action seals the micro-conversion.

Sources describe this prose-style rather than as a formula:
「PREP 法の弱点を補う」(つばき), 「『行動を促す』文章を書く部分が
組み込まれています」(ブログライター). The analytical shorthand
"CREMA = PREP + M + A" is this team's synthesis, not verbatim
JP canon.

### Form and applicability

- **Optimal character count**: 200–1,500 characters. Longer than
  PREP but well below PASONA's 3,000+ threshold.
- **Best fit**: email opt-in pages, newsletter subscribe forms,
  free-download LPs, LINE 登録 pages, light affiliate content,
  article-format content prompting a light action at the end.
- **Weakness**: not for high-ticket purchases. CREMA's Evidence
  stage is too compressed to address deep purchase resistance; its
  Action is too direct without the trust-building ramp PASONA /
  QUEST / PASTOR provide.

| Framework | Origin | Stages | Characters | Action weight | Best fit |
|-----------|--------|--------|------------|---------------|----------|

| CREMA | JP web-marketing ~2021 | 5 | 200–1,500 | Light (explicit CTA) | Opt-in, subscribe, download, CTR |
| BEAF | JP EC practice | 4 | 10s–100s | Light-to-Mid (product page) | EC product page, POP, catalog |
| 旧 PASONA | 神田 2016 | 5 | 1,500–5,000 | Mid-to-Heavy | DM, email, flyer (JP) |
| 新 PASONA | 神田 2016 | 6 | 3,000–10,000 | Heavy | LP, newsletter, white paper (JP) |
| PASBECONA | 神田・衣田 2021 | 9 | 5,000–20,000+ | Heavy (high-ticket) | high-ticket LP (JP) |

| PASTOR | Edwards 2016 | 6 | 3,000+ | Heavy | guide-based, story-driven (EN/intl) |

### Selection criteria for the light-action zone

- **Conversion = email opt-in / newsletter subscribe / download /
  light LINE add**: CREMA. The explicit Action stage seals the
  conversion.
- **Conversion = "finish reading a point" / SNS share / brief
  engagement**: PREP. The Action-less structure preserves reader
  autonomy, which suits share-triggering content.
- **Conversion = EC product-page purchase**: BEAF, not CREMA.
  Product-page purchases are light-to-mid, but BEAF is the JP EC
  industry-specific optimization (Benefit-first ordering for scan
  reading).
- **Conversion = high-ticket purchase, contract, long commitment**:
  PASONA-family / QUEST / PASTOR. Do not compress these into
  CREMA — the trust-building ramp is load-bearing.
- **Uncertain**: default to CREMA for any copy prompting a
  non-purchase action; default to BEAF for EC product pages; default
  to 新 PASONA (JP) or QUEST (EN) for sales letters.

## PREP / CREMA differences (industry confusion correction)

| Axis | PREP | CREMA |
|------|------|-------|
| Stages | 4 (P-R-E-P) | 5 (C-R-E-M-A) |
| Third stage | Example (illustrative) | Evidence (probative) |
| Final stages | Point restatement (rhetorical) | Method + Action (operational) |
| Explicit CTA | None | Required (A stage) |
| Character range | 50–300/block | 200–1,500 |
| Best use | Logical payload delivery | Light-action conversion |
| Origin era | Anglo 1980s corporate training | JP web-marketing ~2021 |

Secondary sources sometimes describe CREMA as "PREP plus Method and
Action," which is a defensible analytical synthesis but lacks
verbatim canonical grounding. The more precise description:
**CREMA reuses PREP's opening (C=P, R=R), strengthens Example into
Evidence, and appends Method + Action.**

## Analytical synthesis disclaimer

The **action-weight framework taxonomy** (this standard's central
organizing axis) is **not documented in JP copywriting canon as a
named taxonomy**. JP copywriting texts distinguish frameworks by:

- **媒体** (sales letter / LP / メルマガ / SNS / EC product page)
- **購買段階** (認知 / 興味 / 比較検討 / 購入)

Not by action commitment weight per se. This standard synthesizes
framework characteristics (length, structure, ending stage) and
anchors the synthesis on Kaushik 2007 + Cialdini 1984 + Freedman-
Fraser 1966. The theoretical grounding is primary-sourced; the
copywriting-specific mapping is this team's analytical contribution.

Users of this standard should:
- Cite PREP and CREMA as industry-standard frameworks (no canonical
  author, parallel to BEAF)
- Cite Kaushik / Cialdini / Freedman-Fraser as theoretical grounding
  for the light vs heavy distinction
- NOT cite any JP copywriting book as originator of the light-action
  / heavy-action framework taxonomy — that framing is this team's
  synthesis

## Ethics boundary

Light-action copy has specific ethical considerations:

- **Commitment escalation must be transparent.** Using
  foot-in-the-door to obtain a small commitment with the intent to
  manipulate into a larger commitment — while the reader is unaware
  of the escalation path — is a dark pattern. Cross-reference with
  `persuasion-ethics.md` §Dark Patterns.
- **Light-action conversions do not permit deceptive scarcity.**
  "Last chance to subscribe!" for an always-available newsletter is
  a violation of 景品表示法 §優良誤認 / 有利誤認 and ethics gate
  criteria.
- **Opt-in friction removal must not cross into deceptive
  auto-opt-in.** UX dark patterns (pre-checked subscription boxes,
  hidden opt-out) are outside this standard's scope and are
  governed by `persuasion-ethics.md` + 景品表示法.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Attributing PREP to Brian Tracy, McKinsey, or Toulmin.** Each is
  a documented drift (#15, #16, #17). Use "Anglo corporate
  communication training, 1980s" as the origin framing; cite
  Toastmasters as the canonical popularizer.
- **Citing the espresso-foam etymology for CREMA.** Not documented
  in any consulted source; likely post-hoc reader speculation
  (drift #18). Present CREMA as a 5-English-head-word initialism.
- **Citing David Kolb as CREMA's originator.** Kolb's experiential
  learning cycle is a comparison analogy in one source, not an
  attribution claim (drift #19).
- **Using CREMA for high-ticket sales letters.** Scope mismatch.
  CREMA's Evidence is too compressed to overcome deep purchase
  resistance; its Action is too direct without trust-building
  ramp. Use 新 PASONA / PASBECONA / QUEST / PASTOR for high-ticket.
- **Using PASONA / QUEST / PASTOR for newsletter opt-in.** Reverse
  scope mismatch. Over-engineered copy suppresses light-action
  conversion because readers will not invest the cognitive cost.
- **Compressing PASONA into 5 stages and calling it CREMA.** These
  are different frameworks with different starting philosophies.
  PASONA starts from Problem (fear/empathy); CREMA starts from
  Conclusion (logical payload). Do not conflate.
- **Flattening CREMA's Evidence into Example.** Evidence is
  probative (data, testimonial, cited fact); Example is illustrative
  (an instance). Substituting Example for Evidence makes CREMA
  structurally identical to PREP + M + A, losing the framework's
  persuasion-depth advantage.
- **Omitting the Action stage in CREMA.** CREMA-without-Action is
  structurally incoherent — it's just "PREP with different E." The
  explicit CTA is the framework's purpose.
- **Claiming "action weight" is a documented JP copywriting
  taxonomy.** It is this team's analytical synthesis over
  documented framework characteristics (length, structure, ending
  stage), anchored on Kaushik + Cialdini + Freedman-Fraser. Do
  not cite JP copywriting books as originating this taxonomy.
- **Treating CREMA's 2021-10-25 earliest documentation as its
  invention date.** That is the earliest **web** documentation.
  A pre-2021 seminar, paid newsletter, or paywalled source may
  predate it; we simply have no access to such sources. Document
  with appropriate epistemic humility.
