### Taika Waititi — deadpan-absurd-warmth (EN | Q3 toward-Q2, screenwriter/director)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Sustain an underplayed deadpan surface so that genuine tenderness, grief, or political horror can surface a beat later and land harder for having been ambushed by a joke. Emotion arrives via interrupted sincerity, not declared feeling.

**Native critical read**:
- "Kiwi deadpan" — recurring NZ/UK critical shorthand (*The Guardian* on *Wilderpeople*, *Boy*; NZ On Screen profile)
- "awkward tenderness / sincerity ambushed by a joke" (A.O. Scott, Manohla Dargis in *NYT* on *Jojo Rabbit*, *Wilderpeople*)
- "mockumentary fourth wall" (critical term for *What We Do in the Shadows* confessional; RogerEbert.com, BFI, *The Guardian*)
- "childlike-intimate / majestical register" (Letterboxd + 1of200.nz NZ critic vocabulary — kids speak with grown-up gravity, adults with kid gravity)
- "genuine-love-interrupted-by-joke" (IndieWire, *The Atlantic* on *Jojo Rabbit*, *Boy*)

## Prose mechanics
- **Underplay the punchline**: drop funny word at end of flat clause, never telegraphed. Cadence period-heavy not exclamation-heavy. "Yeah, majestical." not "It's so majestical!"
- **Mockumentary fourth wall (when form permits)**: address reader/camera as if explaining household admin. Bureaucratise the absurd: chore rosters, towel rules, freelance-Nazi paperwork. Register stays clerical while content is wild
- **Interrupt sincerity with a small joke, then continue sincerely**: never let emotional beat peak unprotected. Joke → half-second → real feeling lands, unamended. Do not resolve the joke; let the feeling resolve it
- **Childlike earnestness carried by adults; adult gravity carried by kids**: invert who speaks with which weight. Child declares a philosophy; adult admits he doesn't know a word. Neither character flags the swap
- **Invent or misuse one word per passage**: "majestical," "skux life," "freelance Nazi." One mis-coinage acts as deadpan signature; do not stack more than one per paragraph
- **No abstract emotion nouns in peak beat** (love / grief / freedom). Substitute concrete action: dancing, a towel, an eagle
- **Avg sentence length short (<14 words) for deadpan turns**; longer only when character is earnestly over-explaining a small thing (which is itself the joke)
- **Never wink**. Voice must not acknowledge it is being funny. Dryness = character believes what they are saying

## Examples
- "I didn't choose the skux life, the skux life chose me." (*Hunt for the Wilderpeople*, 2016, Ricky Baker)
- "Pretty majestical, aye?" / "I don't think that's a word." / "Sure it is." (*Hunt for the Wilderpeople*, 2016, Hec & Ricky)
- "If you're going to eat a victim on my nice clean couch, put down some newspaper on the floor, and some towels. It's not hard to do." (*What We Do in the Shadows*, 2014, Viago)
- "We're vampires. We don't put down towels." (*What We Do in the Shadows*, 2014, Vladislav)
- "Dancing is for people who are free. It's an escape from all this." (*Jojo Rabbit*, 2019, Rosie)
- "Let everything happen to you. Beauty and terror. Just keep going. No feeling is final." (*Jojo Rabbit*, 2019, Rilke epigraph closing credits)
- "What's the first thing you'll do when you're free?" / "Dance." (*Jojo Rabbit*, 2019, Jojo & Elsa)

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLMs overshoot into **quirky-whimsy** — stack wacky coinages, telegraph punchline, add exclamation marks, explicitly announce feelings ("he felt so much love"). Reads as Wes-Anderson-pastiche-lite, not Waititi: deadpan collapses into twee, sincerity never gets ambushed — just gets declared
- **Mitigation**: "One mis-coined word max; end beats flat; never name the feeling — show the action"
