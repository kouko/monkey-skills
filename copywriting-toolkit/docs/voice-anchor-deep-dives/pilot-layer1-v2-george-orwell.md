### George Orwell — political plain prose (EN | Q1 toward-Q4, essayist + novelist)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: Make political and moral argument visible by stripping language down to concrete Anglo-Saxon nouns and verbs, so orthodoxy cannot hide inside stale phrasing; the sentence becomes a windowpane through which the reader sees the thing, not the writer.

**Native critical read** (5):
- "Good prose is like a windowpane." (Orwell, "Why I Write", *Gangrel* No.4, 1946 — author self-citation)
- "Political language is designed to make lies sound truthful and murder respectable, and to give an appearance of solidity to pure wind." ("Politics and the English Language", *Horizon*, April 1946)
- "The great enemy of clear language is insincerity." ("Politics and the English Language", 1946)
- "A mass of Latin words falls upon the facts like soft snow, blurring the outlines and covering up all the details." ("Politics and the English Language", 1946)
- Lionel Trilling: Orwell's virtue is "the virtue of not being a genius… he communicates to us the sense that what he tells us is true" (Introduction to *Homage to Catalonia*, 1952)

## Prose mechanics (7)
- Apply Orwell's six rules from "Politics and the English Language" (1946): (i) never use a metaphor, simile or figure of speech seen in print; (ii) never use a long word where a short one will do; (iii) if possible to cut a word, cut it; (iv) never use passive where active works; (v) never use a foreign phrase / scientific word / jargon if there is an everyday equivalent; (vi) break these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous
- Prefer concrete Anglo-Saxon verb to Latinate noun-cluster: "kill" not "effect the liquidation of"; "show" not "render visible"
- Name the thing, then say what you think about it — never hide judgement inside abstract nouns ("democracy", "freedom", "fascism") without anchoring to a specific referent
- Open declaratively with a single unqualified statement of fact; reserve subordination for proof, not hedging
- Strip intensifiers ("very", "rather", "quite") and dying metaphors ("toe the line", "ride roughshod over"). Orwell's test: if you can picture it, keep it; if it's a phrase seen a hundred times, cut
- Use first person sparingly and only to declare moral position or admit failure ("I am going to criticise...", "I shot the elephant..."); never "I" for ornament
- Let one short sentence land after a longer one — the short sentence carries the verdict

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLMs asked for "Orwellian plain style" default to sermon cadence — stacking moral absolutes, over-using "truth" / "honesty" / "decency" as abstract nouns, producing exactly the stale Latinate warnings-about-Latinate-English that Orwell mocked; sounds like a freshman essay *about* Orwell, not a sentence Orwell would write
- **Mitigation** (≤15 words): "Name one concrete thing per sentence; cut every abstract noun you cannot point at"
