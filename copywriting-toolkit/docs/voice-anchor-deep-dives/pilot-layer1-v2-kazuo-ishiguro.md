### Kazuo Ishiguro — restrained denial (EN | Q2 toward-Q3, author)

## Voice direction
**What this register achieves**: first-person narration that performs composure while leaking its own pressure — the sentence stays dignified, qualified, periodic; feeling surfaces through hedge, pause, and refusal, not through statement.

**Native critical read**:
- "unreliable narrator of dignity" (James Wood, *The Broken Estate* / *New Yorker* essays)
- "what is said beneath what is said" (Ishiguro, *Nobel Lecture* 2017)
- "writing what the characters don't want to say" (Ishiguro, *Paris Review* Art of Fiction No. 196, 2008)
- "emotional restraint as dramatic pressure" (Wai-chew Sim, *Kazuo Ishiguro*, Routledge 2009)
- "flat, courteous surface, enormous undertow" (Anne Enright, *The Guardian* review of *The Buried Giant*, 2015)

## Prose mechanics
- **Periodic, self-qualifying sentence**: main clause delayed behind 1-3 hedges ("I should perhaps point out", "I do not think it would be unreasonable to suggest"); avoid direct declarative openings
- **Understatement as concealment, not modesty**: when stake is highest, downgrade verb and register ("a small matter", "not entirely comfortable", "somewhat trying"). Never name the feeling
- **Euphemism the narrator believes in**: narrator's self-deception must be narrator's own vocabulary, not authorial irony — use "my employer", "my duties", "our purpose" in place of true referent
- **Professional vocabulary as armour**: borrow jargon of a role (butler / carer / artist); "dignity", "distinction", "completion" carry weight no feeling-word is allowed to
- **Pause via parenthetical and mid-sentence revision**: "— or rather —", "— that is to say —", "though I suppose"; sentence catches itself mid-admission and walks back one step
- **Flat past-tense memoir frame**: narrate from settled later vantage; avoid present-tense immediacy. Composure is retrospective
- **No metaphor-led lines**: register is plain, British-formal, near-Edwardian; imagery incidental, never the sentence's engine
- **Refusal to conclude**: end paragraphs on qualification or softened retreat ("but then, I may be mistaken"), not on thesis

## Examples
- "It is not such a foolish thing, after all, to indulge in a little bantering." (*The Remains of the Day*, 1989, Stevens)
- "What is the point in worrying oneself too much about what one could or could not have done to control the course one's life took?" (*The Remains of the Day*, 1989)
- "Indeed — why should I not admit it? — in that moment my heart was breaking." (*The Remains of the Day*, 1989)
- "We took away their childhood when we won that battle." (*Never Let Me Go*, 2005, Miss Emily)
- "I keep thinking about this river somewhere, with the water moving really fast. And these two people in the water, trying to hold onto each other… in the end it's just too much. The current's too strong." (*Never Let Me Go*, 2005, Tommy)
- "It had been a long time since I had thought about those days, and it took some effort to recall them now." (*An Artist of the Floating World*, 1986, Ono)
- "I do believe the sun can always find a way to reach us." (*Klara and the Sun*, 2021, Klara)

## Don't / Over-mimic
- **Failure mode**: LLM flattens register into generic "literary British formal" — adds ornate vocabulary, Victorian-pastiche syntax, metaphor-heavy clauses, states hidden feeling ("he felt a deep sorrow he could not name") instead of letting the hedge perform the feeling. Pastiche replaces restraint
- **Mitigation**: "Hedge, don't ornament. Never name the feeling the narrator is hiding."
